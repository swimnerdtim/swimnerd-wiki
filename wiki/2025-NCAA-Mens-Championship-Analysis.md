**Reaction Time Standards:**
- Elite: 0.00-0.20s

- **Danger zone:** >0.30s or negative (DQ risk)

### 800 Free Relay Benchmarks

**Championship Performance Tiers (Men):**

**Record Territory (Sub-6:00):**
- Average splits: 1:29-1:30 per 200
- First 100: 43.5-44.0
- Consistency: <2.0s variance

**NCAA Finals (Top 8, 6:00-6:10):**
- Average splits: 1:30-1:33 per 200
- First 100: 43.5-45.5
- Consistency: <3.0s variance

**Qualifying Standard (6:10-6:20):**
- Average splits: 1:33-1:35 per 200
- First 100: 44.0-46.0

### Training Applications

**"Sub-6:00 Builder" Set:**
1. 4×200 @ 1:30 base (relay starts, positions 2-4 have +0.3 flying start advantage)
2. Track first 100 split (goal: 43.5-44.5)
3. Negative split each 200 (second 100 faster than first)
4. Rest: 3:00 between swims
5. Mental focus: Position 3-4 swimmers must hold pace under fatigue

**Medley Relay Exchange Drill:**
- 8×50 @ relay exchange speed
- Measure reaction times with timing system
- Goal: Consistent 0.10-0.25s reactions
- Penalty for early takeoff (<0.00): Team does 10 burpees

## Coaching Observations

### What Wins NCAA Relays

**1. Zero Weak Links**
- California's 800 free: All four legs 1:29-1:31
- Texas medley: Every leg was championship-caliber

**2. The Anchor Matters**
- Chris Guiliano (Texas): 17.80 free split = race-winning
- Lucas Henveaux (Cal): 1:31.47 anchor held off Texas

**3. Relay Discipline**
- 3 DQs in men's medley (Florida, Michigan, Virginia Tech)
- Early takeoffs and stroke violations cost NCAA titles
- **Practice exchanges with video review**

### Red Flags to Avoid

❌ **Reaction Time Drift:** If your team averages >0.30s on exchanges, you're leaving 0.5-1.0s on the blocks  
❌ **Inconsistent Splits:** >3s variance between fastest/slowest legs = not a championship relay  
❌ **Weak Anchors:** Don't put your 4th-best swimmer in position 4. Pressure matters.
