# Open Water Swimming For Pool Coaches

**Source:** Open Water Swimming - Steven Munatones (270 pages, Human Kinetics, 2011)

## Why Pool Coaches Should Care About Open Water

**The growing market:**
- 3,600+ open water events in 84+ countries (as of 2011, likely 5,000+ by 2025)
- Olympic sport since 2008 (10K marathon swim)
- Triathlon swim legs (Olympic sport since 2000)
- Fastest-growing demographic: Women over 40

**Key insight for coaches:**  
Open water skills ≠ pool skills. Your fastest pool swimmer may struggle in open water. This is a SEPARATE skill set that requires specific training.

---

## Three Categories of Open Water Swimming

### 1. Short-Distance Swims (Under 5K / 3.1 miles)

**Examples:**
- Triathlon swim legs (750m - 1500m)
- 1 mile ocean swims
- 5K charity swims

**Key characteristics:**
- High-intensity, race-pace efforts
- Tactical positioning critical
- Minimal feeding (if any)
- Wetsuit legal in most cases

**Training focus:**
- Speed endurance
- Sighting skills
- Pack swimming tactics
- Starts and turns (water entry/exit)

---

### 2. Middle-Distance Swims (5K - 25K / 3.1 - 15.5 miles)

**Examples:**
- 10K Olympic marathon swim
- 10 mile charity events
- Multi-hour swims

**Key characteristics:**
- Sustained aerobic effort (1.5 - 6 hours)
- In-race feeding required
- Weather/conditions become major factor
- Wetsuit sometimes legal (varies by race)

**Training focus:**
- Aerobic base building
- Feeding practice
- Temperature acclimation
- Mental endurance

---

### 3. Marathon Swims (Over 25K / 15.5 miles)

**Examples:**
- English Channel (21 miles)
- Catalina Channel (20.2 miles)
- Manhattan Island Marathon Swim (28.5 miles)

**Key characteristics:**
- 6-24+ hour swims
- No wetsuit allowed (channel rules)
- Hypothermia risk
- Support crew required

**Training focus:**
- Extreme endurance
- Cold water acclimation
- Mental toughness
- Crew coordination

## Open Water vs. Pool: Key Differences

### Environmental Factors

**Water temperature:**
- Pool: 78-82°F (25.5-27.8°C) - controlled
- Open water: 35-85°F (1.7-29.4°C) - variable

**Visibility:**
- Pool: Crystal clear, see bottom
- Open water: 0-50 feet visibility, sometimes zero

**Conditions:**
- Pool: Calm, predictable
- Open water: Currents, waves, chop, tides

**Course:**
- Pool: Black line to follow, walls every 25-50m
- Open water: No guideline, sighting required

### Skill Differences

| Skill | Pool | Open Water |
|-------|------|------------|
| **Breathing** | Every 3-5 strokes, bilateral | Adaptive - may need every stroke to sight |
| **Pacing** | Pace clock, intervals | RPE (rate of perceived exertion), no clock |
| **Navigation** | Black line | Sighting buoys, landmarks, sun position |
| **Starts** | Blocks or wall push | Beach run, pontoon dive, water start |
| **Turns** | Wall flip turn | Buoy rounding (sharp turn, body position change) |
| **Pack dynamics** | Lane discipline | Physical contact, drafting, positioning |

## Building Open Water Skills in the Pool

### 1. Sighting Drills

**Why it matters:** Must locate buoys without stopping

**Pool drill: "Water Polo Head-Up Freestyle"**
- 10x100 @ 1:30
- Every 4th stroke, lift head (eyes above water) to see forward
- Minimize body drop (kick harder during sight)
- Goal: Maintain pace within 3-5s of normal 100 time

**Progression:**
- Week 1-2: Sight every 6 strokes
- Week 3-4: Sight every 4 strokes  
- Week 5+: Sight every 2-3 strokes (race simulation)

### 2. Pack Swimming

**Why it matters:** Open water is contact sport - arms, legs, bodies collide

**Pool drill: "Controlled Chaos"**
- 4-6 swimmers per lane
- 4x200 @ 3:00
- All start together (simulates mass start)
- Swim in tight pack (intentional contact OK)
- Rotate positions: front, back, side

**Safety rule:** No intentional hitting, but incidental contact expected

### 3. Bilateral Breathing Under Pressure

**Why it matters:** Waves may force breathing on "wrong" side

**Pool drill: "Forced Bilateral"**
- 8x100 @ 1:30
- Odd 100s: Breathe only RIGHT
- Even 100s: Breathe only LEFT
- Goal: Maintain same pace regardless of breathing side

**Advanced:** Alternate breathing side every 25m

### 4. Buoy Turns

**Why it matters:** Sharp turns around buoys without flip turn

**Pool drill: "Open Turn Sprint"**
- 8x50 @ 1:00
- Sprint to wall
- Open turn (touch wall with hand, pivot 180°, push off)
- Sprint back
- Goal: Minimize time lost on turn vs. flip turn

**Coaching cue:** Keep head down, rotate hips quickly, explosive push-off

### 5. Drafting Practice

**Why it matters:** Swimming in someone's wake saves 10-30% energy

**Pool drill: "Follow the Leader"**
- 3 swimmers per lane
- 10x100 @ 1:30
- Leader swims steady pace
- Swimmers 2 & 3 draft (swim at leader's feet, 1-2 feet back)
- Rotate positions every 2x100

**Coaching points:**
- Drafting swimmer: Stay close but don't touch leader's feet
- Feel the "pull" of the wake
- Adjust stroke rate to match leader without overworking

## Freestyle Technique Adaptations for Open Water

### Head Position: Higher Than Pool

**Pool freestyle:** Head neutral, eyes down, waterline at hairline  
**Open water:** Head slightly higher to sight, waterline at eyebrows

**Why:** Need to see forward for navigation, buoys, other swimmers

**Training adaptation:**
- 2-3x per week: Add 500-1000m of head-up freestyle
- Focus: Maintain core stability, avoid dropped hips

### Stroke Rate: Variable vs. Constant

**Pool freestyle:** Consistent stroke rate (e.g., 75-85 SPM)  
**Open water:** Adaptive rate based on conditions

**Examples:**
- **Calm water:** Lower rate, longer glide (efficiency)
- **Choppy water:** Higher rate, shorter strokes (stability)
- **Sprint to buoy:** Maximum rate (positioning)

**Training drill:**
- 12x100 @ 1:30
- Odd 100s: Low rate (60-70 SPM) - long, smooth strokes
- Even 100s: High rate (80-90 SPM) - quick, choppy strokes
- Goal: Maintain similar pace at both rates

### Kick: Power Reserve vs. Constant Propulsion

**Pool freestyle:** Steady 2-beat or 6-beat kick  
**Open water:** Save legs for positioning moves, buoy turns, finish sprint

**Training adaptation:**
- Build kick endurance: 3000m kick per week
- But practice minimal kick in distance sets (save energy)
- Sprint sets with full kick (simulate buoy positioning)

## Feeding & Hydration Training

### For Middle-Distance (5K-25K) Swimmers

**In-race feeding:**
- Every 20-30 minutes
- Carbohydrate gels, sports drinks, or both
- Must not stop swimming (treading water = DQ in some races)

**Pool training drill: "Feed While Swimming"**
- 10x200 @ 3:00
- Every 3rd 200: Grab cup from deck during swim (coach places at wall)
- Tread water 5-10s, drink, continue swimming
- Goal: Minimize time lost during feed

**What to practice:**
- Grabbing cup/bottle without stopping
- Drinking while treading (head up)
- Discarding container (designated feed zone)

### For Marathon Swimmers (25K+)

**Feeding strategy:**
- Every 30-45 minutes
- Mix of solid food (banana, energy bars) and liquids
- Handed from kayak or boat via feeding stick

**Pool drill: "Kayak Feed Simulation"**
- Coach stands at pool edge with water bottle on stick
- Swimmer approaches, treads, grabs bottle
- Drinks 4-8 oz, hands back, resumes swimming
- Practice: 5-10 feeds per 5000m swim

**Coaching tip:** Train stomach to tolerate food while swimming. Start with liquids only, progress to semi-solids.

## Temperature Acclimation Protocols

### Cold Water Exposure (For 10K+ Open Water)

**Physiological adaptation takes 2-4 weeks**

**Week 1:** 15 min cold showers daily  
**Week 2:** 20 min cold showers + 10 min open water swim  
**Week 3:** 30 min open water swim (60-65°F)  
**Week 4:** 45-60 min open water swim

**Safety rules:**
- Never swim alone in cold water
- Monitor for hypothermia symptoms (confusion, slurred speech)
- Exit water immediately if shivering uncontrollably

### Warm Water Adaptation (For Tropical Events)

**Challenge:** Overheating, not hypothermia

**Protocol:**
- Train indoors with minimal ventilation (if safe)
- Wear extra cap (simulate sun exposure)
- Reduce water intake slightly (train body to tolerate mild dehydration)

**Race day:** Apply sunscreen to shoulders, back of neck. Hydrate aggressively.

## Race Day Tactics (Pool Coach Perspective)

### Pre-Race Warm-Up

**Pool meet warm-up:** 1500-2000m, progressive pace  
**Open water warm-up:** 400-800m MAX (conserve energy, already in race environment)

**Warm-up goals:**
1. Test water temperature (adjust expectations)
2. Check visibility (how far can you see?)
3. Feel current/waves (plan pacing)
4. Sight landmarks (know where to aim)

### Start Strategy

**Beach start (shallow water):**
- Run until waist-deep
- Dolphin dive (push off bottom, dive forward) until water too deep
- Begin swimming

**Pontoon/deep water start:**
- Tread water before gun
- Explosive first 10 strokes (get clear of pack)
- Settle into rhythm by 100m

**Coaching cue:** First 200-400m is chaos. Stay calm, find clear water, THEN settle into race pace.

### Drafting Strategy

**Where to draft:**
- **Directly behind swimmer:** Maximum draft (20-30% energy savings) but can't see ahead
- **At swimmer's hip:** Moderate draft (10-15% savings), better visibility

**When to draft:**
- Middle of race (conserve energy)
- When pace feels comfortable but you want to save energy for finish

**When NOT to draft:**
- Last 500-1000m (position for sprint)
- If lead pack is pulling away (stay with them)

### Finish Sprint

**When to start:**
- Short-distance (under 5K): Last 300-500m
- Middle-distance (5K-10K): Last 500-800m
- Marathon (25K+): Last 1000-1500m

**Tactic:**
- Move to outside (clear water, avoid getting boxed in)
- Increase stroke rate by 10-15 SPM
- Full 6-beat kick
- Sight frequently (ensure you're on direct line to finish)

**Base Phase (12 weeks out)**

| Day | Pool Workout | Open Water |
|-----|--------------|------------|
| Mon | 5000m aerobic base (mix IM, kick, pull) | - |
| Tue | 4000m: 20x200 @ 3:00 (build pace) | - |
| Wed | 3000m easy recovery | - |
| Thu | 6000m: 3000 pull, 3000 swim (negative split) | - |
| Fri | REST | - |
| Sat | 3000m technique focus | 3000m open water (easy pace) |
| Sun | - | 5000-8000m open water (race pace) |

**Total:** 21,000m pool + 8,000-11,000m open water = **29,000-32,000m/week**

**Peak Phase (4 weeks out)**

| Day | Pool Workout | Open Water |
|-----|--------------|------------|
| Mon | 6000m: 30x200 @ 2:45 (hold race pace) | - |
| Tue | 4000m: Speed work (50s, 100s, 200s) | - |
| Wed | REST or 2000m easy | - |
| Thu | 5000m: 2x2500 @ race effort | - |
| Fri | 3000m easy + sighting drills | - |
| Sat | 2000m technique | 6000-10,000m open water (race simulation with feeds) |
| Sun | REST | - |

**Total:** 20,000m pool + 6,000-10,000m open water = **26,000-30,000m/week**

| Day | Workout |
|-----|---------|

**Coach's Bottom Line:**

> Open water swimming is NOT just "pool swimming outdoors." It requires specific skills (sighting, pack tactics, variable pacing) and specific training (temperature acclimation, feeding practice). If your swimmers compete in open water, dedicate 20-30% of training to open-water-specific drills and swims.

**Key Coaching Principles:**

1. **Train the skills in the pool:** Sighting, bilateral breathing, pack swimming, buoy turns
2. **Train in the environment:** 1-2x per week minimum in actual open water
3. **Acclimate to temperature:** Start 4-6 weeks before race
4. **Practice feeding:** Middle-distance and marathon swimmers MUST train nutrition
5. **Race tactics ≠ pool tactics:** Drafting, positioning, navigating are learned skills, not instincts

**Your job as a coach:** Prepare your swimmers for the SPECIFIC challenges of open water, not just general swimming fitness. Pool speed helps, but open water intelligence wins races.
