# Butterfly Training & Technique - Bob Bowman

## Overview

Bob Bowman, a former 50-point butterfly specialist at Florida State, has developed one of the most sophisticated approaches to butterfly training in swimming history. His methods produced Michael Phelps' legendary butterfly and continue to shape elite flyers today.

## Foundation: Quality Over Quantity

### The Limited Volume Approach

Bowman's philosophy fundamentally challenges conventional butterfly training:

- **Michael Phelps never swam more than 75 fly straight in practice** (mostly 100s max)
- Young Phelps didn't swim 200 fly in a meet until age 13-14
- First 200 fly ever: went 2:14 in training, then 2:04.68 (NAG record) at the meet

> "I wanted to refine his stroke and to get that to happen I didn't want him swimming a lot of sloppy butterfly in practice. Whenever he did do fly in practice it was under conditions where he could do the full stroke and do it well."

### The "Baby Stroke" Rule

If swimmers couldn't kick every cycle and maintain perfect technique, they weren't allowed to swim full butterfly. Phelps would sometimes intentionally do "baby stroke" (setup + weak kick) just to annoy Bowman.

## The Kick Philosophy

### Hold Water on Your Feet

The defining characteristic of elite butterfly kicking:

- Push yourself forward by "holding water on your feet"
- Don't just "wag up and down"
- Think of it like cracking a whip with your feet
- **The up-kick is super important** (not just the down-kick)

### No Board Kicking for Fly

**Bowman's rule**: Never fly kick on a board

**Why?** Board kicking is too shallow—swimmers only kick one direction (down) and miss the crucial up-kick.

**Instead, do fly kick:**
- On your side
- On your back  
- Vertically
- Underwater in streamline

All regular kicking = flutter kick on boards. Butterfly kick requires different positions to develop proper mechanics.

## Signature Workouts

### The "Wobble Set" (John Sieben Set)

**Original from Laurie Lawrence:**
- 30 x 100 free on 1:30 LCM (hold under :53)
- After each 100, climb out
- Dive 50 fly **as fast as possible**
- Repeat 30 times

**The wobble**: Happens around 5 left—if swimmers aren't wobbling when they climb out, they didn't work hard enough.

**Recovery time**: Takes Michael Phelps 3-4 days to recover from this set.

### The 45 x 50 Stroke Control Set

**Structure:**
- 10 x 50 fly on :40 (stroke control - specific stroke count)
- 5 x 50 fly on :35 (MAX EFFORT - all-out)
- Repeat 3 rounds with NO REST between sections

**Phelps' numbers:**
- Stroke control: 6 strokes + 6 strokes (holding :28)
- Fast 50s: :22-flat (at American record pace)

**Purpose**: Maintains technique while building massive aerobic capacity. The repeats are short, but the work is big.

### The Video Game Set (20-30 x 50 Fly)

All 50s off blocks, descending effort until swimmers "wobble" at the wall. The goal is finding that maximal challenge point.

## Technique Principles

### Streamline as Foundation

From Bowman's yoga practice influence: **Length is everything**

**Looking for:**
- Flat back (not arched lower back)
- Belly button drawn toward spine
- Head in neutral position
- Arms pressed back
- Everything "locked out" on top
- **LENGTH** from fingertips to toes

### Maintaining Speed Through the Wall

**The Michael Phelps difference:**

> "If we were swimming long course, Michael would push off with other guys and be exactly the same as them until the last five meters, and then at the wall he would be a body length ahead. He just would not slow down."

This appears in other elite butterflyers Bowman has coached:
- **Chase Kalisz** - doesn't slow down through walls
- **Hali Flickinger** - maintains speed, beats training partners at the wall

**The key**: It's not about going faster—it's about *not slowing down*.

## Race Strategy: 100 vs 200 Fly

### Two Different Events

**Michael Phelps was fundamentally a 400 swimmer**, which shaped his fly approach:

**100 Fly Strategy:**
- Never had a particularly fast first 50
- Couldn't sprint like pure sprinters
- Strategy: Get first 50 "fast enough" without using too much energy
- **Beijing 2008**: Under 24.0 at 50 = guaranteed win (he went 24.00, won by .01)
- Goal was always **fastest second 50** in the field

**200 Fly Approach:**
- Train it like a 400 free
- Energy systems are nearly identical
- Need strong 400 in the bag (Phelps swam 1500m once per year through 2008)
- In long course: 200 yard free = 100 meter event; 500 yards = 200 meters

### The 400 Free Connection

> "If you want to train a really good 200 butterfly, I feel like you have to have at least a strong 400 in your bag somewhere."

For middle-distance flyers, the 400 free is the primary training vehicle for the 200 fly.

## Training Philosophy Evolution

### Short Course Advantages for Butterfly

Bowman actually prefers short course for training fly, IM, and breast:

**Why?**
- Maintains technique better
- Can do better aerobic training (higher range)
- Breaks up the work so swimmers maintain intensity longer
- Less "survival swimming"

### Intensity Requirements

> "I don't feel like there's a lot of value in doing easy butterfly. It should be intense and at the top of your energy level. If you're gonna do your best stroke, it's almost a maximal effort."

**Acceptable fly work:**
- Stroke control (technical focus at moderate intensity)
- Descending sets
- Max effort repeats

**Not valuable:**
- Easy, sloppy fly for volume
- Long continuous swims with degrading technique

## Core Training for Butterfly

### Early Phelps Era (Pre-2004)

**Bodyweight only** until 2004 Olympics:
- Tennessee dryland circuit (Ray Bussard)
- Medicine ball program (Mike Betterman) - **1 hour, 5 days/week** in 2003-04
- All core-based, functional movements
- Extremely difficult intensity

### Modern Integration

With yoga influence, emphasis on:
- Core strength for maintaining length
- Functional strength that supports the stroke
- Balance and body control

## Developing Young Butterflyers

### Age Group Progression

**Bowman's approach:**
1. Master kick mechanics first (no board kicking)
2. Short, high-quality fly in practice
3. Perfect technique before adding volume
4. Build through 100s before attempting 200s

**For a 2:16/2:00 level swimmer:**
- Set reasonable goal (e.g., 1:56 for a 2:00 swimmer)
- In practice, work the pieces:
  - If goal is :56 → target :28 on 50s during stroke control
  - Slower intervals at :32
  - Fast efforts at :25-26

### The Curtis Dow Example

Came to Michigan: **1:54**  
Two years later: **1:43** (NCAA Finals)

**How?** Consistent work, proper training systems, addressing limitations away from the pool, breaking down the race into manageable pieces.

## Common Fixes

### For Arm-Dominant Swimmers (Hali Flickinger Example)

**Problem**: Starting strong with arms, then "petering out" when nothing left to add.

**Solution:**
1. Get legs involved **every cycle**
2. Eliminate cycles where legs drop out
3. Use breathing pattern to enforce rhythm: **2-up, 1-down entire race**
4. Breathing pattern calms swimmers down
5. Better horizontal position throughout
6. Legs carry speed through the race

### Building Second 50 Speed (100 Fly)

The key for middle-distance swimmers doing 100 fly:
- First 50: Fast enough, relaxed enough
- Second 50: Where the race is won
- Pride yourself on **fastest second 50 in the field**

## Mental Approach

### Michael vs Leon: Different Athletes

**Michael Phelps:**
- Needed to get intense, almost angry
- "Cavic said there's no way you should win" → immediate motivation
- Thrived on external pressure and competition

**Leon Marchand:**
- Needs to be laid back to swim fast
- Gets worse when intense
- Pre-race: "Have fun out there"
- Indifferent to external comments or pressure

> "If I coached Leon like I coached Michael, he would quit in two days. If I coached Michael like I coach Leon, he wouldn't have done anything near what he did."

**The lesson**: Elite butterfly requires understanding the individual's psychology as much as their physiology.

## The Future of 200 Fly

### 2028 LA Olympics Prediction

**Bowman's call**: Under 1:50 to win gold

**Why?**
- Swimmers going :48-:49 with open turns (200 free speed)
- Similar to Kieren Perkins in the mile

**Profile of future champion:**
- Someone going :49 in 100 fly
- Will cruise out in :51-52
- Won't "hammer it out"
- Basically a fast 100 flyer with 400 free endurance

### Maximizing Underwater Work

Still room for improvement in:
- Underwater kick efficiency
- Breakout timing
- Pushing limits on underwater distance

For elite swimmers on the bubble (like Luca Urlando), the breakthrough often comes from maximizing underwaters and getting the legs "really popping."

## Key Principles Summary

1. **Quality always beats quantity** - Perfect technique in short repeats > sloppy volume
2. **The kick makes the stroke** - Continuous, powerful kicking every cycle
3. **Train the 400 to race the 200** - Build aerobic base first
4. **Don't slow down > go faster** - Maintain speed through walls and into finish
5. **Short course has advantages** - Better for maintaining technique under fatigue
6. **Every swimmer is different** - Adjust intensity, motivation, approach to the individual
7. **Breathing creates rhythm** - Use breathing patterns to enforce proper mechanics
8. **Core strength enables everything** - Length and body control start with the core
