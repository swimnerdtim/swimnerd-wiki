# The Coaching Mastermind: Bob Bowman on Michael Phelps & Leon Marchand

*Source: Exponential Athlete Podcast - Full Career Interview*

**The Legacy Transfer:** Leon Marchand broke Michael Phelps's 200 IM Olympic record (set 2008 Beijing) at 2024 Paris Olympics. Both athletes = disciples of Bob Bowman.

---

## Similarities: The Champions' Playbook

### 1. Big, Specific Goals Broken Into Daily Pieces

Both have **audacious goals** far in the future, but **process-focused daily execution.**

> "You can achieve any goal if you make the first step small enough." — Jordan Peterson (quoted by Bob)

**How to eat an elephant:** One bite at a time.

**Focus hierarchy:**
- Dream goal (vague, far away, emotional)
- Long-term goals (1-4 years, specific but flexible)
- Short-term goals (6 months, very specific)
- **Right now** (this 500 free, this turn, this practice)

> "What is right in front of us. That's the only thing that you really have control of. You don't have control over tomorrow or four years down the road. You do have pretty good control of what's going to happen in the next two hours." — Bob Bowman

### 2. High Average Performance (Consistent 8/10)

Both **rarely give 10/10 practice**, but **almost every practice is an 8.**

- Very rarely substandard (<6/10)
- If they have a 2-3 day, it's **one day**, then right back to 8s
- **Never spend weeks** at substandard practice

**The math:** Consistent 8s over time >> occasional 10s with lots of 5s

### 3. Value Individual Practice

Both recognize that **every practice matters.**

- Want to get as much as possible out of each session
- Almost never leave pool thinking they went backwards
- Find **some way** to get something out of that practice

### 4. Technique Obsession

Both constantly trying to improve technique.

### 5. Underwater Dominance

Both mastered **dolphin kick underwater** better than anyone else to date.

**Why it matters:**
- Every length of the pool = opportunity to use it
- In streamline underwater, you're moving **faster than you will ever swim on the surface**
- Faster you can do it + longer you can do it = way faster overall

**Michael:** First person to ever use it to near its potential

**Leon:** Taking it to a new level altogether

### 6. Relax and Focus Under Pressure

**The rare ability:** Performance level **rises** to meet pressure, doesn't drop.

**Normal athletes:** Performance drops under pressure (goal = don't drop too far)

**Michael & Leon:** Get their **very best performance** under **highest pressure**

> "When the critical moment comes and you're swimming for a gold medal, they can get the very best performance under the highest pressure. That's quite rare." — Bob Bowman

## How Bob Constructs Pressure Performance

### Part Innate, Part Constructed

**Construction methods:**

1. **Artificial stress in training**
   - "On the next one I want to see under 50 seconds."
   - "We're not leaving here till you do one under 50 seconds."
   - Sometimes they do it right away, sometimes they keep trying

2. **Low-risk adversity scenarios**
   - Breaking Michael's goggles (Australia meet)
   - Making drivers show up late
   - Training with Navy SEALs (chaos preparation)

3. **Olympics-specific chaos prep**
   - Buses don't come on time
   - Buses go to wrong place
   - Meals aren't good
   - Unpredictable venue issues

> "The Olympics are a large media event with some athletic contests sprinkled in. You're just in this environment where there's billions of people watching around the world. You have to do your thing. The swimming, the 200 free, is not going to be a problem. But it's doing it in that environment that is the problem." — Bob Bowman

## Differences: Apples and Oranges (Both Grapefruit)

### Physical

**Michael Phelps:**
- 6'4"-6'5"
- Massive, strong
- Prototypical swimming machine

**Leon Marchand:**
- 5'11" (Bob's height)
- Not very physically imposing
- Long arms, big hands/feet
- "When you see him you're like, 'Wow, he's not that tall, he's not that big.'"

### Demeanor

**Michael Phelps:**
- Tiger Woods-level intensity
- Incredibly intense about everything
- **Had to get intense** to do his job
- Very fiery

**Leon Marchand:**
- Most laid-back person in the world
- Has to be **relaxed and laughing and smiling**
- **Gets nervous when he gets intense**
- Lowkey, friendly (how he was raised)

### Pre-Practice Routine

**Michael:**
- Come in early before practice
- Sit with Bob
- Bob goes through entire practice
- Explains exactly what he's expecting on every part
- Michael thinks on it, then does it
- **If Bob changed anything during practice: NO BUENO**
- "What was on that paper and what we had decided is what we had decided. He was prepared for that."

**Leon:**
- Doesn't want to know the practice until **one minute before**
- Bob reads it to him
- Gets in and starts doing it
- **Bob can change things mid-practice** - Leon is fine with it
- "Let's just change this and do three of these instead of four." → "Okay."

### Learning Style

**Michael:**
- Visual learner (wants to see it + be told about it)
- Kinesthetic (sometimes has to feel it)

**Leon:**
- Kinesthetic learner (has to feel it)
- If it doesn't feel right: "Try this with your arm?"
- "Yeah, that's right."
- Doesn't want to analyze it, just feel it

### Coaching Approach Required

**Michael:**
- Bob had to be pretty strong with him
- Challenge him
- Michael would push back
- Bob would push harder back

**Leon:**
- Bob: "I think you ought to do this."
- Leon: "Okay." (Just goes and does it)

> "If I coached Leon like I coached Michael, he would quit in like two days. If I coached Michael like I coached Leon, he wouldn't have done anything near what he did." — Bob Bowman

### Stroke Strengths

**Michael:**
- #1 Butterfly
- #2 Backstroke
- Weakest: Breaststroke (had to work long time on it, eventually got competitive)

**Leon:**
- #1 Butterfly
- #2 Breaststroke
- Weakest: **Freestyle** (odd because you train it so much)
- Backstroke is quite good

**Significance:** Much bigger advantage to be good at breaststroke in an IM than other strokes.

## Michael's 5-Year Streak: How It Actually Happened

### It Wasn't Pre-Planned

**Age 11 (North Baltimore):**
- Offered 7 practices/week (all afternoons + Saturday/Sunday mornings)
- Didn't expect people to come to all
- Michael came to all (wanted to get good + mom needed him to burn energy - "wild child")

**Age 13-14:**
- Started doing some morning practices (not much)
- Just built the habit of training every day

**Age 15 (2000 Olympics):**
Bob thought: "I'm not sure he's going to make it. He's working really hard. If he doesn't, I want him to have something to look forward to."

**The pivot:**
- Entered him in World Cups (January 2001, Europe)
- To prepare: Trained through Christmas holiday
- **Trained on Christmas**
- Didn't want to do lots of doubles, so just trained every day

**Result:** Did pretty well.

**Spring 2001:**
- Made World Championship team that summer
- "We just kind of kept training. It wasn't like 'I don't know, we just kept training.'"
- Wanted to break a world record → did it in the spring

**Fukuoka (World Championships):**
On the bus, older guys on Team USA:
- "Phelps, you train every day?"
- Michael: "Yeah, I don't know any different. We just train every day."
- "You train on Christmas?"
- "Yeah, it's only one time."

**Bob and Michael looked at each other:** "We're just going to keep this going for the **psychological effect**. These people were **terrified** of him."

That fed into 2004 Olympics. Once they got going 3-4 years: "No, we got to keep it going now."

**Clarification:** Sometimes it would be "swim an easy 1500" just so he swam that day. Not training hard every day.

**Michael's calculation:** "In a given year I get 52 more days than these guys who don't train on Sundays. Over four years that's 200 days. That's almost another six or seven months of training."

## Leon's Breaks: The European Difference

### Leon NEEDS Long Breaks

**After 2024 Paris Olympics:** 6 weeks off.

Bob knew this when Leon came.

> "I've never hesitated to let Leon take time off. It works for some people - absolutely grinding. For some people it's completely different." — Bob Bowman

### The Broken French Swimmer Story

Bob had a very good French swimmer train with him for a year (Olympic gold medalist).

**Bob's approach:** Trained him like Michael.

**Result:** "I **broke him**. Could not deal with it."

**Lesson learned:** When Leon came, Bob said "I'm not going to make those same mistakes."

**New approach with Leon:**
- Train him hard
- **Always have a little something** - never push to absolute limit where he doesn't have anything
- Always going to have **a little bit in reserve**
- Kept upping volume/intensity, but always something in reserve
- **Never pushed him over the edge** like Michael could handle

**Michael:** Could go over the edge, be completely exhausted, then be asked to do something else. Next day he'd be fine. Mentally could handle that.

**Leon:** If Bob pushed him to complete exhaustion then asked for more, "it might take weeks or he might not ever come back."

### Cultural Difference: American vs. European

**Americans (Michael):** Give everything every day. When you don't have anymore, keep giving. That's what we do.

**Europeans (Leon):** Very different. Need breaks. Keep perspective.

**American challenge:** Hard to turn it off. "I think it's wonderful, but you see a lot of really great American athletes, they can't go back to normal life because it's so different. That to me is a tough thing. It's also hard to sustain."

**Michael 2012:** Too young to quit, but what else was he going to do? No worthy goal for a while. Finally got him back to the **joy of swimming**.

Bob: "I wish I had maybe handled that part differently."

**European advantage:** Good at taking breaks, keeping it in perspective.

### Optimal Conditions vs. Worst Conditions

**European mindset (Leon had to learn):** Spend a lot of time trying to create **optimal conditions** for performance. If they don't have it, don't expect them to do anything.

**American mindset (Bob's philosophy):** Expect you to do it in the **worst conditions**.

> "My thing is I want the best possible performance in the worst possible conditions. I'm lucky here we have this Taj Mahal of a pool, but I would be just fine. Michael's training, there was nothing like that. We tried to make the everyday stuff - we don't baby them. We don't go out of our way to do stuff. They have to carry their equipment, they have to do all of this stuff. I think that's helped Leon. He's become much more self-sufficient." — Bob Bowman

**Bob's approach:** Taking the best of both cultures and combining it.

## Visualization: The Technique That Won Gold Medals

### The Foundation (Age 11, Michael Phelps)

Bob knew Michael was **super special**, going to go far. Wanted to give him every tool.

**Step 1: Progressive Relaxation**
- Found a book by Dr. Keith Bell (psychologist)
- Script for progressive relaxation technique
- "Tense up your hands... tension and relaxation..."
- Michael's mom read it to him before bedtime
- After a while: She'd read two things and he'd be asleep
- Got **so good at relaxing** he'd just completely relax instantly

**Step 2: Visualization During Relaxation**
- Go through relaxation in the middle of the day
- Play a movie in your head of how you want to swim a race

**Why it works:**
> "The brain cannot distinguish between something that's vividly imagined and something that is real. That's why people have anxiety and all kinds of things. We can use it for good." — Bob Bowman

### The Two Perspectives

Bob had Michael visualize **two ways:**

1. **Third-person:** Sitting in the stands watching yourself swim
2. **First-person:** In the water, seeing yourself swimming, feeling it

### Evolution: Videos + Scenarios

**As Michael became a very good swimmer:**

About **6 weeks before the Big Meet**, Michael would ask for **videos of his best races from the summer before.**

- Start watching those videos
- Informed his visualization (wanted to know what he was doing)
- Start visualizing **changes** he wanted to make in those races
- Practice it daily

### The Beijing Revelation (2008)

After Beijing Olympics (8 gold medals), Michael told Bob something he didn't know:

Michael visualized:
1. **The way he wanted the race to go**
2. **Various scenarios** the race could go
3. **How he would respond** to those scenarios
4. **What would happen if the race didn't go well** and he had to make a big change
5. **Negative scenarios** (goggles leak, etc.) and how he'd deal with it

**The database effect:**

> "I think what you do is you do that over and over, and by the time you stand on the block you've swum this race 300 times. Instead of the first time being the Olympics, this is like number 350 or something. He's already programmed his body to swim, and he has this database of things that he can do subconsciously if something happens in the race that he needed to change. It's very powerful." — Bob Bowman

## Neuroplasticity Technology Experiment

**The protocol:**
1. Watch a video of a skill (edited in a certain way)
2. Watch it repeatedly
3. Get in the water
4. Put on **blackout goggles** (so they wouldn't use visual cortex - biggest part of brain)
5. Do that skill

**Result:** Remarkable results.

### Most Basic Version

**8-year-olds:**
- Sit on side of pool
- "Do not say anything. Watch this Olympic swimmer swim butterfly."
- Olympian swims 3-4 lengths
- "Don't talk, don't do anything" (hard to do but they could)
- "Get in the water and swim just like that."

**Result:** Every one of them could do it.

**Why:** Seeing it live, not talking (not disrupting anything), just visualizing it, get in and do it.

> "There's a lot of stuff that we're just scratching the surface on with that." — Bob Bowman

## The Coach's Role: Creating an Environment of Excellence

> "My main goal and my main job is to create an environment of excellence on my team. If you do that, all the individuals get better, the whole team gets better, and then some special things can happen." — Bob Bowman

**Bob is protective of:**
- Team attitude
- Right people in the program
- If someone's not a good fit: **they have to go**

**Bob's recruiting pitch:**

> "If you come to Texas and you buy into the things that we are doing and asking you to do, **success will be inevitable for you**. That's the kind of environment we're going to create. You get to be a part of this team, you train with these guys, you kind of live the way they're living, you do the things that we're doing here, and your natural outcome will be success." — Bob Bowman

**It's not just Bob:**
- Whole staff
- Weight room
- Everything

**Combined energy** of everything going on.

## Dialogue While Maintaining Culture

**Athlete:** "Why are we doing this?"

**Bob:** "You should be able to ask me why we're doing anything that we do in this program and I should have an answer."

**Exception:** "One out of every 50 times the answer is going to be 'because I've been doing this for 35 years and I think it's going to work and I just try some things and I don't know.'"

**But mostly:** Everything has a reason why.

**Example dialogue:**
- "Why does somebody swimming 100 breaststroke need to do a big IM set?"
- Bob explains, or...
- "Maybe you're right, maybe we shouldn't be doing that."

**A couple times:** Bob thought about it and was like "Yeah, I don't know, probably shouldn't be doing that."

**Key:** When people feel heard and know Bob's going to come up with a reason, that works well.

## Willingness to Experiment (Get Worse Before Better)

**Common theme in greatest athletes:** Willingness to experiment + get a little bit worse before they get better.

**Average great athlete:** "I got here, I want to hold on."

**Best of the best:** "I want to break it and build it up even better."

> "The guys that are great, they know that the way they get better is change. And sometimes change takes them a little bit of time." — Bob Bowman

### Michael's Breaststroke: 3-Year Investment

**Age 11 goal meeting:**

Bob: "What if your only goal was breaststroke? You want to be ranked top 16 in your age group."

(At the time, top 16 times in every age group came out 1-2x/year)

**Year 1 (age 11):** Worked and worked. No top 16 ranking.

**Year 2 (age 12):** Kept working. No ranking.

**Year 3 (age 13):** End of season → **#1 in the 200 breast in the country**

**Key:** All along he was making improvements. He knew he was getting better. Just didn't see it in the outside rankings.

> "You have to have that mindset of delayed gratification a little bit. Best thing sports teaches you." — Bob Bowman

## Make a Deposit in the Bank

**Bob's metaphor to athletes:**

> "Make a deposit in the bank account every day. Sometimes it's pennies, sometimes it's $10, sometimes it's $100. Put everything you can and know that when you left here there's a little deposit made in the bank. Then you can withdraw it at the end of the season. You can do that over four years. Just keep making these deposits, and then we're going to take it all out of the bank in 2024." — Bob Bowman

## How Bob Studies to Get Better

### Early Career: Read Everything

Before internet existed: Read everything about coaching.

**Favorite books:**
- *The Science of Winning* by Jan Olbrecht (physiologist)
  - "Amazing physiological book"
  - Can only read a chapter at a time before your brain explodes
  - Read it 3-4 times
- *The Power of Now* by Eckhart Tolle
  - "Changed my life completely"
  - Favorite book about how to live and coach people

### Work With the Best

**Bob's strategy:** Go work with coaches who were already extremely successful and learn how they did it.

**Three Olympic coaches Bob worked with:**
1. **Paul Bergen**
2. **David Marsh**
3. **Murray Stevens**

All produced Olympic gold medals, world records.

**Bob's goal with each:** "Not to do anything except learn their system."

**Promise to himself:** "If he went out of town for two weeks, I could write a week of workouts and the athletes would think he had left them for them. I knew how he thought about it, all of these things."

**Process:**
- Learn system #1 (Paul Bergen)
- Learn system #2 (David Marsh)
- Learn system #3 (Murray Stevens)
- Identify: "These are the things I really like about this, I'm going to keep that, add this from something, add this..."

**By the time Bob was running his own program:** Had information from very successful people he could integrate.

**Why it works:**
- They've made a lot of mistakes → why make your own?
- "I make plenty, but just... it's very good to be with somebody, learn what they're doing, because they've already made a lot of mistakes and you can learn from their stuff. Just take the stuff they do that they've already tested, then you can test your own stuff."

### What He Actually Learned

**Not just X's and O's:**

Bob remembers sitting with Paul Bergen and other coaches around a table with beers, talking about **when to time weight training in relation to swimming for about 5 hours**. That was the only topic.

**Main lessons from great coaches:**
- How they ran a practice
- How they communicated with athletes
- How they motivated athletes away from the pool (in their lives)
- How they got people to buy into what they were doing
- The metaphors
- The management

**What Bob didn't like:** Some things about all three he didn't particularly like. Some were not good at certain things.

**Bob's approach:** "Tried to take the good things and leave out the other stuff. That's how you develop your own perspective."

### Current Study Habits

**Can't go work for someone now, so:**
- Talk to friends constantly
- Michael Bohl all the time ("best coach in the world in swimming")
- Read everything he can
- **Podcasts now invaluable**
- Always trying to learn something to take, or at minimum **refresh** what you're doing
- "Repackage it even if you're trying to do the same thing, make it look different"

**The pro challenge:** Coaching people for 10 years in a row - "They can't just do the same thing for 10 years."

**College advantage:** Kind of recycle, so for most of the team it's kind of new.

### Reading Outside Swimming

- Management stuff
- Psychology
- **Bill Walsh:** *The Winning Edge* ("Really helped me")
- **Football coaches:** *The Football Coach's Bible* (talks about running practices, recruiting, things they value)

> "Sports in general are kind of the same when you boil it down to the bottom line. I hear Steve Sarkisian talk about how he runs this team and it's exactly how we run this team. The standard is the standard. The process is more important than the outcome. These are the things that we value and these are the things that we are going to do every day. And that's it." — Bob Bowman

**The innovation thesis:**

> "The biggest innovations come from looking outside of your domain. There aren't really new ideas, but there's an old idea transferred into a new domain that is really powerful." — Exponential Athlete Podcast

## Sodium Bicarbonate (Baking Soda)

**Bob and Paul Bergen were using it in 1992** (with Al Seltzer to speed absorption).

**Who it's for:** 200-400 (middle distance)

**Administration:** In water, add instant iced tea to speed absorption.

**Considerations:**
- Has to be timed
- Has to sit in your stomach
- Many factors go into it

**Perfectly legal.** Most recreational athletes completely unaware.

## Lactate Testing: Validation Machine

> "There are very few things that we can do to get an objective physiological standard of any kind. That's one thing we can do." — Bob Bowman

**Other options:** Heart rate monitor, some other things, but lactate is key.

### Leon Marchand Case Study (Worlds Year 2)

**Training camp lactate testing:**

After an effort:
- **12 mmol** lactate (very high - Michael would probably never be over 10)
- 20 minutes later: **2 mmol** (1242 = ideal)
- 10 minutes before next effort: **1-2 mmol**

**What this means:**
- **High lactate production** (anaerobic power)
- **Quick clearance** (amazingly aerobically fit)

**At World Championships:** Every race he swam → **12-4-2, 12-4-2**

**If you don't do that testing, you never know** the training program is doing what you theorize it's doing.

**Athlete impact:** "That validates what we're doing. They believe in it more. They're more invested in it. They expect to do well by doing it. It's a good cycle."

## Bio-Feedback in Swimming

**Challenge:** Hard to do in the water.

**Heart rate monitors:**
- Women: Can wear Polar on chest (run through straps from back of suits, stays up)
- Men: Won't stay up (no top strap), trying ear monitor

**Lactate testing:** Have it, use it.

**Subjective data:** 3x/week - series of questions they answer. "Actually pretty good feedback."

**Whoop:** Pros using it (not college team due to "some issues").

### Carson Foster Whoop Validation

Threshold training practice on Monday:

Carson sends Bob his Whoop data.

Bob: "Damn, that's **exactly** what we're trying to do."

- Heart rates in range
- Duration in range
- The way they went up by the rounds of what we did

**More validation** that what we're doing is actually working.

> "I think we're just again at the early stages of what we can do in terms of that." — Bob Bowman

### Subjective Sleep Data

Dr. Grer (Sleep Expert, University of Arizona): Subjective sleep data you have is arguably **better** than what you're getting from devices.

**We're very good at estimating how well we rested.**

**Bob's experience:** Used to monitor sleep all the time, started judging how his day was going to be based on sleep.

**Solution:** Stopped. Doesn't wear watch at night anymore.

> "I think there's a part of that too - you kind of get 'well it says I can't do anything so I'm going to be tired.'" — Bob Bowman

Switched to Whoop because it just gives **total sleep** - if you got enough, you're at 100%.
