# Discipline, Mastery & The Evolution of Coaching - Bob Bowman (with Ryan Holiday)

## On Taking Risks & Making Decisions

### The Dr. Ellen Langer Principle

**What helped Bowman decide to leave ASU for Texas**:

> "Instead of stressing about making the right decision, make the decision right."

**The philosophy**:
- Nobody has enough information to know if a decision is truly "right"
- You can't live two lives—only your life
- If he stayed at ASU, no way of knowing if that's better, worse, or the same
- If he goes to Texas, same thing—no way of knowing the alternative

**The approach**: "I know I have enough information that Texas is going to be great for swimming and I'm going to be able to do some things there. I'll just make it right."

### The Football Coach Analogy

**Coaches should go for it on fourth down more often** than they do. They should take risks more often. But they don't because:
- They watch SportsCenter
- They don't want to be the idiot who tried it and it didn't work
- Don't want to answer for it in the press conference
- So they do the "safer" thing—even though statistically it's actually the MORE dangerous thing

**Career changes are the same**:
- We're not motivated by whether it won't work
- The consequences of not working actually aren't that significant
- We're motivated by the fear of **seeming stupid for having tried**

**Bowman**: "I've looked stupid for many years now, so that doesn't bother me at all. I'm not very risk-averse. I just go for it."

### Why Success Should Make You Less Risk-Averse

**The paradox**: Once you've accomplished things, you should become LESS risk-averse (you've proven everything). But the opposite happens—we become MORE risk-averse because we don't want to lose what we have.

**We tell ourselves**: "Once I secure myself financially, once I do X/Y/Z, THEN I'll be able to take risks."

**But actually**: We become less willing to roll the dice because we don't want to be "the idiot who lost what we had."

**Bowman's freedom**:

> "I can walk out this door and never coach another minute of my life and I am good. My career's good. I feel good about it. I've achieved some things. I'm doing this because I want to just see how far I could push myself and work with more people and help them do their thing."

### The Ex-Senator Principle

**The article about senators**: Most were unhappy while serving, but after they leave:
- They're doing great
- Usually more financially successful
- Happier
- Pursue different things

**The lesson**: We want to keep things the way they are, even though everything good in our life came from some sort of change—often unpleasant endings (got dumped, got fired).

**The paradox**: We wouldn't have gotten HERE without something getting us NOT THERE. Yet we get here and think "I have to keep it this way or I will die."

## The Ancient Wisdom

### The Wild Horse Parable

**The ancient Chinese story** (Bowman thinks about all the time):

- Wild horse shows up → "What a great thing!" → Maybe.
- Son gets on it, breaks his leg → "Terrible thing!" → Maybe.
- Son doesn't have to go fight the war → "What a great thing!" → Maybe.

**We'll see.** You don't know. It takes a while for results or consequences to be made clear.

**The freedom**: The ability to roll with those punches.

### Marcus Aurelius: The Power of No Opinion

**What Bowman does now** (drives people he works with insane):

**"Don't have an opinion on stuff."**

From Daily Stoic, Marcus Aurelius teachings.

**People**: "Well you have to think something."  
**Bowman**: "No I don't."

**Marcus Aurelius**:

> "Remember, you always have the power to have no opinion about something. Things are not asking to be judged by you."

Same as the Eastern parable: **We'll see.**

**What it means**: You're not saying it's positive or negative. You're saying that **you having an opinion about it isn't the determining factor** as to whether this is good or bad or not.

**Bowman**: "It's very freeing. It doesn't affect me or I'm not going to get into it. I understand both sides, I have no opinion."

### The Myers-Briggs Challenge

**Bowman's type**: INTJ

"It was before long before I started doing reading any of this stuff. So it's been good for me to sort of discipline myself that way—that I don't have to weigh in on everything."

**The easier first step**: Maybe there's some magical Zen place where you actually don't have opinions, but an easier step is to **stop verbalizing so many opinions**.

"I don't have to say it. Even if I have one, I don't have to tell you my opinion. You do yours."

### As You Get Older

**If you're not becoming**:
- Less judgmental
- More "live and let live"
- "Don't yuck other people's yum"

**Younger, more insecure version**:
- "No no no, not only do I have my opinion, but I need you to have my opinion"
- "You having a separate opinion is an indictment of my opinion"
- "I can't handle that"

**Maturity**: "If you like that, go for it. I guess it's good to you."

## The "Do Your Work" Origin Story

### Colorado Springs: The Most Boring Place in the World

**The setting**: ASU pool being resurfaced, spent 6-8 weeks at Colorado Springs Olympic Training Center.

**Daily routine**:
- Only thing to do: work out, coach practice
- Weight room, workout, coach
- Repeat

**One day**: Bowman was exhausted. Talking to Logan Hiero (assistant).

**Bowman**: "Dang, I don't even know if I want to go to the weight room today. Maybe I'll just take a nap."

**Logan**: "Well, I'm just going to do my work."

**Bowman**: "Damn you." (Then went, felt good after)

### It Became a Thing

**Every day**: "Let's go do our work."

**Then with the kids**:

**Athlete**: "Coach, I don't feel this or that, I'm not—"  
**Bowman**: "Hey, just do your work. Just get in and do your work."

**Became part of the team culture.**

### The Practice Quote

**Started putting it on workouts**:

**"What do you do when you've never felt better in your life when you come to practice?"**  
Do your work.

**"What do you do if you feel terrible and you can't, you're not motivated and you just don't know what to do?"**  
Do your work.

**"If you're uncertain?"**  
Do your work.

### The Meaning

> "Just come in every day, do a little something, whatever that is. That's the meaning of it—these incremental, just consistently, mind-numbingly boring, consistent everyday deposits in your bank account are what makes the big changes later."

**Writing equivalent**: Two crappy pages a day.

Eventually → editable manuscript → published manuscript → potentially does well.

**It starts from what seems like low standards**, but that low standard is a way to get to the place where you have high standards.

**Bowman**: "That's an amazing book about this same topic—this resistance to whatever you're trying to do. The way you fight it is you just show up and do whatever you can do that day."

**The book**: "Put your ass where your heart wants to be."

**Bowman**: "If you're not in the pool, you can't have a good workout. But you just do it, even when you don't want to."

## Michael Phelps After Beijing: The Power of Now

### The Post-Beijing Crisis

**After the eight gold medals**: "What the hell do we do now?"

Both Michael and Bob struggled. Michael missing practices a lot.

**Bowman handled it as bad as he could**:

> "I did everything wrong that I could do. I was just like 'You're throwing your life away and disappointing everybody.'"

### Peter Carlisle's Intervention

**Michael's agent** (also Bowman's): "I want you to read this book—*The Power of Now*."

**The message**: "You can't control what Michael does, what he thinks, when he shows up at practice. What you can control is every time he shows up at practice, just give him the best practice you can give him that day."

**Bowman did it**. Michael kept showing up. The more Bowman did that, the more Michael kept coming.

**Michael ended up finishing his career pretty well.**

### The Philosophy

> "It's back to this whole concept: Be in the now. Do what you can do today. Don't worry about tomorrow or yesterday. That's where the magic happens in pretty much everything, not just sports but certainly in swimming."

**Show up today. Do your best right now in this set for the next 30 minutes. Then we'll take on whatever's next.**

If you can focus on that, that's how you're going to end up going where you want to go.

## Surviving Success

### The Hardest Thing

> "The most difficult thing I've dealt with is the people who actually achieve. What do we do next?"

**Why it's hard**: Usually there's a very long buildup. You've spent a lot of time and effort.

**Herb Brooks in Miracle**: After they win, he goes in the parking lot by himself.

**Bowman did that** after Beijing. Wasn't even thinking about it consciously.

> "My God, this actually happened. When you're doing it, I was like 'He'll probably win six or seven, but we're going to pretend like it's eight and be prepared and ready.' All of a sudden it all fell into place. Afterwards you're like, 'Well what the hell do we do now?'"

**Can't replicate it**. You have to find different terms.

**For Michael**: Let's break the record for most medals ever. There were things to work toward.

**Surviving success is by far the hardest part.**

### Leon's Post-Paris Struggle

**Six weeks of star treatment** after Paris Olympics.

**Back to swimming**: "I'm not very good."

**Bowman**: "Yeah, the training is important. You have to find it. You got plenty of time."

### You Don't Learn from Success

> "You don't learn anything from that. You just pat yourself on the back. It's when you fail—it's when you get all the learning."

### Marcus Aurelius: Accepting Success

**The formula**:

> "To accept it without arrogance and let it go with indifference."

**Both ends of the spectrum**:
- Heartbreaking failure, loss, narrow defeat → Can you let that go? It is what it is.
- Gold medals, bestseller list, front page → Can you accept that it doesn't say anything about you either?

**What you come back to**: The work. The thing. Just do that thing.

**Result**: On one hand keeps you humble, on the other keeps you busy instead of dwelling, spiraling, feeling awful. Have something to do.

### Jon Urbanchek's Wisdom

**Bowman's mentor**: Jon Urbanchek (died May 2024, celebration of life that weekend).

**They asked Bowman**: "What is something he would say that you remember?"

**The answer**: "It's like ice cream. Good while it lasts."

**Talking about**: Olympic gold medals, world records.

**The truth**: Yeah, hey, this is like ice cream. It's great right now. But what we're going to have is what we've always had—we had it before, we had it after. **The work**. These things kind of flow from that.

### Success Doesn't Fix Everything

**Ryan's point**: Success can be destabilizing because you told yourself it would mean something or do something for you. That was a powerful motivational force. **But it wasn't true.**

**Bowman coming back from Beijing**: Still had to pay bills, had to do all this stuff. Not all problems were solved. All Michael's problems were the same.

"He would tell you that too, despite all this other wonderful stuff. Probably there are more doors open for us to do things, but in general, yeah. That's a key element—even though that motivates you, in the end you're going to find out that's not going to work."

## Mastery vs. Ego: A Different Fuel

### Can You Achieve Without the Wound?

**Ryan's question**: "Is there another form of motivation? So many greats are motivated by some kind of wound or anger. It's obviously super powerful. Then they kind of get it and go 'Oh maybe that wasn't the best way to be.' But are they only able to see that and say that because they're looking down from the medal podium? Can you be motivated and driven and great from some other fuel?"

**Bowman**: "Uncertain."

**Why?**: Almost every outlier is driven by that, because it's not enough just to have the other stuff.

### Shifting the Focus to Mastery

**Bowman's approach**: "I've tried to take the focus off of—or I've put the focus on—**Mastery**. That's what we're here to do."

**The questions**:
- Let's see how far you can take this
- How good can you be at this?
- How far can you take this event?
- What do you think you could do in terms of this?

**Why it helps**: That's always in your control.

"The process. We control what we focus on. The outcomes flow from that. We have very little control over those. That's what other people do. The outcome in swimming is largely based on what other people do—we have no control over that. Only what we do."

**The measure**: Was it the best performance you were capable of?

### Mastery of Self

> "What you're doing is mastering yourself. Back to Marcus Aurelius. That's what you're trying to do."

**The key element** while doing all this stuff: They are learning about themselves and how to manage those things. Those ultimately give you the performances.

**How you approach**:
- Your swimming
- Difficulties
- Frustration
- These things
- What you expect from yourself

**Bowman believes**: "That's part of Mastery as well."

### Mastery Allows Flexibility

**The advantage of Mastery over winning**:

**If you just love winning races**: Great as long as you can keep swimming.

**If you're like "I want to figure out swimming"**: Then you can transition:
- Athlete → coach → announcer → agent
- You can participate in the thing as a whole

**Actor analogy**:
- Actor who has to be the movie star → narrow career
- Actor who loves being part of great material → can be the up-and-comer, then movie star, then character actor, then "I can't believe so-and-so was in that minor role," then producer

**More expansive definition** of participating in the thing = more sustainable, longer career.

**His reflection**:

> "I had to do that. I was a terrible swimmer. I probably thought about it more than anybody ever thought about swimming and tried and worked and worked and worked. I'm only going to be so good because that's just how I'm made, my background, all these things."

**But**: "Everybody has a path, and I can help them as far along that path as they can go. That's the key."

### What Really Matters

**The things they learn while doing that** are the things that really matter in the end. The lessons:
- The way you approach your work
- The way you keep coming back when you get knocked down
- The way you learn to work with other people
- How you live in an environment where people are more talented than you

**The reversal**:

> "Guess what? When they go out in the other world, this kid who's like a super great engineer but maybe not the best swimmer on my team—probably everything's going to be reversed when these guys get out in the real world."

### The Ceiling on Spaces

**Ryan**: "Coaches are called to swim coaching, and you can reach the heights of that profession, but the contract for the world's greatest swim coach is going to be less than probably a mediocre college football coach."

**Swimming is Bowman's field**:

> "Niels Bohr said an expert's a person that's made every mistake possible in their field. Mine is my field. This big. And I have made every mistake. So I'm probably there. I'm probably an expert in my little narrow field."

**But it keeps you humble**: "It's great to even think about it that way."

### Removing Ego from the Role

**Swimmers' perception**: They come in afraid of Bowman, thinking "Oh my gosh, you're going to dust them and they're going to swim."

**Bowman's reality**:

> "In general I'm just a guy trying to help you do your thing and create an environment where you can learn about yourself, about excellence. I'm happy with that. Super happy with that."

## The Mentor's Dilemma

### Reading: *10 to 25* by David Joerger

**The book**: About how you motivate that age range now, in our current environment.

**The Mentor's Dilemma**:

You want to have high standards. You want to coach these kids to have high standards.

**But**: Today most kids, if you give them criticism, they just see that as "you're not thinking they're good enough. I'm just not good enough. He just told me I wasn't good enough to do [X]."

### The Solution

**How you couch it is very important**:

> "I have very high standards here, but I know you can meet them. That's why I'm asking you to do this. I wouldn't ask you to do this if you couldn't."

**Trying to work on ways to do that** with these guys.

**Where the magic happens**: "If I can get people to just reach beyond where they are and start thinking a little better, then they're going to be able to do this stuff. If they do the higher level training, they have the higher level racing."

## Coaching Different Athletes

### The Too-Intense Athlete

**More common now**: The athlete cares too much, is too intense, too fragile because they put too much on it.

**Bowman has guys on his team**:
- Legitimate world record holder types
- Super Type A
- Going for it every day
- Get very upset if they can't do something

**Bowman's approach**:

> "You know, I'm not even a big golfer, but I don't think their percentages are very high, these guys that win The Masters. They miss a lot more shots than they ever hit. Baseball, my gosh. You're not going to be 100% on all these. What you have to do is know that you gave full effort on every one of these. If you're doing that, things will start to happen."

**Coaching method**:
- When things are going good and can push to another level: "Okay, now you can really do this"
- Most of the time: Just let them do it, say "Nice job on that, could be better in this, try to keep it pretty objective"
- They're already pushing so hard—that perspective is good

### The Talented and Unmotivated

**Don't get anywhere pushing them.**

**What Bowman does**: Try to put the carrot out there.
- "You'd probably be pretty good if you did this"
- "Maybe this could happen if you do that"
- Lead them that way

**As soon as they do something that moves in that direction**: "Dude, that was awesome. That's what we're trying to do here."

**The reasoning**: Clearly some reason if they are incredibly talented that they're not wanting to. It's kind of reverse psychology—how do you take the pressure off? Because that's probably what they don't want.

### Where Time Is Spent

> "That's where I spend the majority of my time. I put out a practice and I can do that and we can kind of manage those and kind of plan a season. But the things that are meaningful are in the practices or outside the practices when we're interacting with these guys and just trying to help them find what their Mastery is and what their path to that is. It's quite different."

## Among Elite Performers: Too Much Discipline

**Ryan's insight**:

> "Among elite performers, probably too much discipline is the problem, not not enough discipline. Especially when you get into the realm of overtraining or not being strategic about when they go all in."

**Bowman**: "100%."

**The meta-skill**: Mastery over your own discipline, your own ambition and drive. Self-awareness.

**Example**: "You're working on this book for the next two years, so sweating every minute is not sustainable and also not how you should measure this project. You've got to be more relaxed or you're going to burn out and not make it through the two years or not sustain the level of performance you need."

**If you're kicking yourself because there was traffic on the way to the office**: "We'll see. Maybe some idea popped into your head while you were in traffic and you needed it."

### In General: Everyone Is Too Hard on Themselves

> "Everybody tends to be too hard on themselves, particularly when you get to the kind of high end of sports. Everybody wants it, for the most part. You're not going to have to drive them into it. They want to do it."

**The questions**:
- What are the obstacles?
- What's holding you back?

## What's Holding You Back?

### The Texas Interview

**Chris Del Conte (Texas AD)**: "Pretty much the best."

**The interview process**: The only thing they really asked Bowman through the whole process:

**"What's holding you back from doing this? What would be the...what's holding you back?"**

**Bowman thinks about that with these kids**: "What's holding you back from taking the next step in this swimming?"

**His answer**: "Can I go back to Arizona and see my grandkids?"

**Texas**: "Oh sure you can."

**Bowman**: "Okay, well that's check. I can do that."

### The Leadership Principle

**How it works in sports, in anything**: You can just make it happen. Sometimes it's just the resistance, fear of something, or you're tired, whatever it is.

**"What's holding you back from doing what you really know that you could do or want to do?"**

### The Underrated Part of Leadership

**Common misconception**: The leader thinks everyone works for them.

**Actually the opposite**: The job of the leader is to go around and go:
- What do you need?
- What's holding you back?
- What are you spending a lot of your time on?

**What often happens**: A set of responsibilities or obligations will fall on a person. They'll have interpreted a request a certain way. They think "My boss wants me to do this."

**But**: The boss wants you to do that thing, but wasn't going "Please waste a lot of the time I'm paying for on something I don't care that much about."

**Only in that conversation**, if you think your job is to alleviate burdens and solve problems for the people that work for you, those things are going to resurface.

**Then you can say**: "Oh I actually don't care about that at all. That's way low on my list. Stop doing that. Let's find someone else whose time is less valuable to do that thing."

**Your job is to find out what's holding people back.**

**But it requires**: A kind of empathy that sometimes leaders don't have, or disinterest.

## Protecting Your Time & Energy

### Delegation vs. Perception

**Bowman has to be careful**: "I don't spend time on stuff that's not important."

**Eric Posegay** (Associate Head Coach, worked together at North Baltimore, knows Bowman inside and out):

**Bowman**: "If you ever think that I ever worked hard in my life, he works twice as hard."

**Eric**: "Well I don't have an off switch."

**Bowman**: "I was like, you need to find me one."

### Why Bowman Protects His Time

> "A lot of my job is about being perceptive and being able to be in a mind state where I can communicate with people and see what's going on and do it. If I am tied up in a hundred things, you just can't do it."

**So**: Part of his job is protecting that part. Tries to get off the things he can not do and make the program function. Happy to delegate those to people.

**"That's a big part of my job—making sure I'm even just in the place where I can do that."**

### The Writer's Equivalent

**Ryan's rule**: "Is this something that only I can do? I should be doing those things—the things only I can do."

**For a coach**: "What are all the things that our athletes are spending time on that we want to take off their plate?"

**Kentucky example** (right before Calipari left): The dorms he had set up—everything you could imagine that would take up one ounce of brain power of one of these athletes is taken care of. They're most—basketball, school. That's all they do.

**Your job**: Remove the impediments that are costing performance out of the people you're supposed to get performance out of.

## Balance for Longevity

### If You Can Compete Longer, Balance Matters More

**Old days**: Swimmer has a 2-year or 5-year career.

**Now**: You can compete at an elite level for longer.

**It actually becomes more important to be a bit more balanced.**

**Writing example**: Unlike music where "we only accept you 30 and under," Robert Caro is in his almost his 90s and performing at elite level. You can do it forever.

**So it actually does become more important** to kind of get your shit together and figure out how to make it more sustainable.

### Leon Marchand: Six Weeks Off

**Bowman's decision**: "Take six weeks. Do whatever you want to do."

### Reagan Smith: A Period of Being None of Those Things

**Reagan**: Did well in the Olympics (two gold, three silver or whatever).

Even relatively younger, but **very focused and intense all the time**.

**They both agreed**: Better for her to have a period after these Olympics where she was **none of those things**.

**What she did**:
- Stayed in Arizona
- Has great friends there
- Did very light training program
- Trained one time a day for a long time
- Doing World Cups now for fun
- Swimming okay but not amazing
- **Who cares**

**The point**: "Let's have four or five months where you get to be you. You get to see what it was like if you would swim in college—have a life, sleep in if you want to, do whatever."

**Bowman thinks**: "It's going to pay huge dividends down the road. When she comes back she will get into this mode. But you just can't do that forever. It's not sustainable."

### Michael: The Extremes

**The problem**: He was so far over here on the focus-dedication spectrum, well beyond normal.

**When he went the other way**: He was way over here.

**If he'd been a little closer**: Probably everything could have been fine.

**Bowman sees this pattern**: "I have some people on my team—this guy can just be great. As long as he's on the track, everything is awesome. But when he gets off track, it's way over here. It almost never—it takes forever to come back."

**So**: "Let's just do what we can to keep him. It's still going to be hard. But let's just try to keep him moving here. That requires more effort from us to kind of check in on him, to kind of see how he's doing, to kind of make sure that if we see something—'Hey I noticed this. How are you feeling about this? Maybe we could do this, maybe take a morning off, just do whatever'—to kind of make those things happen."

**The principle**: "When you operate in one extreme, you're bound to go to the other extreme to kind of balance it."

## The 365-Day Origin Story Revised

### How It Actually Started

**People think**: Bowman decided Michael should train 365 days.

**The reality**: It started quite benignly.

**Michael was 12**: NBAC had limited pool space (like every club in America).

**The group he was in**: Practiced one time per day, plus Sundays.

**Sunday**: Best attended practice. Everybody come Sunday morning.

**Some people had Wednesdays off.**

**When Michael got good**: "Well yeah, Michael needs to swim Wednesdays." He'd come on Wednesdays.

**So he was just going seven days** — but it was like an hour and a half. Not some big deal. He would go play lacrosse and all that stuff.

**Moved into Bowman's group**: Still going seven days, still one time per day at 12, 13, 14.

**Then**: Started doing a little bit more. Put in two doubles a week. Stayed on the Sundays. "Because we were just doing it."

### The National Team Bus

**The moment it became a psychological weapon**:

**National team trip** (one of Michael's very first ones). Older guys on the bus. Don't know how they got into it:

**Older swimmers**: "Hey Phelps, I bet you don't even train every day. Do you train every day?"

**Michael**: "Yeah." (Didn't know any different)

**Older swimmers**: "I bet—do you even—do you train on Christmas?"

**Michael**: "Yeah."

**On Christmas**: Came in and swam for 30 minutes. Played water polo. Wasn't like a big deal. Just enough to kind of keep it going.

**The guys gave him this look.**

**Bowman knew**: "That was a huge psychological advantage."

**Michael's like**: "We're going to keep doing this."

**They were then terrified of him** at that point. Then he just kept going.

**The math**: 52 days a year other people aren't training × 6 years = picked up 6 months or whatever.

### The Wisdom Teeth Example

**Got wisdom teeth out?** "Okay, well can you go on a board the afternoon after the thing? We won't—we'll just go for like kick 500 on the board and call it a practice."

Stuff like that. It wasn't like he was going 8,000 hard every time.

**It took on a life of its own.**

### In Retrospect

**Bowman reflecting**:

> "Honestly, to do that again, I probably would have insisted on one day off a week or something. Wouldn't have hurt his progress, I don't think. Would have been good for me. There's a lot of things that went into that."

**The hard part**: When Michael came back after Beijing and they were doing this stuff, Bowman started to realize "I can't do this 24/7."

**Bowman would take a day or two off.**

**Michael**: "Are you soft now? Are you going to really get serious about [this]?"

**Bowman**: "Dude, I think maybe we could rethink how we do all this."

**Michael never really got to that point.** Even now.

### The Phoenix Visit

**Bowman going to Phoenix** on Friday, coming back Monday just to see the grandkids and visit Michael and the guys.

**Michael will be**: "Whoa, taking a vacation? I guess you don't really care about it."

He'll definitely say something like that.

**Bowman**: "Nope, I just want to keep doing this and enjoying it and be good at it. And the guys here are fine with it. Everybody's fine with it."

### The Lesson

> "When you get into some of those modes where it's just like 'We are doing everything we can possibly do to make this happen,' you get the positive feedback because it does happen. But then you just can't keep making it happen. That's the key."

**Other people can be the canary in the coal mine**: If you're burning out your coach, it's probably a sign you're burning yourself out—you just don't feel it.

**If no one's able to keep up with you over and over again**: It's probably not a humanly sustainable pace.

## The ASU Turning Point

### The Coaches Meeting Three Years Before the Championship

**Feeling**: Doing okay, but weren't really going where they wanted to go.

**One of the guys said**:

> "I think that we're just giving them a practice so they can have a good practice, not so they're necessarily challenged."

**It was so true.**

**Bowman started thinking**: "Yeah, I'm giving these practices that I know that they can do so they'll feel good about what they do. Then I can try to inch them that way."

**But what they really needed**: Give them the practice they need to be doing and see what the difference is.

### The New Approach

**Started doing that**. Not every day. But **a couple times a week**: Give them one that's clearly beyond what they had done before or could do.

**You'd just be shocked at how many people would do it.**

**You just have to have somebody who's willing to ask you for that**: "Hey, this is okay, but you can really do this."

### Why Bowman Had Been Holding Back

> "I just felt like the kids—they weren't as talented, they weren't as successful as other people I work with. So I was just going to try to inch them along through it. At some point you just can't do that. You have to ask them to take a leap and go."

**Then**: Started to get more talented people. It all kind of fed into each other.

### The Reflection

> "I often think about that. It's like, am I doing this practice to really help challenge them today and have a specific thing that they can get better at or learn from if they fail?"

**"Don't be afraid to let them fail on it sometimes."**

**"Just don't write every practice so nobody fails in the practice."**

**"That's where they'll learn everything that they need to learn about how to get better."**

**"Easier again, easier for me. But that's where they'll learn everything."**

## Leon Marchand: The Independent Thinker

### Raised in a Great Family

**Both his parents were swimmers**, but neither one pushed him to swim.

**Didn't even swim for a while**: Too cold. Didn't like cold water.

**Then**: Came back and started to swim. Did a little bit better. Kind of made the Olympics the year before he came to ASU.

**Knew he could do well.**

### What Bowman Loves About Leon

> "He very much knows who he is and how he wants to approach it."

**What Bowman does**:
- Give him a training program
- Maybe some tips on how to swim the things
- All those kind of things
- Kind of just work with him so he can do what he wants to do

**It's not**: "Do this this this this this" and he just says "Yes sir yes sir" and does it.

**He doesn't fight Bowman either.**

**They just work together**: 
- "That didn't seem very [good]." → "No, we should try this."
- "I really liked doing that." → "Why don't we do that again?"

**That's the way it works best.**

### The Shanghai Proof

**Leon's in Shanghai** right now swimming in the World Cups.

**Bowman**: "I'm not even—haven't seen him in two months. He's doing great."

**"That's my job: to teach them to do that."**

### The Self-Sufficiency Principle

> "There's a self-sufficiency to that. The most successful people, everybody, are ones who are equipped to be independent, live their own life, make their own decisions, do their thing."

**As coaches**: "We're almost wired to make all these decisions for them."

**In a team sport**: Probably different (strategies, etc.).

**In swimming**: "We're training them to do something, we're teaching them some skills, but they have to step up on the blocks. I'm not up there."

## The Misconceptions About Coaching

### The Tyrannical Top-Down Myth

**We have this sense**: Tyrannical top-down coach, they're the boss, everyone else is just rowing the oars.

**Yet**: That's not how it works in most creative industries, not how it works in tech.

### The American Military Model

**What we think**: So top-down.

**The core success** (envy of all other nations): The middle—the NCOs (non-commissioned officers).

**The American advantage**: Small unit leadership.
- Independent thinkers
- Set of values and skills
- Firepower to do what they need to do in their individual scenario

**Not**: Tyrannical top-down system.

**What the bosses job is now**: Create an environment where these people can thrive.

**Sometimes**: There are parameters you're setting up, sometimes you have to correct some things.

**In general**: "My job is to create an environment where somebody comes in and buys into what we're selling. Their success will be inevitable. It just flows from what we're doing."

## The Simple Truth of Elite Performance

### The Magic Dust Misconception

**People think**: The coach has some secret. If anyone has access to the playbook, they're going to be great.

**Actually**: "It's so fascinating to me, getting to meet all these different coaches, and what they're actually doing is helping someone bounce back from a disappointing performance or encourage..."

**The simplicity of it**: "Seems almost anticlimactic. No no wait, I thought there's like an X's and O's thing that you do or some formula you have."

**The reality**: "No, we read a motivational quote before practice. They work hard. I have some reasons behind why we do it, but I share that with everybody."

### The Dramatic Reading

**New thing at Texas** (didn't do at ASU):

**Bowman** puts a quote on the end of every practice (a lot of them from Ryan Holiday/Stoics).

**Before they start**: Pick one person, they do a dramatic reading of the quote.

**Why?**: "The key element while we're doing all this stuff is that they are learning about themselves and how to manage those things. Because those ultimately give you the performances."

**It's**:
- How do you approach your swimming?
- How do you approach difficulties?
- How do you approach frustration?
- How do you handle these things?
- What do you expect from yourself?

**All of these sort of things.**

**Bowman believes**: "That's part of Mastery as well."

### The Playbook Is Available

**Why would you share Michael's workout?**

**Bowman**: "If they're not Michael, they're not getting the same [result]. They can do whatever they want with it. Maybe they'll learn something."

**It's just so simple.**

## The Grandfathers & The Kids

### The Competitive Culture Continues

**Everything in that house** with these four little boys is like a world record or a gold medal.

**"I won the gold medal!"** → **"You got bronze!"** (with disdain)

**Bowman**: "Everything's competitive. Everything's 'Try to do this, try to win, be your best, do this. Dad can I beat him in this?' It's all great. But let's just have a little fun with it as we do it."

### Having Kids Changes You

**Success**: You give yourself a lot of credit for it.

**Then you have kids**: "Oh, this is just a thing I have. This is a thing that's just in me. They have it too."

**Ryan's son**: Got obsessed with a podcast about Greek history. Listened not just to all like 500 episodes but going through it again.

**Ryan**: "It's driving me insane because I don't want to hear it. But I'm like, where could he have gotten this interest in ancient philosophy and then this compulsion to drain every drop out of the well until he's wrapped his head around it? Oh, we just both have this thing."

**If pointed at something positive**: Adaptive.  
**If pointed at something negative**: Real danger.

### The Backyard Coaching Session

**The boys**: Running and jumping off a ramp in the backyard.

**To Grandpa**: "You're the coach. You stand right here and tell us what we can do better."

**Bowman loves**: "They think that's what a coach does. They want to be coached."

**They realize all these things.**

**But Bowman wonders**: "Sometimes I wonder if everything has to have a winner or a loser. Maybe we just had fun doing it. Let's just play for fun."

## Key Principles

1. **Make the decision right** - Don't stress about finding the "right" decision; choose and make it work
2. **Have the power of no opinion** - Things aren't asking to be judged by you; we'll see
3. **Do your work** - Incremental, consistent, boring deposits create big changes later
4. **Be in the now** - Show up today, do your best in this set, then take on what's next
5. **Accept success without arrogance** - Let it go with indifference; come back to the work
6. **Focus on Mastery, not outcomes** - How good can you be? That's always in your control
7. **Understand different ceilings** - Everyone has a path; help them as far along as they can go
8. **High standards + support** - "I have high standards here, but I know you can meet them"
9. **Too much discipline is the problem** - Among elites, being too hard on yourself is more common
10. **Find what's holding them back** - Leadership is removing impediments, not commanding obedience
11. **Balance enables longevity** - If you can compete longer, sustainability matters more
12. **Challenge them beyond their current level** - Don't write practices they'll definitely succeed at
13. **Teach independence** - Your job is to make them self-sufficient, not dependent
14. **The work is what you had before, during, and after** - Success is like ice cream—good while it lasts
