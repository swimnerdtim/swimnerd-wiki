# Freestyle Breathing & Technique - Bob Bowman

## The Head Rotation Rule

### One Goggle In, One Goggle Out

**The fundamental principle**: In proper freestyle swimming, the head moves *with* the body to take a breath—never independently.

**What you should see:**
- **One goggle in the water, one goggle out**
- About half the mouth visible
- Breathing inside the bow wave formed by the head
- Head rotating in line with the body's natural rotation

**Common mistakes:**
- Lifting the chin to breathe
- Looking forward to the pool
- Looking to the side of the pool
- Over-rotating (both goggles visible)
- "Checking the weather" (looking up at the ceiling)

### The Bow Wave Technique

When you rotate correctly:
- A bow wave forms naturally on your head
- You breathe with your full mouth **inside that bow wave**
- You're not lifting your mouth out of the water
- The water level stays at mid-mouth

**Michael Phelps example**: Watch his breath—one goggle out, half his mouth visible, but actually breathing inside the bow wave.

### Katie Ledecky's Fix

**Problem**: Over-rotating on the breath (both goggles showing)

**Solution**: Minimize head rotation—work on keeping one goggle in the water throughout the breathing cycle.

**Result**: Head and body move together as one unit, maintaining better body position and reducing drag.

## Practice Structure Philosophy

### The 30-50-20 Formula

**30% Warm-up**
- Gradually warm up joints and shoulders
- Reinforce good technique
- Get swimmers mentally ready for the main challenge
- May include swimming, kicking, pulling, drilling

**50% Main Set**
- Primary objective for that practice
- Focused on specific energy systems:
  - Threshold/endurance training
  - Anaerobic high-quality training
  - Mix of both
- Alternating system through the week
- Tailored to event (distance vs sprint, stroke specificity)

**20% Finisher**
- Small closing piece
- Sprint series
- Technical focus
- Long recovery swim

### The Energy System Rotation

Follow a **daily routine through the week** that hits all energy systems:
- Threshold days
- Anaerobic days
- Mixed days
- Recovery considerations

Event-specific work:
- Distance swimmers ≠ sprinters
- Butterflyers ≠ breaststrokers
- But everyone follows the systematic progression

## The "8 Out of 10" Philosophy

### Consistency Over Heroics

**Michael Phelps and Leon Marchand share this trait:**

> "They very rarely give you a 10 out of 10 practice, but almost every practice is an 8. They have a very high average level of performance."

**What this means:**
- Not many 10/10 practices
- Also not many 2/10 practices
- **Tons of 8s** — day in, day out
- High average level beats occasional heroics

**The power of 8s**: Consistently throwing in eights over time creates significant improvement. It's the cumulative effect that matters.

### Almost Never Go Backwards

Both Phelps and Marchand:
- Recognize the value of an individual practice
- Want to get as much as possible from each practice
- Sometimes it's a lot, sometimes not much
- **But they almost never leave thinking they went backwards**
- Always find some way to get something out of practice

## Breaking Down Big Goals

### Eat the Elephant One Bite at a Time

**The Bowman approach to overwhelming goals:**

> "You can achieve any goal if you make the first step small enough. You don't have to worry about eight gold medals. What you have to worry about is this 500 free that's right in front of your nose right now."

**How it works:**
1. Identify something small they can do
2. Focus on that specific thing (e.g., "do the turns better")
3. Complete that piece
4. Move to the next small piece

**The philosophy**: You achieve big goals by breaking them into little parts.

### Control What You Can Control

**What you DON'T have control over:**
- Tomorrow's conditions
- How you'll feel in four years
- External factors and opponents

**What you DO have control over:**
- What happens in the next two hours
- This specific practice
- This specific turn
- This specific breath

**Both Phelps and Marchand:**
- Have big, specific goals
- Have broken them into plan pieces
- Diligently work toward them every day
- Focus on the controllable present

## The Willingness to Learn

### The Constant Student

**Michael Phelps' defining trait** (according to Bowman):

> "There's no better student of the sport in general than Michael."

**Example from Bowman's dinner visits:**
- Michael cooks most nights
- Constantly asks: "Should I cook these steaks five and a half on each side or five?"
- After serving: "No, I should have probably gone four, four and a half."
- **Constantly trying to raise his game and learn**

**He was exactly like this in swimming:**
- Always trying to improve technique
- Never satisfied with "good enough"
- Seeking small refinements constantly
- Open to feedback and adjustment

### Technique as Ongoing Process

Both elite swimmers:
- Realize the importance of technique
- Try to improve it **all the time**
- See technique work as never "finished"
- Make small adjustments continuously

## The 365 Days Mindset

### The Christmas Story

**National team bus conversation** (young Phelps):

**Older swimmers**: "I bet you don't even train every day. Do you train on Christmas?"

**Phelps**: "Yeah." (He didn't know any different)

**What they did on Christmas:**
- Came in and swam for 30 minutes
- Played water polo
- Not a big deal, just enough to keep it going

**The psychological impact:**
- Other swimmers gave him a look
- They were **terrified of him** at that point
- Phelps decided: "We're going to keep doing this"

### The Math of Consistency

**52 extra days per year × 6 years = 6+ months of training**

That's the advantage of training 365 days a year versus taking weekends and holidays off.

**But it wasn't brutal:**
- Got wisdom teeth out? Do 500 kick on a board that afternoon
- Not always 8,000 hard
- Just enough to keep the momentum going

**It took on a life of its own** and became part of the competitive advantage.

## The Nuclear Reaction Approach

### When to Go Big (Chase Kalisz Example)

**The problem**: Chase had a habit of double-breathing into walls on freestyle in training.

**Bowman's decision**: One night (Arizona heat, a million degrees) - "I am not tolerating this anymore."

**The approach:**
- Got Chase out of the pool
- Used a "nuclear reaction"
- "You cannot do that. You're not going to do it again."
- "Every time I see you do it, you're going to start the whole practice over."
- Had never been like that with Chase before (he's a "good boy")

**The reasoning:**

> "If I don't get this taken care of, I can't get to the other eight things we have to deal with like your backstroke. Chase, if you're going to win a gold medal, nobody's going to double breathe and win a gold medal. So if that's something you will accept, then you're not ever going to get there."

**The result**: Chase never did another double breath. Bowman has never seen him do one since.

**The lesson**: Sometimes you need to make an impression so strong there's no room for the bad habit to return.

## Challenge and Failure

### Writing Practices for Growth

**Couple times a week**: Give them something clearly beyond what they've done before or could do.

**The surprise**: You'd be shocked at how many people would do it.

**The purpose**: Say "This is okay, but you can really do this."

### Don't Be Afraid to Let Them Fail

> "Don't write every practice so nobody fails in the practice. That's where they'll learn everything they need to learn about how to get better."

**Why it matters:**
- Easier for the coach to write "safe" practices
- But athletes learn from challenging attempts
- Failure teaches what success cannot
- Builds resilience and problem-solving

**The coaching question**: "Am I doing this practice to really help challenge them today and have a specific thing they can get better at or learn from if they fail?"

## Sleep as Superpower

### Michael Phelps: The Nervous Coach

**2000 Olympic Trials** (Phelps age 15, first Olympic team):

Bowman was super nervous (never had a kid like that before). Before the final:

**Bowman**: "Did you get any sleep?"  
**Phelps**: "Yeah, three and a half hours."  
**Bowman**: "All right, we're good."

**Very few people can do that** — sleep under extreme pressure.

### Leon Marchand: Sleep is His Superpower

Leon is also very good at sleeping under pressure.

**The only time Leon's ever really compromised in training**: When something upset his sleep.

Sleep becomes the foundation of recovery and performance.

### Pre-Race Mindset

**Michael's approach before major finals:**

> "I've done everything I can do. All I'm going to do is just give my best. I know that I've prepared. We'll see how well the preparation went. That's when you find out."

**What Bowman looked for:**
- Could tell by looking in Michael's eyes if he was rested
- Sleep quality was a key performance indicator
- Readiness showed in the eyes before races

## The Champion's Motivation

### Cannot Tolerate Losing

**What separates the absolute best** (Michael Jordan, Kobe Bryant, Tiger Woods, Michael Phelps):

> "They cannot stand to lose. It's not that they love winning—the winning is great, but that's what they feel like they're supposed to do. They cannot tolerate losing."

**The cycle:**
- Winning is expected, not celebrated excessively
- Losing is unacceptable
- If they get beat → motivates them to do **whatever it takes** not to get beat next time
- This creates an endless improvement loop

**Different from "loving to win":**
- It's deeper than enjoying victory
- It's an internal standard they hold themselves to
- Losing violates their self-concept
- The pain of losing > the joy of winning

## The Michael Phelps Physical Gifts

### The Genetic Win

**Three factors in Phelps' success:**

1. **His physiological makeup** - Made for swimming, genetic gifts
2. **His environment** - North Baltimore Aquatic Club with history of Olympic swimmers, world records, and high expectations
3. **His willingness to learn** - Emotional openness to constant improvement

**The NBAC culture:**
- Young Michael saw older swimmers succeeding
- Thought: "Oh, I'm going to do that"
- Expectations were baked into the environment
- Success was normalized, not exceptional

### The Body Morphing Principle

**For those not built like Phelps:**

The biggest thing holding back swimmers who didn't grow up swimming: **Swim mobility**

**You can actually morph your body into a swimmer** through:
- Consistent mobility work
- Shoulder flexibility development
- Core strength building
- Proper movement patterns over time

Genetics matter, but environment and training create significant adaptations.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Head and body move together** - One goggle in, one goggle out, breathe in the bow wave
2. **Consistency beats intensity** - 8/10 every day > occasional 10/10
3. **Break big goals into small steps** - Control what's in front of you right now
4. **Never stop learning** - Always refining technique, always getting better
5. **Train 365 days** - The psychological and physical advantage compounds over time
6. **Challenge creates growth** - Don't be afraid to write practices they might fail
7. **Sleep is fundamental** - Recovery and mental readiness start with sleep quality
8. **Cannot tolerate losing** - The greatest champions are driven more by fear of loss than love of winning
