# Bob Bowman's Golden Rules

*Source: "The Golden Rules with Coach Bob Bowman" - ASU First Year Success Center, VIP-III Student Success Series*

## The Three Phases of High Performance

### 1. Imagination Phase
**Use your mind and heart to create something exciting that drives you forward.**

- **Dream Goals**: Must be emotional, general, and far in the future
- **Michael Phelps at age 11**: Wrote "I want to swim in the Olympics" — then crossed out "I want to win a gold" because it seemed too big
- **2008 Pre-Beijing Meeting**: Michael said "I want to be the best ever" — that became the catalyst for everything

**What Dream Goals Do:**
- Establish the forest (direction)
- Provide emotional catalyst for change
- Get you out of bed at 4:15 AM when training is boring

### 2. Challenge Phase
**The foundation under your castles in the air**

> "Everybody does the big things. What separates champions are the fine points." — Bob Bowman

**Process vs. Outcome:**
- **Process Goals**: "I want to swim 10x100s on 1:05" (specific, controllable)
- **Outcome Goals**: "I want to win the NCAA Championship" (uncontrollable)

**Mental Activation Level:**
- Thinking about OUTCOME goals → Over-activated, nervous, choke
- Thinking about PROCESS goals → Optimal activation, flow state
- **Coaching strategy**: Manipulate activation by choosing which goals to emphasize

### 3. High Performance Phase
**Natural outcome of your process**

> "High performance is just a natural outcome of your process that led up to it. It's not something you can sit around and think about all day and it'll happen." — Bob Bowman

## The 4 Goal Types

### Dream Goal (Long-term, general, broad)
- "I want to be the best ever"
- Emotional, motivating, directional

### Mid-range (1-4 years, specific but flexible)
- Your college career
- Major milestones
- Example: "Make Olympic trials"

### Short-range (6 months/semester, very specific and measurable)
- "Swim 52 seconds in 100 breaststroke"
- **Then identify 3-4 things in practice that guarantee you hit that goal**

### Right Now (Most important)
- "Are you going to doze off or listen?"
- "Am I going to eat the cookie or not?"
- **Win = What's Important Now**

## Performance Planning System

Bob Bowman's goal-setting template (used with Phelps since age 11):

1. **Write down your specific target** (handwriting > typing — more personal, more meaningful)
2. **Specify your time frame** (when exactly?)
3. **Identify your objectives** (3-4 things you control that guarantee success)
4. **Visualize success** (see below)
5. **Rehearse success every day**

---

## Visualization: The Superpower

> "The brain cannot distinguish between something that is vividly imagined and something that is real." — Bob Bowman

### How Michael Phelps Visualized

**Three perspectives:**
1. **First-person**: Swimming the race, feeling it viscerally
2. **Third-person**: Sitting in the stands watching yourself perform perfectly
3. **Sensory details**: Smell the chlorine, see the venue, feel the water

**Progressive relaxation technique:**
- Age 11: His mom would read relaxation script before bed
- After one week: He'd fall asleep instantly
- Then: Run mental movies of perfect performances before sleep

**The 3:07 Dream:**
Before Beijing 2008, Michael kept dreaming he looked at the scoreboard and saw "3:07" — not a real swimming time. Bob said "That's probably your 300 split in the 400 IM." (For a 4:03 finish, 300 split would be 3:07.)

**In the Beijing 400 IM final, Michael's 300 split: 3:07.0**

Hair stood up on Bob's neck when he saw it.

## The First Rule of Success

> "Find people who are successful in the field that you're in. Learn how they do things. You'll be well on your way to being successful. Do the same kind of things. You'll get the same kind of results. And over time, you'll save time making a lot of mistakes." — Bob Bowman

**Example:** Bob went to Napa, California to learn from legendary coach Paul Bergen, who was semi-retired and training racehorses. Bob worked in the barn every day, talking swimming while learning horses.

---

## Actions = Words

**The Horse Story:**

Bob was learning to ride. The horse got spooked, took off running toward a wall. Bob screamed "WHOA!" while waving his hands and legs frantically. Horse stopped suddenly, Bob flew over the horse's head.

Coach Bergen: **"Whoa means stop. But your hands and feet were going crazy, which means GO LIKE HELL. He got confused and just got rid of you."**

**Coaching lesson:** Your actions and your words must match up. Athletes need to know who you are and what you're about through **consistency**, not just speeches.

## Details Win Gold Medals

### The 0.01 Second Finish (2008 Beijing 100 Fly)

Michael Phelps beat Milorad Čavić by **0.01 seconds** — the smallest margin possible.

**Why Michael won:**
- **Perfect butterfly finish technique**: Fingers fully extended, horizontal, driving straight into the wall
- **Čavić's finish**: Tired, floated up, touched vertical with flat hand

**The kicker:** This wasn't a decision made in the moment. **You can't decide under that pressure. You go with instinct.**

**Michael had ingrained perfect finishes through 12 years of Bob yelling:**
- "Do it over. That finish was terrible."
- "Make sure you're on a straight arm."
- **Every repeat** in practice = chance to practice a perfect finish

> "I bought a 37-acre horse farm and built a 5,000-square-foot house off of that 1/100th of a second. I promise you it mattered." — Bob Bowman

## Get Comfortable Being Uncomfortable

> "There can be no growth without discontent." — Lou Tice, Pacific Institute

### Why Discomfort Matters

**When you set a goal, you create a gap:**
- Current self-image (ego, strongly held beliefs)
- Future self-image (your goal)

**Your subconscious mind gravitates to whichever picture is held strongest.**

If you visualize the future self MORE than you reinforce the current self → you move toward the goal.

**During that process, it gets uncomfortable:**
- "I don't like this"
- "What if people reject me?"
- "What if I can't do it?"

**Don't worry. Go toward the picture.**

---

## Embracing Adversity

### Practice in the Worst Conditions

> "Practice getting the best possible performance in the worst possible conditions." — Bob Bowman

**Examples:**
- Bob stepped on Michael's goggles before a race in Australia → Michael learned to swim with leaking goggles
- Michael forgot his goggles at the blocks → Bob didn't bring them → Michael swam without them and won
- **2008 Beijing 200 Fly**: Michael's goggles filled with water completely. He counted strokes (knew he took ~21 per length in practice). Olympic gold + world record.

**Australian coach story:** Team complained water fountain was too cold. Next day: no water fountain. Teach them to deal with adversity.

## Activation Control: Process vs. Outcome

### The Relay Example

**Outcome focus (makes athlete nervous):**
> "The whole meet's going to ride on you. You're the anchor of this relay. It's all coming down to you. I guess it'll be your fault if we lose."

**Process focus (calms athlete down):**
> "Dive in. Breathe on the second stroke. 1.2 tempo. When you go into the first turn, your head has to be lower than your hips."

**Bob manipulates this strategically:**
- Lethargic athletes → Outcome goals ("If you don't do this, you won't make finals")
- Over-activated athletes → Process goals (technical details they control)

---

## The Standard of Practice

> "The basis of my program is that the process of success is more important than any outcome." — Bob Bowman

**What Bob controls:**
- Standard of practice (how we train)
- Preparation (daily habits)
- Attitude expected
- Things modeled to athletes

**What Bob doesn't control:**
- How fast they swim on race day
- Conditions in the pool
- What opponents do

**Nick Saban principle:** "Don't look at the scoreboard. Play the next play."

## Michael Phelps: 8 Gold Medals (Beijing 2008)

When Michael walked out of the mix zone after winning the 8th gold medal, Bob didn't know what to say.

Bob: **"Good job."**

Michael later told USA Today: *"All I had to do is win 8 gold medals to get a 'good job' from Bob. Nothing's ever good enough for him."*

**Bob's perspective:** When you have somebody super talented, it's easy to do certain things. My job is to keep raising the bar.

"He'd swim 50 seconds and I'd say, '50's pretty good, but I bet if you took 2 strokes off and did 3 more kicks off that wall, you'd go 49.'"

## Building the Toolbox

### Early Career: Only Had a Hammer

"When I first started coaching, the only tool I had was a hammer, so everything looked like a nail."

**"You do this. You WILL do this. Because I said so."**

- Incredibly effective
- Wears you completely out
- **You cannot be other people's motivation. They have to motivate themselves.**

### Expanded Toolbox Now Includes:

- Sledgehammer (when necessary)
- Pat on the back
- Logic
- Empathy
- Setting up a plan
- Visualization guidance
- Process focus

**As mentors: Add to your toolbox. Different tools reach different people.**

## The Breakthrough Moment: Baltimore 1996

Bob was coaching in California, had two world-ranked swimmers who left him right before the 1996 Olympics.

Bob: "Screw this. I've done everything right. I've given up my whole life for this. I'm not ever going to get to the goals I wanted to. I'm going back to school and starting over."

**Friend in Baltimore**: "If you're going to go to school, come here. You can coach a little to make money."

Bob wanted to become a veterinarian. A year later, 11-year-old Michael Phelps showed up.

**Bob's mindset:** "I was kind of out the door. I said, 'I don't know if Michael can make it, but he's got more talent than anybody I've ever seen and I'm going to do everything I can in this year or 2 that I have with him to make him successful down the road.' **I just took myself out of the equation because I never thought I'd ever coach him.**"

When Michael was 12-13, Bob got offered his dream job in Atlanta.

**North Baltimore director:** "This job in Atlanta is great. You could probably take it and do everything you want to do. But if you stay here, you'll be Michael's coach for the duration."

**Bob stayed. "It just started to happen. That really wasn't part of the plan."**

## Create an Environment Where Success is Inevitable

> "My coaching philosophy: I want to create an environment where success is inevitable. That if you're in the environment and you buy in and you do the things we ask you to do, you will be successful." — Bob Bowman

---

## Application to Any Field

Everything Bob teaches applies beyond swimming:

- **Test anxiety?** Focus on process (your preparation) not outcome (the grade)
- **Job interview?** Rehearse specifics (your stories, questions) not worry ("What if I don't get it?")
- **Performance?** Visualize it vividly, repeatedly, from multiple angles
- **Big project?** Win = What's Important Now

---

## Success is NOT a Straight Line

> "For either Michael or myself, it wasn't just a straight line to success. You didn't just have an idea, a dream, and then just start working toward it and go directly toward it. Success has lots of ups, downs, backwards, forwards, and then over time what you hope to do is move in the direction where you want to be." — Bob Bowman
