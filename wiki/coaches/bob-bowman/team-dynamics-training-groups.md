# Team Dynamics & Training Group Philosophy - Bob Bowman & Herbie Behm

## The Combined Program Revolution (ASU 2023)

### From Split to Integrated

**2020 Vision**: ASU had planned to split men's and women's programs completely.

**The setup**:
- Bowman and Behm would take the men
- Rachel Stratton Mills would take the women
- Derek would support both
- Behind the scenes: Creating more single-gender groups
- Recruiting became heavily divided (men vs women focus)

**Why the split seemed necessary**:
- Women's trajectory lagged behind men
- Different developmental stages
- Wanted dedicated focus for each program

### COVID's Hidden Impact

**2020 Women's Pac-12 Championship** - The team was RIGHT THERE:

**Sierra Schmidt**: 4:33 500 free  
**Erica Brown**: 4:34 500 free  
**On no rest, no shave, no taper** (saving it for NCAAs that never happened)

Bowman was "very confident we would have had a top 10 team" at NCAAs.

**Then COVID hit**:

**The men**: COVID red-shirt year was GREAT for them (extra year of development)

**The women**: COVID red-shirt year was TERRIBLE for them:
- Most were at the END of their careers
- Extending another year didn't help
- Sierra Schmidt never swam again
- Cameron Curry never swam again
- Lost the foundation that could have built the program

### The Research Decision

**When Rachel left for Northwestern**, they did the research:
- Talked to programs that had been together, split, then back together
- Looked at data on combined vs split programs
- Made the decision: **Combined program is better for ASU**

**The problem**: Bowman coached ONE woman in the last two years. Herbie coached NONE.

**The solution**: Reorganize everything.

### The New Structure

**Now every group has men and women in it.**

**The immediate result**: "It's just been so much better."

**The unexpected outcome**:

> "One of our concerns was that the women would be kind of overwhelmed by the men because they're kind of on this level and these crazy things are happening in practice and in the meets. It's just been the opposite—they've been uplifted."

**Evidence**:
- Already swimming times at dual meets that they swam at Pac-12s last year
- NC State dual meet with the women was "our best meet we've had since I've been here"
- Women swimming relay splits in October that matched previous season's championship meets

## The Fruit Groups System

### The Current Groups

**Mangos** (Herbie's group)  
**Coconuts** (Austin's group)  
**Limes** (Derek's group)  
**Mandarins** (Alex's group)  
**Grapefruits** (Logan's group)  
**Oranges** (Bob's group)

**But here's the key**: "A lot of people are just VH splashes—they're mixing."

### Why Less Identity is Actually Good

**Old system**: Strong group identity, competition within groups, specificity.

**New system**: Mixing constantly, less rigid identity.

**Why it works better**:
- Learning each other's strengths and weaknesses as a coaching staff
- Looking at each individual's strengths and weaknesses
- Putting swimmers in different places for different days or periods of time
- **Constantly reassessing** because people are constantly changing

**The reinvigoration effect**:

> "Somebody like Jack Dolan's a good example—on his fifth year, been here the whole time, and now he's swimming his best times ever. Every dual meet has been right at his best times. I think part of that is it feels like a whole new team in a very good way."

### Daily Structure

**Every day of the week**, coaches know:
- What each coach is doing
- How that fits into plans for each athlete
- The energy system focus
- The stroke specificity
- How it ladders into long-term athlete development

## Individualization at Scale: The Simone Manuel Example

### Phase 1: All Bob (Building Aerobic Base)

**Why?** She was pretty far behind in fitness level.

**Focus**: More aerobic-type work, building volume capacity.

### Phase 2: Mixed (Adding Power)

Started adding sessions with Herbie for **power and sprint work**.

Built up over a period of time.

### Phase 3: All Herbie (Pure Speed)

Switched completely to Herbie's group.

**Result**: "It didn't really feel like she was continuing to make progress doing that."

### Phase 4: The Optimal Split (Current)

**5 sessions with Bob, 4 sessions with Herbie**

**Bob's days** (first 4 days of week):
- Threshold-based work
- What she did in high school
- Aerobic foundation
- Volume capacity

**Herbie's days** (end of week):
- Power workouts
- Very high quality sprinting
- Race-specific speed work

**Result**: "It's working great. She has made tremendous progress. When you see her you're going to know it's working."

## Individualization at Scale: Other Examples

### David Schlicht & Alex Coulson: The Schedule Conflict

**Problem**: Class conflict on Wednesday, miss middle-of-day practice where both groups do fast quality.

**Decision process**:
- Thursday both groups suiting up
- Wednesday they're also suiting up
- "Do we want them doing both?"
- **"Yeah, let's see how they do."**

**The reasoning**:
- Both guys have pretty high capacity
- Should be able to do back-to-back high intensity
- Friday will be easy (not slamming them every day)
- Testing their capacity with high-intensity back-to-back days

**Same week, opposite approach for Simone**:
- Wednesday: Easy low intensity with Bob
- Thursday: Another low intensity with Herbie
- Friday afternoon + Saturday: Really high quality and fast

**Why the difference?** Based on previous couple weeks, understanding her individual capacity, planning the whole week strategically.

## The Dialogue: How Decisions Get Made

### Constant Evaluation by Staff AND Athletes

**Tools**:
- Recording times
- Keeping track of data
- Concrete information on individual progress

**But also**:
- Constantly looking and seeing how they're doing
- Verbal feedback
- Subjective observations
- Understanding capacity in different areas

### Understanding Individual Capacity

**Questions being asked**:
- What's their capacity in different energy systems?
- What's coming later in the week?
- What have we learned about them in previous weeks?
- What's the better call for the whole week?
- What will allow constant work but never overwork?

**Not**: "This set looks kind of good, they'll be good at it."  
**Instead**: "What'll allow for that constant work but never overwork?"

### The Teaching Effect

**Early years**: Maybe 7-8 people realistically could do anything at NCAA level.

**Now**: Expanding that out.

**The multiplier effect**:
- Not only coaches teaching
- **The kids teach each other**
- All working together
- Everyone has different skill sets
- 70 teammates with different goals, working toward different things
- Everybody bought into: "We're just trying to get ourselves to the highest spot possible"

**Result**: "That makes the results take care of themselves."

## The Suit Philosophy: Constant Feedback

### The Evolution

**Last year**: Training suits first semester dual meets, suited up all second semester.

**This year**: Suit up for all meets, entire year.

**Opponents**: NC State happy to do it, Georgia happy to do it.

**Fan reaction**: "I don't think anybody felt bad about those meets. They were awesome."

### Why Suit Up All Season

**Herbie's explanation**:

> "Every meet is giving feedback to what we're doing and how we're developing. We're a sport where we want to peak at the right time. Getting the actual information of how you've progressed through the season is important."

**The problem with unsuited dual meets**:
- Everyone goes best times at the most important meet
- "Well obviously, because..."
- Dual meet = 2 hours, 4 swims
- Championship meet = 1 race per day
- You're not comparing apples to apples

**The solution**:
- Consistent feedback
- Same suit, same conditions
- Comparing the same thing
- Apples to apples

### The Hard Truth: It Reveals Training Quality

**Herbie (blunt)**:

> "In my honest opinion, I think it's rarely the swimmers who don't want to suit up. It's the coaches who don't want to face the reality that their training might not be good."

**The hiding mechanism**:
- Kid goes really fast without suit
- Put suit on, shave, go a little bit faster
- "They dropped a tenth in a :53"
- "It still worked, season best"
- **NO.** They actually got worse in the taper phase

**The light on coaching**:
- Shines the light on whether training is working
- Get better feedback each season
- Understand what's really happening
- Can't hide behind "when I put a suit on I'll be fast"

### Reducing Pressure Through Repetition

**Herbie's memory** (as an athlete):

> "We get one meet a year to wear a suit, shave, have an actual opportunity to go a best time. There was SO MUCH pressure on that meet. I specifically remember thinking: 'I don't feel like I know how to handle this pressure because it's all new.'"

**The current approach**:
- October: putting suit on
- Seeing where you're at
- A lot of pressure, but preparing them to handle it
- **Preparation, not protection**

**Removing external crutches**:
- Can't say "I'll put a suit on and drop 3 seconds"
- This is where you are
- If you want to go :41 in 100 free and you just went :45.2, you can't rely on the suit
- **Figuring out how to handle it on your own**

### Removing Superstition

**Common swimming superstitions**:
- Brand new suit, cut tag off at pool
- Put it on for first time
- Drink this pre-workout at this exact time
- Specific routines that become crutches

**The suit-up approach**:
- Removes those early, all the time
- Every time: "Why did you go a best time here?"
- Was it preparation? The race? The strategy?
- **Getting experience faster** rather than hoping

### The Accountability Cycle

**For coaches**: "We're accountable because we're going to know what the training is actually doing. All things are equal, the suits are equal."

**For athletes**: "It holds them accountable because they can't hide behind 'when I put a suit on I'll be fast.' No—here it is, do it today or don't."

**Bowman loves that aspect** - and it's been really successful.

## Training Philosophy: Volume + Speed

### The Perceived Dichotomy

**Common belief**: You can't do hard training and swim fast.

**Bowman's counter-example**:

> "Michael was always close to his speed. He hardly ever went to a meet where he didn't swim pretty well. And he did 80,000 a week all the time."

**The key**: What you're adjusted to, what you're adapted to, how work is sequenced.

### The Ilia Karabin Week (Typical)

**MONDAY AM**: 8,000 meters in ~1:42
- Not anything special
- Moving along
- Mix of swimming, kicking, pulling

**MONDAY PM**: Very hard set
- 4 x 100 free on 1:00 (averaging :50, mainly :52s)
- 2 x 100 easy on 1:20
- 6 x 50 fast on :50
- 2 x 50 easy on 1:00
- **Repeat 4 rounds**, each round different stroke (fly, back, breast, free)
- Fly: Going :24-:25 by the end
- Back (his weak thing): Pretty good
- Breast: Pretty good
- Free: Going :22s
- **Total PM**: 8,000 meters

**MONDAY TOTAL**: 16,000 meters

**TUESDAY**: 17,000 LCM
- 7,000 AM long course
- **Main set**: 10 x 300
- Started :3:16, ended :3:04-05
- Not amazing, not terrible
- Basic, didn't ask too much
- Then 6,000 AM: Pretty low-key, technically focused

**WEDNESDAY AM**: 6,000, low-key, technical focus

**WEDNESDAY PM**: "Speed Check" (suit up, warm up like a meet)
- Time trial 150-100-50 every 15 minutes
- Did it by stroke (butterfly)
- **150 fly**: 1:27 (out in :46.9)
- Swim down
- **100 fly**: :47 (school record #2)
- Swim down
- **50 fly**: 19.96 from a dive

**The analysis**:
- He's got speed
- Can do the long stuff
- Can do everything
- Very special

**Bowman**: "I think he can be a lot better. He's only scratching the surface."

### Reagan Smith's Training Before 1:48.3 200 Fly

**Leading weeks**:
- Sets like the one described for Ilia
- Going :25 flat or :24-high for 50s fly
- Over and over and over

**Bowman knew**:
- Told the pool they'd have more than one person in the heat
- Actually: "No, Reagan has to swim by herself because the time needs to count and you got to have the guy ready to measure the pool after."
- Did it all before: "Something's going to happen here. This is going to be ridiculous."

**You just kind of know** when you see them training.

**Result**: 1:48.3 American Record (by herself, in a dual meet)

**Bowman's reaction**: "It wasn't a surprise at all. If you saw her training you'd be like, 'Well of course. Why didn't she go 1:47?'"

### Planning for Speed Within Volume

**Monday**: Very hard  
**Tuesday**: Aerobic but long  
**Set them up to do something Wednesday** (speed check)

**The principle**: When they're very fit, used to the program, and you plan it the right way, you can manipulate it so they can be at their speed.

> "I think we do that pretty well."

### Individual Capacity Determines Volume

**Herbie's key point**:

> "It's raising the individual's capacity, not saying every person has to do exactly what Ilia did to swim those times."

**If they can**: They'll probably swim pretty fast.

**If they can't**: Killing them isn't going to get them there.

**The progression**: Give them one step in front of what they currently have. They keep climbing up. Eventually they're doing what Ilia and several others do.

**Not**: Fitting everyone into one box.  
**Instead**: Creating a program that allows their talent to shine while still building up.

### Avoiding the One-Trick Pony

**Common college pitch**: "You'll come here, you'll never do a threshold set, we'll only do sprint stuff."

**Herbie's response**: "Why are you limiting yourself like that?"

**The reality**: Even if you love a set, if you do it for four whole years, by the end the kids aren't getting anything from it.

**The solution**: Knowing how to change, giving the athlete what they need to keep taking steps forward.

**Not**: "Here's what we do here and it works for everybody."

## Managing Expectations: Men vs Women

### The Funny Position

**Men's team**:
- Not thrilled with relays at early meet
- "Are you guys going to get it together? Come on, step up."
- Relays made A-cut for NCAAs
- Still: "Well maybe that's not too bad, but you can be a lot better than that. Don't just settle for that."

**Women's team**:
- Going best times
- Major improvements
- Beating NC State in 4x100 free relay (Kathryn Berkoff, strong team)
- Winning individual events they haven't been winning

### Same Process, Different Timeline

**Bowman**: "It's a step-by-step thing. I really think it's the same with both, but it's just on a different little time scale."

**Herbie**: "100%. It's like the men a couple years ago—if we beat this team it's going to be a really good and exciting thing. The men's side now: if we ever lose it's going to be very disappointing. It's a fun balance."

### The Helping Dynamic

**Opponent mindset**:
- Men's team: "They're going to put suits on, they're going to do this stuff that probably pisses them off, so they're ready to go."
- Women's team: Benefiting from that energy, stepping up, proving themselves

### Universal Culture

**Herbie's insight**:

> "Every person on the team—literally every single person—is totally into being the best possible swimmer they can be."

**Range**:
- Some: If they get a chance to swim at Pac-12s, that's their lifetime achievement
- Some: Trying to break world records

**Common thread**: Every single person has that viewpoint—trying to figure out how to be the best they can be.

**The benefit**:
- Don't worry too much about the result
- Get them to understand process
- Team goals are slightly different in outcome
- All trying to figure out how to do the process the same way

### The Teaching Ecosystem

**Used to be**: 7-8 people who could realistically do anything at NCAA level, coaches teaching them.

**Now**: 
- The kids teach each other
- All working together
- 70 teammates with different skill sets, different things they're working on, different goals
- Everybody bought into: "We're just trying to get ourselves to the highest spot possible"

**Result**: "Makes the results take care of themselves."

## Midseason Meet Philosophy

### Short Rest, Not Full Taper

**NC State Invite approach**:
- Want a "little bump" in performance off what they've done
- **Short rest** (one-week thing for Bob's group)
- Different requirements for different events and people
- Some rest longer, most don't
- Some shave, some don't (depends on who they are)

**Examples**:
- Leon and Ilia: Probably not shaving
- Some others: Will shave

**Goal**: "Give them a little bit more than they've had, but not everything."

**Past success**: Very fast times doing that.

### The Feedback Loop

**Purpose**: Not necessarily about the time you swim—it's **how you got there**.

**Questions answered**:
- How fast can they swim on what we give them?
- How do they respond to rest?
- Who will need to rest more?
- Who will need to rest less?
- Who will need to do the same exact thing?

**Then**: Figure out how to be better after that.

### The Altitude Cycle (Pros)

**For US Open**:
- Pros going to altitude until US Open
- Will get a bump from altitude cycle (hopefully)
- 4-5 days rest going into US Open
- See how that works

**Same principle**: Process > result. How you got there > what the clock said.

## Key Principles

1. **Integration uplifts** - Combined programs can elevate both genders when done right
2. **Mixing > rigid identity** - Constant reassessment and movement between groups keeps things fresh
3. **Individualization at scale** - 70 athletes, each with custom plans based on their capacity and needs
4. **Suits create accountability** - Can't hide from reality, forces honest assessment
5. **Volume + speed coexist** - When planned correctly, high volume doesn't kill speed
6. **Process over outcome** - How you got the time matters more than the time itself
7. **Constant feedback** - Every meet, every set provides data to adjust the plan
8. **Teaching ecosystem** - Athletes teaching each other multiplies coaching impact
9. **Same standards, different timelines** - High expectations for everyone, patience for development pace
10. **Pressure through preparation** - Suit up often so championship pressure feels familiar
