## Video 1: Week In The Life (China Open Prep - February 2025)

**Source:** "How Cam McEvoy Trained to Break the 50m WR"  
**Total weekly volume:** 1,355 meters  
**Training frequency:** 6 sessions (4 pool, 2 gym... wait, that's wrong)

Actually: **4 pool sessions, 3 gym sessions**

---

### **Monday Pool Session**

**Total volume:** ~300-400m  
**Duration:** ~60 minutes  
**Focus:** Top speed work

**Warmup:**
- 300m loosen
- 2 warm-up reps:
  - 1 × 25 body weight (submax speed)
  - 1 × 20m @ 2kg resistance (1080 machine)

**Main Set:**
- 1 × 25 max dive (body weight, no resistance)
- 4 reps with resistance:
  - 2 × 20m @ 1kg
  - 2 × 20m @ 2kg
- 1 × 25 body weight max

**Metrics tracked:**
- 10m split
- 15m split
- 20m split
- 25m time
- Stroke rate (target: 62+ SPM)
- Segment times (under 43 seconds for certain reps)

**Key moment:**
> "Cam started to feel good on the last rep (9.50 split), asked Tim if he should do another. Tim shut it down: 'No, we got more training this week. It's only Monday.'"

**Result:** ~140m of actual swimming

---

### **Tuesday Gym Session**

**Focus:** Strength work  
**New element:** Introduced this week

**Pull-ups:**
- 70kg weighted pull-ups
- Singles only
- Multiple sets
- Tracking velocity on iPad

**The aerobic joke:**
> "Oh, this is my aerobics. Sunny, we're talking about my heart rate probably hasn't dropped below 100 this whole time. This is where I get my aerobics if everyone's wondering."

---

### **Wednesday Pool Session**

**Total volume:** ~300m  
**Duration:** ~60 minutes  
**Focus:** Assisted work + technique at speed

**Cam's explanation:**
> "Goal of this session is really I want to get as much exposure to movement at race speed and techniques at race speed without actually having to put the energetic cost to have to get at those speeds."

**Session breakdown:**
1. **Assisted work** (pulled by resistance machine to feel race velocity)
2. **Fins + 3kg resistance** (combination allows hitting race speeds comfortably)
3. **Dives into 5kg resistance** (technique work under heavy load)

**Velocity tracking:**
- 2.4 m/s target
- Descending intensity (18 down to 9 on effort scale)
- Same speed, reducing effort = improving efficiency

**Sunny's reaction:**
> "This is ridiculous. Cam, your nose is now out of the water."

**Cam's response:**
> "Feels pretty nice."

**Result:** Shortest pool session (~1 hour total, minimal volume)

---

### **Thursday Gym + Pool Session (BRAND NEW)**

**Innovation:** French contrast - pull-ups → dive 25s

**Setup:**
- Pull-up rack installed poolside
- Weighted pull-ups (near max)
- Immediate transition to pool
- Suited swimming
- Full timing/analysis

**Structure:**
- 6 rounds total:
  - Heavy weighted pull-up (gym)
  - Walk to pool (~30 seconds)
  - Dive 25m max effort (suited)
  - Rest: 8-10 minutes
  - Repeat

**Weigh-in:** 87.5kg (from 86.9kg previous weigh-in)

**Time per round:** 1 minute 3 seconds (first pull-up to wall touch)

**Purpose:** Transfer gym strength directly to pool power

---

### **Friday Pool Session**

**Total volume:** ~200m  
**Duration:** ~90 minutes  
**Focus:** Top speed testing

**Suit:** Changed to Mizuno Sonic 5NV (from 6PW all week)

**Session structure:**
- Warmup: 300m
- Main set (2 rounds):
  - Round 1: As much rest as needed
  - Round 2: Every 3:30
  - Each round:
    - 1 × 25 dive body weight
    - 1 × 20m dive @ 1kg
    - 1 × 20m dive @ 2kg

**Comparison to last week:** Tracking times against previous Friday

**Final rep comment:**
> "Last 20m of the week was a good one apparently."

---

### **Saturday Gym Session**

**Focus:** Same as Tuesday + power work

**Additions:**
- New pull-up rack (with velocity meter)

**The 5K goal:**

**Accessories:**

**Cam's state:**

## Video 2: McEvoy Method vs USRPT Breakdown

**Source:** "The Training Method Behind Cameron McEvoy's World Record"  
**Narrator:** Sunny (Swimsuit Guy)

### **Key Differences from USRPT**

#### **Volume Comparison**

**Cam McEvoy:**
- 2-4 sessions/week
- 150-500m per session
- Maximum 1,500m/week (usually less)

**Michael Andrew (USRPT):**
- 7-8 sessions/week
- 100m race pace sessions: 2.5k total
- 200m race pace sessions: 4.75k total
- Weekly average: ~30,000m

**Conclusion:** "Anyone comparing McEvoy Method to USRPT, we're comparing apples and pears."

---

#### **Periodization**

**Cam McEvoy:**
> "The most sophisticated, nuanced periodization I've ever seen of any elite swimmer or swimmer ever in my life."

- Changes week-to-week based on phase
- Early season: 1-2x/week swimming, heavy gym
- Mid-season: 3-4x/week swimming, gym maintenance
- Late season: 4-5x/week swimming, power work
- Each year has different focus (2024: first 15m, 2025: 15-25m split)

**Michael Andrew (USRPT):**
- Basically no periodization
- Same sessions year-round
- Progression = hitting faster target times in reps
- Taper = reduce reps but still USRPT structure

---

#### **Equipment & Resistance**

**Cam McEvoy:**
- Parachutes (red/blue/yellow)
- 1080 Sprint machine (1-5kg resistance)
- Buckets
- Fins
- Assisted work (pulled to race speed)
- Resisted work (heavy loads)
- Dive starts every session
- Electronic timing to 0.01s

**Michael Andrew (USRPT):**
- No equipment (traditionally)
- No fins, no paddles
- No resistance work
- Push-off starts (not dives)
- Exception: Kaatsu bands (blood flow restriction)

**Note:** Michael adapted post-2020 (added resistance, fins, gym work with Extreme Performance)

---

#### **Gym Work**

**Cam McEvoy:**
> "Getting extremely strong. Perhaps in some lifts the strongest swimmer we've ever seen. Obsessed with mobility."

- Heavy compound lifts
- Pull-ups: 70kg+ weighted
- Vertical jump: 48cm → 63cm
- Strength is PRIMARY focus (Oct-Feb)

**Michael Andrew (USRPT - traditional):**
- Only focus was swimming
- Land warm-ups (jumping rope)
- No traditional strength training

---

### **Similarities**

Both methods:
1. **Obsessed with accumulating volume at race speed or faster**
2. **Zero aerobic training** - "If it's not race pace, it's not in the program"
3. **No middle ground** - No threshold, no red zone, no easy swimming
4. **Technique at speed** - Not drills, working technique at race pace

---

### **Sunny's Recommendations**

**USRPT:**
- Use as "extra spice" in balanced program
- 1-2x/week modified USRPT
- Good for 50m-400m events
- Avoid permanent USRPT (every session)
  - Loads CNS too much
  - Psychologically draining (training to failure every day)

**McEvoy Method:**
- Super nuanced, requires deep understanding
- 50m sprinters: take lots of leaves from this book
- All events (50-200m): Do 1 session/week like Cam's
  - Example: 4-6 × 25m off blocks, suited, electronic timing
- Tap into anaerobic speed work

## Video 3: Inside the Sprint Session (Transition Block - January 2025)

**Source:** "Inside the Sprint Session Behind Cam McEvoy's 20.88 WR"  
**Location:** Queensland Academy of Sport  
**Date:** Monday, first day of new block  
**Goal:** China Open in 6 weeks

---

### **The Facility**

**Queensland Academy of Sport:**
- 4-lane, 25m pool
- "State-of-the-art technology flowing through DNA of this pool"
- Kistler force plates
- K-Pass timing system
- 1080 Sprint resistance machine
- Electronic timing to 0.01s
- Every 5m segment tracked

**What's tracked:**
- First 5m
- Takeoff velocity
- Takeoff force
- 10m split
- 15m split
- 20m split
- 25m split
- Last 10m split
- Last 5m split
- Stroke rate
- All with weight, all at body weight

**Cam's comment:**
> "Cam has PBs for everything."

---

### **Training Phase Context**

**Strength block (Sept - January):**
- Started post-Singapore World Championships
- Heavy focus in gym
- 1-3x/week swimming only
- Technique maintenance, coordination preservation
- "Slightly tapping into things at speed"
- NO suited swimming until late January
- First suit-up: Friday before this session (after 5+ months)

**Quote:**
> "Foot on the accelerator in the gym and just coasting in the water."

**Transition block (Jan-Feb, 4 weeks):**
> "The bridge that connects all the base foundation I've done in the gym and the maintenance I've done in freestyle technique. Introducing power development. Starting to tap into maximum sprinting."

**Purpose:**
- Convert heavy strength → power + speed in freestyle
- Start suiting up for main sessions
- Track times again
- Still heavy gym focus (power + strength)

**Post-China (Feb onwards):**
- Takes all the way to Commonwealth Games
- Full race-specific preparation

---

### **The Session**

**Total volume:** 500m  
**Top speed volume:** 150m  
**Duration:** Just under 2 hours (9:11 AM - 12:54 PM)

**Warmup:**
- Loosen easy
- Submax 20m @ 2kg
- Submax body weight 25m
- Total: ~350m

**Main Set:**
- 4 × 20m resisted (1-2kg)
  - Resistance kicks in at 10m mark
  - Tracking 10-20m segment time
  - Goal times related to 50m freestyle goal (at certain percentage)
  - Full recovery between reps (hot tub between every rep)
  
- 1 × 25m body weight dive
  - At end of session (with neural fatigue)
  - Transfer what was developed under resistance
  - Focus: Hit times while staying relaxed, not spinning/sacrificing technique

**Total main set:** ~105m

---

### **Key Quotes & Philosophy**

**On goal times:**
> "You can't expect to hit 1:1 in training cuz you have competition environments where you step up, you go much faster than in training, but you can still get within a reasonable amount."

**On the session:**
> "This is a standard Monday. We've been doing this weekly, multiple times a week for years now."

**On the swimming world:**
> "This type of approach, this is just general sprint training. The world needs to step up and get to this level as the acceptable baseline."

> "People using stopwatches for sprinting and the eye in terms of repeatability - it should be left in the past."

> "Track world does it, cycling world does it, a lot of other sports have been doing this for decades. Swimming, it's time to catch up."

---

### **Results**

**Cam dropped PBs:**
- Multiple metrics improved
- Specific times not disclosed in video
- Cam: "Times are actually pretty nice just to quietly say"
- Context: Prep #5, year 3 of this method
- Comparison: Better than previous years at same point

---

### **Sunny's Take**

> "What they're doing here is not something that's far out and outlandish and novel. This is how optimal sprint training should be."

> "I was blown away, not because this is so different to what should be done, but because I'm not seeing this attention to detail or this method of sprint training be done anywhere else in the world."

> "I've been to some of the best facilities, some of the best training programs, and sprinters are doing so much more than this and doing it in archaic ways."

> "This is in my opinion how everyone in the world should be training. Why is there not at least one center in each major country swimming like this?"

---

### **Rest Intervals**

**Between resistance reps:** Full recovery
- Hot tub between every rep
- 5-10 minutes minimum
- "As much rest as needed" for quality

**Between gym+pool rounds:** 8-10 minutes
- Walk from gym to pool (~30 sec)
- Full CNS recovery before next round

**Philosophy:**
> "Not trying to prove you can handle it - trying to adapt to it."

---

### **Suit Protocol**

**Strength phase (Sept-Jan):**
- NO suited swimming for months
- Training in "togs" (speedos/briefs)

**Transition phase (Jan-Feb):**
- Suit up for main sessions
- Electronic timing
- Full analysis

**Race phase (Feb onwards):**
- Most sessions suited
- Testing times regularly

---

### **Technology Integration**

**Every session includes:**
1. Electronic timing (0.01s accuracy)
2. 5m segment splits
3. Force plate data (when diving)
4. Velocity tracking
5. Stroke rate monitoring
6. Video analysis (with Nick Smith, biomechanist)

**Why it matters:**
> "Everything is measured. It's not a stopwatch to 10% accuracy. It is electronic. It is objective. Everything is facts."

## Key Takeaways for Coaches

### **1. Volume is NOT the answer**

**Evidence:**
- 1,355m/week during power phase
- Still setting PBs
- "Shortest pool session I've seen Cam do" (~60 min, ~300m)

**Lesson:** Quality > quantity when every rep is measured and purposeful

---

### **2. Rest is part of the training**

**Evidence:**
- Hot tub between resistance reps
- 8-10 min between gym+pool rounds
- "As much rest as needed" on first round of Friday session
- Sessions last 2 hours despite ~500m volume

**Lesson:** CNS recovery = ability to execute at max quality

---

### **3. Technology enables precision**

**Without electronic timing:**
- Can't track 5m segments
- Can't identify breakout improvements
- Can't compare accurately session-to-session
- Forced to use feel/stopwatch (unreliable)

**With technology:**
- Every metric tracked
- PBs for every segment
- Objective feedback
- Can train with precision

**Lesson:** "It's time to catch up" - invest in timing systems

---

### **4. Periodization must be nuanced**

**Strength phase:** 1-3x/week swimming, heavy gym
**Transition phase:** 3-4x/week swimming, gym power
**Race phase:** 4-5x/week swimming, gym maintenance

**NOT:**
- Same sessions year-round
- High volume all the time
- Gradual taper only

**Lesson:** Weekly structure changes based on phase

---

### **5. Technique at speed, not drills**

**Evidence:**
- Wednesday: "Exposure to movement at race speed without energetic cost"
- Assisted work (pulled to race velocity)
- Fins + resistance (feel race speed comfortably)
- NO drill work shown in any video

**Lesson:** Practice the actual movement pattern at race velocity

## Summary: The McEvoy Method in Practice

**Volume:** 1,000-1,500m/week  
**Frequency:** 3-5 sessions/week (phase dependent)  
**Session length:** 60-120 minutes  
**Actual swimming time:** 10-20 minutes

**Every session includes:**
- Electronic timing
- Video analysis
- Force/velocity metrics
- Comparison to previous sessions
- Hot tub recovery
- Coaching discussion

**Phase-specific focus:**
- Strength: Gym primary, pool maintenance
- Transition: Gym power, pool introduction
- Race: Pool primary, gym maintenance

**Technology:**
- 1080 Sprint machine
- Kistler force plates
- K-Pass timing system
- Video analysis setup
- iPad for real-time data

**The result:**
- Olympic gold at 30
- World champion at 31
- 20.88 world record
- Sustainable into mid-30s
