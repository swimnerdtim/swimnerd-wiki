## Part 1: The Foundation - Understanding The Method

### The Core Philosophy (In Cam's Words)

> "If you're trying to get the heaviest squat you can do, they're not doing 30,000 squats a week with a bar and no weights. They go heavy...In terms of swimming I race for 21 seconds but training is what 30-40k a week which is three times the Marathon distance for swimming."

> "Traditional volume creates fitness for finishing a 9-session week, not fitness for finishing a 21-second race. The relevance of 30-40k to a 50 freestyle is drastically different from the relevance it has to managing a 7-9 session week."

> "It's no heroic feat of endurance. 17 seconds of swimming. Anyone can go hard for 17 seconds."

**The fundamental insight:**
Sprint freestyle is a **neuromuscular event**, not an aerobic event. Training should develop the qualities needed for 21 seconds of maximum output, not the qualities needed to survive 10 training sessions per week.

---

### The Systems Approach

Cam studied **Informational Monism and Superposition Theory** under Steven Pauling Richards (IPSA - International Psycho-systems Analysis). He adapted their bio-psycho-social clinical framework to swimming training.

**The key concept:**
> "If I dive in and do a 50 meter max in freestyle, all of these exist at the same time: energy systems, strength output, power (speed of strength), and technique. They're all happening simultaneously."

**Traditional approach:**
- Views events primarily through metabolic lens (aerobic/anaerobic/ATP)
- Volume-based progression
- "Get your meters in" mentality

**McEvoy approach:**
- Views events through **multiple simultaneous lenses**: metabolic, strength, power, technique
- Quality-based progression
- "Every meter must have purpose" mentality

**The pull-up analogy:**
> "Imagine your sport was 10 reps of pull-ups with 40 kilos and you had to do it as fast as you can with strict form. That might take the same time as a 50 freestyle. How would the strength world train for that? They wouldn't do 30,000 reps with no weight. They'd raise max strength (someone who pulls up 100kg can do 10 at 40kg easier than someone who maxes at 60kg). And they'd build strength endurance (being able to do 10 reps at progressively heavier weights)."

**Application to swimming:**
- Build maximum strength in the gym (raise the ceiling)
- Develop technique under resistance (strength endurance in specific movement pattern)
- Transfer to bodyweight/race-specific swimming (utilize the capacity)

---

### The Funnel Analogy

> "Build the biggest funnel possible (max strength in gym), then open the exit hole (technique/coordination). Swimming speed = flow rate through the funnel."

**What this means practically:**

**The funnel (strength capacity):**
- Vertical jump: 48cm → 63cm (+15cm)
- Pull-ups progressing toward 2.0x bodyweight (77.5kg at 85kg BW)
- Time to 15m: 5.56 → 5.02 seconds
- Takeoff velocity: 4.7 m/s → 5.3 m/s

**The exit hole (technique/coordination):**
- 8 months to develop race-ready 50m freestyle technique (Oct 2022 → May 2023)
- Hollow-body core activation holding through entire stroke
- Rib rotation pattern (not traditional hip rotation)
- Overhead stretch position at every extension

**The flow rate (performance):**
- October 2022: 22.8 (first swim back)
- May 2023: 21.8 (technique breakthrough)
- July 2023: 21.10 (World Champion)
- August 2024: 21.25 (Olympic Champion)

## Part 2: Seasonal Structure & Periodization

### The Three-Phase System

Cam's season divides into three distinct phases, each with different training focus and swimming frequency.

---

#### **Phase 1: Maximum Strength Block (October-February)**

**Duration:** ~4 months  
**Swimming frequency:** 3 times/week  
**Swimming focus:** Technique maintenance ONLY

**Philosophy:**
> "At that point I'm so optimized for strength output that I can put more into the water under resistance than at bodyweight. Plus, coordination under resistance will be better than at bodyweight after a heavy strength block."

**Pool sessions:**
- 7-8 RPE (out of 10)
- Stroke rate: ~58 (vs. 60-63 race rate)
- Total volume: 300-500m per session
- NO timed work
- NO suited swimming for MONTHS
- Submax technique focus only

**Gym focus:**
- Maximum strength development
- Heavy compound lifts (squats, deadlifts, pull-ups)
- Singles only (never doubles/triples)
- Progressive overload toward strength targets

**Goal:**
> "The brain doesn't care if you're pulling 80kg in the gym or diving in for a 50 sprint. You're developing the magnitude of that neural signal in its most general form."

**Key insight:**
> "I wouldn't push off the wall without pre-activation. 30 seconds on wall, head underwater, finding the feeling. Week 1-2: Hold for half a rep. Week 3-4: Hold for one full rep. Week 5-8: Hold for 5+ reps. Months later: Maintain through entire 50m."

**Real example (December 2024 - Japan prep):**
- Oct-Dec: Heavy gym block
- 1-2x/week swimming: 6x10m with fins + 3kg resistance
- Total weekly volume: ~120m
- NO bodyweight swimming, NO suited training
- Japan Open result: 21.70 (fastest in-season swim ever)
- 25m split = same as Olympics

**Lesson:** Top speed can be maintained with minimal swimming IF strength and neural output are prioritized.

---

#### **Phase 2: Power Transition Block (Mid-Feb to March)**

**Duration:** ~4 weeks  
**Swimming frequency:** 5 times/week  
**Swimming focus:** Introduce resisted sprinting + technique

**Philosophy:**
> "This is the bridging block between the two. On one side, you have the very optimized strength-based focus where everything in the water is submax. On the other side, you have the swimming-focused preparation that leads into competition. This middle block melds the two."

**Pool sessions breakdown:**
- **3 technique sessions** (same as Phase 1 - maintaining continuity)
- **2 resisted sprint sessions** (~80-100m each)
  - 4-5 × 20m sprints
  - Moderate resistance (1080 machine, 1-3kg)
  - Full recovery between reps (5-10 min)

**Gym work:**
- Transition from max strength to power
- **French contrast sets:** Weighted pull-up → resisted swim
- Maintain strength while adding speed
- Example: Heavy pull-ups (gym) → 20m resisted sprint (pool) → Rest 5-10 min → Repeat 4-5 times

**Key principle:**
> "It's semantics whether you call it a gym session or a swim session. I'm doing a heavy pull-up into a bodyweight pull-up, contrasted to a heavy pull-up into a resisted swim. You can put it in the gym basket or the swim basket - it's just a fast movement that's slightly more swim-specific."

**Why resistance at this phase:**
> "I could put a lot more into the water under resistance than at bodyweight at that point in time. And the coordination of freestyle under resistance will be better than at bodyweight. You can't fake it under resistance - it forces perfect coordination."

---

#### **Phase 3: Race-Specific Block (April-Competition)**

**Duration:** 3-4 months  
**Swimming frequency:** 4-5 times/week  
**Swimming focus:** Race-pace volume accumulation

**Philosophy:**
> "Accumulate volume of sprinting, get that base of fast fitness (not aerobic fitness), then as you get closer to competition, freshen up, expose yourself to higher intensities, adapt to those new speeds, and hopefully when you rest you get that step forward."

**Pool sessions:**
- Build base of "fast fitness"
- Every rep at race pace or faster
- Gradually increase intensity as competition approaches
- Strategic rest between quality efforts
- Total volume: Still low (1,000-1,500m/week)

**Gym focus:**
- **Maintenance only**
- Heavy enough to maintain strength
- Not going to 5RM max - staying ~10% off
- Minimal fatigue spillover to swimming

**Periodization within the phase:**

**Early (April-May):**
- Accumulation: More reps, race pace
- Example: 6-8 × 25m @ race pace with 2-3 min rest
- Building tolerance to race speed

**Mid (June):**
- Intensification: Fewer reps, faster than race pace
- Example: 4-5 × 25m @ 95%+ with full recovery
- Adapting nervous system to higher velocities

**Late (July - Competition):**
- Taper: Minimal volume, maximum quality
- Example: 2-3 × 25m max effort with complete recovery
- Sharpening for championship performance

---

### Volume Across The Season

**Weekly swimming totals:**

| Phase | Frequency | Volume/Session | Weekly Total |
|-------|-----------|----------------|--------------|
| **Strength (Oct-Feb)** | 3x/week | 300-500m | 900-1,500m |
| **Power (Feb-Mar)** | 5x/week | 200-400m | 1,000-2,000m |
| **Race (Apr-Jul)** | 4-5x/week | 300-500m | 1,200-2,500m |

**Context:**
> "1,500m per week total. Most elite swimmers do that in a single warm-up. Traditional programs: 40,000-70,000m per week."

**The math:**
- Traditional program: ~50,000m/week average
- McEvoy program: ~1,500m/week average
- **Reduction: 97% less swimming**

## Part 3: Technique Development

### The 8-Month Journey (Oct 2022 → May 2023)

**Starting point (October 2022):**
> "It was a sped-up 200 freestyle stroke at 22.8. I had to completely rebuild my 50m technique from scratch."

**The challenge:**
> "Most swimmers would panic. But I stayed patient because I understood the development process."

**The breakthrough (May 2023):**
- Sydney Open: 21.8 (-1.0 from previous best of 22.8)
- 8 months of obsessive technical development paid off

---

### The Two Core Anchor Points

#### **1. Hollow-Body Core Activation**

**The concept:**
> "There's an isometric part where the whole body is under one contraction that doesn't break throughout the whole 50. And then a dynamic part on top where your body moves around that isometric point."

**What it is:**
- Gymnastics hollow-body hold position (YouTube "hollow body hold" for visuals)
- Maintain activation THROUGH ENTIRE STROKE
- Never breaks, even during arm recovery
- Most difficult part: Holding during arm recovery phase

**Development process:**
> "I wouldn't push off the wall without pre-activation. 30 seconds on wall, head underwater, finding the feeling."

**Week 1-2:** Hold for half a rep  
**Week 3-4:** Hold for one full rep  
**Week 5-8:** Hold for 5+ reps  
**Months later:** Maintain through entire 50m

**Training frequency:**
- 5x/week during development phase
- 3-4x/week maintenance (current)

**Why it matters:**
- Creates stable platform for force application
- Prevents "falling" (breaking isometric activation when fatigued)
- Allows maximum power transfer from core to limbs

---

#### **2. Overhead Stretch Position**

**The concept:**
> "There's this mobility movement where you put your thumbs into your armpits, lift shoulders, elevate scaps, expand ribs. Hit this position at EVERY full extension."

**What it does:**
- Creates maximum body length
- Opens up ribcage for rotation
- Sets up proper catch position
- Both arms, every stroke

**Training:**
- 30 seconds on wall before every push-off
- Deliberate practice at every extension
- Progressive development over weeks/months

---

### The Rotation Pattern

**Traditional approach:** Hip rotation drives the stroke  
**McEvoy approach:** Rib rotation with hip rotation as byproduct

**The visual:**
> "| (neutral) → ) (right rib flares outward sideways like a bracket) → ( (left rib flares)"

**Why it's different:**
- Flare one rib outward sideways
- Creates torque without excessive body roll
- More direct force application
- Discovered through experimentation, not taught anywhere

**How to cue:**
> "I focus on the ribs, not the hips. The hips follow naturally."

---

### The Kick: Timing Over Propulsion

**Pattern:** 6-beat kick with variations
- 2 shallow, 1 deep, 2 shallow, 1 deep
- Deep kick on same side as pulling arm

**Purpose (NOT forward propulsion in 50m):**
- Balance
- Timing/rhythm
- Anchoring body position
- Torque generation

**The controversial part:**
> "I dig my knees deep into the bottom of the pool. Creates drag, but the lift I get is worth it. Brett Hawke at Singapore: 'I could see your hamstrings. With fins I swear I saw your quads.'"

**Training approach:**
- **ZERO isolated kick training**
- No kickboard work
- No vertical kick sets
- Only integrated kicking within full-stroke swimming

**Philosophy:**
> "The kick in a 50m free is for timing and balance, not propulsion. Train it that way."

---

### Pressing Diagonally Down (Not Back)

**Traditional teaching:** Press water backward (horizontal force)  
**McEvoy approach:** Press diagonally downward

**Effect:**
- Creates lift in the water
- Gets body higher on surface
- Reduces frontal drag
- Visual confirmation: Hamstrings/quads visible from side

**Discovery:**
- Not taught, discovered through experimentation
- Verified through biomechanical analysis with Nick Smith
- Confirmed by video analysis and feel

---

### Stroke Rate

**Training (submax):** ~58 SPM  
**Racing:** 60-63 SPM  
**Difference:** Only 2-5 SPM

**Key insight:**
> "Very small difference between training and racing stroke rate. Quality technique transfers directly."

---

### The "Falling" Concept

**What it means:**
- Losing the isometric core activation
- Breaking away from anchored position
- Usually happens stroke 3-4 when fatigued

**Santos Silva's term:**
> "Stay on top" (maintaining activation) vs. "I fell" (lost activation)

**Training focus:**
- Recognize the feeling of "staying on top"
- Practice maintaining activation when tired
- Build capacity to hold through full 50m

## Part 4: Resistance Training Protocols

### First Exposure (October 2022)

**The parachute introduction:**
> "First resistance session: 5x25m with a parachute. Went home and napped for 2 hours. I'd never done resistance properly in my entire life."

**Neural fatigue:**
> "So neurally fatigued. That's what maximum neural output feels like."

**Adaptation timeline:**
- Week 1: Extreme fatigue, naps required
- Week 2-4: Gradual tolerance building
- Months later: Capacity to handle significant resistance volume

---

### The 8-Week Resistance Block (Pre-Doha 2024)

**Structure:**
- Post-Fukuoka 2023 (August)
- Every session on Destroyo/1080 Sprint
- Broken 50s at various resistances
- NO bodyweight swimming for 8 weeks

**Volume:**
> "~5.6k equivalent sprint volume under tension. Massive neural load."

**Result:**
- Led to Doha breakthrough (21.06 heat PR)
- Also led to Doha burnout (couldn't repeat in final)

**Lesson learned:**
> "Neural fatigue is real. Bulgarian method weightlifters expect 18-24 month depressed periods. It's not psychological weakness - it's biological consequence."

---

### Equipment & Resistance Levels

**1080 Sprint:**
- 1kg = light resistance
- 2kg = moderate
- 3kg = heavy
- 5kg+ = very heavy (rarely used)

**Destroyo Power Rack:**
- 10-40kg in the tub
- 3kg on 1080 ≈ 30-35kg in Destroyo

**Parachutes:**
- Red = light (~1kg equivalent)
- Blue = medium (~2kg equivalent)
- Yellow/Green = heavy (~3kg+ equivalent)

---

### Typical Warm-Up Progression

**The ladder approach:**

1. Red parachute (~70% effort)
2. Blue parachute (~75% effort)
3. Medium 1080 resistance (~85% effort)
4. Light 1080 resistance (~90% effort)
5. Bodyweight (~95% effort)
6. Fins (~100% effort)

**Purpose:**
- Gradual neural activation
- Progressive speed increase
- Technique maintenance at each level
- Prepares body for max efforts

---

### Periodization of Resistance Work

**Heavy block (8-12 weeks out):**
- Frequency: 2-3x/week
- Volume: 150-300m per session
- Recovery: Minimum 48 hours between sessions
- Focus: Building strength-endurance under heavy load

**Power phase (6-8 weeks out):**
- Frequency: 2x/week
- Volume: 100-200m per session
- Lighter resistance, higher velocity
- French contrast integration (gym → pool)

**Race phase (0-6 weeks out):**
- Frequency: 0-1x/week
- Volume: 50-100m max
- Very light resistance or bodyweight only
- Sharpening, not building

---

### The Technique Benefit

> "You can't fake it under resistance. It forces perfect coordination. If you can hold perfect technique under heavy resistance, it becomes automatic at bodyweight."

**Why this works:**
- Resistance exposes technical flaws immediately
- Forces engagement of proper muscle patterns
- Develops strength in race-specific positions
- Creates margin of error for race execution

---

### Recovery Requirements

**Minimum between resistance sessions:** 48 hours

**Cam's experience:**
> "If I do heavy resistance Monday, I'm not doing it again until Thursday at earliest. The neural fatigue is too high."

**Warning signs of overuse:**
- Persistent soreness beyond normal DOMS
- Declining velocity at same resistance
- Mental fatigue/lack of motivation
- Technique breakdown under light loads

## Part 6: Mental Preparation & Competition Psychology

### Pre-Race Emotional Cycles

**Day 3 before competition:**
> "Infinite wellspring of energy. Could run through a brick wall. Overhyped feeling of being ready."

**Day 2 before competition:**
> "Polar opposite. Classic nerves, doubt. Feeling tense, can't get technical rhythm."

**Day 1 before competition:**
> "Neutral. Lack of emotion, lack of engagement. Another representation of anticipatory anxiety."

**Key insight:**
> "All three are normal. Don't judge yourself by emotional state. Trust the preparation."

---

### The Doha Lesson: "Rep in Reserve"

**What happened (Feb 2024):**
- **Heat:** 21.06 (PR) - went ALL OUT
- **Semi:** 21.13 - went ALL OUT again
- **Final:** 21.17 - beaten by Bokov (21.14)

**The lesson:**
> "It doesn't matter how fit you are. If you're at 100% max and go max again 12 hours later, then max again, fitness can't repair that. You need a rep in reserve."

**Ben Proud model:**
- Always gets faster heat → semi → final
- Strategic energy management
- Saves best for when it matters

**Application to Paris:**
- **Heat:** 21.32 - controlled, rep in reserve
- **Semi:** 21.28 - controlled, rep in reserve
- **Final:** 21.25 - NOW go for gold

---

### The Pre-Paris Training Decision

**Traditional path:**
- Taper into trials
- Rest
- Travel to Europe
- Build up
- Taper again for Olympics

**Cam's path:**
- Taper into trials
- Hit 21.3

**Rowan Taylor's support:**

**Why it worked:**
> "I wanted to practice swimming fast repeatedly, not practice resting. The traditional path assumes you can only be fast once. I wanted to prove you can sustain it."

## Part 7: Real Workout Examples

### Monday Pool Session (From Video)

**Total distance:** ~300-400m  
**Total time:** ~60 minutes (includes rest, coaching, video)

**Warmup:**
- 300m loosen
- 2 warm-up reps:
  - 1x25 @ body weight (submax speed)
  - 1x20m @ 2kg resistance (1080 machine)

**Main Set:**
- 1x25 max effort dive (body weight, no resistance)
- 4 reps with resistance:
  - 2x20m @ 1kg
  - 2x20m @ 2kg
- Optional finisher (depends on feel):
  - 1x25 body weight max, OR
  - 2x20m @ 5kg resistance (if not feeling sharp)

**Metrics tracked (every rep):**
- 10m split
- 15m split
- 20m split
- 25m time
- Stroke rate (target: 62+ SPM)
- Segment times

**The "shut it down" moment:**
> "Cam started to feel good on the last rep (9.50 split), asked Tim if he should do another. Tim shut it down: 'No, we got more training this week. It's only Monday.' Preserving energy for the full week."

**Total main set:** ~100-140m

---

### Strength Block Technique Session

**Frequency:** 3x/week  
**Duration:** 45-60 minutes  
**Total volume:** 300-500m

**Format:**
- 300m warmup (choice stroke, easy pace)
- 6-8x50m technique focus:
  - 25 build to 5th gear
  - 25 easy recovery
  - Rest: 1-2 minutes between reps
- 30 seconds hollow-body activation on wall before each push-off
- Focus: Maintaining technique at ~58 SPM
- NO timed swims, NO suited work

**Purpose:**
> "Maintain technique while building strength in gym. Can't let technique degrade during heavy strength phase."

---

### Power Phase Resistance Session

**Frequency:** 2x/week  
**Duration:** 90-120 minutes (includes gym work)  
**Swim volume:** 100-200m

**Format:**
- Gym: 3-5 heavy pull-ups (near max weight)
- Walk to pool (30 seconds)
- 1x20m resisted sprint @ 2-3kg (1080 machine)
- Rest: 8-10 minutes
- Repeat: 4-6 rounds total

**Example numbers:**
- Pull-ups: 77.5kg @ 85kg bodyweight (2.0x target)
- Sprint resistance: 2kg
- Target velocity: Maintain within 5% of bodyweight max
- Stroke rate: 60-62 SPM even under resistance

**Purpose:**
> "Melding gym strength and pool application. Teaching the body to express strength through freestyle stroke pattern."

---

### Race Phase Sprint Session

**Frequency:** 3-4x/week  
**Duration:** 60-90 minutes  
**Total volume:** 400-600m

**Accumulation phase (early):**
- Warmup: 400m (200 easy, 4x50 build)
- Main: 6x25m @ race pace
  - Target: 10.5-10.8 (for 21.0-21.5 goal)
  - Rest: 2-3 minutes
  - Stroke rate: 60-63 SPM
- Cooldown: 100m easy

**Intensification phase (mid):**
- Warmup: 400m
- Main: 4x25m @ 95%+ max
  - Target: 10.2-10.5 (faster than race pace)
  - Rest: 5-8 minutes (full CNS recovery)
  - Stroke rate: 62-65 SPM
- Cooldown: 100m easy

**Taper phase (late):**
- Warmup: 300m
- Main: 2-3x25m max effort
  - No target time, just FAST
  - Rest: Complete recovery (10+ minutes)
  - Stroke rate: Whatever happens naturally
- Cooldown: 50-100m easy

---

**Cam's view:**

### "What About The 100m? 200m? Does This Work For Longer Events?"

**Cam's honest answer:**
> "I don't know. My focus is 50m. For 100m, you'd need more race-pace endurance. For 200m, even more. But the principles might still apply - maximize power output, minimize junk volume, train race-specific qualities."

**The experiment he considered:**

**Hypothesis:**
- 100m: Probably works with modifications (more race-pace reps, slightly higher volume)
- 200m: Maybe? Would need to test
- 400m+: Probably not - aerobic demands become significant

### "How Do You Know When You're Doing Too Much Resistance Work?"

**Warning signs:**
1. **Declining velocity at same resistance**
   - If 2kg used to feel like 80%, now feels like 90%+

2. **Persistent neural fatigue**
   - Still tired 48+ hours after session
   - Mental fog, lack of motivation

3. **Technique breakdown under light loads**
   - Can't hold form with parachute
   - Bodyweight swimming feels "heavy"

4. **Sleep disruption**
   - Trouble falling asleep despite physical fatigue
   - Waking frequently during night

**Cam's rule:**
> "Minimum 48 hours between resistance sessions. If in doubt, take an extra day. Resistance training is a stimulus, not a test. You're not trying to prove you can handle it - you're trying to adapt to it."

---

### "What About Feel For The Water - Don't You Lose It With 3x/Week?"

**The traditional belief:**
> "You need to swim 9-10x/week to maintain feel. 48 hours off and you lose connection."

**Cam's counter:**
> "That's selection bias from high-volume programs. If you're used to swimming 9x/week and take 48 hours off, you'll feel off. But if you're adapted to 3-4x/week, 48 hours off is normal. Your body adapts to the frequency it experiences."

**The evidence:**
> "I swim 3x/week for months during strength phase. When I come back to race-pace work, my feel is fine. Better, actually, because I'm fresher."

**The difference:**
- **High-volume programs:** Easy swimming feels great, pace feels hard
- **Low-volume programs:** Easy swimming feels awkward, pace feels natural

**Why:**
> "You adapt to what you practice. If you practice easy swimming 80% of the time, you'll be good at easy swimming. If you practice race pace 80% of the time, you'll be good at race pace."

---

### "How Do You Periodize Gym Work Around Swimming?"

**General principle:**
> "Gym and pool compete for the same neural resource. You can't max out in both simultaneously. Pick your priority for each phase."

**Strength phase (Oct-Feb):**
- **Gym:** Primary focus, aggressive progression
- **Pool:** Maintenance only, technique preservation
- **Rationale:** Build the funnel as big as possible

**Power phase (Feb-Mar):**
- **Gym:** Transitioning to power work, still challenging
- **Pool:** Introducing resistance, building swim-specific strength
- **Rationale:** Meld the two systems

**Race phase (Apr-Jul):**
- **Gym:** Maintenance only, stay strong but don't push
- **Pool:** Primary focus, race-pace accumulation
- **Rationale:** Express the capacity you built

**The mistake:**
> "Trying to progress gym AND pool simultaneously. You'll fry your nervous system and make no progress in either."

## Part 10: How To Apply This (Practical Guide)

### For Sprint Freestylers (50m/100m)

**Start here:**
1. Get baseline strength assessment (vertical jump, pull-ups, force plate if available)
2. Identify technical weak points (video analysis)
3. Determine current training load (volume, frequency, intensity distribution)

**Year 1: Conservative adaptation**
- Reduce volume by 30% (not 97% immediately)
- Add 2x/week resistance work (light resistance, 100-200m)
- Increase gym frequency to 3x/week (focus on compound lifts)
- Maintain 5-6x/week swimming (transition slowly)

**Year 2: Aggressive adaptation**
- Reduce volume by 60%
- Implement 3-phase periodization
- Heavy resistance blocks (8-week cycles)
- 3-4x/week swimming in strength phase
- Track metrics obsessively (force, velocity, splits)

**Year 3: Full implementation**
- Cam-style volume (~1,500m/week)
- Strength-focused off-season (Oct-Feb)
- Power transition (Feb-Mar)
- Race-specific spring (Apr-Jul)

**Critical success factors:**
- **Coaching support:** Need a Tim Lane-type partnership
- **Facility access:** 1080 Sprint or equivalent resistance system
- **Mental resilience:** Withstand criticism from traditionalists
- **Patience:** Results may take 6-12 months to appear

---

### For Masters Swimmers (30+)

**Why this works even better:**
- Recovery becomes limiting factor (lower volume = better recovery)
- Strength can still improve significantly
- Technique refinement more valuable than volume grinding
- Sustainable for 20+ year competitive lifespan

**Adaptations:**
- Slightly higher volume than Cam (2,000-3,000m/week vs. 1,500m)
- More recovery time between resistance sessions (72 hours vs. 48)
- Emphasize mobility/injury prevention
- Accept slower progression timeline

**Expected results:**
- Lifetime PBs possible even at 40-50 years old
- Better relationship with sport (less grinding)
- Fewer injuries (less repetitive stress)
- More sustainable long-term

---

### For Coaches Implementing With A Team

**The challenge:**
- Can't have one swimmer doing radically different program than everyone else
- Team dynamics matter
- Facility/equipment constraints

**Possible solutions:**

**Option 1: Separate sprint group**
- 3-5 dedicated 50/100 specialists
- Low-volume, high-intensity focus
- Own training schedule/location
- Doesn't disrupt main group

**Option 2: Modified approach for whole team**
- Reduce volume for everyone by 30-40% (not 97%)
- Add resistance work 2x/week
- Emphasize technique quality over quantity
- Maintain group cohesion with shared sessions

**Option 3: Individual athlete experiment**
- One willing athlete tests the method
- Document everything
- Share learnings with team
- Expand if successful

**Warning:**
> "This requires ego death from the coach. If you need to be 'the genius' who designed the program, this won't work. The athlete has to lead."

## Conclusion: The McEvoy Method In One Page

### Core Principles

1. **Sprint freestyle is a neuromuscular event, not aerobic**
   - Train the neural system, not just the metabolic system

2. **Quality >> Quantity**
   - 1,500m/week of perfect technique beats 50,000m of junk volume

3. **Build strength first, then convert to speed**
   - Funnel analogy: Max strength = big funnel, technique = opening the exit

4. **Technique takes time**
   - 8 months to develop race-ready 50m technique
   - Hollow-body activation + overhead stretch position = core anchors

5. **Resistance training is not optional**
   - 20-30% of training volume should be under resistance
   - Forces perfect coordination, builds strength-endurance

6. **Periodization matters**
   - Strength (Oct-Feb) → Power (Feb-Mar) → Race (Apr-Jul)
   - Don't try to progress everything simultaneously

7. **Relationship with sport > medals**
   - Sustainable > optimal
   - Love of swimming > fear of failure

8. **Athlete as expert**
   - Coach provides expertise, athlete makes decisions
   - CEO and caddy model
