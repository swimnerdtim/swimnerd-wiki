# Queensland Academy of Sport Training

Cam McEvoy's training base at the Queensland Academy of Sport just outside Brisbane represents one of the most technologically advanced sprint training facilities in the world.

## Facility Overview

**Location:** Queensland Academy of Sport, Brisbane, Australia

**Pool Specifications:**
- 4-lane, 25m pool
- Smaller than most elite training facilities
- Compensates for size with cutting-edge technology

## Technology Integration

The QAS pool is described as having "technology flowing throughout the DNA of this pool." The facility includes:

### 1080 Sprint Resistance System
- Programmable resistance from 1kg to 5kg+
- Allows precise measurement of speed at different resistance levels
- Tracks 10m, 15m, 20m, and 25m splits in real-time
- Can measure stroke rate throughout each rep

### Performance Tracking
- Real-time data boards visible on deck
- Immediate feedback on:
  - Split times (10m, 15m, 20m, 25m)
  - Stroke rates
  - Segment times
  - Velocity metrics

### Recovery Tools
- Hot tub for post-session recovery
- Strategic placement for immediate access after high-intensity work

## Training Philosophy at QAS

The facility's design supports McEvoy's unique training methodology:

### Volume Approach
**Total session distance:** ~500m
**High-intensity volume:** 150m at top-end speed
**Session duration:** Approximately 2 hours (despite low volume)

This represents the opposite of traditional "get your meters in" philosophy. The extended session time comes from:
- Extensive rest between reps
- Detailed metric analysis
- Technical adjustments
- Strategic recovery periods

## Typical Top-Speed Session Structure

**Example from Monday block leading into China Open (6 weeks out):**

**Warm-up:**
- Submax 20m @ 2kg resistance
- Submax 25m at body weight

**Main Set:**
- 6 reps maximum effort
- Focus: top-end speed development
- Every meter measured and analyzed

**Equipment:**
- Mizuno racing suit worn throughout (no suit changes)
- Same suit for warm-up and max-effort work

## Training Block Periodization

### Off-Season/Strength Phase
**Duration:** September through January
**Pool work:** 1-3 sessions per week
**Focus:** 
- Heavy gym work (foot on accelerator)
- Technique maintenance
- Coordination and patterning
- Minimal top-speed work

**Example timeline:**
- September: Post-World Championships, begin strength block
- December 1st: Japan Open (50m comeback race)
- Late January: First suit-up since previous World Championships
- February: Transition to speed-focused blocks

### Speed Phase (Pre-Competition)
**Duration:** 6-8 weeks before major competition
**Pool work:** 3 sessions per week
**Focus:**
- Top-end speed development
- Race-pace specificity
- Competition preparation

## Training Environment

### Team Culture
Despite individual event focus, McEvoy trains with a consistent group:
- International training partners from different disciplines
- Shared high-performance standards
- Collaborative technology use
- Supportive competitive environment

### Coaching Setup
- **Tim Lane:** On-deck coaching and session design
- **Sports Science Support:** Real-time data analysis
- **Performance Staff:** Metric tracking and feedback

## The QAS Advantage

### Why a Small Pool Works

Traditional thinking suggests elite swimmers need:
- Olympic-sized (50m) pools
- 8+ lanes for large squads
- Extensive deck space

**QAS flips this model:**
- 25m allows for more precise turn and underwaters work
- 4 lanes = smaller groups = more individual attention
- Technology density > facility size
- Quality of training environment > quantity of space

### Technology as Force Multiplier

The 1080 Sprint system and integrated tracking allow McEvoy to:
- Train at race pace with added resistance
- Build power without traditional high-volume work
- Get immediate objective feedback
- Make session-to-session adjustments based on hard data

## Session Timing & Structure

**Typical arrival:** 9:11 AM
**Session duration:** 2+ hours for 500m total distance

The extended time allows for:
- Thorough warm-up protocols
- Maximum recovery between high-intensity reps
- Data analysis and technical discussion
- Cool-down and recovery processes

## Comparison to Traditional Facilities

| Traditional Elite Facility | Queensland Academy (McEvoy) |
|---------------------------|----------------------------|
| 50m Olympic pool | 25m short course |
| 8-10 lanes | 4 lanes |
| Large squads (20-30 swimmers) | Small groups (3-4 consistent partners) |
| High volume (5,000-10,000m/session) | Low volume (500-1,500m/session) |
| Basic timing equipment | Advanced 1080 Sprint + real-time tracking |
| General preparation | Race-specific precision |

## Training Innovations

### Resistance Protocols
Using programmable resistance to:
- Build power at race pace
- Develop acceleration patterns
- Train force production without volume
- Simulate race conditions with additional load

### Data-Driven Adjustments
Every rep informs the next:
- Stroke rate feedback guides effort distribution
- Split times show acceleration patterns
- Segment times reveal efficiency points
- Real-time adjustments during sessions

## Recovery Integration

Post-session protocols at QAS:
- Immediate hot tub access
- Team recovery environment
- Social connection (including Call of Duty discussions!)
- Mental decompression alongside physical recovery

## The Bottom Line

The Queensland Academy facility proves that **technology and methodology can overcome facility size limitations**. With the right tools and coaching expertise, a 4-lane 25m pool can produce the fastest swimmer in the world.

The QAS approach represents the future of sprint training: precision over volume, data over tradition, and quality over quantity.
