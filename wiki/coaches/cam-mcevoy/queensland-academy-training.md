# Queensland Academy of Sport Training Environment
**Where world-class sprint training meets state-of-the-art technology**

**Location:** Queensland Academy of Sport, just outside Brisbane  
**Pool:** 4-lane, 25m technical pool  
**Context:** Cam McEvoy's training base since returning in October 2022  
**Video source:** "How Cam McEvoy Trains to be the Fastest Swimmer in the World" (ASP production, January 2025)

## The Facility

### Pool Specifications

**Not your typical training pool:**
- **4 lanes, 25 meters** — intimate, focused environment
- **Technical pool** — designed for precision work, not volume
- State-of-the-art technology integrated throughout
- "The technology flowing throughout the DNA of this pool is just incredible" — ASP

### Why 25m?

**Strategic choice:**
- Better for sprint-specific work
- Easier to measure and track 5m, 10m, 15m, 20m, 25m splits
- Allows for more frequent turns = more dive practice
- Easier to set up resistance equipment
- More controlled environment

## The Technology Stack

### 1. **1080 Motion Resistance Machine (Dextro Power Rack)**

**What it is:**
- Cable-based resistance system
- Attaches to swimmer's waist
- Provides constant, programmable resistance

**How Cam uses it:**

**Resistance levels:**
- **3kg** + fins = race velocity simulation
- **Light resistance (1-2kg)** = speed work with slight load
- **Medium resistance** = power development
- **Heavy resistance (30-35kg equivalent)** = pure strength work

**Key feature:** Provides 5m split times automatically

**Cam's warm-up example:**
> "I dive in, resistance kicks in at the 10m mark, and I'm getting that 10-20m segment. I've got goal times related to my goal 50m free times at a certain percentage."

**Technical detail:**
> "3 kilos with fins is roughly the race velocity that I hit in competition. If I wanted to keep feel of technique, I wanted to just hit the same velocity that I do on a race. Doing it with fins and 3kg is way easier than being fresh enough to do it by myself with no equipment."

### 2. **Kistler Force Plates**

**Purpose:**
- Measures takeoff force from dive
- Tracks takeoff velocity
- Provides objective data on explosive power

**Why it matters:**
- The dive is ~15% of a 50m race
- Tracks power development over training blocks
- Objective feedback on strength gains translating to starts

### 3. **Keypower Timing System**

**Precision:**
- Electronic timing to **0.01 seconds**
- Measures every 5-meter segment
- Instant feedback on every rep

**What gets tracked:**
- 0-5m (dive + breakout)
- 5-10m
- 10-15m
- 15-20m
- 20-25m
- Full 25m time

**The microscope:**

Cam on the mental challenge:

> "You're working on a fine line between plus or minus 0.05 that basically determines whether or not you had a successful session or if something's wrong. That detail under the microscope is massive and takes a lot of mental toll."

> "Multiple times a week you're working where a blink of an eye — 0.05 seconds — is the difference between if you're going in the right direction or if things are about to blow up."

### 4. **Kistler Block Technology**

**Integration with starts:**
- Measures force application during dive
- Tracks consistency across reps
- Helps optimize dive technique

**Combined with Keypower:**
- See the force applied → see the resulting velocity
- Immediate cause-and-effect feedback

## A Typical Monday Session (Day 1 of New Block)

**Date:** January 2025  
**Context:** 6 weeks out from China Open  
**Focus:** Top speed work  
**Total reps:** 6 max efforts  
**Total volume:** ~500 meters including warm-up

### Session Breakdown

**Warm-up: (~400m)**

1. **200m easy with fins** (super easy)

2. **Parachute progression** (5-6 reps of 15-20m):
   - Start with red parachute (heavy)
   - Progress through blue parachute (medium)
   - Then body weight
   - Finish with fins
   - Each rep: 70% → 80% → 90% → 95% → 100% effort

3. **Three dives:**
   - Dive 1: Super easy, dive and glide
   - Dive 2: Max 15m
   - Dive 3: Dive + 25-30m, progressive effort (every 4 strokes go up a gear)

**Main set:**

Example from this session:
- 1 x 35m all-out
- 1 x (20, 25, 30, 35, 40m) progression

**Between reps:**
- Hot tub recovery (yes, actually)
- Long rest intervals
- Tim monitoring energy, technique, readiness

**Cool-down:**
- None. Just done.

**Total time:** Just under 2 hours (9:11 AM start → 11:06 AM finish)

**ASP's observation:**

**The standard:**

**Cam's PB tracking:**

> "Cam has PBs for everything. Not just his 50m time — his 5m split, his 10m split, his 15m, his 20m, his 25m. Body weight, resisted, fins, no fins. Every combination."

### 2. **Minimal Volume, Maximum Precision**

**The contrast to traditional training:**

Most sprinters globally:
- 7-10 sessions/week
- 5-7km per session
- Stopwatch timing
- "Feel" based feedback

Cam at QAS:
- 4 sessions/week
- 1-2km per session
- Electronic timing on every rep
- Objective data-driven decisions

**ASP's commentary:**

> "This is how optimal sprint training should be. I'm not seeing this anywhere else in the world. Why is there not at least one center in each major country swimming like this?"

### 3. **Nervous System-Focused**

**The hot tub between reps:**

Not a luxury — a **strategic recovery tool** for neural fatigue.

**Cam on neural fatigue:**

> "There's huge amounts of fatigue around this type of work. A lot of neural fatigue in the development of strength. You get some DOMS, but nothing crazy. It's definitely a different type of fatigue to the general approach. Neural fatigue is very different."

> "From a metabolic, burning point of view, it's not that bad. The 50 — take the dive and underwater out — is about 17 seconds of sprinting. That's no heroic feat of endurance. Anyone in the world can do something for 17 seconds pretty hard."

> "But the neural fatigue has larger ramifications on motivation, on burnout, on mood, and just the general stability. That was a lot of what I had to learn initially because it was so different from a day-to-day point of view."

### 4. **Space to Experiment**

**What QAS provided that nowhere else would:**

- Freedom to do 6x10m twice a week for 6 months while doing heavy gym
- No pressure to "get in the water more"
- Support for unconventional programming
- Trust in the process even when it looked nothing like traditional training

**Cam:**

> "When I first came, I had a vague idea of what I wanted to do. I was looking for a space to be able to do it. There was literally nowhere in Oz that could give me the space and freedom I needed, but also have the dynamic of the setup in terms of the mental guidance, the mentorship, everything."

## The Technology Philosophy

### Track Everything, Trust the Data

**Cam's approach:**

> "We're timing electronically to the 0.01 every time we do something. We're not only getting the end result, but we're getting every 5m segment, we get the dive, we get all that type of stuff."

**Why this matters:**

**Before:**
- Cam: "I think I felt fast"
- Coach: "Looked good"
- Reality: ¯\_(ツ)_/¯

**Now:**
- 35m goal: 14.50
- Actual: 14.48
- Result: Successful session, on track

**Or:**
- 35m goal: 14.50
- Actual: 14.61
- Result: Neural fatigue accumulating, adjust volume or rest

**The confidence this builds:**

Cam:
> "I can go into a session, I know what I'm going to do — if I've got 5x35m, whatever it may be — I've got a pretty good idea of where I'm going to be to the 0.1 at least. If that's off, then something's got to change. If that's on, then things are moving sweet."

> "I can be within 0.05 of basically every split if I'm in the right zone. You've got a pretty astute understanding of where you are. That goes a long way in terms of confidence as well."

### The Race-Day Translation

**Cam behind the blocks at Paris Olympics:**

> "Based on how I feel, I've had so much exposure to knowing that this state I'm in now more than likely is going to hit this time. The confidence that brings to just relax and just let it unfold after I dive in is massive."

> "If you were to lay out along a bell curve all of my in-training races, my 50th percentile is where I want to be at for a 35m split in a race. My average output — if I feel like this, I'm going to hit this split — there's a good chance I'm up there fighting for at least a medal. That takes a lot of load off the shoulders."

## What Other Programs Can Learn

### 1. **Invest in Technology**

**Minimum viable setup:**
- Electronic timing system with 5m splits
- Video analysis capability
- Resistance training equipment (1080, parachutes, or similar)

**ROI:**
- Objective data = better decisions
- Precision tracking = faster improvement
- Athlete confidence = better performances

### 2. **Rethink Volume**

**The QAS model proves:**
- 500m sessions can produce world champions
- 4 sessions/week is enough
- Quality >> quantity for sprinting

**The key:** Every meter has purpose. Every rep is measured. No junk volume.

### 3. **Focus on the Nervous System**

**Traditional training optimizes:**
- Aerobic capacity
- Lactate tolerance
- Muscular endurance

**QAS optimizes:**
- Neural output (force production)
- Technique at race velocity
- CNS recovery and freshness

**Different physiological systems = different training methods**

### 4. **Create Space for Individuality**

**QAS didn't force Cam into a program.**

They said: "What do you need? Let's build the environment around that."

**Result:** Olympic gold, world titles, athlete thriving in his 30s.

## The Data-Driven Mindset

### What Gets Measured Gets Managed

**Cam's philosophy:**

> "You can't expect to hit 1:1 in training because you have competition environments where you step up, you go much faster than what you can hit in training. But you can still get within a reasonable amount."

**His benchmark system:**

For 10-20m segment resisted work:
- Has a goal time correlated to his 50m free goal
- Tracks every rep
- Aims to accumulate as much volume as possible under that goal time

**Example:**
- Goal 10-20m @ 2kg: 4.15
- Session 1: 4.18, 4.16, 4.17, 4.14 ✓
- Session 2: 4.12, 4.11, 4.13, 4.10 ✓✓
- Session 3: 4.19, 4.20, 4.22, 4.24 ✗ (fatigue accumulating)

**Tim's role:** Recognize session 3 means adjust volume or add rest day.
