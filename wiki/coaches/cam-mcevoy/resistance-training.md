# Cam McEvoy's Resistance Training Protocols
**How the 1080 Sprint and resistance work revolutionized his sprinting**

**Source:** Multiple interviews (2023-2024)  
**Context:** October 2022 → Paris 2024  
**Equipment:** 1080 Sprint, Destroyo Power Rack, parachutes

---

## The Starting Point: Never Done Resistance Training

**October 2022:**
- Cam had **never** done resistance swimming before
- Tim Lane asked: "Have you ever done any resistance work?"
- Answer: No

**First session:**
- 5 reps x 25m with blue/red parachute
- Went home and napped for 2 hours
- Completely neurally fried
- "I didn't know what to expect from this stuff"

**The buildup:**
- Took weeks/months to build tolerance
- Progressive overload through exposure
- Eventually built to full resistance blocks

---

## Why Resistance Training?

### The Funnel Analogy

**The concept:**
1. Build the biggest "funnel" possible (max strength in gym)
2. Open the exit hole (technique/coordination)
3. Swimming speed = flow rate through the funnel

**Why resistance specifically:**
- Can't apply max strength at bodyweight swimming initially
- Body is optimized for heavy loads after strength block
- Resistance creates the bridge between gym and pool
- Strengthens technique at specific joint angles

**McEvoy:**
> "At that point in the season, I'm so optimized for strength output that I can put a lot more into the water under resistance than I could at bodyweight. Plus, the coordination of freestyle at bodyweight will be less than under resistance."

### Force-Velocity Curve Progression

**Seasonal progression:**
1. **Gym phase:** Far left on curve (high force, low velocity)
2. **Resistance phase:** Moving right (moderate force, moderate velocity)
3. **Bodyweight phase:** Far right (low force, high velocity)

**The strategy:** Take time moving from left to right across the season.

## The 8-Week Resistance Block (Post-Fukuoka 2023)

### Structure

**Duration:** 8 weeks  
**Frequency:** Every single session on the Destroyo/1080  
**Volume:** ~5.6k equivalent sprint volume under tension

**Format:**
- Broken 50s at various resistances
- NO bodyweight swimming
- All work under load
- Timed electronically to 0.01

### The Intensity

**Neural load:**
- Massive neurological fatigue
- Learned about neural vs. metabolic fatigue
- Required strategic recovery planning
- Led to mental burnout by Doha (needed April-May mental break)

**The learning:**
> "The volume of time sprinting under tension equaled 5.6k of 50m sprints. That's a huge 8-week period of extreme neural load, strengthening of strength-endurance, and strengthening of technique."

### What It Develops

**Primary adaptations:**
1. **Strength-endurance** — ability to maintain high force output repeatedly
2. **Technique under load** — pattern locked in under resistance
3. **Neural efficiency** — more muscle fiber recruitment
4. **Joint angle strength** — specific positions reinforced

**McEvoy:**
> "You can't fake it under resistance. It forces perfect coordination."

## Resistance Equipment and Weights

### 1080 Sprint / Destroyo Power Rack

**What it is:**
- Motorized resistance system
- Straps to waist
- Provides constant resistance through stroke
- Can adjust weight precisely
- Gives 5m splits electronically

**Typical weights (in the tub):**
- **Light:** 10-15kg
- **Medium:** 20-30kg  
- **Heavy:** 30-40kg
- **Very heavy:** 40kg+

**Conversion to Destroyo Power Rack:**
- 3kg on 1080 ≈ 30-35kg in Destroyo tub
- Used for technique maintenance with fins

### Parachutes

**Colors (typical progression):**
- **Red:** Lightest
- **Blue:** Medium
- **Yellow/Green:** Heavy

**Use cases:**
- Warm-up progressions
- Travel situations (no 1080 access)
- Variety in resistance profile (different feel than belt)

## Resistance Training Protocols

### Warm-Up Progression (Standard)

**5-6 x 15-20m progression:**
1. Rep 1: Red parachute (~70% effort)
2. Rep 2: Blue parachute (~75% effort)
3. Rep 3: Medium 1080 resistance (~85% effort)
4. Rep 4: Light 1080 resistance (~90% effort)
5. Rep 5: Bodyweight (~95% effort)
6. Rep 6: Fins (~100% effort)

**Purpose:**
- Moves from heavy force/low velocity → low force/high velocity
- Primes nervous system
- Reinforces force-velocity curve adaptation
- Teaches body to maintain technique across resistance spectrum

### Main Set Examples

**Example 1: Broken 50s**
- 3-5 x (25m + 25m) @ medium-heavy resistance
- Rest: 3-5 minutes between reps
- Goal: Maintain target times across all reps
- Electronic timing on every rep

**Example 2: Build Series**
- 20m heavy
- 25m medium-heavy
- 30m medium
- 35m light
- 40m bodyweight
- Rest: 3-4 minutes between reps

**Example 3: French Contrast (Power Phase)**
- Weighted pull-ups (5 reps at 80-85% max)
- Immediately into pool
- 25m resisted sprint (medium weight)
- Rest 4-5 minutes
- Repeat 4-6 times

### Volume Guidelines

**Resistance block (heavy focus):**
- 2-3x per week
- 150-300m total resisted volume per session
- Example: 6 x 50m = 300m
- Rest intervals: 3-5 minutes

**Power transition block:**
- 2x per week
- 100-200m total resisted volume
- Combined with gym contrast work
- Focus on explosiveness

**Maintenance (race-specific phase):**
- 1x per week (optional)
- 50-100m total
- Keep feel for applying force
- Don't interfere with bodyweight adaptation

## Technique Under Resistance

### What Changes

**Bodyweight swimming:**
- Relies more on timing and rhythm
- Can "fake" technique with momentum
- Easier to hide flaws

**Resistance swimming:**
- Exposes technique flaws immediately
- Can't rely on momentum
- Must maintain perfect positions
- Forces proper force application angles

**The benefit:**
> "If you can hold perfect technique under heavy resistance, it becomes automatic at bodyweight."

### Key Technical Points Under Resistance

**1. Maintain hollow-body core activation**
- Even harder under load
- Can't let activation break
- Builds isometric endurance

**2. Hit overhead stretch position every stroke**
- Resistance forces you to find strong positions
- Weak positions collapse under load
- Reinforces optimal joint angles

**3. Press diagonally downward**
- More obvious under resistance
- Horizontal pressing doesn't work
- Teaches correct pressure angles

**4. Kick timing becomes critical**
- Mistimed kick = instant breakdown
- Resistance highlights coordination errors
- Fixes itself through practice

## When to Use Resistance Training

### Early Season (Strength → Power Transition)

**Timing:** After max strength block, 8-16 weeks before major competition

**Purpose:**
- Bridge from gym to pool
- Convert gym strength to swimming-specific strength
- Build work capacity at race-specific angles

**Volume:** High (majority of swimming is resisted)

### Mid-Season (Power Phase)

**Timing:** 6-12 weeks before major competition

**Purpose:**
- French contrast protocols
- Maintain strength while adding speed
- Progressive decrease in resistance

**Volume:** Moderate (some resisted, some bodyweight)

### Late Season (Race-Specific Phase)

**Timing:** 0-6 weeks before major competition

**Purpose:**
- Occasional exposure to maintain feel
- Mostly bodyweight swimming
- Minimal interference

**Volume:** Low to none

## The Japan Example (December 2024)

### The Setup

**Training leading into Japan Open:**
- October-December: Heavy gym block
- 1-2x per week swimming: 6 x 10m with fins + 3kg resistance
- Total weekly volume: ~120m
- No bodyweight swimming
- No suited training

**The test:**
- One week of no gym before Japan
- Slightly more technique work that week
- Suited up at Japan Open

**The result:**
- 21.70 (fastest in-season swim ever)
- 25m split = same as Olympics (21.25)
- Top speed maintained with almost zero swimming
- Died in last 25m (expected — no speed endurance work)

**The insight:**
> "Top speed (first 25m) can be kept with very little swimming. Speed endurance (last 25m) requires specific work."

## Resistance vs. Bodyweight Adaptation

### Why Not Just Bodyweight?

**McEvoy's reasoning:**

**After strength block:**
- Body optimized for heavy loads
- Neural pathways wired for high-force output
- Jumping straight to bodyweight = mismatch
- Need bridge to transfer strength

**Resistance provides:**
- Gradual transition from gym → pool
- Specificity at the joint angles that matter
- Overload that bodyweight can't provide at that point
- Technical reinforcement under load

### The Timing Challenge

**Too much resistance:**
- Neural fatigue accumulates
- Can interfere with bodyweight speed
- Motivation drops (Doha experience)
- Need recovery time

**Too little resistance:**
- Miss opportunity to strengthen technique
- Don't fully transfer gym gains
- Potentially leave performance on the table

**The balance:**
- Heavy resistance early (8-12 weeks out)
- Moderate resistance middle (6-8 weeks out)
- Light/none late (0-6 weeks out)

## Common Mistakes

### 1. Too Much Volume

**The error:** Treating resistance like aerobic training

**The fix:** Think strength training, not yardage
- Quality over quantity
- Full recovery between reps
- Stop when technique breaks down

### 2. Wrong Resistance Level

**The error:** Using weight that allows "swimming through it"

**The fix:** Resistance should:
- Slow you down significantly
- Force perfect technique
- Feel like you're pulling against a wall
- Not be sustainable for more than 25-50m

### 3. Poor Technique Under Load

**The error:** "Just get through it"

**The fix:**
- Every rep must have perfect technique
- If technique breaks, lighten the load
- Film and analyze
- No garbage reps

### 4. Not Recovering Enough

**The error:** Resistance work on back-to-back days

**The fix:**
- Minimum 48 hours between resistance sessions
- Treat like heavy squats/deadlifts
- Neural system needs recovery
- Quality sessions 2-3x/week > volume

## The Sprint-Float-Sprint Protocol

**Origin:** Track and field speed-endurance work

**Pool application:**
- Dive in, max effort 0-15m
- Easy speed ("float") 15-25m
- Max effort 25-35m
- Electronic timing on each segment

**Why it works:**
1. Teaches pacing and control
2. Develops coordination at speed when semi-fatigued
3. Often produces faster 35m times than all-out 35m
4. Builds race-specific endurance
5. Lower neural cost than max-max-max

**When to use:**
- Power transition phase
- Race-specific phase
- As progression from shorter reps
- When teaching race execution

**Resistance variation:**
- Can do first 15m resisted, rest bodyweight
- Or first 25m resisted, rest bodyweight
- Bridges resistance → bodyweight adaptation

## Periodization: When Resistance Fits

**Phase 1: Max Strength (Oct-Feb)**
- Gym: Heavy, 3-5x/week
- Swimming: Technique only, 2-3x/week, no resistance

**Phase 2: Strength → Power Transition (Feb-Mar)**
- Gym: Maintenance
- Swimming: Heavy resistance, 2-3x/week, low volume
- French contrast protocols

**Phase 3: Power → Speed Transition (Mar-Apr)**
- Gym: Maintenance
- Swimming: Moderate resistance, 2x/week, moderate volume
- Start adding bodyweight work

**Phase 4: Race-Specific (Apr-July)**
- Gym: Minimal maintenance
- Swimming: Mostly bodyweight, 4x/week, race-pace volume
- Occasional light resistance for feel

## Key Principles (Summary)

### ✅ **Resistance Bridges Gym to Pool**
- Don't go straight from heavy squats to bodyweight swimming
- Use resistance to transfer strength
- Progressive decrease across season

### ✅ **Think Strength Training, Not Yardage**
- Quality over quantity
- Full recovery between reps
- 150-300m per session is plenty

### ✅ **Perfect Technique Every Rep**
- Resistance exposes flaws
- No garbage reps
- If technique breaks, stop or lighten load

### ✅ **Periodize Strategically**
- Heavy early season
- Moderate mid-season
- Light/none late season

### ✅ **Manage Neural Fatigue**
- Resistance work is neurally demanding
- Need 48+ hours between sessions
- Monitor motivation and mood
- Take mental breaks when needed
