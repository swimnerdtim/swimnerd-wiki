# Cam McEvoy Weekly Training Breakdown

**Source:** "How to Sprint Faster: Cam's 2026 Sprint Training Blueprint" (Cam McEvoy Episode 11), pool session video analysis

## Overview

Cam McEvoy's training is radically different from traditional high-volume programs. His weekly swimming volume **never exceeds 1,500m per week** - less than most elite swimmers do in a single warm-up session. This represents a **98% reduction** compared to traditional sprint training.

## Training Philosophy

**"If it's not race pace or faster, it's not in the program."**

This principle eliminates:
- ❌ Aerobic base work
- ❌ Threshold training  
- ❌ Middle-distance sets
- ❌ Easy swimming/recovery sets
- ❌ Traditional periodization phases

## Seasonal Structure

Cam's season is divided into three distinct phases:

### Phase 1: Strength Block (Nov-Feb)

**Duration:** ~3-4 months  
**Swimming frequency:** 3 times/week  
**Swimming focus:** Submax technique maintenance only

**Pool sessions:**
- 7-8 RPE (out of 10)
- Stroke rate: ~58 (vs. 60-63 race rate)
- Total volume: 300-500m per session
- Purpose: Maintain technique while building raw strength in gym

**Gym focus:**
- Maximum strength development
- Heavy compound lifts
- Singles only (no doubles/triples)
- Goal: Increase neural output capacity

**Key insight:** Swimming is purely maintenance during this phase. The real adaptation happens in the gym.

---

### Phase 2: Power/Transition Block (Mid-Feb to March)

**Duration:** ~4 weeks  
**Swimming frequency:** 5 times/week  
**Swimming focus:** Introduce resisted sprinting + technique

**Pool sessions breakdown:**
- **3 technique sessions** (same as Phase 1)
- **2 resisted sprint sessions** (~80-100m each)
  - 4-5 × 20m sprints
  - Moderate resistance (1080 machine)
  - Full recovery between reps

**Gym focus:**
- Transition from max strength to power
- French contrast sets (weighted pull-up → resisted swim)
- Maintain strength while adding speed

**Example session:**
- Heavy pull-ups (gym)
- → Resisted 20m sprint (pool)
- → Rest ~5-10 min
- → Repeat 4-5 times

---

### Phase 3: Race-Specific Block (April-Competition)

**Duration:** 3-4 months  
**Swimming frequency:** 4-5 times/week  
**Swimming focus:** Race-pace volume accumulation

**Pool sessions:**
- Build base of "fast fitness" (not aerobic fitness)
- Every rep at race pace or faster
- Gradually increase intensity as competition approaches
- Taper with reduced volume, increased quality

**Gym focus:**
- Maintenance only
- Minimal fatigue spillover to swimming

---

## Sample Monday Pool Session (From Video)

**Total distance:** ~300-400m  
**Total time:** ~60 minutes (includes rest, coaching, video review)

### Warmup
- 300m loosen
- 2 warm-up reps:
  - 1 × 25 @ body weight (submax)
  - 1 × 20m @ 2kg resistance (1080 machine)

### Main Set
- 1 × 25 max effort dive (body weight, no resistance)
- 4 reps with resistance:
  - 2 × 20m @ 1kg
  - 2 × 20m @ 2kg
- Optional finisher (depends on feel):
  - 1 × 25 body weight max, OR
  - 2 × 20m @ 5kg resistance (if not feeling sharp)

**Total main set distance:** ~100-140m

### Metrics Tracked (Every Rep)
- 10m split
- 15m split
- 20m split
- 25m time
- Stroke rate (target: 62+ strokes/min)
- Segment times

### Recovery
- Hot tub post-session
- Mobility work
- Strategic rest (coach shut down session when Cam started feeling *good* - saved energy for rest of week)

## Volume Context

**To put 1,500m/week in perspective:**

- **Warm-up comparison:** Most elite swimmers do 1,500m+ in a single warm-up
- **Daily comparison:** Many programs do 7,000-10,000m per session (10× Cam's weekly total)
- **Reduction:** 98% less volume than traditional programs

**The McEvoy Method proves:** For sprint events (50m), quality dramatically outweighs quantity.

---

## Why This Works

**Neural adaptation over metabolic:**
- Sprint freestyle is a neuromuscular event, not aerobic
- Maximum strength → maximum power → maximum speed
- Technique quality maintained through low-volume, high-focus sessions
- Fresh nervous system = better power output

**Recovery optimization:**
- Less swimming = more time for strength development
- Minimal accumulated fatigue
- Can go max effort every session (no grinding through fatigue)

**Specificity:**
- Every meter in water is race-relevant
- No "junk volume" that doesn't transfer to 50m performance

## Coaching Insight

**From the video:** When Cam started to "feel good" on his last rep (9.50 split), coach Tim Lane immediately shut down the session. 

**Reason:** It's Monday. There's more training this week. Save the energy.

**Lesson:** In low-volume training, managing freshness is more important than "getting your meters in."
