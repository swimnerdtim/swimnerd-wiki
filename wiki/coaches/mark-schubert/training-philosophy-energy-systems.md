# Mark Schubert: Training Philosophy & Energy Systems

**Source:** *Competitive Swimming: Techniques for Champions* (1990, 1996)  
**Author:** Mark Schubert (legendary USC coach, multiple Olympic teams)  
**Context:** Comprehensive training methodology from one of swimming's greatest coaches  
**Significance:** Foundation principles for developmental swimming — aerobic base, specificity, adaptation

---

## The Big Picture

### Core Training Principles
1. **Specificity of Training** — Train the systems you'll race with
2. **Energy Systems** — Three distinct fuel tanks (ATP-CP, Lactic, Aerobic)
3. **Developmental Training** — Age-appropriate progressions (10 & under → College)
4. **Adaptation** — The stress + rest equation
5. **Training Consistency** — Long-term commitment beats short bursts

**Key Philosophy:**
> "The body responds better to frequent, stimulating stress and complementary rest than it does to a few overwhelming sessions. Once the body is in a state of considerable fatigue, going deeper into fatigue is of no benefit whatever."

---

## 1. Specificity of Training

### The Principle
**You must train the specific energy systems that your race will use.**

Sprint events (50-100) rely heavily on:
- ATP-CP system (immediate energy)
- Anaerobic glycolysis (lactic acid system)

Distance events (800-1500) rely heavily on:
- Aerobic system

Middle-distance (200-400) requires:
- **BOTH anaerobic AND aerobic systems**
- Most challenging to train properly

### Practical Application

**For Sprinters:**
- Build aerobic base first (early season)
- Transition to anaerobic work mid-season
- Emphasize pure speed work 3× per week in championship phase

**For Distance Swimmers:**
- Heavy aerobic training early season
- Maintain aerobic base throughout
- Add anaerobic work 2× per week mid-season
- Focus on pace work in championship phase

**For Middle-Distance:**
- Balance is critical
- Cannot neglect either system
- Aerobic sets with shorter rest intervals
- Anaerobic work twice weekly

## 2. Energy Systems

### The Three Fuel Tanks

#### 1. ATP-CP System (Phosphagen System)
**Duration:** 0-10 seconds  
**Power:** MAXIMUM  
**Recovery:** 2-3 minutes for full replenishment  

**What it does:**
- Immediate energy source
- No oxygen required
- Powers explosive starts, turns, finishes
- Depletes rapidly

**Training implications:**
- Pure speed work (12.5 - 25 yard sprints)
- Long rest intervals (3-5 minutes)
- Maximum effort required
- Quality over quantity

---

#### 2. Lactic Acid System (Anaerobic Glycolysis)
**Duration:** 10 seconds - 2 minutes  
**Power:** VERY HIGH  
**Byproduct:** Lactic acid accumulation (the "burn")  

**What it does:**
- Breaks down glycogen without oxygen
- Produces energy FAST but creates lactic acid
- Dominant system in 100-200 events
- Critical for all sprint/mid-distance racing

**Training implications:**
- Short rest intervals (work-to-rest ratio ~3:1)
- High-intensity repeats (50s, 75s, 100s)
- Tolerance training = teaching body to buffer lactic acid
- Painful but necessary

**Example set:** 8 × 100 on 1:30, holding 200 race pace

---

#### 3. Aerobic System (Oxidative System)
**Duration:** 2+ minutes  
**Power:** MODERATE (but sustainable)  
**Fuel:** Carbohydrates, fats (with oxygen)  

**What it does:**
- Produces energy efficiently using oxygen
- Sustainable for long periods
- Foundation for ALL swimming
- Allows recovery between hard efforts

**Training implications:**
- Long, continuous swims (1000-3000+)
- Comfortable interval training
- Base-building work
- Must be developed FIRST in every season

**The 75% Rule:**
> "Swimmers should be able to complete 75% of a set at the prescribed pace. If they cannot, the interval is too fast or they are not properly conditioned."

---

## 3. Developmental Training (Age-Appropriate)

### Ages 10 and Under

**Primary Goals:**
1. **Stroke mechanics** — Perfect technique first
2. **Fun & enjoyment** — Build love of sport
3. **Aerobic base** — Long, easy swimming
4. **Basic fitness** — Games, relays, variety

**Training characteristics:**
- Volume: 2,000-4,000 yards per session
- Frequency: 3-5 times per week
- Intensity: LOW (mostly aerobic)
- Focus: Learning proper movement patterns

**DO NOT:**
- Emphasize anaerobic training
- Push for times
- Create burnout through excessive volume
- Sacrifice technique for speed

**Schubert's warning:**
> "Early specialization and high-intensity training before physical maturity can lead to early plateaus and burnout. Build the foundation slowly."

---

### Ages 11-14 (Junior High Years)

**Transition period:** Increased physical capacity meets continued learning

**Training progression:**
- Volume increases: 4,000-6,000 yards per session
- Frequency: 5-7 times per week
- Intensity: Still mostly aerobic, introduce anaerobic work carefully
- Stroke refinement: Technique should be "locked in" by age 14

**New elements:**
- **Threshold training** — Swimming at or near lactate threshold
- **Pace work** — Learning to hold target speeds
- **Dry-land training** — Light resistance, high reps, flexibility
- **Racing strategy** — Learning to race smart

**Key principle:**
> "Biomechanics will change as the body grows. A 10-year-old butterfly is NOT the same as a 16-year-old butterfly. Limb ratios change, power changes. Adapt technique to the maturing body."

---

### Ages 14-18 (High School Years)

**The shift:** Physical maturity allows real strength and speed development

**Training characteristics:**
- Volume: 6,000-10,000 yards per session (depending on event specialty)
- Anaerobic work: 2-3 times per week
- Dry-land: Increased resistance training
- Power development: Sprint work becomes more important

**What changes:**
- **Strength training** becomes productive (hormonal maturity)
- **Anaerobic capacity** can be significantly developed
- **Speed work** yields better results
- **Aerobic base** must still be maintained

**Balance:**
- Early season: Aerobic foundation (60-70% of training)
- Mid-season: Anaerobic work increases (30-40%)
- Championship: Taper with quality speed work

---

### College Age & Beyond

**Peak performance years:** Physical tools are fully developed

**Focus shifts to:**
1. **Refinement** over learning
2. **Consistency** over novelty
3. **Recovery** becomes more important
4. **Mental preparation** separates good from great

**Training approach:**
- Volume similar to high school (or slightly higher)
- Intensity can be higher due to maturity
- Recovery must be monitored closely
- Stress management (academic, social) is critical

**Schubert's insight:**
> "The college swimmer faces academic stress and social temptations. The dedicated college swimmer is in a wonderful situation for personal development — if they can maintain training consistency."

## 4. Adaptation: The Stress + Rest Equation

### The Fundamental Principle

**Training = Stress + Rest**

- **Stress** breaks down the body
- **Rest** allows super-compensation (rebuilding stronger)
- **Imbalance** leads to overtraining OR undertraining

### Stress Sources

#### Voluntary Stress (Training)
- Pool workouts
- Dry-land training
- Competitions

#### Involuntary Stress (Life)
- **Weather:** Extreme cold or heat
- **Illness:** Even minor colds affect performance for days
- **School:** Exams, homework, academic pressure
- **Emotional:** Relationships, family issues, anxiety

**Critical insight:**
> "Stress is cumulative. You cannot isolate training stress from life stress. To maximize training adaptation, minimize unnecessary external stressors."

---

### Rest: The Key to Adaptation

**What is rest?**
1. **Sleep** — The most substantial recovery mechanism
   - Adolescents need 8-10 hours per night
   - Swimmers training hard need MORE sleep, not less
   - American society rarely allows this luxury

2. **Active recovery** — Easy swimming, stretching
3. **Complete rest** — Days off (strategic, not frequent)

**The super-compensation curve:**
```
Stress → Fatigue → Rest → Recovery → STRONGER (super-compensation)
                                   ↓
                              (next stress applied HERE)
```

**Timing matters:**
- Too little rest = chronic fatigue, poor performance, overtraining
- Too much rest = detraining, loss of adaptations
- Just right = steady improvement

---

### Work/Rest Cycles

**Weekly microcycle:**
- Hard-Easy-Hard-Easy-Hard-Moderate-Easy
- Alternate muscle groups (kick vs. pull vs. swim)
- Vary energy systems (aerobic vs. anaerobic)

**Seasonal mesocycle:**
- High volume → Moderate volume + intensity → Lower volume + high intensity
- Build → Train → Taper

**4-Year macrocycle (Elite):**
- Year 1 (post-Olympics): Low stress, recovery
- Year 2: Increase stress, World Championships
- Year 3: Moderate stress, developmental year
- Year 4: ALL-OUT effort for Olympics

**Schubert's warning:**
> "If you place too much stress on the body's systems, they will become chronically fatigued—'stale' or 'overtrained'—and will perform poorly and recover very slowly between efforts."

---

## 5. Training Consistency

### The Non-Negotiable

**Research shows:**
- Long breaks from training are detrimental to high performance
- Frequent, shorter sessions beat infrequent long sessions
- Base level of conditioning rises with successive seasons (if consistent)

**Weekly pattern:**
- Better: 5-6 moderate sessions per week
- Worse: 3 exhausting sessions per week

**Yearly pattern:**
- Better: Year-round training with strategic breaks
- Worse: On-again, off-again seasonal training

**Career pattern:**
- Better: Multi-year progression with increasing base
- Worse: Peaks and valleys, starting over each year

### Hidden Training

**The 80/20 rule:**
- 20% of your day = training in the pool
- 80% of your day = everything else

**"Hidden training" = how you live the other 80%:**
- Sleep quality and quantity
- Nutrition choices
- Stress management
- Recovery practices
- Avoiding alcohol, drugs, behavior-modifying substances

**Schubert's philosophy:**
> "Consistency demands that you do nothing during the other 80 percent of the time to undermine that positive work."

**Performance-destroying substances:**
- **Alcohol:** Long-term detriment to performance
- **Anabolic steroids:** Extremely harmful long-term effects
- **Drugs:** Behavior-modifying substances destroy consistency

## Practical Applications for Coaches

### Building a Season

**Early Season (Weeks 1-4):**
- Aerobic foundation
- Long, slow distance
- Easy interval work
- Stroke technique refinement
- Volume: Build from 60% → 100% of target

**Mid-Season (Weeks 5-12):**
- Maintain aerobic base
- Introduce anaerobic work (2× per week)
- Increase intensity, maintain volume
- Begin race-pace work
- Threshold sets become common

**Championship Season (Weeks 13-16):**
- Reduce volume 20% per week
- Maintain quality
- Emphasize race-specific work
- Taper begins 3 weeks out
- Focus on speed, starts, turns

---

### Setting Intervals (The 75% Rule)

**The Animal Night Test:**

If 75% of your swimmers cannot complete the set at the prescribed pace, the interval is WRONG.

**Adjust by:**
1. Slowing the interval (more rest)
2. Reducing distance per rep
3. Breaking set into smaller chunks
4. Checking if swimmers are properly conditioned

**Example:**
- Set: 10 × 100 on 1:20
- If only 3 of 10 swimmers can complete it → interval too fast
- Better: 10 × 100 on 1:30 (achievable by 8+ swimmers)

---

### Monitoring Adaptation

**Signs of positive adaptation:**
- Morning heart rate stays low
- Times improve week-over-week
- Swimmers recover quickly between efforts
- Energy levels are good
- Enthusiasm for training

**Signs of overtraining:**
- Elevated resting heart rate
- Times plateau or decline
- Sluggish recovery
- Chronic fatigue
- Loss of motivation, irritability
- Increased illness

**Schubert's remedy:**
- Reduce volume immediately
- Increase rest
- Review external stressors (school, life)
- Possibly add recovery week

## Mental Framework

### The Long Game

**Key mindset shifts:**
1. **Process over results** — Trust the training cycle
2. **Patience** — Development takes years, not weeks
3. **Consistency** — Small daily wins compound
4. **Adaptation** — Stress + rest = growth
5. **Individuality** — Each swimmer adapts differently

**Schubert's wisdom:**
> "No one workout, week, month, or even year will make or break you. You must have a training plan that takes into account specificity, developmental training, and adaptation, and you must carry it out in a thorough and consistent manner."

---

## Key Takeaways for Coaches

1. **Train the right systems** — Sprinters need speed work, distance swimmers need aerobic base, middle-distance needs BOTH
2. **Respect developmental stages** — Don't rush young swimmers into high-intensity work
3. **Stress + Rest = Adaptation** — The equation must balance
4. **Consistency wins** — Frequent moderate sessions beat sporadic brutal ones
5. **Monitor external stress** — School, illness, emotions all affect training
6. **Sleep is non-negotiable** — 8-10 hours for adolescent swimmers
7. **Use the 75% rule** — If most swimmers can't complete the set, you've miscalculated
8. **Build aerobic base first** — EVERY season starts with foundation work
9. **Taper is part of training** — Reduce volume 20% per week for 3 weeks pre-championship
10. **"Hidden training" matters** — What swimmers do outside the pool affects performance
