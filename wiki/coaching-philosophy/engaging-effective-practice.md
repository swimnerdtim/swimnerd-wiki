# Building Engaging and Effective Practice

**Source:** *Every Movement Matters* by John O'Sullivan  
**Core Principle:** "Great on paper, s#!% on grass" — Practice design must create transfer, not just repetition  
**Application:** How to design swim practices that develop skill, engagement, and love of the sport

---

## The Central Problem

### The Magic Practice Myth
**O'Sullivan's realization after 30 years of coaching:**

> "I spent my coaching career looking for the magic practice session. I filled binders with Xs and Os. But there was no magic session. The sport-specific knowledge was critical, but NOT SUFFICIENT. The human side came first."

**The truth:**
- There's no "secret workout" that creates champions
- Technique knowledge matters, but it's only 15-20% of what athletes need
- **Coaches are architects of engaging learning environments**, not just transmitters of information

---

## What Athletes Actually Want from Coaches

### Research: Top 5 Qualities Kids Want (Ages 8-18)
**Amanda Visek, George Washington University:**

1. **Respect and encouragement** — 25%
2. **Positive role model** — 20%
3. **Clear, consistent communication** — 20%
4. **Knowledge of the sport** — 15%
5. **Good listener** — 20%

**The gap:** 6.5 million youth coaches in the US—fewer than 1 in 5 get training in motivation and communication (the other 80% of what kids want).

**Result:** 26% dropout rate for athletes coached by untrained volunteers vs. 5% for trained coaches.

## The "Architects of Learning" Mindset

### Old Model: Transmitter of Information
❌ Coach knows → Coach tells → Athlete executes → Repeat

**Problems:**
- Passive learning (athletes don't think)
- No transfer to competition (can't adapt)
- Boring (athletes check out mentally)

### New Model: Architect of Environment
✅ Coach designs → Athlete explores → Athlete discovers → Coach guides

**Benefits:**
- Active learning (athletes solve problems)
- Transfer to racing (decision-making under pressure)
- Engaging (athletes are invested)

## Principles of Effective Practice Design

### 1. Start Small, Add Gradually
**O'Sullivan's advice:**

> "Imagine if I said, 'Everything you're doing is wrong.' You'd say 'Get out!' But if I said, 'Replace 10 minutes of this with 10 minutes of that,' you'd try it."

**Application for swim coaches:**
- Don't overhaul your entire program at once
- Pick ONE principle from this page
- Try it for 2-4 weeks
- If it works, add another

**Example:**
- Week 1-2: Add music to dryland (energy/joy)
- Week 3-4: Add competitive element to one main set per week
- Week 5-6: Ask athletes "What did you feel?" instead of just telling them what they did wrong

### 2. Create "Safe-to-Fail" Opportunities
**The problem with fear-based coaching:**
- Athletes play it safe
- No experimentation
- No growth

**The solution:**
- Designate some sets as "experiment time"
- "Today, try a different pacing strategy—no consequences if it flops"
- "This is a learning set, not a test set"

**Example: Race Strategy Practice**
- Time trial: 200 IM with 3 different pacing strategies
  - Strategy A: Blast the fly, survive the back/breast
  - Strategy B: Controlled fly, attack the back
  - Strategy C: Even split all 4 strokes
- Debrief: "What did you learn? Which felt best? Why?"
- **No judgment—just discovery**

### 3. Build Engagement Through Variety
**The 3 Killers of Engagement:**
1. **Monotony** — Same sets every week (50 × 100 on 1:20, every Tuesday)
2. **Boredom** — Long, slow, mindless yardage
3. **Disconnection** — No input from athletes, no autonomy

**The Antidotes:**

| Engagement Killer | Solution | Example |
|-------------------|----------|---------|
| **Monotony** | Surprise elements | "Today we're doing something totally different" |
| **Boredom** | Competitive games | Relay races, stroke count challenges, team battles |
| **Disconnection** | Athlete input | "You design the next round—here are the parameters" |

### 4. Make Practice Feel Like Competition
**Pete Carroll's Seahawks principle:** Every rep is a competition

**Swimming applications:**
- **Relay format everything** — Even warm-up can be "first lane to finish wins"
- **Leaderboards** — Track weekly totals (distance, stroke counts, times)
- **Team vs. Team** — Split practice group in half, battle it out
- **Individual challenges** — "Beat your time from 2 weeks ago"

**Why it works:**
- Mimics meet environment (adrenaline, focus)
- Develops competitive instinct
- More engaging than just "grinding yards"

### 5. The 3:1 Positive-to-Corrective Ratio
**Research shows top coaches give 3 positive comments for every 1 corrective.**

**Why:**
- Athletes hear criticism 5x louder than praise
- Constant correction = fear, shutdown, disengagement
- Positive feedback = confidence, openness to coaching

**How to implement:**
- **Catch them doing it RIGHT** — "Great breakout speed on that one!"
- **Praise effort/process, not just results** — "Love the focus on your turns today"
- **Use "yes, and" not "no, but"** — "Yes, your tempo is good, AND let's add more power on the catch"

**Important:** This doesn't mean no correction—just balanced feedback.

## Practical Practice Design Strategies

### Strategy 1: The "Why Before What"
**Before every main set, explain the PURPOSE.**

❌ **Old way:**
> "Alright, 20 × 100 on 1:20. Go."

✅ **New way:**
> "Alright, 20 × 100 on 1:20. The purpose is to train your ability to hold speed under fatigue—race pace for the middle 10. This mimics what you'll feel in the 3rd and 4th 100 of your 500. Let's go."

**Why it works:**
- Athletes understand the "why" (not just following orders)
- They can self-regulate better ("Is this race pace? Should I adjust?")
- Builds buy-in and ownership

### Strategy 2: The "Discovery Set"
**Instead of telling, let them discover.**

**Example: Teaching breakout timing**

❌ **Old way:**
> "You're breaking out too early. Break out at the flags."

✅ **New way:**
> "Do 4 × 50 fly. First one: break out at 10m. Second: 12m. Third: 15m. Fourth: 18m. Which one felt fastest? Which one gave you the best momentum into your stroke?"

**Why it works:**
- Athletes FEEL the difference (proprioception)
- They own the discovery ("I found this!")
- More likely to apply it in races (internal vs. external cue)

### Strategy 3: The "Rotate the Challenge"
**Don't just train the same energy system every day.**

**Weekly rotation example:**

| Day | Focus | Set Example |
|-----|-------|-------------|
| **Monday** | Speed/Power | 20 × 25 on :40 — dive 15m FAST, glide to wall |
| **Tuesday** | Threshold | 5 × 400 on 5:00 — hold 200 race pace |
| **Wednesday** | Lactate Tolerance | 8 × 100 on 3:00 — descend 1-4, 5-8 (long rest) |
| **Thursday** | Technique/Efficiency | Stroke count challenge — 12 × 50, can't exceed X strokes |
| **Friday** | Competition Prep | Time trial or relay race day |

**Why it works:**
- Variety = engagement
- Targets different physiological adaptations
- Athletes stay mentally fresh

### Strategy 4: The "Athlete Input" Set
**Once per week, let athletes design part of practice.**

**How it works:**
1. Give parameters: "8-10 × 100, rest interval 1:15-1:30, focus on IM"
2. Athletes decide: Stroke order? Descending? All-out? Negative split?
3. Debrief: "Why did you choose that? How did it feel?"

**Why it works:**
- Ownership → motivation
- Teaches race strategy and self-awareness
- Reveals how athletes think (coaches learn too!)

### Strategy 5: The "Music + Energy" Boost
**Use music strategically to shift energy.**

**When to use music:**
- Dryland (always — pump-up energy)
- Warm-up (upbeat, get them moving)
- Easy recovery sets (chill vibes, mental reset)
- Sprint sets (hype music between reps)

**When NOT to use music:**
- Technical instruction (they need to hear you)
- Race simulation (meets don't have music)

**Pro tip:** Let athletes curate a team playlist (rotates weekly). Builds connection + ownership.

## The "Win the Day" Mindset

### Don't Show Up to Win. Show Up to Compete.
**The difference:**

| Mindset | Focus | Result |
|---------|-------|--------|
| **"Show up to win"** | Outcome (times, places) | Fear of failure, playing it safe |
| **"Show up to compete"** | Process (effort, execution) | Freedom to attack, resilience |

**How to train this:**
- Redefine "winning" in practice
  - ✅ "Did you execute your race plan?"
  - ✅ "Did you give max effort?"
  - ✅ "Did you support your teammates?"
  - ❌ "Did you get the fastest time?"

**Example: Time Trial Day**
- Before: "Your goal is to win."
- **Better:** "Your goal is to execute your plan with total focus and courage. The time will follow."

**Why it works:**
- Shifts focus to controllables (effort, strategy)
- Reduces anxiety (not obsessing over outcome)
- Builds resilience (can "win" even with a bad time if process was solid)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Too Much Volume, Not Enough Purpose
**The trap:** "More yardage = better"

**The fix:** Quality > quantity
- Every set should have a clear purpose
- If you can't explain why you're doing it, cut it

**Example:**
- ❌ 5000m straight swim for time (what's the point?)
- ✅ 10 × 400 with specific paces and rest intervals (training threshold)

### Mistake 2: Treating Practice Like Punishment
**The trap:** "You didn't perform, so we're doing extra sets"

**The fix:** Practice is a privilege, not punishment
- If athletes associate training with punishment, they'll burn out
- Consequences for poor effort should be different (conversation, not extra laps)

### Mistake 3: No Athlete Voice
**The trap:** "I'm the coach. I decide everything."

**The fix:** Co-create culture and strategy
- Ask: "What did you feel on that set?"
- Ask: "What do you need from me this week?"
- Ask: "How should we approach this meet?"

**Why it works:**
- Athletes who have voice = athletes who have ownership
- Ownership = motivation = performance

### Mistake 4: Ignoring the Social/Connection Side
**The trap:** "We're here to train, not socialize"

**The fix:** Connection IS training
- High-trust lanes train harder
- Friendships = retention (kids stay in sport)
- Build connection through team activities, partner sets, relay challenges

## Measuring Effective Practice

### Ask These 3 Questions After Every Practice:

1. **Did athletes leave better than they arrived?**
   - Physically? (Yes—they trained)
   - Mentally? (Did they learn something?)
   - Emotionally? (Do they feel valued and motivated?)

2. **Would athletes describe today as engaging?**
   - Were they mentally present? Or checking out?
   - Did they have moments of fun/joy?
   - Did they feel challenged (not crushed)?

3. **Can I explain the "why" of every set?**
   - If a swimmer asks "Why are we doing this?", can I answer in 1-2 sentences?
   - Does the set align with our season goals?

## Key Takeaways

✅ **No magic practice exists**—focus on the human side first  
✅ **Coaches are architects**, not just instructors  
✅ **Athletes want respect, communication, and role models** (80% of what matters)  
✅ **Start small, add gradually**—don't overhaul everything at once  
✅ **Create safe-to-fail opportunities**—learning > perfection  
✅ **Variety + competition = engagement**  
✅ **3:1 positive-to-corrective ratio**—catch them doing it right  
✅ **Explain the "why"**—athletes who understand train smarter  
✅ **Let athletes discover**—internal learning > external instruction  
✅ **"Show up to compete," not "show up to win"**—process > outcome
