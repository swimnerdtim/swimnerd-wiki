# Athlete Archetypes & How to Coach Them

**16 personality types every swim coach will encounter — and how to adapt your communication for each one.**

---

## Why Archetypes Matter

**The problem:** Coaches often use a one-size-fits-all communication style.

**The reality:** Different athletes need different approaches.

**Your job:** Identify each swimmer's archetype and adapt your coaching accordingly.

> "Successful training is more than just the sum of various physical drills and exercises. Success depends upon the interactions of the physical, psychological, emotional, and social components of a training program."  
> — Brett Bartholomew, *Conscious Coaching*

---

## The 16 Archetypes

### 1. The Technician

**Who they are:**
- Wants detailed explanations (science, biomechanics, "why")
- Loves data, metrics, split times
- Needs to understand the mechanism

 behind everything
- Often asks "Why are we doing this?"

**How to coach them:**
- Provide rationale and cite research
- Explain biomechanics ("High elbow increases propulsion by...")
- Show them their splits, stroke counts, data
- Give them autonomy to experiment within boundaries

**Swimming example:**
> **Bad:** "Do 10×100 butterfly. Go."  
> **Good:** "We're doing 10×100 fly to build lactate tolerance. Research shows repeated efforts at threshold pace improve your ability to clear lactate during races. Your target is 1:05-1:08 — that's your 200 fly race pace. We'll track your stroke count to ensure you maintain efficiency even when fatigued."

**Red flag:** If you can't explain WHY, they won't buy in.

---

### 2. The Leader

**Who they are:**
- Self-motivated, leads by example
- Wants autonomy and ownership
- Respected by teammates
- Doesn't need hand-holding

**How to coach them:**
- Give them responsibility (lead warm-up, mentor younger swimmers)
- Ask for input on training
- Trust them to execute without micromanaging
- Let them set the tone for the team

**Swimming example:**
> "I trust you to set the pace today. Show the team what championship effort looks like. If anyone's slacking, hold them accountable."

**Red flag:** Over-coaching a Leader will backfire. They need space.

---

### 3. The Skeptic

**Who they are:**
- Questions everything
- Needs proof before buying in
- "I'll believe it when I see it"
- May have been burned by bad coaching before

**How to coach them:**
- Show data and evidence
- Let them test and compare results
- Earn trust through consistency
- Don't take their skepticism personally

**Swimming example:**
> "I know you're skeptical about breathing every 3 strokes. Let's time you breathing every 2 vs. every 3 in practice and compare. We'll also look at video to see if your body position changes. You decide which works better."

**Red flag:** Don't get defensive. Use their skepticism to refine your coaching.

---

### 4. The Novice

**Who they are:**
- New to competitive swimming (or your program)
- Needs encouragement and simple instructions
- Overwhelmed by complexity
- Lacks confidence but eager to learn

**How to coach them:**
- Break down steps into small wins
- Celebrate progress ("Great job today!")
- Avoid overwhelming with technical detail
- Build confidence first, refine later

**Swimming example:**
> **Bad:** "Your catch is late, elbow drops, and you're crossing over midline. Fix it."  
> **Good:** "Great effort today! Your streamline looked way better. Next week, let's work on your hand entry. One thing at a time."

**Red flag:** Too much too soon = overwhelmed Novice = quitting.

---

### 5. The Royal

**Who they are:**
- High achiever, often from privileged background
- Expects special treatment
- May be talented but entitled
- Responds well to challenge (not coddling)

**How to coach them:**
- Set clear boundaries and expectations
- Don't give special treatment
- Challenge them (they respect toughness)
- Praise effort, not status

**Swimming example:**
> "I don't care if you were state champion at your old club. Here, everyone earns their spot. Show me in the water."

**Red flag:** Coddling a Royal reinforces entitlement. Hold them accountable.

---

### 6. The Soldier

**Who they are:**
- Follows orders without question
- Disciplined, reliable, shows up every day
- Doesn't ask "why," just executes
- May lack creativity or self-direction

**How to coach them:**
- Give clear, structured instructions
- Reward consistency and discipline
- Encourage them to think for themselves (gradually)
- Don't exploit their compliance

**Swimming example:**
> "You've been crushing it all season — super consistent. I want you to start thinking about YOUR race strategy. What feels best for you in the 200 free? Let's experiment this week."

**Red flag:** Soldiers can burn out if overworked. They won't complain until it's too late.

---

### 7. The Specialist

**Who they are:**
- Hyper-focused on ONE event or stroke
- Resists training other strokes
- "I'm a sprinter, why do I need to swim distance?"
- Narrow mindset

**How to coach them:**
- Explain how cross-training benefits their specialty
- Use their event as the "carrot"
- Show examples of specialists who train broadly
- Make it relevant to THEIR goal

**Swimming example:**
> "You want to drop time in your 50 free, right? This 400 IM set builds your aerobic base, which improves recovery between sprints. Nathan Adrian swam IM in practice — he knew it made him faster in the 50."

**Red flag:** Don't force variety without explaining the benefit.

---

### 8. The Politician

**Who they are:**
- Charming, socially intelligent
- May manipulate to avoid hard work
- Knows how to play the game
- Can divide a team if unchecked

**How to coach them:**
- See through the charm — hold them accountable
- Don't let them off the hook
- Redirect their social skills positively (team captain, recruiter)
- Private conversation > public call-out

**Swimming example:**
> (Private conversation) "You're great with people. I need that energy focused on lifting the team up, not cutting corners. If you put that effort into training, you'll be unstoppable."

**Red flag:** Politicians are master manipulators. Stay firm.

---

### 9. The Self-Sabotager

**Who they are:**
- Talented but self-destructive
- Misses practice, shows up late, skips meets
- May have personal issues (family, mental health, etc.)
- Needs support, not punishment

**How to coach them:**
- Ask questions: "What's going on? How can I help?"
- Show empathy without lowering standards
- Connect them with resources (counselor, sports psych)
- Be patient but set boundaries

**Swimming example:**
> "I've noticed you've missed three practices this week. I'm not mad — I'm concerned. Talk to me. What's going on? How can we adjust to help you stay on track?"

**Red flag:** Punishment alone won't fix the root cause. Dig deeper.

---

### 10. The Mouthpiece

**Who they are:**
- Loud, opinionated, vocal
- May challenge you publicly
- Can be disruptive OR a great hype person
- Craves attention

**How to coach them:**
- Channel their energy positively (hype team, lead cheers)
- Address disruption privately
- Give them a platform (if earned)
- Don't let them undermine you

**Swimming example:**
> (Private) "I love your energy, but when you talk during my instructions, it distracts the team. Help me out — if you have feedback, pull me aside after. Deal?"

**Red flag:** Public power struggle = you lose. Handle privately.

---

### 11. The Wolverine

**Who they are:**
- Tough, gritty, never quits
- Thrives on challenge
- May push TOO hard (injury risk)
- Respects toughness in coaches

**How to coach them:**
- Match their intensity
- Challenge them (they love it)
- Monitor for overtraining
- Teach them smart toughness vs. reckless toughness

**Swimming example:**
> "I love your fight. That's championship mentality. But swimming hurt is different from swimming injured. Let's get that shoulder checked before it sidelines you for the season."

**Red flag:** Wolverines will train through injury. Protect them from themselves.

---

### 12. The Free Spirit

**Who they are:**
- Creative, spontaneous, hates routine
- May struggle with structure
- Talented but inconsistent
- Needs variety to stay engaged

**How to coach them:**
- Build in variety (different sets, drills, challenges)
- Give autonomy within structure
- Make training fun
- Don't over-regulate

**Swimming example:**
> "Today we're doing a 'Choose Your Own Adventure' set. You need to hit 2000 yards of threshold work. You pick the format — 10×200, 5×400, or a broken 2000. Your call."

**Red flag:** Too much rigidity = bored Free Spirit = checked out.

---

### 13. The Manipulator

**Who they are:**
- Uses others to avoid work
- May fake injury, exaggerate symptoms
- Divides the team
- Toxic if unchecked

**How to coach them:**
- Call them out (privately)
- Set clear consequences
- Don't tolerate manipulation
- Remove from team if necessary

**Swimming example:**
> (Private) "I've noticed you 'hurt' your shoulder every time we do fly. I care about your health, but I also see you doing butterfly in warm-up. What's really going on?"

**Red flag:** Manipulators are poison. Act quickly.

---

### 14. The Underdog

**Who they are:**
- Overlooked, underestimated
- Chip on their shoulder
- Hungry to prove themselves
- Thrives on being doubted

**How to coach them:**
- Fuel their fire ("Show them what you're made of")
- Give them opportunities to shine
- Celebrate their progress publicly
- Don't overlook them

**Swimming example:**
> "Nobody expects you to make finals. Use that. Prove them wrong. I believe in you."

**Red flag:** Underdogs need belief. Don't ignore them.

---

### 15. The Crusader

**Who they are:**
- Mission-driven, passionate about a cause
- Wants to make a difference
- May prioritize values over performance
- Needs purpose beyond winning

**How to coach them:**
- Connect training to a bigger mission
- Use their passion to inspire the team
- Give them leadership roles
- Respect their values

**Swimming example:**
> "You want to inspire younger swimmers, right? Show them what's possible. Your effort today sets the standard for the next generation."

**Red flag:** Crusaders need meaning. "Just win" won't motivate them.

---

### 16. The Hypochondriac

**Who they are:**
- Always hurt, always complaining
- May exaggerate symptoms
- Needs validation
- Genuinely anxious about injury (or avoiding work)

**How to coach them:**
- Take complaints seriously (but verify)
- Work with medical staff to rule out real issues
- Address anxiety if that's the root cause
- Set boundaries on complaining

**Swimming example:**
> "I hear you. Let's have the trainer check your shoulder today. If it's clear, we'll adjust your training. But if you're healthy, I need you all-in. Deal?"

**Red flag:** Hypochondriacs can drain your energy. Set boundaries.

## How to Use This in Practice

### Step 1: Identify the Archetype

**Observe your swimmers:**
- How do they respond to feedback?
- Do they ask "why" or just execute?
- Are they vocal or quiet?
- Do they lead or follow?

**Most swimmers are a MIX of 2-3 archetypes.** Identify the dominant traits.

---

### Step 2: Adapt Your Communication

**Example: 200 Fly Set**

**For the Technician:**
> "We're doing 8×50 fly on :50 to build lactate tolerance. Target: 28-30 seconds. Research shows repeated high-intensity efforts improve your buffering capacity."

**For the Leader:**
> "You set the pace. Show the team what championship fly looks like."

**For the Skeptic:**
> "I know you think you're a freestyle specialist. Let's time your 200 IM before and after 4 weeks of this set and see if your fly improves."

**For the Novice:**
> "Great job! You finished all 8. Next week, let's work on your tempo."

**For the Wolverine:**
> "I dare you to hold 27s. Let's see what you're made of."

**For the Free Spirit:**
> "You can do this set as 8×50 or 4×100. Your call."

---

### Step 3: Track What Works

**Keep notes:**
- Which swimmers respond to data?
- Who needs encouragement vs. challenge?
- Who resists authority vs. thrives on structure?

**Adjust over time.** Archetypes can shift (especially Novices → Leaders).

---

## Key Principles

✅ **No archetype is "bad"** — they're just different  
✅ **Adapt your style, don't force swimmers to adapt to you**  
✅ **Most swimmers are a mix of 2-3 archetypes**  
✅ **Archetypes can change over time** (novice → leader, skeptic → believer)  
✅ **Understanding archetypes = better buy-in = better performance**  

> "Conscious Coaches don't coach TO athletes — they coach WITH athletes."  
> — Brett Bartholomew
