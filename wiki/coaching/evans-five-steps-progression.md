# Janet Evans: Five Steps to Swimming Success

**Source:** *Janet Evans' Total Swimming* (2007)  
**Context:** Olympic champion's framework for building swim fitness from beginner to elite  
**Audience:** Coaches working with swimmers at all levels

---

## Core Philosophy: Swimming is Unnatural

> "Swimming is an unnatural activity in the sense that people do very little, if anything, to replicate it outside of the pool."

**Key insight:** Unlike walking/running (daily activities), swimming movements NEVER happen outside the pool. This creates:
- **Steep learning curve** for new swimmers
- **Significant improvement potential** with regular practice
- **Need for systematic progression** through skill stages

---

## The Five Steps (In Order)

### Step 1: Learn to Breathe ← **MOST IMPORTANT**

**Foundation:** You can't relax if you can't breathe.

**Skills to master:**
- Blowing bubbles underwater
- Floating on back
- Rhythmic breathing patterns

**Why it matters:** Breathing is the gateway to everything else. Tension from poor breathing ruins stroke mechanics, body position, and endurance.

**Evans' drill:** Start with simple bubble-blowing exercises (Chapter 2). Practice until breathing feels natural, not panicked.

---

### Step 2: Get Comfortable in the Water

**Closely related to Step 1 — hard to relax without breathing control.**

**Focus:**
- Float on back → ease tension
- Feel weightlessness
- Eliminate fear/anxiety in water

**Progression marker:** When you can float/tread water without tension, move to Step 3.

---

### Step 3: Core Muscle Engagement + Stroke Technique

**The technical foundation.**

**Core muscles (abdominals, hips, lower back):**
- Must be **consciously engaged** early in this stage
- Keep body in streamline position
- Reduce drag, increase efficiency

**Stroke mechanics:**
> "Having excellent form in each stroke isn't a prerequisite for getting exercise in the pool; even people who flail from one end of the pool to the other can get a fine workout. But they won't be able to improve much because of the vast amount of energy it takes them merely to move through the water."

**Key principle:** Efficiency unlocks improvement. Poor technique = energy waste = plateau.

**What "honing stroke technique" means:**
- Learn gross movements first (arm path, kick timing)
- Progress to fine details (hand angle, rotation degree)
- Use drills to reinforce motor patterns

---

### Step 4: Build Fitness Base in the Water

**Fitness ≠ land fitness. Swimmers must appreciate that.**

**Starting protocol (newer/returning swimmers):**
1. Swim 1-4 laps at a time
2. Rest after each group
3. Shoot for 10-20 laps total in first couple workouts
4. **Don't make it so negative you don't want to return**
5. Add 1-4 laps each successive workout
6. Gradually decrease rest
7. Build to 30-40 laps with minimal rest

**Progression rate:** As much as you can handle without dreading the next session.

**Evans' wisdom:** Stop before you hate it. Come back for more.

---

### Step 5: Turns + Intensity + Distance

**The performance phase.**

**Components:**
- **Turns:** Flip turns for freestyle/backstroke, open turns for breast/fly
- **Increased intensity:** Interval training, pace work, effort zones
- **Greater total distance:** 2,000+ yard workouts

**Why this is last:**
> "Swimming dozens of straight laps is very good exercise, and fitness will improve up to a point. But eventually progress will plateau because the body's muscular and cardiovascular systems are no longer being taxed in ways different enough to produce marked improvement."

**The adaptation principle:**
- Body adapts to repeated stimuli
- What once challenged you becomes routine
- Must vary intensity/distance/intervals to keep progressing

**Result:** Without progression through intensity/distance, you hit a ceiling. The body needs NEW challenges to adapt.

---

## Ongoing Process, Not Checklist

**Critical mindset shift:**

> "You don't check off step 1 after you can breathe in the water and then rush through the other four steps to magically become an elite swimmer."

**What ongoing progression looks like:**

| **Step** | **Beginner focus** | **Advanced focus** |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 1. Breathing | Learn to blow bubbles | Improve lung capacity |
| 2. Comfort | Get relaxed in water | Develop "feel for the water" with hands/forearms |
| 3. Technique | Hone gross movements (arms, legs, core) | Fine-tune minute details (angle changes, rotation precision) |
| 4. Fitness | Build base endurance | Sustain higher intensities longer |
| 5. Intensity | Learn intervals | Race-pace training, taper strategies |

**The spiral:** You revisit each step at higher levels as you improve.

---

## Goal Setting Within the Framework

**Make goals specific, challenging, realistic.**

**Process-oriented goals (stepping stones):**
- Improve butterfly kick
- Learn flip turn
- Swim 3x/week for a month
- Hold :30/25 for 10 × 100

**Outcome-oriented goals (end results):**
- Lower 500 free time by 10 seconds
- Win age-group race
- Keep up in masters fast lane
- Lose 2 inches off waist

**Evans' approach:** Short-term process goals → long-term outcome goals.

**Example progression:**
1. Work on technique (process)
2. Swim certain # of laps per workout (process)
3. Introduce intervals (process)
4. Lower intervals to goal time (outcome)

**Result:** Series of smaller goals = systematic path to larger ones.

## Charting Progress: The Motivational Tool

**Evans' mandate:** Keep a log book.

> "There is no better way to witness the progress being made than by writing down every aspect of each of your workouts."

**Why it works:**
- Daily progress is hard to see (can't see forest for trees)
- Flipping back through weeks/months = visible improvement
- Discouragement antidote when times/distances aren't dropping

**What to log (minimum):**
- Every swim workout (sets, distances, intervals)
- Dryland workout (type, distance, time, weight amounts)
- Time spent stretching
- Core work (# and type of exercises)

**Optional details (for serious swimmers):**
- How you felt during workout
- Times for each interval
- Heart rate data
- Pattern of good/bad weeks
- What worked/didn't work

**Evans' log books:** Reached "urban legend status" — coaches passed them around to inspire (and terrify) their teams with her training volume and pace.

## Injury Prevention Through Progression

**Most important safeguards:**
1. **Warm up** (10-20% of workout)
2. **Cool down** (10% of workout)
3. **Stretch** before getting in
4. **Progress gradually** — don't jump intensity/distance too fast

**When pain appears:**
- **Stop immediately** — not at end of length/lap/set, RIGHT AWAY
- Float on back, kick to end, get out
- Stretch shoulders 2-3x/day for 15-20 min
- Ice
- Rest 1-2 days
- Return with easy warm-up only
- **Don't jump back into intense sets** — re-strain risk high

**Evans' rule:** `trash` > `rm` — rest a day or two beats missing weeks.

## Application for Coaches

**New swimmer assessment:** Identify which step they're on.

**Returning swimmer (long break):** Often need to restart at Step 4 (fitness base), even if technique is decent.

**Plateaued swimmer:** Likely stuck at Step 5 — need variety in intensity/distance/intervals.

**Training plan design:** Build workouts that progress through steps systematically.

**Patience reminder:** Steps 1-3 can take months. Don't rush to Step 5 (intensity) before foundation is solid.

**Log book culture:** Encourage (or require) swimmers to track workouts. Make it part of team culture.

---

## Key Takeaway

**Swimming improvement is a five-step process:**
1. Breathe
2. Get comfortable
3. Engage core + hone technique
4. Build fitness base
5. Add turns + intensity + distance

**Not a checklist — a spiral.** You revisit each step at higher levels throughout your career.

**Secret sauce:** Systematic progression + consistent logging = visible improvement over weeks/months.

**Evans' proof:** This framework took her from age-group swimmer → Olympic champion with 4 golds, 1 silver, multiple world records, 10+ year injury-free career.
