# External Focus Cueing — The Science of Better Feedback
**Source:** *The Language of Coaching* by Nick Winkelman (2020)  
**Application:** Motor Learning Research → Swimming Technique Instruction

---

## The Breakthrough Discovery

**1998:** Dr. Gabriele Wulf made a pivotal discovery while windsurfing on Lake Garda, Italy. She noticed her performance improved dramatically when she **stopped focusing on her body** and **started focusing on the board's motion**.

This insight led to 20+ years of research and **160+ studies** confirming: **External focus cues produce superior motor learning compared to internal focus cues.**

---

## Internal vs. External Focus: The Critical Distinction

### Internal Focus Cues
**Definition:** Direct attention to body parts, muscles, or joints.

**Examples in swimming:**
- ❌ "Bend your elbow 90 degrees"
- ❌ "Engage your core muscles"
- ❌ "Flex your ankle"
- ❌ "Rotate your hips"

**Why they fail:**  
The brain can only process **one thing at a time**. Focusing on individual body parts **overwhelms working memory** and prevents fluid movement.

> "The more our cues zoom into the technical error, the harder it is to change."  
> — Nick Winkelman (The Zoom Fallacy)

### External Focus Cues
**Definition:** Direct attention to the **effect of movement** on the environment or outcome.

**Examples in swimming:**
- ✅ "Grab the water and pull it back"
- ✅ "Create a bow wave with your forehead"
- ✅ "Shoot your hands to the wall"
- ✅ "Make your body a torpedo"

**Why they work:**  
External cues let the nervous system **self-organize**. The brain automatically coordinates muscles/joints when focused on the **desired outcome**, not the mechanics.

## The Continuum of Cues (Most → Least Effective)

### 5. Far External ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
**Focus:** Distant goal or target

**Swimming examples:**
- "Drive toward the wall like a missile"
- "Race your shadow on the bottom"
- "Chase down the T on the bottom"
- "Sprint through the flags, not to them"

**When to use:** Racing, full-speed efforts, technique under fatigue

---

### 4. Close External ⭐⭐⭐⭐
**Focus:** Immediate environment (water, implements, surface)

**Swimming examples:**
- "Press the water down and back"
- "Slice through the surface like a knife"
- "Anchor your hand and pull your body past it"
- "Snap the kickboard down"

**When to use:** Technique development, drills, moderate pace

---

### 3. Hybrid ⭐⭐⭐
**Focus:** Body interaction WITH environment

**Swimming examples:**
- "Drive your hand to the wall"
- "Push your feet through the water"
- "Point your fingertips to the bottom"

**When to use:** Transition from learning to performance; bridges internal/external

---

### 2. Broad Internal ⭐⭐
**Focus:** Entire limb or body region

**Swimming examples:**
- "Extend your arm fully"
- "Keep your body straight"
- "Drive your legs back"

**When to use:** RARELY. Only when position/power deficits limit movement (see 3P Profile below)

---

### 1. Narrow Internal ⭐
**Focus:** Single muscle or joint

**Swimming examples:**
- "Squeeze your glutes"
- "Flex your hip"
- "Contract your lat"

**When to use:** ALMOST NEVER in swimming. Maybe rehab/activation exercises.

## The 3P Performance Profile: When Internal Focus Might Help

**Framework:** Position → Power → Pattern

Winkelman's diagnostic model explains why **two swimmers with identical technique may need different coaching approaches**.

### Position (Mobility + Stability)
**Question:** Can the athlete **get into and hold** the required body position?

**Deficit signals:**
- Can't achieve streamline (shoulder mobility)
- Can't maintain flat back in breakouts (core stability)
- Can't achieve full ankle extension (ankle mobility)

**Coaching response:**  
If position is the limiter, **mobility/stability work comes first**. External cues won't fix a mechanical restriction.

---

### Power (Strength + Explosiveness)
**Question:** Can the athlete generate **enough force quickly enough**?

**Deficit signals:**
- Weak underwater kicks
- Slow reaction off blocks
- Can't maintain tempo in final 25

**Coaching response:**  
If power is the limiter, **strength training comes first**. External cues optimize coordination, but can't create force that isn't there.

---

### Pattern (Coordination + Technique)
**Question:** Can the athlete **organize movement effectively**?

**Signal:**  
Position ✅ + Power ✅ + Pattern ❌ = **Pure coordination issue**

**Coaching response:**  
**THIS is where external focus cues shine.** The athlete has the physical capacity—they just need better motor organization.

## The Coaching Loop: When to Use External Cues

Winkelman's **DDCDD Framework** maps communication throughout a practice:

### 1. **DESCRIBE it** (Setup/Context)
- Explain drill/skill structure
- Keep it brief
- **Use internal language here if needed** (anatomy, positions)

### 2. **DEMONSTRATE it** (Visual Model)
- Show correct execution
- Optional: Show A/B comparison (good vs. flawed)

### 3. **CUE it** ⭐ ← **THIS IS WHERE EXTERNAL FOCUS LIVES**
- **One rep, one cue, one focus**
- Last thought before they move
- External > Internal

### 4. **DO it** (Execute)
- **Stay silent** (don't interrupt flow)
- Position yourself to observe target features

### 5. **DEBRIEF it** (Feedback Loop)
- Ask questions, don't lecture
- Athlete-led reflection > Coach monologue

## Translation to Swimming: Before & After

### Scenario: Swimmer Drops Elbow on Entry

❌ **Internal Focus (Old Way):**
"You're dropping your elbow. Keep it high. Engage your shoulder stabilizers."

✅ **External Focus (Better):**
"Reach over a barrel."  
or  
"Spear the water with your fingertips aiming 2 feet in front of your head."

---

### Scenario: Weak Underwater Kicks

❌ **Internal:**
"Flex your ankle more. Use your glutes and hamstrings."

✅ **External:**
"Drive your heels to the wall behind you."  
or  
"Make bubbles explode off your feet."

---

### Scenario: Poor Streamline Off Wall

❌ **Internal:**
"Squeeze your ears with your biceps. Engage your core."

✅ **External:**
"Be a bullet shooting through a tube."  
or  
"Make your body skinnier than the lane line."

## Research Highlights

### Wulf's 1998 Ski Simulator Study
- **External focus group:** 25% greater movement amplitude
- **Effect persisted** even after cues removed (retention test)
- **Conclusion:** External focus = faster learning + better retention

### Balance Studies (Wulf, 1999)
- External focus ("keep markers level") > Internal focus ("keep feet level")
- **Same visual input, different mental focus = different results**

### Golf Chip Shots (Wulf, 1999)
- External ("pendulum motion of club") > Internal ("swinging motion of arms")
- **80 practice shots:** External group = far more accurate

### Distance Matters (Wulf, 2003)
- **"Far" external cues** (focus on target/goal) > **"close" external cues** (focus on implement)
- Example: "Ball landing on target" > "Trajectory of ball"

## Practical Rules for Swim Coaches

### ✅ DO:
1. **Default to external focus** for all technique instruction
2. **Use vivid imagery** ("grab-slice-throw" vs. "high elbow catch")
3. **Focus on outcomes** ("fast finish" vs. "tricep extension")
4. **Embed the goal** in the cue ("drive to the wall" not just "drive")
5. **Ask athletes for their focus** before giving yours (autonomy)

### ❌ DON'T:
1. **Zoom into joints/muscles** during live swimming
2. **Overload with multiple cues** (1 rep = 1 cue)
3. **Narrate during execution** (silence during DO it)
4. **Assume internal cues build awareness** (they build clutter)
5. **Ignore position/power deficits** (fix the car before coaching the driver)

## The "Zoom Fallacy"

> "The closer you get to an elephant, the harder it is to know you're looking at an elephant."

**Coaching trap:** Identifying a technical error and zooming into that error with internal cues.

**Better approach:**  
1. Identify the error (coach's job)
2. Cue the **desired effect**, not the mechanics (athlete's job)

**Example:**
- ❌ "Your elbow is dropping because your lat isn't engaging."
- ✅ "Grab the water in front and pull it to your hip pocket."

---

## Memory Aid: "The Windsurf Test"

Ask yourself:

> "If I were learning to windsurf, would focusing on THIS help me catch wind and move forward?"

- "Extend your knees" → NO (falls in water)
- "Drive off the board" → YES (catches wind)

**Swimming equivalent:**
- "Flex your ankle" → Sinks
- "Push the water back" → Moves forward
