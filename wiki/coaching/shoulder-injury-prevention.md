# Shoulder Injury Prevention for Swimmers

**Source:** *Swimming Anatomy* by Ian McLeod  
**Category:** Coaching | Injury Prevention | Shoulder Health  
**For:** Coaches, swimmers dealing with shoulder pain, strength coaches

## The Problem

**Swimming = repetitive overhead motion** → high risk of shoulder injury

**Most common issues:**
- Swimmer's shoulder (impingement syndrome)
- Rotator cuff tendinitis
- Scapular winging (weak serratus anterior)
- Instability from muscle imbalances

**Root cause:** Imbalances between:
- Overdeveloped: Lats, pecs, internal rotators
- Underdeveloped: Scapular stabilizers, rotator cuff, external rotators

---

## Anatomy Primer

### The Shoulder Blade Stabilizers (Scapular Stabilizers)

**Why they matter:**
> "All propulsive forces generated by the arm and hand rely on the scapula having a firm base of support."

**Key muscles:**
1. **Serratus anterior** — prevents scapular winging, allows upward rotation
2. **Rhomboids** — retract (pinch) shoulder blades together
3. **Levator scapula** — elevates shoulder blade
4. **Trapezius (middle & lower fibers)** — retract + depress shoulder blades
5. **Pectoralis minor** — stabilizes scapula against rib cage

**Signs of weakness:**
- Scapular winging (shoulder blade "pops out" during push-ups)
- Inability to hold streamline position without shrugging
- Shoulder blades ride upward during recovery phase

---

### The Rotator Cuff

**Four muscles that stabilize the shoulder joint:**
1. **Supraspinatus** — initiates arm abduction (lifting away from body)
2. **Infraspinatus** — external rotation
3. **Teres minor** — external rotation
4. **Subscapularis** — internal rotation

**All four arise from the shoulder blade** → scapular stability is prerequisite for rotator cuff function

**Swimming bias:**
- All strokes emphasize INTERNAL rotation
- Only backstroke emphasizes EXTERNAL rotation
- **Result:** Internal rotators overdeveloped, external rotators weak → imbalance → injury

## The Injury Prevention Framework

### 1. Strengthen Scapular Stabilizers

**Goal:** Create firm anchor point for arm movements

#### Exercise: Scapular Push-Up

**Muscles:** Serratus anterior (primary), pectoralis minor

**How to perform:**
1. Start in push-up position (or knees if needed)
2. Keep elbows locked throughout
3. **Pinch shoulder blades together** (retract scapulae)
4. **Push shoulder blades apart** (protract scapulae)
5. 15-20 reps × 3 sets

**Coaching cues:**
- Movement comes from shoulder blade, NOT arms
- Small range of motion (~2 inches / 5 cm)
- If shoulder blades "wing" (stick out), serratus anterior is weak → regress to wall push-ups

**Swimming benefit:** Controls scapular rotation during all strokes; prevents winging

---

#### Exercise: T-Y-A (Prone on Ground or Physioball)

**Muscles:** Rhomboids, trapezius (middle/lower), teres minor, infraspinatus, posterior deltoid

**How to perform:**
1. Lie face down (or on physioball for added difficulty)
2. Slightly arch upper back, lift shoulders off ground
3. **T position:** Arms out to sides (90° from body), palms down, oscillate up/down 30s
4. **Y position:** Arms overhead at 45° angle, palms down, oscillate up/down 30s
5. **A position:** Arms by sides, palms down, oscillate up/down 30s
6. Switch all positions to palms UP, repeat

**Coaching cues:**
- Focus on **squeezing shoulder blades together**
- Small, rapid oscillatory movements (not large swings)
- Start bodyweight; add 0.5-1 lb dumbbells only when form is perfect for 60s holds

**Progression (Physioball T-Y-A):**
- More challenging: mimics swimming position (body horizontal)
- Must hold body in straight line (feet → head)
- Trains stabilizers in swim-specific position

**Swimming benefit:** Targets MOST scapular stabilizers; enhances shoulder blade control; reduces impingement risk

---

#### Exercise: Scapular Dip

**Muscles:** Lower trapezius (primary), pectoralis major/minor, latissimus dorsi

**How to perform:**
1. Sit upright between two 6" (15 cm) boxes
2. Hands on boxes, elbows bent 90°, forearms resting on box
3. **Push down** (reverse shrug), lifting butt off ground
4. Emphasize **dropping shoulders DOWN** (not shrugging UP)
5. Lower until barely touching ground, repeat
6. 12-15 reps × 3 sets

**Coaching cues:**
- Focus on **shoulder depression** (opposite of shrugging)
- Keep torso upright (don't lean forward)
- Movement comes from shoulder blades

**Swimming benefit:**
- Strengthens lower trapezius (often weak in swimmers)
- Corrects rounded-shoulder posture
- Reduces impingement by improving scapular positioning

---

### 2. Balance Rotator Cuff (Internal vs. External Rotation)

**Goal:** Counteract internal rotation dominance from swimming

#### Exercise: External Rotation With Tubing

**Muscles:** Infraspinatus, teres minor (primary); posterior deltoid (secondary)

**How to perform:**
1. Stand sideways 4 ft (120 cm) from pole with tubing attached at elbow height
2. Hold tubing in hand farthest from pole
3. Bend elbow to 90° (keep forearm parallel to floor)
4. **Optional:** Place rolled towel between elbow and torso (keeps elbow tight, reduces shoulder tension)
5. **Rotate hand AWAY from torso** (6-degree arc)
6. Keep forearm parallel to floor entire movement
7. Slowly return to start
8. 12-15 reps × 3 sets

**Coaching cues:**
- **Stabilize shoulder blade first** (pinch down and back)
- Keep elbow locked at side (towel helps)
- Controlled, slow movement (no jerking)

**Swimming benefit:**
- Isolates external rotators (infraspinatus, teres minor)
- Critical for backstrokers
- Balances internal rotation bias from other strokes

---

#### Exercise: Internal Rotation With Tubing

**Muscles:** Subscapularis (primary); pectoralis major, latissimus dorsi, anterior deltoid (secondary)

**How to perform:**
1. Stand sideways 4 ft from pole with tubing at elbow height
2. Hold tubing in hand closest to pole
3. Bend elbow to 90°, forearm parallel to floor
4. **Rotate hand ACROSS front of body** until it contacts torso
5. Keep forearm parallel to floor entire movement
6. Slowly return to start
7. 12-15 reps × 3 sets

**Coaching cues:**
- Stabilize shoulder blade (pinch down/back)
- Towel between elbow and torso (optional but helpful)
- Keep elbow tight against side

**Swimming benefit:**
- Strengthens subscapularis (one of four rotator cuff muscles)
- Prevents imbalance (even though internal rotation is swimming-dominant, subscapularis still needs targeted work)

---

### 3. Correct Postural Imbalances

**Problem:** Swimmers develop rounded-shoulder posture from:
- Overdeveloped lats + pecs
- Weak middle/lower traps + rhomboids

**Visual test:** Stand sideways in mirror
- Healthy: Shoulders aligned over hips
- Swimmer's posture: Shoulders rolled forward, scapulae protracted

#### Exercise: Reverse Shrug Movements

**Incorporated into T-Y-A and Scapular Dip**

**Coaching cue:** "Pinch shoulder blades DOWN and BACK like putting them in back pockets"

**NOT:** Shrugging upward (overdevelops already-tight upper trapezius)

---

### 4. Avoid Overtraining Pecs/Lats

**Common mistake:** Dryland programs heavy on:
- Bench press (pecs)
- Pull-ups (lats)
- Push-ups (pecs)

**Result:** Exacerbates existing imbalance

**Solution:**
- **Push/pull ratio:** For every 2 "pulling" exercises (lats), include 1 scapular stabilizer exercise
- **Prioritize:** Rows (scapular retraction) over pull-ups (pure lat work)
- **Add:** Rear delt flies, reverse flies, face pulls

## Comprehensive Shoulder Health Program

### Pre-Season (Off-Season Focus)

**Goal:** Build foundation, correct imbalances

**Frequency:** 3× per week

**Session structure:**
1. **Dynamic warm-up** (arm circles, band pull-aparts, 5 min)
2. **Scapular stabilizers:**
   - Scapular push-ups (3 × 15)
   - T-Y-A (3 × 30s each position, bodyweight)
   - Scapular dips (3 × 12)
3. **Rotator cuff:**
   - External rotation with tubing (3 × 15 each arm)
   - Internal rotation with tubing (3 × 15 each arm)
4. **Strength work:**
   - Rows (scapular emphasis, 3 × 10)
   - Lat pull-downs (3 × 10)
   - Light push-ups (3 × 12, focus on scapular control)
5. **Cool-down:** Static stretching (pecs, lats, anterior shoulders)

**Duration:** 40-50 minutes

---

### In-Season (Maintenance)

**Goal:** Maintain stability without fatigue

**Frequency:** 2× per week (after easier pool sessions)

**Session structure:**
1. **Dynamic warm-up** (5 min)
2. **Scapular + Rotator Cuff Circuit** (2 rounds, minimal rest):
   - Scapular push-ups (12 reps)
   - T-Y-A (20s each, lighter volume)
   - External rotation (12 reps each arm)
   - Scapular dips (10 reps)
3. **Optional:** 1-2 compound lifts (light, 2 × 8)
4. **Stretch:** Focus on tight areas (5-10 min)

**Duration:** 25-30 minutes

---

### Pre-Competition (Taper)

**Goal:** Maintain stability, avoid fatigue

**Frequency:** 1× per week (or eliminate last 10 days)

**Session structure:**
1. **Light activation only:**
   - Band pull-aparts (2 × 15)
   - External rotation (2 × 12, very light resistance)
   - T-Y-A (1 × 20s, bodyweight only)
2. **Stretch** (10 min)

**Duration:** 15-20 minutes

## Early Warning Signs of Shoulder Issues

### Red Flags (STOP and assess)

1. **Pain during swimming** (not just soreness after)
2. **Clicking, popping, grinding** in shoulder joint
3. **Pain reaching overhead** (putting on shirt, reaching back)
4. **Weakness** in arm movements
5. **Night pain** (wakes you up)
6. **Scapular winging** visible during push-ups

### Yellow Flags (Modify training, add prevention work)

1. **Soreness** lasting >48 hours post-swim
2. **Stiffness** in shoulder upon waking
3. **Tightness** in pecs/lats restricting range of motion
4. **Fatigue** in shoulder during sets (not cardiovascular fatigue)
5. **Postural changes** (increased rounding)

## Rehabilitation Considerations

**If pain is present:**

### Phase 1: Pain Reduction (Week 1-2)
- **Rest** from aggravating movements
- **Ice** after activity (15-20 min)
- **Gentle range of motion** (pendulum swings, wall slides)
- **Consult** physical therapist or sports medicine physician

### Phase 2: Restore Function (Week 3-4)
- **Scapular stabilization** (emphasis: serratus anterior)
- **Rotator cuff activation** (very light resistance)
- **Postural correction** (rhomboid/lower trap work)
- **Pool:** Kick-only, pull buoy (no arms), easy swimming

### Phase 3: Rebuild Strength (Week 5-8)
- **Progress resistance** gradually (5-10% per week)
- **Reintroduce swimming** (technique focus, low volume)
- **Monitor pain** (should be decreasing, not increasing)

### Phase 4: Return to Full Training (Week 9+)
- **Gradual volume increase** (10-15% per week)
- **Continue prevention exercises** (2-3× per week)
- **Reassess** regularly (video analysis, strength tests)

**Key principle:** Pain-free movement before loading. Loading before volume.

---

## Integration with Pool Training

### Timing
**Best:** Dryland on easier pool days (or as separate session, 6+ hours apart)  
**Avoid:** Heavy dryland immediately before hard pool sessions

### Volume Management
**High pool volume weeks** → Reduce dryland intensity  
**Taper weeks** → Reduce dryland volume alongside pool volume

### Communication
**Coach-swimmer check-ins:**
- "How do your shoulders feel?" (daily)
- "Pain or soreness?" (differentiate)
- "Any clicking/popping?" (joint issues)

## Practical Coaching Tips

### 1. **Prioritize Prevention Over Correction**
- Easier to prevent injury than rehab
- Build scapular/rotator cuff work into routine EARLY

### 2. **"Set the Shoulder Blades" Cue**
- Before EVERY upper-body exercise
- "Pinch down and back, like back pockets"
- Creates stable base for all movements

### 3. **Start Light, Progress Slowly**
- Stabilizers = small muscles, fatigue quickly
- Start bodyweight, add 0.5-1 lb increments
- Perfect form > heavier weight

### 4. **Monitor Scapular Winging**
- Quick test: Push-ups
- If shoulder blades stick out → weak serratus anterior
- Regress to wall push-ups or scapular push-ups

### 5. **Balance Is Key**
- For every bench press, do a row
- For every lat pull-down, do scapular stabilization
- Don't just add more pulling (lats already strong)

## Self-Assessment: Shoulder Health Checklist

**Rate 1-5 (1 = poor, 5 = excellent):**

- [ ] Can hold streamline overhead without pain (___/5)
- [ ] Shoulder blades stay flat during push-ups (no winging) (___/5)
- [ ] Equal strength in internal + external rotation (___/5)
- [ ] Shoulders aligned over hips when standing (not rounded forward) (___/5)
- [ ] No clicking/popping during arm circles (___/5)
- [ ] Can sleep on shoulder without pain (___/5)
- [ ] Full overhead range of motion (___/5)

**Score:**
- **30-35:** Excellent shoulder health (maintain current program)
- **24-29:** Good (add 1-2 prevention exercises)
- **18-23:** Fair (implement full prevention program)
- **<18:** Poor (reduce pool volume, focus on rehab, see professional)

## Key Exercises Summary

| Exercise | Primary Muscle | Sets × Reps | When to Use |
|----------|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Scapular Push-Up** | Serratus anterior | 3 × 15 | All phases; foundation exercise |
| **T-Y-A** | Scapular stabilizers | 3 × 30s (each) | Pre-season, maintenance |
| **Scapular Dip** | Lower trapezius | 3 × 12 | Correcting rounded shoulders |
| **External Rotation** | Infraspinatus, teres minor | 3 × 15 | All phases; critical for balance |
| **Internal Rotation** | Subscapularis | 3 × 15 | Balance with external rotation |

**Golden rule:** Never skip scapular stabilization. It's the foundation of shoulder health.
