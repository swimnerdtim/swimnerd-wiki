# Bill Sweetenham's Five Golden Rules for Swimming
**Source:** *Championship Swim Training* by Bill Sweetenham & John Atkinson (2003)

## Philosophy

> "Speed is a result of efficiency."

These five rules form the foundation of Sweetenham's coaching philosophy. They apply to **all swimmers** - age-group, youth, senior, masters, and triathletes. Every drill, every set, every technical correction should trace back to these principles.

**Key insight:** These aren't suggestions. They're non-negotiables for technique development.

---

## The Five Golden Rules

### Rule 1: Every Hand Movement is Slow to Fast

**The principle:**
- All pulling motions accelerate from entry to exit
- The learning process must mirror this: slow → fast
- If you can't do it efficiently at low speed, you can't do it efficiently at high speed

**Teaching method:**
✅ Master the movement pattern slowly first
✅ Add speed only after efficiency is locked in
✅ Use video to confirm acceleration through the pull

**Common mistake:** Athletes rush technique at high speeds before mastering it at control speeds

**Coaching cue:** "Show me perfect form at 70% before we go 90%"

---

### Rule 2: Learn to Feel the Water (The Wrong Way First)

**The principle:**
- Athletes must spend time developing "feel for the water"
- Learning the WRONG way helps them recognize the RIGHT way
- Contrast training creates kinesthetic awareness

**Teaching method - Freestyle pull pattern:**
1. Swim 25m with arms pulling OUTSIDE body line (wrong)
2. Swim 25m with arms pulling UNDER body (wrong)
3. Swim 25m with narrow pull under body (correct)

**Athletes discover:** The correct feel by experiencing incorrect patterns first

**Teaching method - Streamlining:**
1. Push off wall like a starfish (wrong - maximum drag)
2. Push off with hands at shoulder width (wrong - medium drag)  
3. Push off hand-over-hand streamlined (correct - minimum drag)

**The learning insight:**
> "Most children do things the wrong way first as part of natural learning progression. Then, if they get it right later, they appreciate the benefits."

**Winners vs. Losers:**
- Winners don't accept failure → move on to find success
- Losers accept failure → find excuses

---

### Rule 3: Fingers Pull First, Stable Wrist Position

**The principle:**
- On every stroke, fingers engage the water before the forearm
- Wrist position remains stable (not floppy) throughout the pull
- Early vertical forearm (EVF) depends on finger-first entry

**What this means:**
- Fingertips lead, forearm follows
- No "dropping" the elbow
- Wrist acts as rigid extension of forearm

**Coaching check:**
✅ Watch the catch - do fingertips point down first?
✅ Is wrist locked or flexing?
✅ Does the hand "slip" through water?

**Applies to:** All four strokes

---

### Rule 4: Head Down, Hips Up (Flat-Spine Position)

**The iron law:**
> "No matter what the athlete does in swimming, if the head comes up, the buttocks go down."

**The physics:**
- Head up = hips drop = increased drag = slower swimming
- Flat spine = minimal resistance = maximum force application

**Flat-spine position:**
- Head neutral (looking at bottom)
- Back flat (not arched)
- Hips at surface
- Body forms a plank

**Application to breathing:**
- Freestyle: rotate to breathe, don't lift head
- Butterfly: chin stays low on breath
- Breaststroke: minimize head lift on breath

**Coaching cue:** "If I see daylight under your belly, you're sinking"

---

### Rule 5: Hips High Before Hand Entry

**For Butterfly & Breaststroke:**
- Hips must be ELEVATED before the outsweep begins
- If hips are low, you're pulling UPWARD (to surface) instead of FORWARD
- Forward propulsion requires hips at the surface first

**The sequence (Butterfly):**
1. Hips rise as hands enter
2. Hands sweep out while hips are HIGH
3. Pull occurs with body in streamlined position
4. Not: hands enter → hips sink → pull yourself UP

**For Backstroke & Freestyle:**
- Hips should ROTATE before hands start the pulling action
- Rotation generates power
- Flat swimming = weak swimming

**Coaching check:**
- Butterfly/Breast: Are hips up when hands sweep out?
- Free/Back: Do hips rotate BEFORE the catch?

## Sweetenham's Training Philosophy

### Participation vs. Performance

**The critical progression for maturation-age athletes:**

**8 hours/week:**
- Participation, fun, involvement, health benefits
- **NOT competition swimming**
- Never produces competitive results

**10-12 hours/week:**
- "The competitive swimming twilight zone"
- Too much to be fun
- Not enough to produce results  
- Athletes never feel good → frustration builds

**18-24 hours/week:**
- THIS is competitive swimming
- Athletes are committed (not just involved)
- Satisfaction from improved results
- The minimum for serious performance

**Coaching challenge:** Sell the dream to the 10-12 hour swimmers and convince them to commit to 18-24 hours

## Applying the Five Golden Rules

### Daily Practice Reminders

**For Age-Group Coaches:**
1. ✅ Teach slow first (Rule 1)
2. ✅ Use contrast drills (Rule 2)  
3. ✅ Check finger entry on every stroke (Rule 3)
4. ✅ Enforce head position (Rule 4)
5. ✅ Watch hip timing on fly/breast (Rule 5)

**For Senior Coaches:**
1. ✅ Video review at control speeds
2. ✅ Contrast sets in warm-up
3. ✅ Paddle work for wrist stability
4. ✅ Backstroke for flat-spine awareness
5. ✅ Rotation drills before main sets

### Drill Design Based on Golden Rules

**Every drill should address at least one golden rule:**

| Drill | Golden Rule(s) |
|-------|---------------|
| Sculling | 2, 3 (feel water, fingers first) |
| Distance per stroke (DPS) | 1, 4 (slow to fast, flat spine) |
| Single-arm freestyle | 3, 5 (fingers first, hip rotation) |
| Underwater streamline kicks | 4 (flat spine position) |
| Build swims (50% → 100%) | 1 (slow to fast progression) |

## Coaching Philosophy: One-Page Model

**Sweetenham's mandate:**
> "All coaches should have a one-page statement of their coaching philosophy and model that covers physical and mental preparation of athletes."

**What matters MORE than the model:**
- Coach's daily dedication to the task
- Belief in the model
- Confidence from both coach AND swimmers in the model

**The model must include:**
1. Technical principles (the five golden rules)
2. Training progression
3. Mental preparation approach
4. Competitive focus
5. Development pathway

**Without this:** You're winging it. With this: You have a system.

## Competition Applications

### Race Technique Checklist

**Before blocks:**
- [ ] Visualize Rule 4 (flat spine, head down)
- [ ] Remind: fingers first (Rule 3)
- [ ] Plan: hips high on breakout (Rule 5)

**During race:**
- [ ] Accelerate pulls (Rule 1)
- [ ] Trust the feel (Rule 2)
- [ ] Maintain head position (Rule 4)

**Post-race review:**
- Which golden rule broke down under fatigue?
- Where did technique revert to "habit"?
- What drill progression fixes this?

## The Bottom Line

> "For every stroke fault, a drill or progression is available to correct it."

The five golden rules aren't abstract theory. They're practical, testable, observable principles that apply to every stroke, every swimmer, every day.

**Coaches:** Print these rules. Laminate them. Put them on the pool deck. Reference them in every technical conversation.

**Swimmers:** You already know these rules. The question is: are you APPLYING them?
