# Essential Swim Gear: What Works, What Matters, What to Skip

**Source:** *The Swimmer's Workout Handbook* by Terri Schneider  
**Context:** Practical gear selection for swimmers & coaches — from goggles to training tools  
**Audience:** New swimmers, coaches equipping teams, parents buying gear for kids

---

## The Non-Negotiables: Core Gear Every Swimmer Needs

### 1. Swimsuit

**Purpose:** Comfort, streamlining, durability

**What to look for:**
- **Comfortable** — No chafing, digging, or constant adjusting
- **Streamlined** — Minimal drag in water
- **Durable** — Resists chlorine/salt degradation
- **You enjoy wearing it** — Motivation matters!

**Types:**

#### Practice Suits (Daily Training)
- **Men:** Jammers (mid-thigh) or square-cut briefs
- **Women:** One-piece, moderate coverage
- **Key:** Durability over speed — will be in chlorine 5-7 days/week

**Recommendations:**
- Polyester blends last longer than nylon
- Avoid fashion suits for practice (they fall apart fast)
- Buy 2-3 suits, rotate them (extends life)

#### Competition Suits (Racing)
- **Tech suits:** Compression, water-repellent, expensive ($200-500)
- **Training race suits:** Moderate compression, $30-80
- **When to use:** Championship meets, time trials, important races

**Key distinction:** "Some use their suits to make a fashion statement at every workout, while others wear an inexpensive suit even after it starts to sag in the butt due to dissolving elastic. Whichever your style, choose a suit that feels comfortable in the water."

**Rookie mistake:** Wearing a tech suit to practice. Save it for racing.

---

### 2. Goggles

**Purpose:**
- Protect eyes from chlorine/salt water
- See clearly above AND below water
- Essential for proper swimming

**What to look for:**
- **Fit your face** — No leaking
- **Comfortable for long periods** — No headaches, pressure pain
- **Adjustable** — Nose piece, head strap
- **Fog-resistant** — Built-in coating or use anti-fog

**How to test goggles in-store:**
1. Press goggles to your eye area (no strap)
2. They should create suction and stay on
3. If they fall off immediately → wrong fit
4. If they stick comfortably → good candidate

**Once in the water:**
1. Adjust strap to feel snug but comfortable
2. Wear for 10+ minutes without taking them off
3. If discomfort/fogging/leaking → adjust or replace

**Anti-fog tips:**
- Spit in lenses, swish around, dip in water (classic method)
- Use commercial anti-fog spray (follow package directions)
- Never wipe inside of lens with towel (removes coating)

**For outdoor swimmers:**
- **Dark/mirrored lenses** — Midday sun, bright conditions
- **Light/clear lenses** — Early morning, evening, indoor
- Consider 2 pairs if swimming outdoors often

**Goggles are personal.** What works for your teammate might not work for you. Try 3-5 pairs before settling.

---

### 3. Swim Cap

**Purpose:**
- Keep head streamlined
- Insulate in cold water
- Keep hair out of face
- Protect hair from chlorine (partially)

**Types:**

#### Silicone Caps
**Pros:**
- Most comfortable
- Longest lasting
- Warmer than latex
- Doesn't pull hair

**Cons:**
- More expensive ($8-15)

**Best for:** Daily training, most swimmers

#### Latex/Rubber Caps
**Pros:**
- Cheap ($2-5)
- Very streamlined

**Cons:**
- Pull hair when putting on/taking off
- Less durable
- Can cause allergic reactions

**Best for:** Racing, budget-conscious swimmers

#### Lycra Caps
**Pros:**
- Extremely comfortable
- No hair pulling
- Easy on/off

**Cons:**
- Water moves through fabric → NOT streamlined
- Doesn't keep head warm
- Defeats purpose of a cap

**Best for:** Casual lap swimming, water aerobics (NOT competitive training)

**Schneider's take:** "Some opt for lycra swim caps for comfort, but take note that water can move through a lycra cap, making your head much less streamlined in the water than a sealed rubber cap."

**Bottom line:** Silicone for training, latex for racing, skip lycra for serious swimming.

---

### 4. Earplugs

**Purpose:** Prevent swimmer's ear (infection)

**Why you need them:**
"All swimmers should wear them." Not optional.

**The science:**
1. Excess moisture in ear canal
2. Skin becomes waterlogged
3. Acidity (natural infection barrier) dilutes
4. Bacteria/fungi penetrate skin
5. Swimmer's ear develops (painful, requires antibiotics)

**Prevention:** Earplugs.

**Types:**

#### Soft Moldable Silicone Plugs
- Wax/silicone putty
- Mold to outer ear shape
- Very comfortable
- **Best for:** Most swimmers, sensitive ears

#### Pre-Molded Rubber Plugs
- Flanged design, fits in ear canal
- Reusable
- Some swimmers find them uncomfortable
- **Best for:** Swimmers who prefer firmer fit

**Try both, use what keeps water out and feels good.**

**Rookie mistake:** Swimming without earplugs because "I don't have a problem yet." By the time you have swimmer's ear, you've already damaged your ear canal. Prevention > treatment.

## Training Tools: When to Use, How to Use

### 5. Kickboard

**Purpose:**
- Isolate legs for kick sets
- Build leg strength
- Practice kick technique

**How to use correctly:**
1. Place hands over top of board (fingers wrap around)
2. Arms extended overhead
3. Forearms rest naturally on board
4. Head neutral (look down/forward, not straight ahead)

**Common mistake:** Arms too narrow, head too high → neck strain

**When to use:**
- Dedicated kick sets in practice
- Beginners learning kick technique

**When NOT to use:**
- Every kick set (also learn to kick without a board!)
- Swimmers with shoulder issues (can aggravate)

**Schneider's guidance:** "Use a kickboard for kick sets in training, but also learn to do kick sets without a board."

**Why kick without a board:**
- Teaches proper body position
- Engages core
- More realistic for racing (you don't get a board in a race!)

**Set example:**
```
4×50 kick w/board w/:20R
4×50 kick no board, arms extended w/:20R
```

---

### 6. Pull Buoy

**Purpose:**
- Isolate upper body (legs float behind you)
- Focus on arm stroke mechanics
- Build upper-body strength

**How to use:**
1. Place buoy between thighs (at top, not knees)
2. Squeeze gently to keep it in place
3. Legs stay relaxed, no kicking

**Effect:** Legs float up, body position improves, focus shifts to pull.

**When to use:**
- Pull sets (designated in workout)
- Technique work (feel the catch/pull without kick distraction)
- Swimmers with leg injuries (can still train upper body)

**When NOT to use:**
- Every main set (you need to train with your kick!)
- Swimmers who rely on it as a crutch (learn body position without it)

**Set example:**
```
4×100 pull w/:20R
— With pull buoy, focus on high elbow catch
```

**Combo with paddles:** Often used together (pull + paddles), but start with just the buoy to learn technique.

---

### 7. Hand Paddles

**Purpose:**
- Increase resistance
- Build upper-body strength
- Teach proper hand/arm path through water

**Types:**
- **Large paddles:** More resistance, more strength
- **Small paddles:** Less resistance, better for beginners
- **Finger paddles:** Minimal resistance, technique focus

**How to use:**
1. Strap paddles to hands (some have wrist/finger straps)
2. Swim normally (usually freestyle, can do other strokes)
3. Focus on feeling the water, full pull-through

**Important safety warning:**
"Only use swim paddles if you have no shoulder issues as they can put extra stress on the shoulder."

**If you have ANY shoulder pain:** Skip paddles. See a coach or PT.

**When to use:**
- Pull sets with paddles (designated in workout)
- 1-2× per week (not every session)
- After warm-up (never cold)

**When NOT to use:**
- Beginners (learn technique first)
- Swimmers with shoulder problems
- Every single set (overuse leads to injury)

**Progression:**
1. Start with small paddles
2. Use 1-2× per week for 200-400 meters
3. Gradually increase volume over months
4. If shoulder pain develops → stop immediately

**Set example:**
```
4×100 pull w/paddles & buoy w/:20R
— Focus on long, powerful strokes
```

---

### 8. Fins (Full-Length or Short)

**Purpose:**
- Strengthen legs
- Teach proper body position
- Increase speed for drills
- Raise heart rate

**Types:**

#### Full-Length Fins (Zoomers, Scuba-Style)
- **Pros:** More propulsion, easier to maintain position
- **Cons:** Can create bad habits (too much kick from knee)
- **Best for:** Body position drills, speed work

#### Short Fins (Chopped, Training Fins)
- **Pros:** More natural kick, less stress on ankles, strength building
- **Cons:** Less propulsion, requires more effort
- **Best for:** Kick technique, strength, regular training use

**Schneider's recommendation:** "If you are looking to generate a more productive kick, focus on proper drill technique, or increase your heart rate a bit, try using the short or chopped-off style of fins."

**When to use:**
- Drill sets (vertical kick, body position drills)
- Kick sets (for speed/intensity)
- IM work (helps with fly/back kick)

**When NOT to use:**
- Every set (you need to swim without them!)
- Freestyle main sets (unless specifically called for)

**Key insight:** "Fins can be an excellent teaching aid but should not be the focus of your swim workouts."

**Set example:**
```
6×50 kick with short fins w/:15R
— Focus on kick from hips, not knees
```

## Gear Mistakes Swimmers Make

### ❌ Mistake 1: Relying on Tools as Crutches

**Problem:** Always using pull buoy because "I sink without it"

**Why it's bad:**
- Never learn proper body position
- Weak kick persists
- Can't race without tools

**Fix:**
- Use tools for designated sets only
- Spend equal time swimming without tools
- Work on body position drills (streamline, float test)

---

### ❌ Mistake 2: Using Paddles Too Soon

**Problem:** Beginner buys large paddles, uses them every practice

**Why it's bad:**
- Reinforces bad technique at high resistance
- Shoulder injury risk
- Develops power without proper mechanics

**Fix:**
- Master technique first (6+ months of swimming)
- Start with small paddles or finger paddles
- Use 1-2× per week max
- Stop immediately if shoulder pain develops

---

### ❌ Mistake 3: Wrong Goggles (or Not Trying Enough Pairs)

**Problem:** Swimmer buys first pair, they leak/fog, swimmer quits

**Why it's bad:** Swimming without clear vision is miserable

**Fix:**
- Try 3-5 pairs in store
- Test suction before buying
- Adjust straps properly in water
- Use anti-fog spray/spit
- If they don't work after 2-3 swims, try different pair

---

### ❌ Mistake 4: Skipping Earplugs

**Problem:** "I don't need them, my ears are fine"

**Why it's bad:** Swimmer's ear can develop after months/years of exposure

**Fix:**
- Wear earplugs from day 1
- Prevention is easier than treatment
- Cheap insurance ($3-10) against painful infection

---

### ❌ Mistake 5: Wearing Fashion Suits for Training

**Problem:** Buys cute/expensive suit, wears it daily, it falls apart in 2 weeks

**Why it's bad:**
- Chlorine destroys fashion suits fast
- Expensive
- Needs constant replacement

**Fix:**
- Buy polyester practice suits
- Save fashion/tech suits for meets
- Rotate 2-3 practice suits

## Gear Checklist by Swimmer Type

### Beginner Swimmer (First 6 Months)
**Must have:**
- [ ] Comfortable swimsuit (1-2 practice suits)
- [ ] Goggles (try 2-3 pairs, find what works)
- [ ] Swim cap (silicone recommended)
- [ ] Earplugs (moldable silicone)

**Optional:**
- [ ] Kickboard (pool may provide)
- [ ] Pull buoy (pool may provide)

**Skip for now:**
- Paddles (learn technique first)
- Fins (focus on basic swimming)

**Budget:** $50-80

---

### Intermediate Swimmer (6 Months - 2 Years)
**Must have:**
- [ ] 2-3 practice suits (rotate them)
- [ ] 2 pairs of goggles (backup pair)
- [ ] 2 swim caps (racing + practice)
- [ ] Earplugs
- [ ] Kickboard (own, don't rely on pool's)
- [ ] Pull buoy

**Add:**
- [ ] Small hand paddles (for designated sets)
- [ ] Short fins (for kick/drill sets)

**Skip for now:**
- Tech suit (not racing at high level yet)
- Large paddles (injury risk)

**Budget:** $120-180

---

### Competitive Swimmer (2+ Years, Racing)
**Must have:**
- [ ] 3-4 practice suits
- [ ] 2-3 pairs of goggles (indoor, outdoor, backup)
- [ ] 3 swim caps (latex for racing, silicone for practice)
- [ ] Earplugs
- [ ] Kickboard
- [ ] Pull buoy
- [ ] Hand paddles (small + medium)
- [ ] Short fins
- [ ] Tech suit (for championship meets)

**Nice to have:**
- [ ] Tempo trainer (beeping metronome for pacing)
- [ ] Waterproof watch (track intervals)
- [ ] Snorkel (for technique work)
- [ ] Ankle strap (for advanced pull sets)

**Budget:** $250-400 (including tech suit)

## Gear Maintenance & Lifespan

### Swimsuit Care
**Do:**
- Rinse in cold water immediately after practice
- Hang dry (never wring)
- Rotate suits (don't wear same one every day)

**Don't:**
- Leave in bag overnight (chlorine keeps degrading)
- Wash in washing machine (breaks down elastic)
- Put in dryer (heat destroys fabric)

**Lifespan:**
- Practice suit: 3-6 months (depending on frequency)
- Tech suit: 6-10 wears (yes, really — they're not meant to last)

---

### Goggle Care
**Do:**
- Rinse after every use
- Store in case (protect lenses)
- Replace when straps lose elasticity

**Don't:**
- Wipe inside of lens with towel (removes anti-fog coating)
- Leave in sun (degrades rubber)
- Over-tighten straps (causes headaches, shortens life)

**Lifespan:** 6 months - 2 years (depending on use/care)

---

### Cap Care
**Do:**
- Rinse and dry after use
- Dust with baby powder if latex (prevents sticking)

**Don't:**
- Ball up wet (causes mildew)
- Stretch excessively when putting on

**Lifespan:**
- Silicone: 1-2 years
- Latex: 6 months - 1 year
- Lycra: 1-2 years

---

### Paddles/Fins/Buoy Care
**Do:**
- Rinse after use
- Air dry
- Store in mesh bag

**Don't:**
- Leave in enclosed bag (mildew)
- Overtighten straps (paddles)

**Lifespan:** 2-5 years (very durable)

## Budget-Friendly Gear Tips

### Start Minimal
**First month:**
- Suit ($15-30)
- Goggles ($10-20)
- Cap ($8-12)
- Earplugs ($5)
**Total: ~$50**

**Use pool equipment** for kickboards/pull buoys until you decide to invest.

---

### Buy in Bulk or Team Orders
**Many teams/clubs:**
- Order suits/caps in bulk
- Get 20-40% discount
- Share equipment (kickboards, paddles)

**Ask your coach** if team has partnership with swim shop.

---

### When to Splurge vs. Save

**Splurge on:**
- Goggles (worth paying for good fit)
- Tech suit (if racing competitively)

**Save on:**
- Practice suits (cheap polyester is fine)
- Kickboards (they all work the same)
- Training caps (basic works great)

## Actionable Coaching Tips

### For New Coaches: Set Up a Team Gear Library
**Provide at practice:**
- 10-15 kickboards
- 10-15 pull buoys
- 5-10 sets of paddles (S/M/L)
- 5-10 pairs of fins (various sizes)

**Let swimmers:**
- Try before buying
- Use during practice
- Learn what they like

**Don't require swimmers to own everything immediately** — ease into gear purchases.

---

### For Parents: What to Buy First

**Week 1-2:**
- Suit, goggles, cap, earplugs (~$50)
- See if kid likes swimming

**Month 2-3:**
- Own kickboard + pull buoy (~$30)
- Second pair of goggles (backup)

**Month 6+:**
- Paddles + fins (~$40)
- Extra suits

**Total first year:** $120-150 (spread over time)

---

**Always in your bag:**
- [ ] 2 swimsuits (in case one is wet)
- [ ] 2 pairs of goggles (primary + backup)
- [ ] 2 caps
- [ ] Earplugs
- [ ] Towel
- [ ] Water bottle

**At practice (use pool's or bring):**
- Kickboard
- Pull buoy
- Paddles

**Optional:**
