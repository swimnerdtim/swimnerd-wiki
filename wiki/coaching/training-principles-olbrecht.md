# Five Principles of Training Structure
**Source:** *The Science of Winning* by Jan Olbrecht

## Overview

These 5 principles govern ALL effective training design - from weekly microcycles to multi-year plans. Ignore them = stagnation, injury, or burnout.

---

## Principle 1: Optimal Work-Rest Ratio (Super-Compensation)

### The Concept

Training breaks you down. **Adaptation happens during recovery.**

**Super-compensation** = the period after training where your body rebuilds stronger than before.

**Critical timing:** Train again too soon → no adaptation. Wait too long → lose gains.

### Two Training Models

#### Model A: Wait for Full Super-Compensation (Preferred for Age Group)
- Complete one hard workout
- Wait for peak super-compensation (24-72 hours depending on type)
- Do next hard workout
- Result: Steady, stable conditioning gains

**When to use:**
- Age group swimmers (10-16 years)
- 3-4 workouts/week programs
- Base training periods
- Injury-prone athletes

**Pros:** 
- Lower injury risk
- More stable adaptations
- Better long-term development

**Cons:**
- Slower short-term gains
- Requires more weeks to peak

#### Model C: Accumulated Fatigue → Super-Compensation (Elite Swimmers)
- Complete 3-7 consecutive hard/high-volume workouts
- Accumulated fatigue builds (no full recovery between)
- Insert recovery phase (3-7 days easier training)
- Super-compensation happens after recovery phase
- Result: Larger adaptation, faster gains, BUT riskier

**When to use:**
- Elite swimmers with high conditioning base
- Competition training periods
- Preparing for multi-day competition schedule
- Short-term performance peaks

**Pros:**
- Faster conditioning gains
- Mimics competition demands (multiple races/weekend)
- Efficient for time-limited training camps

**Cons:**
- Higher overtraining risk
- Requires excellent conditioning base
- Can backfire if recovery phase mistimed

**Critical:** Model C requires swimmers who can handle consecutive hard efforts. NOT for beginners.

### Super-Compensation Timing by Training Type

| Training Type | Recovery Time | Peak Compensation |
|--------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Aerobic capacity (high volume) | 24-48 hours | 48-72 hours |
| Anaerobic capacity | 48-72 hours | 72-96 hours |
| Anaerobic power | 36-48 hours | 48-72 hours |
| Maximal strength | 48-72 hours | 72-120 hours |
| Sprint/speed | 12-24 hours | 24-48 hours |

**Key Insight:** Different systems recover at different rates. Plan workouts accordingly.

### Enhancing Recovery

**After strength training:**
- Stretch loaded muscles (below pain threshold)
- Light dynamic work for non-loaded muscle groups

**After anaerobic power:**
- Extended cool-down swimming
- Stimulates lactate clearance
- Accelerates waste product elimination

**After all training:**
- Eat within 20-30 minutes (easy-digestible carbs first, then protein)
- Multiple small meals > 2-3 large meals
- Light dinner, low-fat for better sleep
- Sauna (30+ min after training), hot/cold showers, massage
- Autogenic training, progressive muscle relaxation

## Principle 2: Progressive Build-Up of Training Load

### The Core Problem

**Same stimulus → no further adaptation.**

Your body adapts to training. Once adapted, that training no longer improves fitness. You MUST progressively increase load.

### How to Increase Load

1. **Increase volume** (more distance)
2. **Increase intensity** (faster swimming)
3. **Longer intervals** (100s → 200s)
4. **Harder implementation** (small paddles → large paddles)
5. **Increase load density** (less recovery between hard sessions)
6. **Change environment** (altitude training)

### Volume vs. Intensity Priority

**Volume increases ability to handle training load** (capacity to assimilate work).

**Intensity increases performance** (race results).

**Sequence:** Build volume first → then intensity.

**Why:** High-volume base allows more effective later intensity increases.

### Sudden vs. Gradual Increases

**Gradual increases:**
- 5-10% volume increase per week
- Smooth, predictable
- Lower injury risk
- Less effective for performance gains

**Sudden increases:**  
- 20-30% volume jump for 1-2 weeks
- More effective for performance improvement
- Higher injury/overtraining risk
- Requires careful implementation

**Critical:** Sudden increases MUST be followed by recovery phase (reduced volume/intensity) for super-compensation to occur.

### Real Example: Monthly Volume Jumps

**Study tracked aerobic endurance vs. monthly swimming distance:**

| Month | Volume (km/month) | 30-min Test Distance (m) |
|-------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| 1 | 40 | 2,450 |
| 2 | 65 | 2,470 |
| 3 | 90 | 2,480 |
| 4 | 100 | 2,490 |
| 5 | 75 (taper) | 2,550 |
| 6 | 85 | 2,560 |

**Key Observations:**
1. **Improvement lagged volume increase by ~1 month** (super-compensation delay)
2. **Biggest gain came during taper/recovery month** (Month 5)
3. **Non-linear improvement** - more volume ≠ proportionally more improvement
4. **Diminishing returns** - better the fitness, smaller the improvement per volume increase

**Practical:** Don't expect instant results from volume increases. Wait 4-6 weeks + recovery.

### Overload Guidelines

**Age group swimmers:**
- Increase volume 5-10% per week max
- Sudden increases only during training camps with supervision
- Focus on years-long progression, not week-to-week jumps

**Elite swimmers:**
- Can handle 15-25% volume jumps
- Requires high base conditioning
- Must document carefully and watch for overtraining signs
- Recovery phase MANDATORY after overload block

## Principle 3: Variation in Training Loads

### Why Vary Training?

**Problem:** Unchanging training → performance stagnation.

**Solution:** Systematic variation of load, exercises, and methods ensures continued adaptation.

### What to Vary

1. **Training load** (volume and intensity)
2. **Training exercises** (drills, sets, distances)  
3. **Training methods** (continuous, interval, tempo, test sets)
4. **Stroke focus** (freestyle → IM → specialty stroke)
5. **Environment** (short course → long course, altitude)

### When Consistency IS Appropriate

**Technique automation:** Unchanging drill work needed to automate movement patterns under pressure.

**Maintaining adaptations:** During in-between competition periods, repeating similar workouts maintains acquired fitness.

**Elite swimmer caveat:** Less room for variation - must train very specifically. Variation comes from subtle intensity/volume manipulations, not radical changes.

### Practical Application

**Bad:** Same workout every Monday for 8 weeks.

**Good:** Monday focus stays aerobic capacity, but vary:
- Week 1: 30x100 @ 1:20
- Week 2: 3x1000 @ 12:00
- Week 3: 6x500 @ 6:00  
- Week 4: 20x100 @ 1:15 + 10x100 @ 1:10
- (Same energy system, different stimulus)

## Principle 4: Optimal Timing & Dosing of Training Types

### Sequencing Rules

**Fundamental sequence (non-negotiable):**

1. **General → Specific → Competition-Specific**
   - Early season: Other sports, general conditioning, non-swim drills
   - Mid-season: Stroke-specific technique, capacity building
   - Late season: Race-pace work, starts/turns, competition simulation

2. **Capacity → Power**
   - Aerobic capacity BEFORE aerobic power
   - Anaerobic capacity BEFORE anaerobic power
   - Maximal strength BEFORE explosive/endurance strength

3. **Low Intensity → High Intensity**
   - Base period: Extensive (low intensity), high volume
   - Competition period: Intensive (high intensity), lower volume

### Exercise Classification System

**GPE - General Preparatory Exercises**
- No similarity to competition stroke or workload
- Build basic conditioning/coordination
- Examples: Other sports, general dryland, swimming other strokes

**GCONDE - General Conditioning Exercises**  
- Differ from race kinetically and dynamically
- Moderate to high intensity OK
- Examples: Kick sets, pull sets, IM swimming

**BTE - Basic Technique Exercises**
- Partially similar to race stroke
- Address basic conditioning properties
- Examples: Stroke drills, build sets, underwater work

**BCONDE - Basic Conditioning Exercises**
- Stroke-specific
- Often progressive (easy → hard within set)
- Examples: Descending sets, threshold training in main stroke

**STE - Specific Technique Exercises**  
- Imitate competition movement precisely
- Automate technique under difficult conditions
- Examples: Race-pace technique, breathing pattern work, tempo sets

**SCONDE - Specific Conditioning Exercises**
- Very closely resemble competition kinetically and dynamically
- Psychologically and physically demanding
- Examples: Broken swims, race-pace intervals, lactate tolerance sets

**COMPE - Complex Exercises**
- Same as race but MORE difficult
- Examples: Overspeed training, altitude simulation, broken swims faster than race pace

### Dosing: Frequency & Volume

**Not just "what" but "how much per week":**

**Aerobic capacity training:**
- Base period: 85-95% of weekly volume
- Competition period: 60-75% of weekly volume
- Frequency: 5-6 days/week

**Anaerobic capacity training:**
- Base period: 2-3 blocks/week (preserve during volume phase)
- Competition period (sprinters): 3-5 blocks/week
- Competition period (distance): 1-2 blocks/week

**Aerobic power training:**
- Not needed for 100-200m races
- Essential for 200m+ races
- Frequency: 2-3x/week for 4-6 weeks (distance swimmers)

**Anaerobic power training:**
- Competition period only
- Frequency: 2-4x/week for sprinters
- Duration: 2-3 weeks maximum without aerobic refresh

## Principle 5: Durability of Development (Long-Term Build-Up)

### Core Truths

1. **Only continuous, consistent training over years leads to optimal development**
2. **Fast development = temporary and unstable conditioning**
3. **Long interruptions = deterioration of acquired adaptations**

### Conditioning Deterioration Rates (from fastest to slowest)

1. Aerobic power (loses fastest)
2. Aerobic capacity
3. Strength  
4. Speed
5. Technique (loses slowest)

**Practical:** Aerobic power must be maintained during competition season or it vanishes quickly.

### Retraining Speed (how fast fitness returns after break)

**Fastest recovery:**
- Elite athletes (best conditioning stability)
- Young swimmers  
- Athletes with long, consistent training history before interruption

**Slowest recovery:**
- Inconsistent trainers
- Older masters swimmers
- Athletes who peaked quickly (unstable conditioning base)

### Maintenance Training Requirements

**When emphasizing one conditioning component during a mesocycle, other components need maintenance training.**

**Example:** During 6-week aerobic power mesocycle (distance swimmers):
- Maintain anaerobic capacity: 1-2 short blocks/week
- Maintain sprint speed: 3-6 x 25m fast @ 2-3 workouts/week
- Maintain strength: 1-2 dryland sessions/week

**Why:** Without maintenance, other qualities deteriorate while you build the focus quality.

### Multi-Year Perspective

**Year 1-2 (Age Group):** Build general conditioning base, technique foundation. Don't chase times.

**Year 3-4 (Build-Up):** Increase swim-specific training. More structure. Begin periodization.

**Year 5+ (Top-Level):** High specificity. Advanced periodization. Maximal performance.

**Key:** Each year builds on previous years. Shortcuts create ceilings later.

## Practical Implementation Checklist

### Designing a Mesocycle (4-6 weeks):

**Step 1:** Which principle matters most this phase?
- Base period → Principle 2 (progressive load)
- Competition period → Principle 4 (optimal timing)
- Recovery week → Principle 1 (super-compensation)

**Step 2:** Plan intensity/volume distribution
- What % aerobic vs. anaerobic?
- How many intensive blocks/week?
- Volume increase or decrease?

**Step 3:** Sequence exercises properly  
- General → specific
- Capacity → power
- Easy → hard

**Step 4:** Build in variation
- Change sets weekly
- Rotate stroke focus
- Vary training methods

**Step 5:** Plan recovery
- Which days light/heavy?
- Model A or Model C super-compensation?
- Recovery week needed?

## Common Violations & Consequences

| Violation | Consequence |
|-----------|-------------|
| Train hard every day (Principle 1) | Chronic fatigue, no super-compensation, stagnation |
| Never increase load (Principle 2) | Plateau after 6 weeks |
| Same workout every week (Principle 3) | Rapid stagnation, boredom |
| Power before capacity (Principle 4) | Unstable fitness, injury risk |
| Skip base training phase (Principle 5) | Fast gains, rapid deterioration, short career |
