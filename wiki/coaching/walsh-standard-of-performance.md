# Bill Walsh: Standard of Performance for Swim Coaches
**Source:** *The Score Takes Care of Itself* by Bill Walsh (2009) — NFL Leadership Philosophy Applied to Swimming

---

## Core Philosophy

> "If you aim for perfection and miss, you're still pretty good. But if you aim for mediocre and miss? Well..."  
> — Bill Walsh (via Joe Montana)

**The Central Insight:** Walsh's 49ers went 2-14 his first season. But he ignored the scoreboard and focused on creating a **Standard of Performance**—detailed expectations for how every person in the organization would behave, prepare, and execute.

Result: 5 Super Bowl championships in 14 years.

**For swim coaches:** Stop obsessing over meet results. Build a culture where excellence in preparation, technique, and attitude is the standard. The times will follow.

---

## Walsh's Standard of Performance Framework

### 1. **The Prime Directive Was NOT Victory**

Walsh's #1 priority wasn't winning—it was **teaching perfect execution** in every aspect of the program.

**What he measured instead of wins:**
- Precision in fundamentals
- Consistency in effort
- Attention to detail
- Professional behavior
- Relentless preparation

**Swim coaching translation:**
- Don't make "podium finishes" your primary goal
- Measure stroke count, underwater distance, wall times, practice pace execution
- Track attendance, on-time arrival, equipment preparedness
- Judge meets by race execution quality, not just results
- Celebrate perfect turns, breakouts, finishes—not just PRs

**Actionable:**  
Create a **weekly scorecard** that tracks:
- % of sets completed at prescribed pace
- Average stroke count on technique sets
- # of perfect walls (no breathing into/out, tight streamline)
- Attendance & punctuality rate
- Effort rating (1-10 self-assessment after each practice)

### 2. **Winners Act Like Winners (Before They're Winners)**

Walsh inherited the worst team in the NFL. First thing he changed? **How people dressed, walked, and carried themselves.**

**His exact standards:**
- Coaches wore ties on road trips
- No profanity in meetings
- Players arrived early, stayed late
- Locker room was clean and organized
- Everyone took notes during film sessions

**Why it mattered:** "You can't behave like a loser and expect to win. The way you act becomes the way you think."

**Swim coaching translation:**
- Swimmers bring working equipment (no broken goggles, missing fins)
- Caps on, goggles up when coach is talking
- Vertical push-offs, not lazy wall touches
- Every interval starts from a streamlined push, not a glide
- Champ swimmers write workouts in log books immediately after practice

**Story from Walsh:** He held a "bellhop stunt" where coaches dressed ridiculously to emphasize the dress code. Made a serious point with humor. Consider: Use humor to enforce standards without being a drill sergeant.

### 3. **Teach Them to Hate Mistakes**

Walsh (via Montana): "He taught us to hate mistakes. You didn't want to see any balls on the ground, no fumbles, no turnovers."

**How he did it:** Without screaming. By making precision the norm.

> "Bill didn't jump on you for a mistake; he came right in with the correction: 'Here's what was wrong; this is how to do it right.' Over and over, without getting all upset, he taught the smallest details of perfecting performance."  
> — Joe Montana

**Swim coaching application:**

**Bad approach:**  
"That turn sucked! Do it again!"

**Walsh approach:**  
"Your feet hit 6 inches too high. Plant them at the T, knees at 90°, hands shoot over your head before you twist. Let's do 5 perfect ones right now."

**The Standard:**
- No breathing into walls on freestyle (ever)
- No breathing out of walls for 3 strokes (butterfly/breast)
- Underwaters hit the 15m mark minimum (or 5-7-9 depending on age)
- Streamlines are TIGHT—hands locked, biceps squeezed to ears, toes pointed
- Interval times hit within :02 of target

**Key distinction:** Walsh didn't tolerate sloppiness, but he also didn't berate. He **taught** the correct way relentlessly until it became automatic.

### 4. **Establish Specific, Measureable Standards**

Walsh created detailed checklists for every role—quarterback, lineman, receiver, equipment manager.

**Sample swim coaching standards (adapt to your team):**

**Freestyle Standards:**
- Breathing: One-goggle breathing (never lift entire face)
- Stroke count: <16 per 25y (elite), <18 (competitive), <20 (age group)
- Kick: 6-beat kick from hips, not knees
- Finish: Accelerate final 3 strokes into wall

**Practice Standards:**
- Show up 10 minutes early
- Enter pool within 90 seconds of posted start time
- Equipment ready (fins, paddles, snorkel, kickboard in reach)
- Log workouts within 24 hours
- Communicate absences 24hrs in advance (illness excepted)

**Butterfly Standards (example):**
- Lead with head, not hands
- Chest presses, hips rise (no "porpoise")
- Hands enter shoulder-width apart, thumbs down
- Kick occurs when hands pass hips

**Make your standards visible:** Post them in the locker room. Print them on practice plans. Reference them daily.

## The 2-14 Season Lesson

**Context:** Walsh's first season with the 49ers was a disaster. 2 wins, 14 losses. Media crucified him.

**What Walsh did:**
- Ignored the scoreboard
- Focused on "becoming a one-point underdog" (competitive in every aspect)
- Measured progress by **execution quality**, not outcomes

**Quote:**
> "The score takes care of itself. Do all the right things to precision and the score will take care of itself."

**Swim coaching parallel:**

Your team goes to a big invite and gets crushed. Zero finals swims.

**Wrong response:** "We're not good enough. We need to train harder."

**Walsh response:**  
- Review film: Were turns executed to standard?
- Check pacing: Did swimmers hit their race strategy?
- Evaluate warm-up: Did they follow the pre-race routine?
- Assess effort: Did they finish each race (no coasting final 10m)?

If they executed the **process** correctly and still lost, that's data. Adjust training. If they executed poorly, that's a teaching opportunity.

**The insight:** You can't control outcomes (other teams, judges, timing systems). You CAN control preparation, technique, and effort. Control the controllables.

## Building Your Own Standard of Performance

**Walsh's method:**

1. **Define your ideal.** What does perfect preparation look like? Perfect technique? Perfect racing?
2. **Write it down.** Specificity matters. "Swim fast" is not a standard. "15m underwater off every wall" is.
3. **Teach it relentlessly.** Daily reminders. Daily corrections. Daily reinforcement.
4. **Model it yourself.** Show up early. Be organized. Be prepared. Your behavior sets the tone.
5. **Measure it.** You can't improve what you don't track.
6. **Enforce it without excuses.** Standards with exceptions aren't standards.

**Sample coach checklist (Walsh-style):**
- [ ] Practice plan written & posted 24hrs before practice
- [ ] Arrive 15 min before swimmers
- [ ] Pace clock synced, equipment staged
- [ ] Warm-up includes mobility work (not just swimming)
- [ ] At least one teaching moment per set (stroke correction, race tactic)
- [ ] Cool-down enforced (no early exits)
- [ ] Post-practice debrief with assistants (what worked, what didn't)

## Walsh's 12 Leadership Habits (Applied to Coaching)

From *The Score Takes Care of Itself*, Chapter: "Be a Leader—Twelve Habits Plus One"

1. **Be Yourself** — Don't try to be Bob Bowman or Gregg Troy. Find your authentic coaching voice.
2. **Be Committed to Excellence** — Mediocrity is a choice. Choose precision.
3. **Be Positive** — Contagious energy > constant criticism.
4. **Be Prepared** — Script your practices. Know your season plan. Anticipate problems.
5. **Be Detail-Oriented** — The smallest things (goggle straps, hydration, sleep) matter.
6. **Be Organized** — Chaotic practices produce chaotic swimmers.
7. **Be Accountable** — When the team fails, look in the mirror first.
8. **Be Near the Top** — Study the best. Learn from Olympic coaches. Read, watch, grow.
9. **Be Clear** — Ambiguity breeds confusion. Say exactly what you mean.
10. **Be Firm** — Compassion without standards is enabling.
11. **Be Flexible** — Dogma kills. Adapt to your athletes.
12. **Believe in Yourself** — Your confidence (or lack of it) is contagious.

**Plus One: Be a Teacher.** Walsh believed teaching was the top priority (see [Teaching Over Winning](#related-pages)).

## Real-World Application: The First 90 Days

**Scenario:** You take over a struggling program. Results have been poor. Culture is weak.

**What NOT to do (most coaches):**
- Ramp up volume immediately
- Scream about effort
- Focus on winning the next meet

**The Walsh Way:**

**Week 1-2: Establish Standards**
- Define non-negotiables (attendance, equipment, behavior)
- Teach ONE technique focus (e.g., perfect streamlines)
- Measure baseline: stroke counts, turn times, underwater distances

**Week 3-6: Relentless Teaching**
- Every practice includes stroke correction time
- Video swimmers, show them the gap between current & ideal
- Celebrate small wins ("Every turn this week hit 15m underwater!")

**Week 7-12: Measure Progress**
- Re-test baselines
- Ignore meet results; track execution metrics
- Reinforce: "We're getting better every day."

**Result (Walsh's 49ers timeline):**
- Year 1: 2-14 (disaster)
- Year 2: 6-10 (progress)
- Year 3: 13-3, Super Bowl Champions

**Patience + Standards = Dynasty.**

## Key Takeaways

✅ **Process > Outcome** — Control what you can control (preparation, technique, effort)

✅ **Culture First** — How you do anything is how you do everything

✅ **Teach, Don't Yell** — Precision requires instruction, not intimidation

✅ **Measure What Matters** — Stroke counts, turn times, underwater distances > meet results

✅ **Be Specific** — "Swim better" is useless. "Hit 7 dolphin kicks off every wall" is actionable.

✅ **Model the Standard** — Your professionalism sets the tone
