# Dave Salo: The Freedom of Being an Assistant Coach

**Category:** Coaching Philosophy, Career Transition, Assistant Coaching, Mentorship  
**Coach:** Dave Salo  
**Source:** SwimSwam Podcast - Why is Dave Salo an Assistant Coach at Orange Coast College

## Summary

After decades as a head coach at elite programs, Dave Salo made the intentional choice to become an assistant coach at Orange Coast College under his former swimmer Anthony Eddie. This interview reveals his philosophy on assistant coaching, the freedom it provides, and how veteran coaches can serve without ego while maintaining high standards.

---

## Why Become an Assistant?

### The Dream

> "It's always been kind of in the back of my mind—a dream of being an assistant coach for a change. My entire career except for the five years I coached with Peter Daland, I've been a head coach. Head coach responsibilities as you know are pretty enormous, and it was kind of cool to think about the idea of just being somebody's assistant."

**The Vision:**
- Come on deck
- Sink the clocks
- Fix the lane lines ("which I'm really good at—tight lane lines")
- Be there with experiential resources
- If you just need to get your watch out, that's good too

**Common Desire:**
> "There's a lot of us as retired or semi-retired head coaches [who] look forward to not having as much of the head coaching responsibilities."

### The Plan with Anthony Eddie

**John Urbanchek Parallel:**
- Swam for John at Long Beach State when John got into college coaching
- John is long-time friend
- When John retired from Michigan, moving back to Fullerton (close to USC)
- Asked John to be volunteer assistant at USC
- "Great to have John on the deck as a friend, as a mentor"

**Promise to Anthony:**
> "I told Anthony that at the point at which I would retire, I would certainly come back, and if he would have me, I'd be his volunteer assistant, which is what I suggested."

**No More Volunteers:**
- They don't accept volunteers anymore
- Only take paid positions
- "I said okay, pay me—that's good"

### Why Orange Coast College

**Perfect Fit:**
- Great beautiful new facility
- Anthony swam for Dave, similar ideas about coaching
- "I'm just there to help him out—young coach, good coach, very successful"
- "I thought I could be of service"

## Assistant Coach Responsibilities

### Not What You'd Expect

**Interviewer:** "What other kind of assistant responsibilities do you have to step back into?"

**Dave's Response:**
> "Anthony's never had really dedicated assistant coaches that are just swimming assistant coaches. I'm just trying to help him be better, help him take some things off his plate."

**Family First:**
- Wife expecting second child
- "Really important thing for him and his family"
- Always tried to create environment for assistants to be good family people, not just coaches
- "I don't have a wife or kids or dog or anything, so where I can pitch in to help—be a little bit easier for him"

**Specific Examples:**
- Can take care of practice when Anthony needs to get home to wife
- Taking son out to aquarium
- Anthony doesn't have to worry about getting to deck
- Can trust Dave because of coaching experience

### Adaptability

> "I'm tremendously adaptable, whatever the circumstances are with the athletes."

**Community College Spectrum:**
- Kids who barely can swim
- To bounce-backs from Division I
- "I can adapt to either end of the spectrum and probably be pretty effective"

**Relaxing:**
> "It's extremely relaxing. I love to coach, I love to create training design and implement my ideas about the methodology. It's just fun to be able to watch these kids get excited about training."

**Shared Methodology:**
> "Both Anthony and I employ the same kind of methodology for these community college kids—come in and have an experience that's really really positive."

## What Dave Doesn't Miss

### NCAA Division I Recruiting

> "The one thing I don't miss from NCAA Division I swimming is the landscape of recruiting and that process."

**Used to Love It:**
- Calling kids
- Talking about program
- Getting to know them

**Became an Endeavor:**
- Have to recruit juniors
- Have to recruit seniors
- Transfer portal—don't have to sit out
- Got to keep recruiting your own kids

**The Fear:**
> "We've all been in that position where you've had a kid that's come into your program, has done well, and they walk in your door and say 'well I'm transferring.' It's like WHAT?! You're just kind of always on edge about that whole thing. I don't miss that."

### Future Recruiting at OCC

**Top-Notch Athletes:**
- Urging Anthony to push towards recruiting really top-notch athletes
- California community college rules allow it
- Also allowed to consider international athletes

**Unique Opportunity:**
- Beautiful facility
- Dave's experiences
- Will intrigue international athletes
- "Great training environment—that's what we can provide at Orange Coast College"

## What Coaches Can Prove at This Stage

### Not About Proving Anything

> "I'm not trying to prove anything. I've had a great career, and I could—I don't need to do this. It's great to be at this point in time where you're financially fine, professionally you've had an exceptional career. Hall of fame coach, Olympic coach, and all that stuff. Now it's just being able to give back as much as I can."

### Role as Sounding Board

**Anthony is Bright:**
- Knows what he wants and how he wants to do it
- Bounces things off Dave
- "We have great conversations about swimming, great conversations about Elon Musk and his principles"
- "Really a bright kid—I shouldn't say kid, he's 40 years old"

**Just There:**
> "I'm just there to allow him—it's I go back to when I was coaching at USC."

### USC Philosophy on Assistant Coaches

**After First Two Years:**
> "I was so focused on the athletes and trying to take care of my coaches, and I got really uncomfortable about just what I was doing."

**The Talk:**
> "I said look, I have to coach the way I coach. I can't coach how you want to coach or what your needs are. I can't be distracted by what your needs are as coaches. You just have to know that I trust you, that I'm going to give you a lot of responsibility."

**Not Writing Everything:**
> "I'm not going to write every single workout you've got to do verbatim. You're going to get your hands in the mix. But I'm going to lead the troops, I'm going to run it the way I'm comfortable with. You just have to fall in line and figure it out."

**Be Prepared:**
> "If I say 'okay, in the next five minutes you're taking the sprinters, go do something with them, make them faster,' then they just have to trust that's how I operate. That's how I operate as a coach. I'm better that way."

**For Anthony:**
> "I'm there to help Anthony just be Anthony and to be able to say 'hey Dave, take the next set,' it's like no problem, I can take it. I don't have to ask him."

**Anthony's Comment:**
> "We got to start using that [new equipment], otherwise it was a waste of money to buy that equipment."

**Dave's Response:**
> "I took that as a directive to make sure okay, the next set we do, we're going to use that piece of equipment because I want there to be value to the equipment they purchased."

### Lane Line Example

**Anthony's Comment:**
> "Tomorrow I'm gonna get to that [broken lane line]."

**Dave's Action:**
> "I took it upon myself to take care of that day, so he didn't have to worry about it. He could come back and go 'oh good, I don't have to worry about that lane line.'"

**Philosophy:**
> "I'm really good at listening to what a coach says."

## Freedom of Assistant Coaching

### Ask Other Veterans

> "If you ask any of us that have gotten back into an assistance role after so many years of being head coaches—Dennis Dale comes to mind when he left Minnesota, went to Indiana, or Mark Bernardino who was at Virginia for a long time and he's still coaching as an assistant coach—you'll ask any of us:"

**The Answer:**
> "It's freeing. It's tremendous freedom to be able to just coach—not worry about recruiting to the extent that you do as a head coach, or giving up scholarships, or doing the compliance paperwork, or all the nonsense that goes along with especially college coaching."

### At Nova Too

**Same Approach:**
> "I do that with my club team too at Nova. I'm there to allow my coaching staff to focus more on the coaching."

**Parental Influences:**
> "When they have issues with parental influences and stuff like that, I can take the lead on that and put parents back where they need to be, and let the coaches coach—not be frustrated or worried about some parent peeking over their shoulder or whatever."

### Skill Set for Anthony

**At Orange Coast College:**
> "Let him just coach and do what he needs to do. Be there for his backup. Be there if he can't make practice because his wife's gonna have a baby, or just gotta go watch his little boy do T-ball. That's how my role is. I'm not trying to prove anything—just help Anthony continue the successes that he's developed at Orange Coast College."

## The Best Interview Answers

### Authentic, Not a Stepping Stone

**Interviewer's Observation:**
> "It sounds like all the best interview questions—or interview answers I should say—because that's exactly what I would hope young coaches would be able to understand as they step into assistant roles. They're just always so hungry to step up and do more and stuff that it's really hard for them to be good assistants, to be satisfied with where they're at."

**Industry Push:**
> "Especially with our industry, they're constantly being pushed: push push, you gotta get next, gotta be stepping next."

**Fortunate for Anthony:**
> "It's very fortunate to have a coach who: A) knows exactly how to step in and be a head coach if you need him in a situation where you're gonna have two kids—he's gonna need you, Dave. And B) this guy's not coming for my job. This guy's not in it for any of this. He appreciates the lack of responsibility as opposed to envies the position in that way."

**Why It Works:**
> "It makes the best interview answers because it's genuine and it's authentic. It's pretty cool to hear from a top-level guy who has all of the results you could possibly ask for—all the checkmarks on the resume, it's all happened—for you to be talking about the root role of it is cool."

## Creating Sandboxes

### For Coaches at Nova

> "I'm not a micromanager. I don't want to stand over them and say 'well what is that, why not this, why?' I just want to make sure they have their room."

**Communication:**
> "I would love to have continuous communication to some degree with them to make sure that if they run into problems, they have it."

### For Athletes

**The Reputation:**
> "You write the workout and then you let the athlete do it. You're there on deck and engaging, but it's not like you're constantly talking to them or talking them through it. It's much more like: here it is, here's the sandbox, now do what you can with it. Not because you're not hearing—I hear you're hearing and observing—but you're taking out your control of the entire pool so the kids can get some autonomy, the young adults and adults, the pros that you coach can get some autonomy over what they're doing."

**Dave's Response:**
> "I don't know if that's true of me actually. I do give my athletes a lot of choice in terms of the training, but I'm adamant about detail. Detail has to be done exactly the way I envision things."

---

## Detail-Oriented Coaching

### Choice Within Structure

> "Yeah, they get some room for choice."

**Never Writing Workouts in Advance:**
> "I don't write my workouts in advance of a workout. In particular with the group I'm coaching now, I'm never quite sure who's going to be in practice. To design a workout based on who may or may not come to practice doesn't make sense—it's not very fruitful."

### Driven by Stimuli

> "I'm very much driven by all kinds of different stimuli that's going to help create an environment where we can be really effective, really fast, really efficient."

**What Impacts Him:**
- What do kids look like when they get through warmup?
- Are they in good moods or not in very good moods?
- The weather—sometimes will dictate what a workout might look like
- Not worried about what tomorrow's practice is
- Really don't care about what yesterday's practice is

### Famous for Not Telling Reps

> "I'm famously known for not telling the kids how many repetitions of a set that we're doing. When they ask how many we're doing, I always tell them: you can't do anything about number 22, so don't worry about how many we're doing. Just work, focus on the one that we're on."

**They Get Used to It:**
> "Sometimes they've gotten used to that: 'he's not going to tell us how many we're doing.'"

**The Reason:**
> "I purposely don't do that. Not every set, but there are a number of sets that I don't have a certain number of repetitions that I want done because I need to see how this evolves—how the set evolves—and get a sense that we're getting where I want it to be."

**The Crescendo:**
> "Then there's kind of a crescendo of how a set is running to the point where: okay, now they're becoming disinterested or they're not putting in the same effort. Now it's time to pull back and do something else."

### Moment-by-Moment

> "I'm really reflective of how my athletes are performing on a moment-by-moment basis. Not even a macro cycle or a mini cycle. It's just day-to-day. It's minute to minute."

---

## Constant Communication

### Always Talking

> "If anybody watches me work on deck, they'll find that I'm constantly talking, constantly making communication with the athletes."

**General Pronouncements:**
> "A lot of times it's kind of a general pronunciation of what we're trying to do, just to keep reminding the kids what we're after. Sometimes it's individualized."

**Fear of Silence:**
> "I've always felt ill at ease if I'm not constantly coaching because I think it almost feels like I'm afraid that the kids will think I don't care because I'm not saying something or chirping out something or saying some profound statement about this set and how important it is."

**Responsive:**
> "I'm really responsive to my athletes on a moment-by-moment basis."

### The Painting Metaphor

> "I've got very certain elements of my training that I want to accomplish in every given workout. I look at it as I'm trying to put together a painting, and all these components of this painting have to be put in there."

**Components:**
- Power
- Strength
- Flexibility
- Mechanics
- All these different components go into performance

**Implementation:**
> "I'm trying to implement them all into my painting. Everything—one set leads to the next set leads to the next set leads to the next set. But like I said before, I don't plan that out way in advance."

### Each Workout Is Its Own Entity

> "I just look at each workout is in itself its own entity. It's cumulative in the sense that you've got workouts going from day one to day 144. But I look at it: put yesterday aside, today is different. It's going to be just as hard as yesterday and tomorrow will feel like we never get a break."

**The Truth:**
> "It's like no, your body's tremendously adaptable and plastic, and it's got some capacities that we just need to reach towards."

### Characterization

> "In characterizing me, I'm constantly yapping, talking, whistling, giving feedback. I think some of my statements are really profound, although I'm not sure they think it's profound. Sometimes they speak in different languages because I know how to say 'fast' in a bunch of different languages."

## No Metrics Tracking

### Why Not

> "I don't track anything. I've seen over the years too many coaches who get caught up in tracking whatever metrics they're looking at or looking at a particular set and determining what kind of success they're going to have based on that, and it's so unpredictable."

**Lactate Example:**
> "We went for a number of years where coaches were monitoring lactate, and all of a sudden the lactates weren't going the direction they wanted them to or expected them to, and they'd start freaking out and think their season's lost."

**His Approach:**
> "I don't do that. I just expect my athletes to come in every day with the intent to work really hard and swim fast every day, and not worry about the metrics per se."

**The Reality:**
> "If you're doing the work every day, you're working really hard, you're going to get in shape. It just comes with working hard. So I don't focus too much attention on that. I've used some metrics in the past but I've kind of gotten away from it because things will plateau out and then you're going 'oh no! Uh-oh!'"

### Figure It Out (FIO)

**Terry McKeever's Phrase:**
> "I borrow a phrase that probably Terry McKeever uses a lot and I use a lot as well with my athletes."

**When They Ask Questions:**
- "Watch my stroke rate or whatever"
- "FIO. FIO. You have to figure it out."

**Simple Metrics:**
> "You got to figure it out. You're trying to get from A to B and you're trying to get there fast. Count your strokes—it's real simple. Count your strokes. If you're going faster and you're getting less strokes, that's more efficiency."

**Result:**
> "A lot of times there's a lot of FIOs. I know Terry does that with her kids all the time too—figure it out."

## Detail-Oriented Yet Autonomous

### The Paradox

> "A lot of my athletes will tell you that I'm very detail-oriented and have high expectations. At the same time, sometimes they characterize me as 'he lets us do whatever we want.'"

**The Truth:**
> "It's not that simple. It's yeah, you can do whatever you want as long as you do what I want you to do."

### Discovery in Workouts

> "There's a lot of discovery that goes on in my workouts too, because sometimes I have an intent of what I think something needs to be, and they will misread my intent. I will learn from that misinformation—they kind of take it and run with it."

**The Learning:**
> "I kind of use that as a vehicle to: 'oh well, I didn't really think that, but I didn't communicate what I wanted. But they showed something to me that might be a little bit different.'"

## Player's Coach?

### Pete Carroll Comparison

**Interviewer:** "Would you consider yourself a player's coach? I don't want to go Pete Carroll with the USC connection..."

**Dave's Response:**
> "I'm not sure I would characterize myself. Pete Carroll is awesome, and I loved watching Pete Carroll. I wish I had his—there's a lot of characteristics that I like about Pete."

### John Urbanchek Parallel

> "He's a lot like Urbanchek. Urbanchek might be considered a 'player's coach' kind of guy. He gets away with so much because he's got that stupid Hungarian accent of his."

**The Accent:**
- Been here 60 years, still has an accent
- Playing it up
- "One of the most endearing qualities among the many about him"
- At home talking to wife Mel, speaks perfect English
- Around everybody else: Hungarian accent

**We Need a Recording:**
> "We've got to get a recording of John's American accent."

### "I Hate Them All"

> "I used to tell people 'do you like so-and-so better?' I said no, I don't like anybody, I hate everybody. It's just the same. But that's not true."

**In Club Coaching:**
> "You play favorites, I don't play favorites. I hate them all. If they're all hated, then they're fine."

**Evolved Thinking:**
> "I went through phases of that. I went through the coach phase where you were supposed to play favorites—that was like how you divvied out your currency, your attention. If you gave it, the only other option is that you just get angry at all the kids who are not doing it right and not there. Yeah, I do have favorites: the kids who are coming every day. Then I realized that's still not quite enough."

## Community College Unique Characteristics

### Broad Skill Range

> "The skill set of these kids is a really broad range. Water polo kids have just finished their season, now they're going into swim season. You just got to be a lot more aware that their skill sets are so different."

**Unique to Community College:**
> "Community college has its own kind of characteristics that are kind of unique. You got to be careful with these kids a lot because they do this purely out of the joy of the sport."

**No Scholarship:**
- Not on scholarship
- Can do it or not do it
- They choose to be there

### Flexibility Required

**One Thing to Learn:**
> "There's a lot more class conflict and they've got jobs that they've got to go to, so their attendance is sometimes scattered. I want people at practice, and I want them in on time—those kind of things that are really important to me. But you've got to be a lot more flexible with the community college kids."

**Can Still Ask the Same:**
> "But I think you can still ask of them the same thing."

### Hit the Gas

**Conversation with Anthony:**
> "I told him a couple weeks ago, because we have such an abbreviated season: 'Look, you're being a little too cautious. I think these kids can handle a lot more than you're giving them. I think you just said we don't have much time—so let's just kind of get after it.'"

**Anthony's Approach:**
> "He's a seasoned veteran, so he's been trying to be very progressive, and I'm like 'just do it, let's go. We don't have time to wait, we've only got five weeks left—hit the gas!'"

**Dave's Adjustment:**
> "I gotta kind of roll that back a little bit. Somebody's a little out of breath when they're sitting on the wall, I gotta kind of ignore that a little bit because I'm like 'come on!' That's my tendency, so I gotta make sure: 'are you okay? Is everything all right?'"

### Why They're There

> "It's a different group. But they're there because they want to—they want to swim. No other, there's nothing else driving them. It's a really interesting dynamic."

## Key Lessons for Coaches

### How to Be a Great Assistant
- **Check your ego:** Not about proving anything anymore
- **Listen actively:** Anthony's comments about equipment or lane lines become directives
- **Adapt fully:** Support head coach's vision, not impose your own
- **Add value quietly:** Fix problems before head coach has to think about them
- **Trust the process:** Young coach knows what he wants, just needs support
- **Family first:** Cover practice so head coach can be with family
- **Experience without ego:** Offer insights when asked, don't impose

### Freedom of Letting Go
- **Head coaching burden:** Recruiting, compliance, scholarships, transfer portal
- **Assistant freedom:** Just coach, just teach, just be on deck
- **Veteran perspective:** After decades at top, service without ego is fulfilling
- **The dream realized:** "Always been in the back of my mind"

### Detail vs. Autonomy Balance
- **Detail-oriented:** Expects precise execution of vision
- **Room for choice:** Athletes make decisions within structure
- **Moment-by-moment:** Responds to what's happening now, not yesterday
- **Discovery learning:** When athletes misread, learn from what they show you
- **FIO (Figure It Out):** Simple metrics (stroke count + speed) beat complex tracking

### Community College Differences
- **Broad skill range:** Water polo players to D1 transfers
- **Flexibility required:** Jobs, class conflicts, scattered attendance
- **Pure joy:** No scholarships, they're there because they want to be
- **Abbreviated seasons:** Hit the gas, don't be too cautious
- **Different dynamics:** Can still ask for high standards with flexible approach

### Painting the Workout
- **Don't plan ahead:** Respond to who shows up and how they look
- **Each workout standalone:** Today is different from yesterday
- **Multiple components:** Power, strength, flexibility, mechanics all in one session
- **Crescendo awareness:** Know when set is reaching peak, when to pull back
- **Constant communication:** Always coaching, always talking, never silent

### No Metrics Necessary
- **Work hard every day:** That's the metric that matters
- **Plateau fear:** Tracking numbers can create anxiety when they plateau
- **Body adapts:** If doing the work, getting in shape will follow
- **Simple over complex:** Count strokes, compare to speed
- **Trust the process:** 40+ years of experience says this works
