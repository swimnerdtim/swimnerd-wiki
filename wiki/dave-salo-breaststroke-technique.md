# Dave Salo: Breaststroke Technique and Coaching Philosophy

**Category:** Breaststroke, Technique, Coaching Philosophy, Stroke Mechanics  
**Coach:** Dave Salo - "The Breaststroke Coach"  
**Athletes Coached:** Rebecca Soni, Jessica Hardy, Amanda Beard, Staciana Stitts, Eric Shanteau, Kosuke Kitajima, Yulia Efimova, and many others

## Summary

Dave Salo is widely considered the greatest breaststroke coach of all time. His success stems from being a former breaststroker himself, giving him an intimate feel for the stroke's timing and nuances. While he recognizes that elite breaststrokers all look different, Salo focuses on key technical components that apply across all swimmers while respecting individual variation based on flexibility, body type, and feel.

## The Timing Problem

### The Feel Issue

Salo understands something unique to breaststroke:

> *"I tell breaststrokers or coaches - you've all had breaststrokers come into practice and they're complaining that stroke doesn't feel right. It doesn't feel good. 'I don't know what happened, it felt great yesterday.' I remember what that's like because you come in and all of a sudden your stroke doesn't feel right, it feels awkward. Because I experienced that, I know how to get the kids back into the stroke that's right for them because it's a TIMING issue."*

**Key Insight:** Breaststroke is the most timing-dependent stroke, and "feel" can disappear suddenly

## Key Technical Components

### 1. The Line at Extension

**Most Important Check:**  
What does the body look like at completion of extension?

**Goal:**  
A perfect line from fingertips through head down to feet

- Fingertips → Head → Feet alignment
- No breaks in the line
- Everything streamlined
- This is the starting point for analysis

---

### 2. Recovery Path

**Salo's Philosophy:**  
"I don't believe in recovering over the surface of the water"

**Correct Recovery:**
- Turn hands and elbows into extension
- Elbows rotate (don't point downward)
- Hands travel in line with elbows inside
- Recover underwater, not over

**What to Avoid:**
- Elbows pointed downward
- Recovering over water surface
- Hands coming out of water
- Creating drag during recovery

**Drag Check:**  
If you're creating drag between fingertips and shoulders during recovery, you must eliminate it

---

### 3. Elbow Position on Outsweep

**Critical Element:**  
Elbow position determines effectiveness

**Path of Hands:**
- Inside the path of elbows
- Elbows stay high/out
- Maintains pressure on water

**Common Error:**  
Elbows dropping, losing leverage

---

### 4. Head Position

**Alignment:**
- In line with back
- In line with shoulders
- NOT forced one way or another
- Shoulders drive movement forward, not head

**What to Avoid:**  
"Head thing" - head-driven stroke mechanics  
Salo doesn't like this, tries to tone it down even in athletes who naturally do it

---

### 5. The Kick

#### Evolution of Technique

**Old School (when Salo was young):**  
"Up, Out, and Together"

**Modern Technique:**  
Straighter path, more direct

#### Knee-Heel Relationship

**Key Points:**
- Relationship between knees and heels matters
- Heels should be outside knees (even if abbreviated)
- Ankle flexibility crucial
- Hip flexibility matters
- Knee position determines effectiveness

## The Kitajima Analysis: Mastering "The Line"

### Kosuke Kitajima - The Gold Standard

**Background:**  
Salo coached Kitajima into retirement, got to study him extensively

**Physical Profile:**
- No more than 5'7"
- Maybe 150 pounds
- "Tremendous speed" despite small stature

**What Made Him Special:**  
THE LINE

### Downward Deflection Hierarchy

Salo identified a spectrum from beginner to elite based on how swimmers achieve streamline:

**ELITE (Kitajima):**
- Slight downward deflection of kick as extending
- Almost seamless transition to line
- "Just kind of goes here and then it's lined up"
- Everyone talked about toes finishing together
- Reality: Perfect alignment through entire body

**ADVANCED (Eric Shanteau):**
- Good at achieving the line
- "Little bit of a jolt into that positioning"
- Still very effective, minor disruption

**JUNIOR NATIONAL:**
- Downward deflection
- Then very dramatic lift into line
- Noticeable break in flow

**BEGINNER:**
- Down, down, down
- Maybe they come up to line
- Constant deflection
- Never achieves smooth alignment

### The Kitajima Secret

**Heel Speed + Hand Speed:**  
Not just about moving fast

**REAL SECRET:**  
Getting alignment from feet through legs seamlessly

**Timing:**  
Shoulders through head into recovery - everything lined up perfectly

## Individual Variation

### The Rebecca Soni Story

**Early Workout Crisis:**  
Long course, first year together, Salo doing race-pace drill work

**Scene:**
- Rebecca stops 15 meters from wall
- In tears
- Best swimmer on team
- Looks at Salo: *"I hate your drills"*
- Salo panics: "Oh my god, my best swimmer's gonna quit"

**Salo's Response:**

> *"Rebecca, I understand your stroke and I'm not going to have you swim like Amanda or Jessica or Staciana. I'm going to swim you YOUR stroke and I understand YOUR stroke. I'll get better at understanding your stroke. There's some key things to your stroke that I think we can enhance versus take away from. I'm not going to change your stroke. I know breaststroke and you're just gonna have to trust me."*

**Result:** Calmed her down, got through first year, went on to Olympic gold and WR

## The Nuance Factor

**Why Breaststroke Is Different:**

1. **Most complex stroke** - Timing-dependent
2. **Most variable** - Eight finalists, eight different strokes
3. **Most feel-dependent** - Can lose it overnight
4. **Most flexibility-dependent** - Ankles, knees, hips, shoulders all matter
5. **Most individually specific** - Must respect body types

**Salo's Advantage:**  
Having been a breaststroker, he can feel what they're feeling and guide them back

## Drill Work Philosophy

### Race Pace Drills

**Salo's Method:**
- Lot of drill-type work at race pace
- Not slow, technical drilling
- Full intensity with technical focus

**Why It Works:**
- Develops power while refining technique
- Simulates race conditions
- Builds stroke-specific strength
- Maintains timing under pressure

**Caution:**  
This can be emotionally intense (see Rebecca Soni story)  
Requires trust between coach and athlete

## Key Principles Summary

### Non-Negotiables:
1. Line at extension (fingertips to feet)
2. Recovery underwater (elbows rotate, don't point down)
3. Elbow position on outsweep (high and wide)
4. Head alignment (in line with back/shoulders)
5. Heel-knee relationship (heels outside knees)

### Flexible:
1. Amplitude of pull
2. Amplitude of kick
3. Stroke rate
4. Hand/foot speed ratios
5. Surface vs. underwater emphasis
6. Recovery width/pattern
7. Breathing pattern

## Practical Applications

### For Coaches:

**DO:**
- Study key components across all elite breaststrokers
- Recognize individual variation is normal
- Feel what your swimmers are feeling (if possible)
- Understand timing issues destroy feel overnight
- Have fixes for when stroke "doesn't feel right"
- Respect athlete's natural stroke while improving it

**DON'T:**
- Force one "perfect" model on everyone
- Ignore flexibility limitations
- Change a stroke drastically if it's working
- Overlook the importance of "feel"
- Neglect the line at extension

### For Swimmers:

**Focus On:**
- Perfect line at extension every stroke
- Elbow position (high and wide on pull)
- Recovery path (underwater, elbows rotating)
- Heel-knee relationship on kick
- Timing (the most critical element)

**Trust:**
- Your coach when stroke feels "off"
- The process even when drills feel wrong
- Your individual style within technical framework
