# Dave Salo: Coaching Philosophy and Approach

**Category:** Coaching Philosophy, Detail, Flexibility, Communication  
**Coach:** Dave Salo  
**Roles:** USC (retired), Irvine Novaquatics, Orange Coast College (assistant)

## Summary

Dave Salo's coaching philosophy blends rigid detail-orientation with responsive flexibility. He's intensely detail-focused, demanding exact execution, yet reads athletes moment-by-moment and adjusts in real-time. He doesn't pre-write workouts or track metrics, instead creating each practice as its own "painting" based on what athletes bring that day. Communication is constant, expectations are high, and athletes must "figure it out" (FIO) while being guided through every detail.

---

## Core Philosophy

### The Paradox: Detail + Flexibility

**The Stereotype:**  
"Dave writes the workout, athletes do it, he observes but doesn't micromanage"

**The Reality:**  
*"I don't know if that's true of me actually. I'm really really adamant about detail. Detail has to be done exactly the way I envision things."*

**The Balance:**
- **Rigid:** Detail execution must be perfect
- **Flexible:** Workout design adapts moment-by-moment
- **Autonomy:** Athletes get choices within framework
- **Control:** But those choices have parameters

---

## The Painting Analogy

### Creating a Workout

> *"I look at it as I'm trying to put together a painting and all these components of this painting have to be put in there."*

**Components to Include:**
- Power
- Strength
- Flexibility
- Mechanics
- Speed
- Efficiency
- Technique

**Process:**
- One set leads to next
- Everything cumulative
- Each element purposeful
- Creates complete picture

## No Pre-Written Workouts

### Why He Doesn't Plan Ahead

**Practical Reason:**  
*"I'm never quite sure who's going to be in practice, so to design a workout based on who may or may not come to practice doesn't make sense."*

**Philosophical Reason:**  
Driven by multiple real-time stimuli:

1. **How athletes look after warm-up**
2. **Their moods** - Good or bad
3. **The weather** - Sometimes dictates workout
4. **Recent training** - Though he doesn't dwell on it
5. **Where they are in the season**
6. **Energy on deck**

---

## The "Don't Tell Them How Many Reps" Philosophy

### Why He Withholds Information

**Famous for:**  
Not telling athletes how many repetitions they're doing

**When they ask:**  
*"You can't do anything about number 22, so don't worry about how many we're doing. Just work focus on the one that we're on."*

**Purpose:**
- Keep athletes present
- Focus on current rep
- Not mentally checking out
- Not saving energy for "last one"
- Stay engaged throughout

### Evolving Sets

**Sometimes:**  
He genuinely doesn't know how many reps

**Why:**  
*"I need to see how this evolves, how the set evolves, and get a sense that we're getting where I want it to be."*

**The Crescendo:**
- Set builds
- Athletes perform
- Reach peak
- Start becoming disinterested
- Effort drops
- **TIME TO PULL BACK**

## Moment-by-Moment Coaching

### Not Macro or Micro Cycles

> *"I'm really reflective of how my athletes are performing on a moment-by-moment basis. Not even a macro cycle or a mini cycle. It's just day-to-day. It's minute to minute."*

**Constant Monitoring:**
- How they move
- How they look
- Energy levels
- Engagement
- Technical execution
- Effort quality

**Real-Time Adjustments:**
- Sometimes keeps going
- Sometimes cuts it short
- Sometimes changes direction entirely
- Based on what he sees NOW

## Communication Style

### Constantly Talking

**Observation:**  
*"If anybody watches me work on deck they'll find that I'm constantly talking. I'm constantly communicating with the athletes."*

**Types of Communication:**

1. **General pronouncements** - Reminding whole group what they're after
2. **Individualized feedback** - Specific corrections
3. **Profound statements** - About the set and its importance (though athletes may not always find them profound)
4. **Multilingual encouragement** - "Fast" in multiple languages
5. **Whistling**
6. **Constant feedback loop**

### Why So Much Communication?

> *"I've always felt ill at ease if I'm not constantly coaching because I think it almost feels like I'm afraid that the kids will think I don't care because I'm not saying something."*

**Concern:**  
If he's quiet, athletes might think he's disengaged

**Solution:**  
Never stop coaching

## The "FIO" Principle

### Figure It Out

**Borrowed from Terry McKeever:**  
When athletes ask endless questions about stroke, mechanics, rate, etc.

**Salo's Response:**  
**"FIO" - Figure It Out**

### The Simple Metric

> *"You're trying to get from A to B and you're trying to get there fast. Count your strokes. It's real simple. Count your strokes. If you're going faster and you're getting less strokes, that's more efficiency."*

**Philosophy:**
- Athletes must discover
- Not everything can be told
- Some learning is experiential
- Coach can't solve everything
- Self-awareness matters

---

## Detail Orientation

### High Expectations + Precise Execution

**Characterization:**  
Very detail-oriented with high expectations

**Athlete Perspective:**  
Sometimes they say "he lets us do whatever we want"

**Salo's Clarification:**  
*"It's not that simple. Yeah, you can do whatever you want as long as you do what I want you to do."*

### Discovery Through Misreading

**Unexpected Benefit:**

> *"Sometimes I have an intent of what I think something needs to be and they will misread my intent and I will learn from that kind of misinformation. They take it and run with it and so I kind of use that as a vehicle - oh well I didn't really think that but I didn't communicate what I wanted, but they showed something to me that might be a little bit different."*

**Openness:**  
Even with detail focus, he learns from athletes

## No Metrics Tracking

### Why He Doesn't Track Progress Markers

> *"I don't track anything. I've seen over the years too many coaches who get caught up in tracking whatever metrics they're looking at or looking at a particular set and determining what kind of success they're going to have based on that and it's so unpredictable."*

**Problems He's Seen:**

**Lactate Monitoring Era:**
- Coaches monitored lactate
- Numbers didn't go the direction expected
- Coaches freaked out
- Thought season was lost
- Became self-fulfilling prophecy

### The Simple Solution

> *"I expect my athletes to come in every day with the intent to work really hard and swim fast every day. Not worry about the metrics per se. I think if you're doing the work every day, you're working really hard, you're going to get in shape. It just comes with working hard."*

**Philosophy:**
- Work hard = get in shape
- It's that simple
- Don't overthink it
- Don't cloud minds with parameters
- Trust the process

---

## Each Day Is Its Own Entity

### Not Worrying About Yesterday or Tomorrow

> *"I don't worry about what tomorrow's practice is. I really don't care about what yesterday's practice is."*

**Approach:**
- Put yesterday aside
- Today is different
- Will be just as hard as yesterday
- Tomorrow will feel like "we never get a break"

**Reality:**  
*"Your body's tremendously adaptable and plastic and it's got some capacities that we just need to reach towards."*

## Relationship with Assistant Coaches

### The USC Model (When He Was Head Coach)

**First Two Years:**  
Trying to please everyone - athletes AND coaches

**Realization:**  
Got uncomfortable, couldn't focus

**The Speech to His Coaches:**

> *"Look, I have to coach the way I coach. I can't coach how you want to coach or what your needs are. I can't be distracted by what your needs are as coaches. You just have to know that I trust you. I'm going to give you a lot of responsibility. I'm not going to write every single workout you've got to do verbatim. You're going to get your hands in the mix. But I'm going to run it the way I'm comfortable with. You just have to fall in line and figure it out. And be prepared that if I say 'okay in the next five minutes you're taking the sprinters, go do something with them, make them faster' - that's how I operate."*

### The Freedom This Created

**For Salo:**
- Could coach his way
- Not distracted
- Clear leadership
- Focused on athletes

**For Assistants:**
- Given responsibility
- Trusted
- Hands in the mix
- Learn to adapt

## Being an Assistant Coach (Orange Coast College)

### The Dream Role

**Career History:**  
Entire career as head coach except 5 years with Peter Daland

**The Dream:**  
*"Always had kind of in the back of my mind a dream of being an assistant coach for a change."*

### Why Assistant?

**Head Coach Responsibilities:**
- Enormous burden
- Recruiting (especially post-transfer portal)
- Scholarship decisions
- Compliance paperwork
- Parental management
- Constant pressure

**Assistant Freedom:**
*"It's so freeing. It's tremendous freedom to be able to just coach. Not worry about recruiting to the extent that you do as a head coach or giving up scholarships or doing the compliance paperwork or all the nonsense that goes along with especially college coaching."*

### The John Urbanchek Inspiration

**Background:**
- Salo swam for Urbanchek at Long Beach State
- Urbanchek retired from Michigan
- Moved back to Fullerton (near USC)
- Salo asked him to be volunteer assistant at USC
- "Great to have John on the deck and as a friend as a mentor"

**Lesson:**  
Great coaches can still contribute in supporting roles

## Working with Anthony Eady (His Former Swimmer)

### The Role

**Not trying to prove anything:**  
*"I've had a great career. I'm financially fine. Professionally I've had an exceptional career. I'm a Hall of Fame coach and all that stuff and an Olympic coach. Now it's just being able to give back as much as I can."*

### What He Brings

**1. Sounding Board**
- Bright young coach
- Knows what he wants
- Bounces things off Salo
- Great conversations about swimming
- Great conversations about Elon Musk's principles

**2. Adaptability**
*"I'm really really adaptable. Whatever the circumstance is, whatever the circumstances are with the athletes... I can adapt to either end of the spectrum and probably be pretty effective."*

**3. Listening**
- Takes directive from head coach
- Makes sure equipment gets used
- Fixes lane lines before coach has to
- Handles details so head coach can focus

**4. Takes Responsibility**
- Can run practice alone when needed
- Coach's wife expecting second child
- Family time matters
- Salo covers so coach can be present for family

## Community College Coaching

### Different Population

**Skill Set:**  
Really broad range - barely can swim to D1 bounce-backs

**Characteristics:**
- Purely out of joy of the sport
- Not on scholarship
- Can do it or not
- Lot of class conflicts
- Have jobs
- Attendance scattered

### Learning to Adapt

**Salo's Natural Tendency:**
- Want people at practice
- Want them on time
- Those things really important to him

**Community College Reality:**
- Must be more flexible
- Students have legitimate conflicts
- Can't demand same attendance

**But:**  
*"I think you can still ask of them the same thing."*

### The Conversation with Anthony

> *"You're being a little too cautious. I think these kids can handle a lot more than you're giving them. We don't have much time. So let's just kind of get after it."*

**Salo's Approach:**
- Hit the gas
- Don't wait
- Five weeks left
- Let's go

**But Also:**
- Someone out of breath on wall
- Gotta ignore that a little
- Ask "Are you okay?"
- Different dynamic

## The Painting Components Applied

### What Goes Into Every Workout

Even without pre-planning, these elements are always present:

1. **Power Development** - Stroke by stroke building
2. **Strength** - Resistance work, load
3. **Flexibility** - Range of motion, stroke mechanics
4. **Mechanics** - Detail execution, posture
5. **Speed** - Race pace, maximum effort
6. **Efficiency** - DPS, surge, feeling
7. **Technique** - Constant refinement

**Result:**  
Complete athlete development in every session

## Players Coach?

### The Question

Is Salo a "players coach" (like Pete Carroll at USC)?

**Salo's Response:**  
*"I'm not sure I would characterize myself that way."*

**Comparison to Urbanchek:**  
*"Urbanchek might be considered a players' coach kind of guy. He gets away with so much because he's got that stupid Hungarian accent of his."*

**The Joke:**  
*"When he's at home talking to Mel he speaks perfect English. Around everybody else he's got that Hungarian accent. It's one of his most endearing qualities."*

### Favorites

**Old Approach:**  
*"I used to tell people 'Do you like [swimmer] better?' I said 'No I don't. I don't like anybody. I hate them all.' It's just the same. But that's not true."*

**Evolution:**  
Went through "coach phase" where you were supposed to play favorites - how you divvied out currency (attention)

**Current:**  
Favorites are the kids who come every day

**But:**  
Realizes that's still not quite enough

## Practical Takeaways

### For Coaches:

**DO:**
- Be intensely detail-oriented
- But also read the room moment-by-moment
- Communicate constantly
- Let athletes figure things out (FIO)
- Create each practice as its own entity
- Trust hard work over metrics
- Give assistants responsibility and trust

**DON'T:**
- Get caught up in metrics tracking
- Over-plan workouts
- Tell athletes how many reps (sometimes)
- Let quiet moments make you think you're disengaged
- Compromise detail for flexibility (you can have both)

### For Athletes:

**Understand:**
- Detail matters - execute exactly as coached
- But you have choices within framework
- Figure it out (FIO) - coach won't solve everything
- Focus on current rep, not total number
- Hard work = results, not metrics
- Every day is its own challenge
- Body is adaptable

---

## Bottom Line

**Salo's Coaching DNA:**

1. **Detail-obsessed** yet responsive
2. **Doesn't plan ahead** yet every session is purposeful
3. **Constantly communicating** yet expects athletes to figure it out
4. **Rigid in execution** yet flexible in design
5. **Moment-by-moment** yet creates cohesive training
6. **No metrics** yet athletes get faster
7. **High expectations** yet gives freedom within framework

**The Paradox That Works:**  
Seeming contradictions create an environment where Olympic champions are made while athletes discover their own path within a detailed structure.
