# Dave Salo: Innovation and Creativity in Coaching

**Category:** Coaching Philosophy, Career Development, Training Innovation, Methodology  
**Coach:** Dave Salo  
**Source:** Inside With Brett Hawke Podcast - Coaches Corner Interview

## Summary

Dave Salo's career represents a unique blend of academic rigor and practical coaching innovation. With a PhD in Exercise Science and over 40 years of coaching experience, Salo built his reputation on challenging conventional training wisdom—particularly around volume-based training—and developing a race-pace focused methodology that has produced Olympic champions and world record holders.

## Development of Training Philosophy

### Philosophical Evolution

**Experiential vs. Academic:**
- Most coaches have experiential philosophies (based on what they experienced)
- Salo's philosophy rooted in academics and research
- Professor Mastropolo taught him about sprint training effects
- Studied physiology to understand performance mechanisms

**Early Innovation (Mid-1980s):**
- Started implementing race-pace concepts 10+ years before they became mainstream
- Used 20-yard pool as much as 25-yard pool
- Never went over 100 yards per repetition
- All race pace work

**Famous Example:**
- 12-year-old boy breaking 5:00 in 500 freestyle

## Core Training Philosophy

### Race Pace Focus

**Why Speed in Practice:**

> "Swimming slow is boring to watch."

**Mechanical Reasons:**
- Drag forces increase exponentially with speed
- Must train at drag force encountered at racing speed
- Body positioning changes dramatically at race speeds

**Physiological Reasons:**
- Buffering system only enhanced by going fast
- Can't improve buffering capacity at slow speeds
- Must recruit all muscle fiber types at maximum intensity

### Volume Philosophy

**The 20-Minute Concept:**
> "I package my work in 20-minute segments of really intense work. You've got to equate what you're doing in that 20 minutes to going 1:48 speed or 1:45 speed—whatever your race time is."

**To Athlete Worried About 200 Fly:**
> "We train once a day for an hour and a half to 1:45, and you're asking me if that's enough work for an event you want to go 1:48 in? Come on. An hour and 45 minutes to get you ready for 1 minute 45 seconds should be enough."

**Walking Analogy:**
> "I could literally walk a marathon. I couldn't go very fast because I'm not doing anything very fast. Same with swimming—if you want to go 46 seconds in 100 fly, you have to train at that speed."

### XYZ Set Innovation

**Created for 2004 Olympic Trials:**
- Had 25 kids going to trials
- Range from Jason Lezak (sprinter) to Haley Pearsall (distance)
- Only coach on deck during fine-tuning phase

**Set Structure:**
- X = 1:00 interval
- Y = 1:15 interval  
- Z = 1:30 interval
- Athletes choose distance, stroke, equipment
- **Requirement: FAST**

**Purpose:**
- Learn what athletes think they need
- Jason going 25-25-25 = thinks he needs more rest
- Haley going 100-150-200 = thinks she needs more volume
- Use information to guide championship preparation
- Athletes get what they want while coach gets data

**Modern Variations:**
Example: 100 + X, 75 + X, 50 + X
- 100/75/50 on set intervals (1:30, 1:15, 1:00)
- X always on 1:20
- "Decide what you want on X, but I just want it fast"
- Minimum 25 or 50, varies by workout
- Constant evolution based on feedback

## Breaststroke Mastery

### Why Breaststroke Success

> "The reason I've been successful with breaststroke is because I was a breaststroke swimmer."

**Personal Experience:**
- Made first Junior Nationals in 100 breast (illegally entered without qualifying time)
- Had a cold, stressed about $100 fine
- Came off last turn, looked 50m down thinking "I'm going to be fined $100"
- Touched wall, made Junior Nationals
- Still remembers what that stroke feels like

**Understanding the Feel:**
- Knows what it's like when stroke "doesn't feel right"
- Common breaststroker complaint: "It felt great yesterday, today it feels awkward"
- Knows how to get kids back to correct timing
- Timing issue, not strength or technique issue

### Key Breaststroke Components

**1. The Line:**
- Completion of extension = line from fingertips through head to feet
- Starting point for stroke analysis

**2. Recovery:**
- Turn hands and elbows into extension
- **Does not believe in recovering over surface**
- Rotation of elbows critical
- Elbows pointed downward create drag

**3. Head Position:**
- In line with back and shoulders
- Not forced one way or the other
- Shoulders drive movement forward, not head
- Dislikes excessive "head thing"
- Dislikes hands coming out of water (tries to tone down)

**4. The Kick:**
- Relationship between knees and heels
- Old way: "Up, out, together"
- Modern: Straighter path
- Heels outside the knees even if abbreviated (like Rebecca Soni)

**5. The Pull:**
- Elbow position critical
- More of a scull than a pull
- Teaches sculling action vs. pulling action

**6. Lower Back:**
- Is it flattening out or caving in?
- Water caving in on top = problem

### Individualization

**Eight Different Looks:**
> "Watch the finals of the Olympics—eight swimmers, eight different looks to what this stroke looks like."

**Athletes Coached:**
- Jessica Hardy
- Amanda Beard
- Stacey Anna Stitts
- Rebecca Soni
- Julie Efimova (Russia)
- All very different stroke mechanics
- All successful at highest level

**Proud Moment:**
Short Course World Championships—his three breaststrokers went 1-2-3 in 50m breast:
1. Julie Efimova (Russia)
2. Jessica Hardy
3. Rebecca Soni

**Stroke Comparisons:**
- **Rebecca Soni:** "The hummingbird of breaststroke—doesn't make any sense"
- Half scull, brief abbreviated kick, but devastatingly fast
- Similar to Adam Peaty's stroke
- **Adam Peaty:** Very abbreviated kick, straight path, strong scull, maintains tempo entire 100
- **Lydia Jacoby:** Completely different from Lilly King
- **Tatjana Schoenmaker (South Africa):** Completely different with extremely late breath

### Coaching Rebecca Soni

**First Year Challenges:**
- Third coach in one year (club coach Speedling, Mark Schubert, then Salo)
- Went from distance-oriented 400 IM work to Salo's philosophy
- **During workout, stopped 15 meters from wall, crying:**

> "I hate your drills!"

**Dave's Response:**
> "Rebecca, I understand your stroke and I'm not gonna have you swim like Amanda or Jessica or Stacyanna. I'm gonna swim you YOUR stroke. I understand your stroke and I'll get better at understanding your stroke. There are some key things to your stroke that I think we can enhance versus take away from. I'm not going to change your stroke because she knew her stroke was different. Look, I know breaststroke, and you're just going to have to trust me."

**Evolution:**
- Won NCAAs sophomore year
- Went to meet in Australia right after NCAAs
- Swam 2nd in 200 breast long course with no LCM training
- Came back: "Maybe what we're doing is adequate, maybe it's going to be okay"

**Rebecca's Philosophy (taught by Dave):**
> When asked about tapering by recruits: "You don't worry about that stuff. Whether taper was good or bad or wasn't perfect, you're always going to get up and race fast because that's what you're supposed to do."

## Pride and Psychology

### Empowerment Philosophy

**Running Away From Something:**
> "A lot of these athletes are running away from something. They think their life will change if they just win that gold medal or break a world record. Then they find out—oh my gosh, this didn't really change my life. That's kind of a dysfunctional thing."

**Empowering Women:**
> "Stop swimming for me or stop swimming for your parents. Do it because this is what YOU want to accomplish. Don't look at it as life-changing—it can impact your life positively, but don't look at it so disruptively that it makes you happy. That's not what's going to make you happy."

### The Pride Message

**Constant Theme:**
> "Be prideful of your work, what you do—not looking for somebody else's pride in you. Don't look for your parents' pride. They have to be proud of you because they were cheering you on when you learned to go potty. That's what they do, that's what they're supposed to do."

**Personal Pride:**
> "Learn to be prideful in your daily work, your performance, your results—not look for pride from somebody else: your coach, your parents, your grandparents. This is YOU. It's YOURS. You've got to take it, embrace it, and your life will be a lot better off."

**On His Own Career:**
> "Do I worry about criticisms of me? No. I'm proud of my career. I'm really proud of what I've done. I know my parents would be proud of me even though they're deceased, but I didn't seek their pride. I want to be proud of ME and what I've done and how I've had a positive impact on athletes—not just the gold medals."

## Supplement Philosophy & Doping Issues

### Anti-Supplement Stance

**Core Position:**
> "I'm probably the most anti-supplement coach there ever was. I don't encourage my athletes to take any kind of supplement, I don't care what it is."

**Dietary Philosophy:**
- Good balanced diet
- Time-sensitive eating
- Will replenish glycogen stores without supplements
- Stopped encouraging even general vitamins after Kicken Vencil incident

**Athlete Response:**
- Once kids knew his position, they would hide supplements
- Would shuffle items into lockers when he walked in
- "I don't care what it is—glycogen repletion, vitamins—you don't need it, just eat properly"

### Systemic Problem

**International Athletes:**
> "The systemic problem, especially when coaching international athletes—they're transitory. They go to international championships, do their race, sit in the jacuzzi with other great athletes talking about 'What are you taking?'"

**Creatine Era:**
- Before 2008, whole group taking creatine
- "Everyone said they had to take creatine for phosphocreatine stores"
- Gave them research consensus on creatine
- "Got exhausted trying to tell them you don't need this stuff"

### High-Profile Cases

**Jessica Hardy:**
- Tested positive for tainted vitamin
- Proved supplement was contaminated
- Sued supplement company, made a lot of money
- Still ended her career trajectory

**Kicken Vencil:**
- Proven tainted vitamin supplement
- Career ended
- "Both proved they had a supplement that was contaminated"

**Julie Efimova:**
- USC told him to curtail relationship
- She trained with him through Russian trials when allowed
- Second test was for "concept versus real performance enhancing drug"
- Supplement all Eastern European athletes taking for "heart protection"
- WADA banned it despite limited scientific evidence it enhanced performance
- "Unfortunate—everyone doing it so there must be something wrong with it"

**Katinka Hosszú:**
- Criticized constantly
- Never tested positive, tested all the time racing worldwide
- "One of the hardest working individuals in weight room and pool"
- "Amazing athlete, clean as can be"
- "We come under criticism for being exceptional"

### The Real Standard

> "When American athletes do amazing things, nobody accuses Katie Ledecky when she won her first 800 free gold. Her leap in performance was huge. This girl works hard, we've seen it, she's got a great work ethic—those results are possible."

**Position:**
> "I wish swimmers would just come to practice, train really hard, and have a good healthy diet. I think that takes care of itself."

## Philosophy on Goals

### Anti-Goal Setting

**Recent Conversation:**
- International swimmer after one month: "Are we gonna have that goal meeting?"
- Dave: "I don't talk to my athletes about goals"

**His Actual Goal:**
> "My goal is to train you to be the best athlete that you can be. It doesn't matter what your goal is. I'm trying to make you the best you can be—not pigeonhole it based on a particular time."

**USC Philosophy:**
> "I don't know if it was good or bad, just my philosophy. I would never talk to kids about specific goals. If they had them, they could relate them to me—that's fine. But I told them my goal and my responsibility is to drive everybody towards NCAAs podium."

**The Charge:**
> "I'm looking at that. If your goal is something less than that, that's fine. But my goal is to coach you towards making NCAAs and scoring at NCAAs. So your goals are really irrelevant to what my charge is—that's what my job is. I don't have a lot of goal meetings with athletes. I'm just trying to make them better than they think they are."

## Evolution and Innovation

### Constant Evolution

**Feedback Loop:**
> "I keep evolving the way I do things. I evolve in part by the feedback I get from other coaches who try some of these things."

**Example: Balancing Distance & Race Pace**

Coach complaint: "I want to do what you do, but I took over a team and they think they're not doing enough yardage, not enough distance. How do I balance that?"

**Dave's Solution:**
"Here's a way to do it—let's say you want to go 2x200s but implement my philosophy (race pace):

- Each 200 is broken
- 3x25 + X
- X is the remaining 125 yards
- First three 25s: FAST, 5 seconds rest after each
- Then choose what the 125 looks like
- Break it how you want, 20 seconds total rest time
- Go straight, I don't care
- **You get fast work I want + distance work they think they need**
- Must be under your best 200 total swim time"

**Result:**
> "They'll find out in short order, 'Well okay, let me do that.' Gives balance between fast work and volume perception."

### Drill Philosophy

**High-Intensity Drills:**
- Does fast work in drill sets
- Drill at high intensity vs. slow intensity
- Warm down and recovery at slower intensities

**Rebecca Soni Reaction:**
> Long course workout, mostly drill at race pace. About 400m in she stops 15m from wall, in tears crying: "I HATE YOUR DRILLS!"

**Philosophy:**
> "A lot of drill type work at race pace. Recovery work between sets. Not focused on how far we go—that's not very important. Challenging athletes to see if they can't work faster."

## Key Takeaways for Coaches

### Be Creative and Innovative
- Don't just follow what everyone else does
- Ground your philosophy in science and research
- Be willing to challenge conventional wisdom
- Constantly evolve based on feedback and results

### Individualize Training
- "Is everybody meeting the expectation around themselves, not some other standard?"
- Rebecca Soni was a great trainer "relative to Rebecca"
- Different strokes for different folks (literally in breaststroke)
- XYZ sets allow athletes to self-select while maintaining quality

### Fast Swimming Requires Fast Training
- Can't walk your way to a marathon PR
- Drag forces change exponentially with speed
- Buffering systems only improve with high intensity
- Volume is less important than velocity

### Psychology Matters
- Keep athletes happy and engaged
- Make training fun
- Empower athletes to swim for themselves, not others
- Teach pride in work, not seeking others' pride
- Manage egos and relationships carefully (especially women in same events)

### Trust the Process
- Taper doesn't matter if you're not ready to race
- Rebecca Soni: "You're always going to get up and race fast because that's what you're supposed to do"
- Consistency over magic
- Believe athletes can exceed what they think possible

### Academic Foundation
- PhD gave him credibility and deeper understanding
- But also learned from experience and observation
- Watched other coaches when recruiting
- "How do they interact with their team to advance their philosophy?"

### Anti-Supplement
- "Most anti-supplement coach there ever was"
- Good diet and time-sensitive eating is enough
- Supplements are athletes talking to each other, not coach-driven
- Protect athletes from tainted products and bad decisions

### Professional Development
- Read research
- Write and share (he wrote Swimming World column)
- Learn from mentors even if philosophies differ
- Separate yourself through education and innovation
- Don't be afraid to be different
