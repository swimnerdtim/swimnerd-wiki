# Dave Salo on Supplements and Doping in Swimming

**Category:** Sports Ethics, Nutrition, Coaching Philosophy, Anti-Doping  
**Coach:** Dave Salo  
**Context:** USC tenure, international athletes, high-profile cases

## Summary

Dave Salo is one of the most vocal anti-supplement coaches in swimming, despite being associated with several athletes who tested positive during his career. His stance is clear: hard work pays off, supplements are unnecessary, and coaches should actively discourage their use. This article examines his philosophy, experiences, and recommendations for managing supplement culture in competitive swimming.

---

## Core Philosophy

### Salo's Position: "I'm probably the most anti-supplement coach there ever was"

**What he discourages:**
- ANY kind of supplementation
- Creatine
- Glycogen repletion drinks
- Vitamins (even general multivitamins)
- Performance enhancers
- Recovery supplements

**What he promotes:**
- Hard work
- Good, balanced diet
- Time-sensitive eating
- Natural recovery

---

## The Paradox

### High-Profile Positive Tests

Despite being the most anti-supplement coach, Salo has been associated with more positive tests than perhaps any other coach:

**Cases include:**
- **Yulia Efimova** - Russian breaststroker (meldonium)
- **Jessica Hardy** - Tainted vitamin supplement
- **Kicker Vencill** - Proven tainted supplement

**The Irony:** The coach most against supplements has the most supplement-related issues

---

## Why Athletes Take Supplements Anyway

### The "Hot Tub Problem"

Athletes go to international championships:
1. Swim their race
2. Sit in the jacuzzi with other elite athletes
3. Have "the conversation" - "What are you taking?"
4. See successful athletes using supplements
5. Want the same "edge"
6. Start taking things without coach knowledge

### The Creatine Era (Pre-2008)

Salo recounts:
- Everyone was taking creatine
- Athletes convinced it would help phosphocreatine stores
- Didn't matter what Salo told them
- He provided research showing it didn't work
- Athletes took it anyway
- Eventually he got "exhausted trying to tell them"

---

## The Hiding Game

### What Happens When Coaches Take a Stance

**At USC:**
- Once athletes knew Salo's position
- They would hide supplements
- Walk into locker room → quick shuffle into lockers
- Didn't matter the supplement type
- Athletes knew his stance and concealed use

**The Problem:**
- Coaches can't monitor what they can't see
- Athletes take advice from peers over coaches
- International athletes from different regulatory environments
- Varying supplement quality standards across countries

---

## International Supplement Standards

### Quality Control Issues

**Salo's observations:**
- Different countries have different manufacturing standards
- U.S. has higher standards
- Brazil and other countries may have lower grading
- Athletes don't know where supplements are made
- Don't know what's actually in them
- Don't even know WHY they're taking them
- "Just taking it because the guy next to you is taking it"

## Specific Cases

### Jessica Hardy - Tainted Supplement
- Took a vitamin
- Tested positive
- PROVEN to be tainted
- Successfully sued the supplement company
- Made significant money from lawsuit
- BUT: Career impact was devastating

### Kicker Vencill - Career Ended
- PROVEN tainted supplement
- Career ended despite proof of innocence
- Shows the brutal reality of strict liability

### Yulia Efimova - Meldonium
- Russian breaststroker
- Second positive test
- Heart-protection concept drug
- All Eastern European athletes taking it
- WADA banned it after widespread use
- "Not a prevalence of legit scientific background"
- Banned because "everyone's doing it so there must be something wrong"

**University Response:**
- USC told Salo to cut ties with Efimova
- He had to end coaching relationship
- Great relationship otherwise
- She trained with him during 2016 Russian trials

---

## The Meldonium Controversy

### What Is It?

- Supposedly protects the heart from exhaustion
- Eastern European athletes widely using it
- Belief: saves the heart during intense training
- NO strong scientific evidence of performance enhancement
- More of a "concept" than proven PED

### WADA's Response

- Saw widespread use
- Assumed it must work
- Banned it
- Created retroactive problems for athletes

**Salo's Frustration:** Banning substances based on use patterns rather than proven performance enhancement

---

## The "Monsterizing" Problem

### Double Standard in Swimming

**When Americans swim fast:**
- "She works hard!"
- "Great technique!"
- "Deserved success!"

**Example: Katie Ledecky**
- Huge leap in performance for 800 free gold
- Nobody accused her of doping
- Response: "This girl works hard, we've seen it, great stroke"

**When internationals swim fast:**
- "Must be on drugs!"
- Immediate suspicion
- Guilty until proven innocent

### Katinka Hosszu Example

- Constantly criticized
- Never tested positive
- Tested frequently worldwide
- One of the hardest workers Salo ever coached
- Exceptional in weight room and pool
- "Driven and amazing athlete"
- "As clean as can be"

**Yet:** Constant accusations based only on exceptional performance

## Current Supplement Rules

### FINA/World Aquatics Policy

**Rule implemented:** Coaches cannot coach athletes who:
- Have tested positive
- Are under investigation for positive test
- Have doping-related suspensions

**Salo's View:** Uncertain if this is the best approach

**Alternative:** Keep athlete under coach's wing rather than cut them loose

---

## What Salo Learned

### Evolution of His Approach

1. **Initially:** Tried to educate athletes, provide research
2. **Mid-career:** Got exhausted fighting the culture
3. **Current:** Complete anti-supplement stance

### Post-Hardy/Vencill Changes

**After Jessica Hardy tainted vitamin:**
- Stopped encouraging even general vitamins
- "You just don't know what's happening with those things"
- Complete zero-tolerance for ANY supplements

---

## Salo's Recommendations for Coaches

### What You Can Control:

1. **Make your position clear**
   - Be explicit about anti-supplement stance
   - Explain why
   - Be consistent

2. **Educate on tainted supplements**
   - Show real cases (Hardy, Vencill)
   - Explain strict liability
   - Show career consequences

3. **Promote proper nutrition**
   - Good balanced diet
   - Time-sensitive eating
   - Natural recovery
   - Replenish stores through food

4. **Accept limitations**
   - Athletes will hide supplement use
   - International athletes have different influences
   - You can't monitor 80-100 people 24/7
   - Peer influence is powerful

5. **Stay informed**
   - Know what's being taken
   - Understand the culture
   - Check locker rooms occasionally
   - Ask questions about unknown substances

## The Glycogen Repletion Example

### Michael Phelps Story (2004 Olympics)

**Scene:** Phelps age 15, opening care package from home

**Contents:**
- Glycogen repletion drink
- Three big boxes of Mike & Ike candies

**Salo's observation:**  
*"I think it's the Mike and Ikes, not the glycogen repletion drink"*

**Point:** Kids are on every supplement imaginable, but performance comes from training, not pills

---

## The Systemic Problem

### Why It Persists:

1. **Transitory athletes** - Internationals not always at your pool
2. **Peer influence** - Hot tub conversations worldwide
3. **Marketing** - Supplement companies target athletes
4. **Desperation** - Athletes looking for any edge
5. **Coaches promoting it** - Some coaches DO push supplements
6. **Cultural differences** - International norms vary
7. **Lack of education** - Athletes don't understand risks

---

## Practical Takeaways

### For Coaches:

- **Be the anti-supplement voice** even if you're swimming against the tide
- **Show real consequences** - Hardy, Vencill, Efimova cases
- **Promote food-based nutrition** - It works
- **Inspect locker rooms** occasionally - Know what's happening
- **Accept you can't control everything** - But maintain your standards
- **Educate on tainted supplements** - Strict liability is real

### For Athletes:

- **Listen to your coach** - Not the guy in the hot tub
- **Understand strict liability** - Doesn't matter if it was an accident
- **Know what you're taking** - Where it's made, what's in it, why
- **Consider the risk** - Career-ending for a marginal benefit?
- **Trust the process** - Hard work and good food work

## The Bottom Line

**Salo's Message:** 
- Supplements are unnecessary
- They carry massive risk
- Natural nutrition works
- Hard work pays off
- The culture is toxic
- Coaches must fight it
- But accept you can't control everything

**The Irony:** The most anti-supplement coach has had the most supplement problems - proving how pervasive and insidious the culture really is.
