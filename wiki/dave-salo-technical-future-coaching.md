# Dave Salo: Technical Coaching and Sports Psychology

**Category:** Sports Psychology, Coaching Philosophy, Technical Coaching, Communication  
**Coaches:** Dave Salo (Tokyo Frog Kings), Tom Rushton (Energy Standard)  
**Source:** ISL Podcast - Discussion about technical future of swimming

## Summary

Wide-ranging discussion with Dave Salo and Tom Rushton covering sports psychology in swimming, communication skills for coaches, visualization techniques, the role of trust in coach-athlete relationships, managing elite athletes, and technical innovations including the controversial freestyle with dolphin kick concept.

---

## Sports Psychology in Coaching

### Value of Sports Psychologists

**Dave Salo's Position:**
> "The use of sports-specific psychologists or counselors can be a really valuable asset to our athletes and coaches. It's not clinical—that's completely different. A really good sports psychologist works on communication skills."

**For Coaches, Not Just Athletes:**
- Worked with Kathy Wickstrand (not sports psychologist, but communication expert)
- Helped understand skill set and ability to listen
- Better communicate with athletes
- Recognize differences between people

**For Athletes:**
- Relaxation skills
- Visualization skills  
- Go-to person who's not the coach
- Removes barrier between coach and athlete
- Athletes sometimes think coaches only want great performance, don't care about them personally
- Third party can intervene helpfully

### Tom Rushton's Experience

**Past Use:**
- Common part of support staff with national sport federations
- Bring in psychologists before big problems arise
- Help athletes develop "toolkit" of mental skills
- Visualization, coping mechanisms under stress
- Also valuable for coaches

**Relationship Building:**
- Not necessarily brought into ISL team (athletes may have own at home)
- Big bonus: Can work over Zoom remotely
- Relationship already built is positive
- Helpful working with psychologists during practice/training scenarios
- Learn different ways of teaching skills to athletes

**Example:** Worked in Montreal with Veronique Richard on developing teaching methods for athletes

## Visualization Techniques

### Types of Visualization

**Tom Rushton's Toolkit Approach:**
- First person perspective
- Third person perspective
- Future tense (imagining positive outcome)
- Analyzing past error or good performance
- Seasonal plan for addressing different scenarios

**Teaching Moments:**
> "If we were coming into a competition where athletes would be likely challenged and beaten, we might in advance rehearse some visualization around how that might go both during the swim and afterwards, just for an example."

**End of Season:**
> "Same with imagining a positive outcome, especially towards the end of the season when you want and expect them to succeed."

### Dave Salo's Daily Practice Use

**Relaxation First:**
> "I started to do it every day my last couple years at USC. These kids are rushing around—busy, going to class, rushing from dining hall to classroom to workout. I use it as a time to bring them down—take a breath, relax, we're not going to rush into this training session yet."

**Re-energize Past Performance:**
- Focus on past performance to re-energize enthusiasm
- Process of complete relaxation
- Dive through that experience
- Bring back euphoric feel of what that felt like
- First person in that stroke, in that finish
- Carry that energy (great swims come with out-of-body type experience)
- Engineer that performance as they go into workout or competition

**Forward-Looking Visualization:**
> "Sometimes very specific to a race they've had in the past, or might be visualizing racing Cal in February where it's raining and the wind sucks and it's cold and we're back in that little corner. Get them to embrace the discomfort that's coming their way so it's not going to be a big surprise."

**Purpose:**
> "So when you arrive at the facility and it is exactly as I've just described, they're comforted with: okay, we've been through this, we know what to encounter."

### Visualization for Coaches

**Dave Salo's Personal Use:**
> "I use visualization for myself. About an hour before I get up out of bed, I visualize what my day is going to be like. I've got to think these things through a little bit (I'm 62 years old now). I visualize what my day is going to look like, what I'm going to do, so I'm more efficient with my time."

**For Practice Planning:**
> "I visualize my training sessions. Kids will tell you the reason I wear sunglasses at practice more often is to close my eyes and visualize what a set is going to look like. I put a lot of details in my sets—I've got to visually think it through to make sure it's going to work and what I'm after."

**Benefits:**
> "So that as I see it being done by the athletes, I can go 'that's what I want' or 'that's not what I want, I want it to look more like this.' It's really helped me be a more efficient coach."

**Coach Recommendation:**
> "Coaches, if you can utilize these skills a lot, it helps them present their methodology a lot more clearly."

## Facility Walk-Through Importance

### Tom Rushton's Standard Practice

**ISL Protocol:**
- Do walk-through of area when arriving at facility
- See ready room locations
- See all different places they'll move through
- Production team walks through: first ready room, second ready room, marshaling, camera positions
- Important from production POV but also helps athletes
- Athletes go through visualization process
- Knowing what's going to happen gives sense of comfort

### Dave Salo's Rebecca Soni Lesson

**2008 Olympic Trials - 100 Breast Final:**
> "I didn't give her awareness of what it was going to be like walking out on that deck with 15-20,000 people in the stands, all eyes front and center. As I watched her go out there for that final, you could tell she was a deer in headlights. She was so distracted by this crowd. It was my failure."

**Result:**
- Finished 4th instead of winning
- Apologized to her for not really preparing her
- Not sure how he should have done it, but took it for granted she was prepared
- She was ready—until the bright lights, then it was just scary

**Redemption:**
- Went on to win silver in 100 breast at Olympics
- Won gold in 200 breast at Olympics

**Lesson Learned:**
> "That experience I had to own up to—I let her down. It became part of the process that I would use. You kind of go through that production walk-through so they get comfortable: here's the first ready room, here's the second ready room, you're gonna walk up some stairs into the stadium—be prepared for that."

**Key Point:**
> "Probably one of the most important pieces to preparation for an athlete that sometimes we forget and take it for granted the athletes are all ready to go and can handle that."

## Doc Counsilman's Three Pillars of Coaching

### Tom Rushton's Framework

**The Three Essential Pieces:**

**1. Biomechanics:**
- Must be well-schooled in biomechanics
- Know the technique of how to swim well

**2. Physiology:**
- Know the physiology of training
- Know the physiology of the event
- Be able to train athlete to perform under those demands and stress

**3. Psychology:**
> "Coaching is all about psychology—getting people to do things they either don't want to do, or they're not willing to do, or they know they need to do but don't know how to do."

**Bottom Line:**
> "The biggest thing about coaching is the psychology piece."

## Communication Skills

### Dave Salo's Evolution

**Late Career Breakthrough:**
> "I've only in the last four or five years really better understood my delivery of information and how to deliver that information such that the athletes will appreciate it, understand it, and be able to utilize that to really test their boundaries."

**Took A While:**
> "It took me a while to understand that. All of us are very demanding coaches—that's why we've been able to get kids to perform as well as they have. But some kids we might lose because they just don't respond to the same kind of skill set that we have in terms of communication."

**Understanding Differences:**
> "Knowing that all of us are very different in how we receive and how we deliver information is really important. I think that's helped me be a better coach. My athletes will tell you that the last three or four years of my career at USC I really got better at it, and the kids appreciated that."

**More Than Just Showing Up:**
> "It's not that you're just a coach showing up on the deck every day giving them great workouts. It's they gotta know that you care about them, and they'll give more effort as they go along."

### Central Governor Theory

**Dave Salo's Explanation:**
> "I'm a big believer in the central government theory of training and fatigue. It's basically the balance you have to overcome: the brain trying to tell your body to quit doing all these extraordinary physical things because it wants to conserve all energy for the brain. You've got to have that constant battle of talking your brain out of talking the body out of doing the work."

## Coach-Athlete Relationships

### Professional Boundaries

**Dave Salo's Position:**
> "I used to tell my kids I hate them all equally so that they never felt like they were special."

**Friend vs. Coach:**
> "You've got to be really careful with the friend thing. We're friendly, we care about each other, but you gotta be very careful with that relationship."

**Gender Considerations:**
> "Especially when you're talking about women in particular. You've got to understand—in our sport, women and men are very different. Research and psychologists will tell you that what's important with men is very different from what's important with women."

**Risk of Manipulation:**
> "You gotta be careful that you don't take this relationship and use them to your advantage and disadvantage the athletes."

**Possessiveness Warning:**
> "Gotta be really careful as coach that we don't become so possessive of athletes, possessive of their performance. Our goal is really to be partners with our athletes to help them realize what they're capable of, push them towards that within realistic boundaries."

**More Parental Than Buddies:**
> "We need to be more parental than buddies. You gotta maintain those boundaries and be very very careful with them."

### Athlete Manipulation

**Athletes Can Manipulate:**
> "If you've got an athlete who thinks they're really friendly and tight with the coach, they can manipulate the coach in terms of changing that relationship."

**Standing Firm:**
> "You got to be careful not to let an athlete manipulate you to back off that track. I've had some really really outstanding athletes that I've had to stand firm about what they're going to do and how it's going to be done."

### Trust as Cornerstone

**Especially with Older Athletes:**
> "When you're talking about postgraduate athletes, ISL athletes, you have to sometimes just tell them: 'Look, just don't question, just trust me.'"

**Ous Mellouli Example (2006):**
- Would come in couple days here and there, then disappear
- After about a month, Dave stopped him before he got in

**The Conversation:**
> "I told him: Ous, you can't swim with me anymore. He looked at me like 'what do you mean I can't swim with you?' I said look, I'll coach you and I'll coach you to be really good, but you're going to do it my way. My way is: you come every single day, you do what I ask you to do, and most importantly you just have to trust me."

**Ous's Reaction:**
> "He looked at me like 'are you kidding me? I'm really good.' I said 'we just agreed you have to trust in my ability to coach you, and if you do, I think we're going to go really far.'"

**Result:**
- Went on to win a few gold medals

**Friendship vs. Coaching:**
> "We're friendly and we're friends, but there are times I had to ask him to do things that are very difficult. It's tough to have a friend do something that's hard. But if you're really a coach and you can separate yourself out from that, I think it works."

**Going Toe to Toe:**
> "We've gone to battle with athletes sometimes, and we have to be as unemotional about it as we can be. Otherwise it just doesn't work very well. I've had enough stories—sometimes you win, sometimes you lose, but you got to stand your ground about what you believe as a coach based on all your experiences."

## Coaching Talented Athletes

### Will They Be Great Anyway?

**The Myth:**
> "There exists a myth in swimming that there are some people with exceptional talent like Michael Phelps, Caleb Dressel, and the myth goes that no matter which coach these people would come to, they would have been superstars regardless. They're so talented, all they need to do is just swim."

**Dave Salo's Response:**

**Taking Ownership Without Possessing:**
> "What's really important is coaches need to take ownership of responsibility for igniting the flame in our athletes to do what they're capable of doing. Being realistic about what you see and envision and really believe in what it is they can do. But at the same time NOT being possessive over that performance."

**The Problem:**
> "A lot of times coaches get caught up in possessing that performance or possessing that athlete—that only by the grace of God that I'm your coach will that actually happen. I just don't believe that. I think those athletes are going to be great athletes no matter what."

**The Right Link:**
> "But there's a really strong link to the right kind of coach—the coach who doesn't manipulate, doesn't take ownership of all performance."

**Pride in Not Being a One-Hit Wonder:**
> "I'm really proud of the fact that I was not a one-hit wonder. I did not coach just one athlete to gold medal but several athletes to gold medals and world record performances. I'm really proud that I was able to separate myself from their performance to the extent that: boy, I'm really fortunate to coach Ous Mellouli, Rebecca Soni, Jessica Hardy, or all these other athletes. But also knowing that I was an important link to that performance, but NOT that it couldn't have happened without me. That's just silly."

### Biggest Coaching Challenge

**Not the First Olympics:**
> "I've had experiences coaching kids to Olympic performance and gold medal performance. My biggest challenge and fear was not coaching that kid their first Olympics, or coaching that same kid their second Olympics, but coaching that kid who'd been to Olympics and come to you subsequently from another coach, and coach that kid to that performance as well."

**When Coaching Young Athletes:**
> "When I was coaching Aaron Peirsol, Amanda Beard, Michael Cavic, I was always in my mind trying to get them to swim really really well under my coaching so that when they went off to college and swam for Frank Busch, Teri McKeever, Dave Durden, or Eddie Reese, they were receiving from me a really good, challenged athlete who accomplished great performances."

**The Handoff:**
> "Now don't screw it up. My hands are clean. But I just really think a good coach is really important with those athletes that are capable."

**The Truth:**
> "Caleb Dressel would have swum well with anybody. Michael Phelps would have swum well with anybody—as long as that coach had the skills to give them that direction."

### Staying One Chapter Ahead

**Teaching Assistant Analogy:**
> "When I was working on my PhD at USC, I was a teaching assistant in biology lab for a semester. I always told people I just stayed one chapter ahead of the students. If I was a chapter ahead of the students, I'd be okay. If I was a chapter behind and they knew it, I'm screwed. I think that's the way coaching is as well."

**Aaron Peirsol Example:**
> "I always tried to when I had young kids developing, let them know I felt they could go here. Whenever they're ready for that, I'm already there—but not to push them there, but to let them know."

**The Prediction:**
> "Aaron, I think in four years' time you can be at the Olympic Games. I told him that at 13. At that time, thinking a 17-year-old could make the Olympic team was just inconceivable back then. But in fact I did tell Aaron Peirsol I thought he could make the Olympic team four years from when he was 13 because I thought he was that good."

**The Approach:**
> "It wasn't to push him there. It was to let him know I'm a chapter ahead. But he would have been great. He was great with Eddie Reese as well."

## Managing Different Athlete Types

### Michael Cavic vs. Aaron Peirsol

**Michael Cavic:**
- Probably one of the most naturally talented athletes Dave ever coached
- Didn't always like to do the work
- Kind of a battle sometimes to get him to work
- Extremely talented

**Aaron Peirsol (same age as Cavic):**
- Would thrive on work
- Do all the work, all the challenges

**Coaching Adjustment:**
> "You had to kind of adjust. That's the thing that's really important to coaches: don't throw away some kid who lacks that desire to train great. You got to find out how to manipulate your training to get them to work hard and do it well."

**Jason Lezak:**
- Also pretty talented
- Didn't really like to work the way Dave wanted him to work
- Had to find ways to get him to work

**Understanding Physiology & Mechanics:**
> "Understanding the physiology, understanding the mechanics, and then influencing how they train consistently enough to drive performance."

### Racing Styles

**Cavic:**
> "When he was up and ready to go, he was really good. When he felt good and ready to go. More like: I'm just gonna try to stay up with me."

**Peirsol:**
> "Aaron would be good when he felt like crap. I don't care where you are, I'm gonna finish off and beat you."

**From Coaching Standpoint:**
> "It was kind of fun, difficult at times to work with such dichotomous, both ends of the spectrum in terms of athletes who could be really good."

## Breaststroke Coaching

### Individual Approach

**Core Philosophy:**
> "It really is dependent upon each individual athlete. You work on the strengths that an athlete presents you, and then you try to improve upon the weaknesses."

**What Impacts the Stroke:**
- Timing of the stroke
- Flexibility of ankles, hips, knees, shoulders
- All those components add to a stroke

**Reverse Engineering Problem:**
> "We look at the successful athletes generally, then we take that information, reverse engineer it, and say 'oh that's the key.' With breaststroke, each one of these athletes I think is separate, and you work within the framework of that stroke."

**Not Coaching Everyone the Same:**
> "To think we're all going to coach athletes like [narrow-kicking sprinters] is not really the best route to go for breaststroke."

### Dave's Breaststroke Advantage

**Personal Experience:**
> "I'm fortunate with breaststroke swimmers. I've had a lot of really successful breaststroke swimmers over my career, and I always attributed to the fact that when I was a kid swimming, I was a breaststroker. So I know what it feels like. I can still feel it again when I think through it and visualize it and remember what that's like. I think that's helped me coach breaststroke swimmers—having that kinesthetic sense of 'I get your stroke, I understand it.'"

### Rebecca Soni vs. Yulia Efimova

**Differentiation:**
> "I understand the differentiation between a Rebecca Soni stroke and a Yulia Efimova stroke."

**Efimova's Three Strokes:**
> "When she joined my group and I watched her race, she basically had three very distinct methodologies for swimming breaststroke—very different. She would incorporate these three different skill sets through the course of a 200 breaststroke. We tried to tone her down into more two different strokes, which I think she's mastered pretty well. But yet again, it's her ability to kind of change gears significantly different."

**Rebecca Soni:**
> "Rebecca Soni swam very much like the breaststrokers you see on the men's side now. She does not have a great kick. Her stroke—you'd watch it and it was just like this little thing out in front and this little complimentary kick."

**The Hummingbird:**
> "A coach once referred to speaking about Rebecca Soni: she was kind of the hummingbird of breaststroke swimmers—what she does doesn't make any sense."

**Coaching Approach:**
> "It's not what I would teach. I would never coach advanced breaststroke like Rebecca Soni swam. I would coach Rebecca Soni to really emphasize what worked for her. Same thing with Efimova—I would make a couple little changes but really not change up her stroke too dramatically because it works well for her."

### Adam Peaty Comparison

> "I think Peaty probably swims breaststroke a lot like Rebecca Soni did, and obviously it works really well for Peaty."

### Three Key Components

**Universal Elements (look different for each swimmer):**

**1. The Kick:**
> "I teach my athletes regardless of how they kick to really drive that kick straighter back. Compared to when I was a kid it was 'up, out, together.' Now it's very quick and very rapid, straighter motion backwards. I look for that, but it may look different from Efimova and might look different from Rebecca Soni."

**2. (Implied: The Pull/Scull)**

**3. (Implied: Timing/Coordination)**

## Freestyle Technical Discussion

### Leah Smith's Stroke Evolution

**High School:**
- Stroke was a lot more choppy
- Would recover more from the elbow versus the wrist

**Change Over Time:**
> "She has lengthened it out. There are a few different components I look for in each stroke that are the same, but at the same time within the framework of each athlete, they're incorporated just a little bit differently."

**Key Component - Acceleration Past Pockets:**
> "One of them is accelerating that stroke—I call it past the pockets. If you had pockets in your suit, you'd be accelerating past your pockets into the recovery. That's where Leah has changed her stroke dramatically."

**Recovery Change:**
> "She really has exited into the recovery from the wrist a little bit more than what it was when she was in high school, which was more elbow driven."

## Freestyle with Dolphin Kick

### The Concept

**Historical Attempts:**
- Michael Klim used it in last 15m before finish
- Alex Popov worked on it but didn't have core flexibility
- Every time he did a stroke to do fly kick, had to readjust body
- Didn't work out for him

**Dave's Experience:**
> "We've done drills like that in practice to kind of engage the core a little bit more. Coordination becomes the key. It's tougher to keep that dolphin kick going as fast as you probably have to to go along with a freestyle pull."

**Not New:**
> "It's not something new, it's something that has been out there before in the past."

### Will It Work?

**Dave's Opinion:**
> "It's like everything. There's going to be a coach out there that incorporates some of these ideas and thinks that's the direction to go, and they'll incorporate it with their athlete and they'll stumble upon an athlete that does it really really well."

**Mechanics Consideration:**
> "It's kind of like that backstroke breakout that some athletes are doing where they're breaking out their arm in front of their body into the recovery. It kind of works. You have to weigh the increase in drag force that accompanies greater force when you pull in front of your body."

**Awkwardness:**
> "I get that, I understand the mechanics of it, but it just seems awkward to roll into the first stroke when you break out."

**Patient Coach Required:**
> "It's really a coach who's patient enough to really push through—keep pushing through—and you're going to find an athlete that will be able to do that."

### Breaststroke Over-Water Recovery Analogy

**Similar Pattern:**
> "Go back years and years ago—it drives me crazy, it still does—when athletes would recover over the water in breaststroke and coaches started to develop drills for recovery over the water."

**The Requirement:**
> "The only way a breaststroke pull recovery over the water works is you've got a really effective kick. If you don't have a very effective kick, your hips drop and your kick drops, and it doesn't make a lot of sense."

**Drill Problem:**
> "Coaches for a while were incorporating these different drills because somebody was successful doing that."

**Rūta Meilutytė Example:**
> "Rūta Meilutytė who's got her elbow recovery—I get it, I understand you reduce the resistance going over the top versus going through the water. But what happens if you don't have a good kick? Hips start to sink, you put a lot of pressure on the lower body, and she gets disqualified a lot of times because she does it."

**Selective Application:**
> "The dolphin kick freestyle thing has probably more chance. Maybe it's selective into a finish or selective into a turn or something like that where you can kind of endure that."

## Age-Appropriate Coaching

### Don't Downsize Elite Technique

**The Problem:**
> "A lot of times the developmental coaches who are really motivated to get their kids to learn well—we take an adult elite athlete stroke and try to downsize it to an introductory level of swimming. I think that's the wrong approach. You got to do age-appropriate and skill-appropriate work."

### Butterfly Teaching Example

**Observing Elite Swimmers:**
> "If you watch butterfly swimmers underwater—Michael Phelps or a really good athlete—you'll see there's two strokes per kicks, or two kicks per stroke cycle."

**The Mistake:**
> "You introduce that really early, and what kids do is they STOP their stroke to accommodate these two kicks."

**The Better Way:**
> "I make my age group coaches coach from the hips up instead of from the legs up. What I mean is: don't teach them two kicks per stroke. Teach them to pop the hips on the catch in front and just feel the flow of the stroke. Don't even teach the kick with the stroke."

**Result:**
> "When you do that, kids won't stop in front to kick-pull-stop in back to kick-stop the kick-pull-stop the kick. If you teach them early on that process, it takes them for years to get over that."

**Natural Flow:**
> "If you just teach the hips connected to the upper body, the stroke will flow through. The second kick is natural—it's a very natural flow to the stroke. You don't even teach that."

**Key Lesson:**
> "We try to teach how Michael Phelps swims versus what an eight-year-old should—how the eight-year-old should swim. You gotta be really careful with that kind of stuff with developmental kids."

### The Michael Phelps Two-Kick Problem

**Another Example:**
> "When I was 19 years old, the coach telling me: 'Look at Michael Phelps' video—his two kicks are equal. This is what you have to achieve because this doubles your kick effort. If your first kick is that powerful, your second kick is that powerful, it's twice as fast.'"

**Doesn't Always Work:**
> "But it doesn't always work that way. They can learn that when they're 14, 15, 16—but not when they're seven, eight, nine."

## Key Takeaways for Coaches

### Sports Psychology Integration
- Use sports psychologists for communication skills, not just athletes' mental health
- Develop toolbox of mental skills (visualization, coping mechanisms, relaxation)
- Can work remotely via Zoom if relationship established
- Complements coach-athlete relationship rather than replacing it

### Visualization Mastery
- Use daily before practice to calm athletes
- Re-energize past great performances
- Prepare for future discomfort (weather, venue, pressure)
- Coaches should visualize their own practice sessions
- Visualize your day for better time efficiency

### Communication Skills
- Understand you're always different in how you receive and deliver information
- Better communication = better performances
- Athletes need to know you care, not just that you want results
- Last years of USC: Dave got better at this and athletes appreciated it

### Facility Preparation
- Always do walk-through before competition
- Show ready rooms, camera positions, procedure
- Reduces anxiety through familiarity
- Rebecca Soni Olympic Trials lesson: never assume they're ready for bright lights

### Trust Over Friendship
- Be friendly, not friends
- Maintain professional boundaries
- Be more parental than buddy
- Don't let athletes manipulate you
- Don't be possessive of their performance
- Stand firm on what they need to do
- Sometimes: "Just trust me" is enough

### Talent Recognition
- Great athletes will be great regardless
- Right coach is important link, not only possible coach
- Don't be possessive: "only I could coach this athlete"
- Stay one chapter ahead (teaching assistant analogy)
- Let young athletes know their potential without pushing them there
- Biggest challenge: coaching athlete who came from another successful coach

### Individualization in Technique
- Breaststroke especially: each athlete is separate
- Work within framework of their stroke
- Don't reverse engineer success and apply universally
- Know what it feels like (Dave's breaststroke advantage)
- Three key components look different for each swimmer
- Freestyle: accelerate past the pockets into recovery

### Age-Appropriate Development
- Don't downsize elite technique to beginner level
- Do age-appropriate and skill-appropriate work
- Butterfly: teach from hips up, not legs up
- Don't teach two kicks—teach hip pop and flow
- What works for Michael Phelps doesn't work for 8-year-olds
- They can learn advanced concepts at 14-16, not 7-9

### Managing Different Athletes
- Michael Cavic vs Aaron Peirsol: both talented, totally different work ethic
- Don't throw away kid who doesn't like to train
- Find ways to manipulate training to get them to work
- Understand their racing style (front-runner vs closer)
- Adjust coaching to athlete, not athlete to your one method

### Innovation with Patience
- Freestyle with dolphin kick: requires patient coach
- Will work for someone eventually
- Selective application (finish, turn) more likely
- Compare to backstroke breakout or breaststroke recovery variations
- Understand mechanics but don't dismiss because it seems awkward
