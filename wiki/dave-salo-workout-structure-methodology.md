# Dave Salo: Workout Structure and Training Methodology

**Category:** Training, Workout Design, Coaching Philosophy, Progressive Training  
**Coach:** Dave Salo  
**Environment:** Irvine Novaquatics (Nova), USC (retired), post-grad athletes

## Summary

Dave Salo's workout methodology is formulaic, progressive, and detail-oriented, stemming from his academic background (PhD, laboratory work). Every practice follows a thematic structure designed to build power stroke-by-stroke, recruit maximum muscle fibers, and create race-specific stress. His approach prioritizes quality over volume, packages work into digestible rounds, and maintains athlete engagement through variety within structure.

## Universal Workout Structure

### Thematic Consistency

> *"Thematically every practice is the same. I don't bury things up."* - Dave Salo

**Every Practice:**
1. Warm-up (minimal heart rate elevation)
2. Stroke development (power-building, detail-oriented)
3. Resistance work (cords, parachutes, wet cords)
4. Race pace sets
5. Cool-down

---

## Stroke Development Phase

### Philosophy: Power Per Stroke

**Goal:**  
Maximize power ONE stroke at a time

**Progression:**
1. One stroke
2. Two strokes
3. Five strokes
4. Whole length (25m/y)
5. 50+ meters

### Example Sequence:

**Knuckle Paddles + Fins (75s):**
- Grab paddle with knuckles
- Moves pressure point back into mid-arm
- Forces elbows to stay up
- Focus on THREE FAST STROKES per 25
- "Surge" - feel body move with speed

**Regular Paddles (50s):**
- Contrast with knuckle paddles
- Feel the difference
- Focus on FOUR STROKES coming off wall
- Emphasis on posture off wall
- Passive speed, then surge into strokes

**Full Speed (25s):**
- Pick a breathing spot
- Only one breath, same spot every rep
- Full speed execution
- Controlled breathing pattern

---

## Detail-Oriented Emphasis

### What Salo Watches:

**Posture:**
- Coming off walls
- During passive glide
- Throughout stroke cycle
- Back flat, streamlined

**Speed Into Walls:**
- Habit formation
- Last 10m of each 25 = fast
- Good kick
- Maintain velocity approaching wall

**Speed Off Walls:**
- Passive glide
- Minimize resistance/drag
- Maximize efficiency
- Then surge into strokes

**The Surge:**
- Body should feel speed change with each stroke
- Swimmers must FEEL their body move
- Not just moving fast, but feeling the acceleration

## The Formula Approach

**Salo's Method:**

## Workout Packaging

### The 3-4 Round Philosophy

**Old Salo (Early Nova Days):**
- 7-8 rounds
- Just kept adding more
- "Not functioning"

**Modern Salo (Last 2+ years):**
- Restrict to **3-4 rounds max**
- 15-20 minutes of really good work per package
- Move to next thing
- "Fewer rounds, do it really well"

**Result:**  
"I don't think any of my athletes consider practices boring because there's no repeat. There's repeat things but the way I package them they're generally pretty different."

## Engagement Strategy

### Why Break Things Up?

**Question:** "Dave, why do you break things up so much? How come you like it broken up like this?"

**Answer:**  
*"Then they're engaged in the process, right? It's more interesting. It just keeps your mind thinking about what the next thing is to do. And all of it's purposeful. It's not just throw stuff out there, it's all purposeful."*

**Method:**
- Constant variety within structure
- Mind stays engaged
- Always something new coming
- No monotony
- Every element has purpose

## Resistance Training

### Friday Protocol (Example)

**Sequence:**
1. Stroke development work (paddles, drills)
2. Parachute resistance sets
3. Race pace application

### Parachute Work Philosophy

**Why Singapore Parachutes:**
- Can customize branding (Nova logo)
- Hold up better than metal-framed versions
- Variable resistance (open/closed)

**Two Modes:**

**1. Maximum Resistance (Closed):**
- 100s - feel for stroke
- Accentuate stroke feel
- Mostly posture and stroke awareness
- Using resistance to enhance proprioception

**2. Minimum Resistance (Open):**
- 25s - race tempo
- Speed work with slight resistance
- Accommodating resistance

### The Race Tempo Under Load Principle

> *"My mantra the last few years is trying to get race tempo when loaded. I think that's the key to that next development."*

**Problem:**
- Athletes put load on → slow things down
- Compromise stroke to manage resistance
- Elbows drop

**Salo's Goal:**
- Race tempo WITHOUT compromising stroke
- More specific training
- Don't let elbows drop under load
- Maintain stroke integrity at race pace

**Specific Protocol (Example):**
- 4 × (100 + 4×25 + 75 + 25)
- 100: max resistance, feel stroke
- First 3 × 25: max resistance, only FOUR STROKES max effort, race tempo
- 4th × 25: open parachute, minimum resistance, whole 25 race tempo
- First 3 × 75: max effort ANY TWO of the 25s
- Last 75: whole 75 maxed out
- Plus 25: fins, active recovery

## Mid-Pool Speed Development

### Inspired by Vlad Morozov

**Observation:**  
Morozov could move his body forward on the FIRST stroke

**Most Swimmers:**  
Takes 3-4 strokes to really get body velocity moving

**Salo's Challenge:**  
How to teach everyone that same capability?

**Method:**
- Dead stops mid-pool
- 4-5 strokes maximum effort
- Learn to grab water immediately
- Develop instant body movement

**Sets Include:**
- Push and glide to line 4
- One stroke per black line starting at line 3
- Avoid catch-up drill trap
- Every stroke = full power
- Distance per stroke emphasis

## Gear Systems (Tempo Control)

### Third Gear, Fourth Gear, Fifth Gear

**By Time:**
- Chart-based on 100 best time
- Not by effort/feel
- Specific target times
- Objective measurement

**Purpose:**
- "Sneak up on speed a little bit"
- Don't want loss of efficiency in stroke
- Build into speed progressively
- Maintain stroke integrity

**Background:**

**Early Nova (1990s):**
- Aaron Peirsol, Amanda Beard, Jason Lezak
- Young kids who didn't know better
- "Just go fast all the time"

**Post-Grad Group:**
- Older, experienced athletes
- More fragile mentally/physically
- Must build to speed more carefully
- But still hit specific target times

## Volume Philosophy

### Not Volume-Centric

**Question:** "How do you keep track of volume in the workout?"

**Answer:**  
*"I don't keep track of any of that stuff. I'm not volume-centric and so I don't worry about how far we're going. It's really the content. I'm trying to take as many maximum effort strokes as possible in the course of a workout."*

**Focus:**
- Maximum effort strokes
- Quality over quantity
- Content matters, not distance
- Can't tell you total volume without adding it up

## Friday Sample Workout

### Complete Breakdown

**WARM-UP:**
- Not detailed (minimal)
- "Bunch of pansies" on heart rate monitor
- Low intensity

**STROKE DEVELOPMENT:**

**Set 1: Knuckle Paddles + Regular Paddles**
- 75s knuckle paddles (3 fast strokes per 25)
- 50s regular paddles (4 strokes off wall, surge)
- 25s full speed (one breath, pick spot)
- Focus: Power development, feel, surge

**Set 2: Distance Per Stroke (DPS)**
- Push and glide to line 3 (no stroke before)
- One stroke per black line minimum
- Head reaches line = pull
- Avoid catch-up drill
- Long, powerful strokes
- Strong kick + posture

**3rd/4th/5th Gear by Time:**
- Chart-based targets
- Progressive speeds
- Maintain efficiency

**POWER WORK:**

**Rotation Kicks + Breakouts:**
- 360° rotation kick × 3
- Break out into 4 strokes freestyle full speed
- Casual kick to finish (first rounds)
- Later rounds: full speed to wall

**Push + Glide + Fast Kick + Fast Swim:**
- Push/glide to line 4
- Fast kick to line 5
- Fast swim to wall
- 5-second fast kick against wall
- Fast flip turn

**RESISTANCE WORK:**

**Parachute Set (4 rounds):**
- 100 max resistance (feel stroke)
- 4 × 25 max resistance (4 strokes race tempo each)
- 75 max effort (two 25s of choice, then all three)
- 25 + fins (active recovery)

**COOL-DOWN:**
- Active recovery
- Minimal

## Practical Applications

### For Coaches:

**DO:**
- Package work in 3-4 round sets
- Make every element purposeful
- Build power stroke by stroke
- Use resistance to enhance feel
- Base work on best times, not arbitrary effort
- Break things up to maintain engagement
- Sneak up on speed with older athletes

**DON'T:**
- Add rounds just to add volume
- Lose detail focus
- Let athletes slow down under load
- Use test sets
- Ignore stroke integrity for speed
- Bore athletes with monotony

### For Swimmers:

**Focus On:**
- Every stroke with maximum effort
- Feel the surge
- Posture off every wall
- Speed into every wall
- Race tempo under load
- Specific targets (not just effort)

## The Nightmare Philosophy

### Why Create Stress?

**Goal:**  
Prepare athletes for the worst moments of racing

**Method:**
- Physical fatigue (cords → parachutes → race pace)
- Mental challenge (constant variety)
- Psychological stress ("when you feel your worst")
- Technical demand (perfect execution under duress)

**Result:**  
When race gets hard, athletes have been there before

## Bottom Line

**Salo's Workout DNA:**

1. **Academically rooted** - Formulaic, purposeful
2. **Progressively built** - Stroke by stroke to race pace
3. **Detail-obsessed** - Every element matters
4. **Engagement-focused** - 3-4 rounds, constant variety
5. **Quality over volume** - Maximum effort strokes, not distance
6. **Race-specific** - Everything tied to race times and tempo
7. **Stress-inducing** - "Where dreams become nightmares"

**Result:** Olympic champions across all distances and strokes
