# Competition Psychology for Swimmers

**Source:** *Mental Training for Peak Performance* by Dr. Steven Ungerleider  
**Core Principle:** "An immobilized brain is why the strongest or fastest athlete will lose to a competitor who, on paper, seems inferior. Physical skill is important, but mental conditioning, toughness, focus, and ability to recover swiftly from setbacks determine the champion."  
**Application:** Race-day strategies to control nerves, maintain focus, and perform under pressure

---

## The Competition Mindset: What Separates Winners from Losers

### Nick Bollettieri (trained Agassi, Sampras, Serena, Venus Williams)

**On elite competition:**
> "At this level of the game, all of these players (and their opponents) have roughly similar physical and mechanical skills. Where the consistent winners outperform their opponents is in their mental abilities. They can access their physical talents 'on demand.' They recover quickly, almost instantaneously, from adversity."

**Swimming translation:**

In championship finals:
- Everyone is fast (or they wouldn't be there)
- Everyone has trained hard
- Everyone has good technique

**The winner is decided by:**
1. Who stays relaxed under pressure
2. Who executes their race plan (not someone else's)
3. Who recovers when things go wrong (bad start, slow turn)
4. Who doesn't panic in the pain cave

**Your job as a coach:** Teach these mental skills.

## Pre-Race Psychology: Managing Nerves and Anxiety

### The Tension Paradox

**The problem:**  
Too relaxed = no fire, no edge  
Too tense = tight muscles, shallow breathing, panic

**The goal:** Find the optimal arousal level—energized but not frantic.

### Janet Evans (4 Olympic golds, 3 world records)

**On pre-race relaxation:**
> "No matter how pumped I was before a race, no matter how important the event, I always reminded myself that I had to be completely relaxed so that my breathing and mental imagery flowed. If I'm not relaxed, it sets off a negative chain of events."

**What happens when you're too tight:**
1. Muscle tension → restricted range of motion
2. Shallow breathing → less oxygen → faster fatigue
3. Increased anxiety → adrenaline dump → "blowing up" mid-race
4. Overthinking → paralysis by analysis

**Evans' solution:**
- **Week before:** Deep breathing practice daily (10 min)
- **Race morning:** Meditation or quiet visualization (10-15 min)
- **Ready room:** Breathing exercises, NOT frantic pep talks
- **Behind the blocks:** Three deep breaths, shoulders relaxed

---

## The Relaxation-Tension Cycle (Finding Your Sweet Spot)

### How to Practice This in Training

**Concept:** Alternate between tight and relaxed to learn the difference and control it.

**In practice (after warm-up):**

**Round 1: Swim TIGHT (50m easy)**
- Tense shoulders, clench jaw, short breathing
- Feel how RESTRICTED and SLOW this is

**Round 2: Swim RELAXED (50m easy)**
- Drop shoulders, loose jaw, full breathing
- Feel how FLUID and EASY this is

**Round 3: Repeat the cycle 4-6 times**
- Tight → Relaxed → Tight → Relaxed

**Debrief:**
- "Which felt faster?" (Spoiler: relaxed)
- "Which felt easier?" (Also relaxed)
- "Can you call up relaxation on command now?"

**Race application:**

**Behind the blocks:**
- Shrug shoulders to ears → DROP (release tension)
- Clench fists → RELEASE (let go)
- Three deep breaths (4 sec in, 6 sec out)
- "I'm loose. I'm ready. Let's go."

## Managing Pre-Race Anxiety: Practical Strategies

### Strategy 1: Control What You Can Control

**Terry Laughlin (USA Swimming technique specialist):**
> "The things that cause anxiety are the things over which you don't believe you have control."

**What you CAN'T control:**
- Who else is in the final
- How fast they went in prelims
- The pool conditions
- What the crowd thinks

**What you CAN control:**
- Your warm-up routine
- Your race plan
- Your breathing
- Your self-talk
- Your focus

**Application:**

**Anxious thought:**  
❌ "What if I don't make finals?"  
❌ "What if I swim slower than prelims?"

**Redirect to controllables:**  
✅ "I'll execute my race plan."  
✅ "I'll focus on my turns—they're weapons."  
✅ "I'll stay loose and attack the last 50."

**Result:** Anxiety drops when you focus on process, not outcome.

---

### Strategy 2: Narrow Your Focus

**Laughlin (continued):**
> "In open-water triathlons, there's the distraction of swimming in a knot of people. You need to go through a rehearsal process, learning how to concentrate on your technique. I tell triathletes to swim as if there are no other people out there and they're doing a swimming workout."

**Swimming application:**

**Wide focus (causes anxiety):**
- "Everyone in this final is faster than me"
- "I need a best time or I'm a failure"
- "What if I blow up on the back half?"

**Narrow focus (builds confidence):**
- "First 25: smooth, controlled"
- "Turn 1: aggressive push-off"
- "Last 50: this is where I attack"

**How to practice narrow focus:**

**In practice:**
- Before a hard set: "Your only job is to execute perfect turns. Times don't matter."
- Result: Athletes focus on ONE THING, not ten things

**In races:**
- Break the race into chunks (not 200m of pain, but 4 × 50m with specific jobs)
- Chunk 1: Nail the start and underwater
- Chunk 2: Smooth, controlled, hit the race pace
- Chunk 3: Stay loose, keep the tempo
- Chunk 4: ATTACK—this is my race

**Result:** Less overwhelm, better execution.

---

### Strategy 3: Pre-Race Rituals (Anchoring Calm)

**Concept:** Build a repeatable pre-race routine that anchors calmness and confidence.

**Elite examples:**

**Justin Dumais (13 national diving championships):**
- ✅ Get plenty of rest (confidence fails when body is fatigued)
- ✅ Run through mental tape before competition
- ✅ Repeat mantra: "I'm the best"
- ✅ Focus on ONE technique

**Kimiko Soldati (US National Diving Team, 7 years):**
- ✅ Use rituals: Visualize, meditate, talk to other athletes
- ✅ Some athletes distract themselves; others stay hyper-focused
- ✅ Find YOUR ritual—there's no universal formula

**Swimming ritual examples:**

**Athlete A (distraction-focused):**
- Listen to pump-up music in ready room
- Chat with teammates
- Quick visualization (2 min)
- Behind blocks: headphones out, game face on

**Athlete B (focus-focused):**
- Sit alone in ready room
- Eyes closed, mental race walk-through (5 min)
- No talking, just breathing
- Behind blocks: three deep breaths, affirmation

**The key:** Whatever you choose, DO THE SAME THING EVERY TIME. Rituals create familiarity → familiarity reduces anxiety.

## Mid-Race Psychology: Staying Present and Executing

### The "Centipede Effect" (Nick Bollettieri)

**Foreword to the book:**
> "If the lowly centipede had to think about coordinating each of its legs to get from Point A to Point B, it would be totally immobilized—and I have worked with some athletes who were not far from that state of mind."

**Swimming translation:**

**Overthinking MID-RACE kills performance:**
- "Am I going too fast?"
- "Did I just miss my turn count?"
- "Is that guy catching me?"

**Result:** Paralysis. You tighten up. You slow down.

**The solution: Trust your preparation and EXECUTE.**

### Justin Dumais' "Stupid Smarts"

**His competitive advantage:**
> "I have 'stupid smarts'—the ability to turn off my brain and let doubt disappear. I can win it if I let my mind go."

**How to train this:**

**In practice:**
- **Hard race-pace sets:** "Your only job is to FEEL the speed. Don't think—SWIM."
- **Example:** 8 × 100 IM on 3:00 at race pace
  - Instruction: "No counting strokes. No checking the pace clock mid-100. Just ATTACK."

**Result:** Athletes learn to trust their bodies, not overthink.

**In races:**
- **Mental cue at the turn:** "ATTACK this wall" (quick, instinctive)
- **NOT:** "Okay, let me analyze my stroke count and compare it to prelims and adjust my tempo and..." (overthinking)

## Dealing with Pain: The "Deflect and Attack" Strategy

### Benji Durden (2:09 marathon PR)

**3-stage mental pacing for marathons:**

**Stage 1 (first 10 miles):** Warm-up, leisurely, 10 sec slower than optimal pace  
- Talks to other runners, observes scenery, stays relaxed

**Stage 2 (next 10 miles):** Quiet down, get serious, check in with body  
- Introspective, lets go of internal chatter, prepares for the real race

**Stage 3 (last 6.2 miles):** THE ACTUAL RACE  
- Intensity high, pain is there BUT IS DEFLECTED away from body and mind
- Intimidates competitors by surging, creating psychological damage

**Swimming application:**

### The 200 IM Mental Race Plan (Durden-style)

**Chunk 1 (Fly):** Controlled aggression  
- "This is my warm-up stroke. Smooth, powerful, NOT an all-out sprint."
- Goal: Get ahead, but don't blow up

**Chunk 2 (Back):** Stay loose, hold position  
- "This is where I stay relaxed and efficient. No wasted energy."
- Goal: Maintain speed, don't tighten up

**Chunk 3 (Breast):** Check in, prepare to attack  
- "Pain is starting—irrelevant. I'm about to unleash on free."
- Goal: Strong breast, set up the finish

**Chunk 4 (Free):** THE ACTUAL RACE  
- "This is MY leg. This is where I hunt the wall."
- **Pain is acknowledged but DEFLECTED**—"Yeah, it hurts. Doesn't matter. GO."

**Result:** You're not surprised by pain. You EXPECT it, and you have a plan for it.

## Post-Mistake Recovery: The "Instant Rebound" Mindset

### Nick Bollettieri (on Roger Federer, 2004 Tennis Masters Canada final)

**Situation:** Federer down 0-40 on his serve (match point for opponent)

**Outcome:** Federer wins the game, wins the match

**Bollettieri:**
> "Federer was in total control of his mind and obviously believed he could still win that crucial game—and he did exactly that."

**Swimming application:**

**Bad start? Slow turn? Missed underwater?**

**Two options:**

**Option A (mental collapse):**
- "Ugh, I blew it. This race is over."
- Result: You give up, slow down, confirm your fear

**Option B (instant rebound):**
- "Okay, that wasn't great. Doesn't matter—LET'S GO."
- Result: You attack the next 50, salvage the race, maybe still PR

### How to Train Instant Rebound

**In practice:**

**Scenario 1: Blown start**
- Instruction: "Deliberately screw up your first underwater. Then ATTACK the rest of the 50."
- Goal: Learn to recover from mistakes without spiraling

**Scenario 2: Missed turn**
- Instruction: "Miss your streamline off one wall. Then make the next turn PERFECT."
- Goal: One mistake doesn't define the race

**Result:** Athletes who can shake off errors and refocus IMMEDIATELY.

## Race-Day Focus Strategies

### Strategy 1: The "One Thing" Focus

**Summer Sanders (4 Olympic medals):**
> "I have to concentrate on the end of my training laps because that is where I have problems. I try and visualize a full stroke—not a short, quick one, but a full, relaxed one. If I can do this in practice, then it will come naturally in competitive races."

**Application:**

**Pick ONE focus point for your race:**
- "Full strokes on the last 25" (not "swim fast and don't die")
- "Explosive underwaters" (not "beat everyone")
- "Loose shoulders, fast tempo" (not "hit my goal time")

**Why this works:**
- Simple cue = executable under pressure
- Complex cue = paralysis by analysis

---

### Strategy 2: The "Trust Your Training" Mantra

**Eddie Reese (University of Texas, 38 NCAA champions, 22 Olympians):**
> "Each kid must get in the pool each day and see himself win. Work hard, both in the pool and on dry land; you have to visualize your best effort."

**Race-day translation:**
- You've visualized this race 50+ times
- You've trained the plan
- You've done the work
- **Now TRUST IT and EXECUTE**

**Affirmation behind the blocks:**
- "I've done the work. I'm ready. Let's go."

---

### Strategy 3: Tuning Into Your Body (Flow State)

**Summer Sanders:**
> "You have to tune in to the movement and the dance of swimming. You have to love the feel and sensation of the water to learn the basics."

**Mike Pigg (triathlete, #2 ranked in the world, 1995):**
> "Swimming allows me to go into that altered state, that performance zone that is a relaxed, completely focused place."

**How to access flow:**

**Pre-race:**
- Breathing exercises (calm the nervous system)
- Visualization (rehearse the feeling of smooth, fast swimming)

**During race:**
- Feel the water pressure on your forearms (the "catch")
- Feel the rhythm of your breathing
- Feel the explosiveness of your turns

**Result:** You're PRESENT in your body, not stuck in your head.

## Dealing with Competition Pressure: High-Stakes Meets

### The "Celebrate Competition" Mindset (Janet Evans)

**Evans:**
> "I want to enjoy the celebration of competition, the rhythm and dance in the water."

**Translation:** Reframe pressure as OPPORTUNITY, not threat.

**Threat mindset:**
- "If I don't make finals, I'm a failure"
- "Everyone is watching—what if I mess up?"
- Result: Anxiety, tension, poor performance

**Opportunity mindset:**
- "I get to race the best swimmers in the country—this is awesome"
- "I've trained for this moment—let's see what I've got"
- Result: Excitement, energy, peak performance

**How to build this:**

**In practice:**
- Reframe hard sets: "This is a PRIVILEGE, not a punishment"
- After brutal main sets: "That was HARD—and I just got better"

**On race day:**
- "I'm not here to survive. I'm here to COMPETE."

## Practical Implementation for Coaches

### Week 1: Teach Pre-Race Relaxation
- Breathing exercises (10 min after practice)
- Tension-relaxation cycles (practice behind the blocks)

### Week 2: Build Pre-Race Rituals
- Each athlete designs their personal ritual (music vs. silence, visualization vs. distraction)
- Practice it before time trials

### Week 3: Practice Mid-Race Focus
- Hard sets with ONE focus cue ("Full strokes on the finish")
- Debrief: "Did you stay focused, or did you drift?"

### Week 4: Train Instant Rebound
- Deliberately mess up starts/turns in practice
- Practice recovering without mental collapse

### Taper: Lock in the Mental Game
- Daily visualization (10-15 min)
- Pre-race ritual rehearsal
- Affirmations before every swim

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: "Get Pumped Up!" (Too Much Arousal)
**Problem:** Frantic energy → tight muscles → poor execution  
**Solution:** Stay calm and controlled; energy is good, but RELAXED energy is better

### Mistake 2: Focusing on Opponents Instead of Your Race
**Problem:** "I need to beat that guy" → reactive racing, not proactive  
**Solution:** "I need to execute MY plan" → you control the controllables

### Mistake 3: No Pre-Race Routine
**Problem:** Every race feels different → inconsistent performance  
**Solution:** Build a ritual, practice it, repeat it

### Mistake 4: Mental Collapse After a Mistake
**Problem:** One bad turn → "This race is over" → giving up  
**Solution:** Train instant rebound—shake it off, ATTACK the next 50

## Measuring Progress

### Ask your athletes after meets:

**Anxiety management:**
- "On a scale of 1-10, how relaxed were you behind the blocks?"
- "Did your pre-race routine help, or did you skip it?"

**Mid-race focus:**
- "Did you execute your race plan, or did you panic and go rogue?"
- "When pain hit, did you deflect it or let it shut you down?"

**Recovery from mistakes:**
- "When you had a bad start/turn, did you rebound or give up?"
- "How fast did you refocus?"

**The goal:** Athletes who perform their best WHEN IT MATTERS, not just in practice.

## Key Takeaways

✅ **Relaxation under pressure = access to speed**—tension kills performance  
✅ **Control what you can control** (race plan, breathing, focus)—ignore the rest  
✅ **Pre-race rituals anchor calm and confidence**—build them, practice them  
✅ **"Stupid smarts" = trust your training, don't overthink mid-race**  
✅ **Pain is expected and deflected**—acknowledge it, then ATTACK through it  
✅ **Instant rebound from mistakes** separates champions from everyone else  
✅ **Celebrate competition**—it's an opportunity, not a threat
