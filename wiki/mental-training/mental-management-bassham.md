# Mental Management for Swim Coaches

**Source:** *With Winning in Mind* by Lanny Bassham (Olympic shooting champion, 1976 gold medal)  
**Significance:** Mental system tested under Olympic pressure — winner's mindset, Triad State, mental programs  
**Application:** Race preparation, consistency under pressure, building champion attitudes

---

## Core Insight: Winning is About How You Think

> "95% of all winning is accomplished by only 5% of participants. The primary thing that separates winners from others is the way they think. **Winners are convinced they will finish first. Others hope to finish first.**"

### Malcolm Cooper Story (1978 World Championships)
- Expected to win standing position (held world record)
- Rifle damaged — had to borrow another shooter's rifle
- Different trigger pull, weight, balance = huge disadvantage
- **His response:** "Wouldn't it be something special to win with a borrowed rifle. I'm going to do it!"
- **Result:** Won + set world record

**Lesson for coaches:** Circumstances don't matter when an athlete expects to win. Build that mindset.

## The Triad State: Balance of Mental Power

### Three Mental Processes

When **Conscious, Subconscious, and Self-Image** work together in balance = **Triad State** = effortless high performance.

#### 1. **Conscious Mind**
- Focuses on ONE thing at a time
- Controls what you picture
- Runs your mental program

#### 2. **Subconscious Mind**
- Source of all mental power
- Handles billions of functions simultaneously
- Automates skills through repetition
- **Skill = doing something consciously long enough for it to become automated**

#### 3. **Self-Image**
- "Who you are" — your identity
- Controls what you believe is "like you"
- If winning isn't "like you," you have no chance of winning at all

---

## The Triad State in Swimming

### Balanced Swimmer (Triad State):
- **Conscious:** Focused on race process (tempo, stroke count, turn execution)
- **Subconscious:** Technique is automated — 6-beat kick, high elbow, streamline happen without thinking
- **Self-Image:** "It's like me to swim fast. I am a champion."
- **Result:** Race flows effortlessly, minimal effort, maximum performance

### Out-of-Balance Swimmer (Common Problems):

**Problem 1: Too much Conscious (beginner)**
- Thinking about every stroke detail
- Technique not automated yet
- Self-Image: "I'm a beginner"
- **Fix:** More quality repetitions, build subconscious skill

**Problem 2: Low Self-Image (skilled but doesn't win)**
- Has the physical skills (subconscious technique is good)
- Trains well, races poorly
- Self-Image: "I'm a practice swimmer, not a racer"
- **Fix:** Directive affirmations, performance journaling, imprint winning identity

**Problem 3: Wrong Conscious focus (distracted by environment)**
- Focused on competitors, scoreboard, crowd, what coach will say
- Not running their own mental program
- **Fix:** Pre-race mental program, refocus cues

## Principles of Mental Management®

### Principle #1: Conscious Mind Can Only Concentrate on ONE Thing at a Time

**Bad:** "Don't false start. Don't swim too fast. Don't mess up your turn."  
- Swimmer pictures false starts, going out too fast, bad turns

**Good:** "Explosive start. Hold your tempo. Accelerate into the wall."  
- Swimmer pictures what TO DO

**Action Statement:**  
*"I take control of what I picture, choosing to think about what I want to create."*

---

### Principle #2: What You Say is Not Important. What You Cause Athletes to Picture is Crucial.

**Example:**  
- Coach says: "Whatever you do, DON'T BREATHE on your underwater!"
- Swimmer pictures: breathing on the underwater
- **Result:** Swimmer breathes

**Better:** "Hold your breath until breakout!"  
- Swimmer pictures: staying underwater, explosive breakout

**For swim coaches:**  
- Never say "Don't" — always state what TO DO
- "Just hit the wall" not "Don't miss your turn"
- "Fast hands on the finish" not "Don't glide into the wall"

**Action Statement:**  
*"I always give my swimmers commands in a positive way."*

---

### Principle #3: Subconscious Mind is the Source of All Mental Power

**Why this matters in swimming:**

- Conscious can only do ONE thing (focus on tempo)
- Subconscious can do BILLIONS (stroke mechanics, kick rhythm, breathing pattern, streamline, rotation — all at once)
- **To race fast, you MUST be in subconscious mode**

**How to build subconscious skill:**
1. **Repetition** — Do it over and over until automated
2. **Quality reps** — Perfect practice makes perfect (see: Phelps 10-day cycle — development occurs in repetition, not one fast swim)
3. **Mental rehearsal** — Visualization builds neural pathways (see separate page)

**Testing for subconscious automation:**
- Can the swimmer execute the skill while thinking about something else?
- Example: 6-beat kick happens automatically while counting strokes
- If they have to "remember" to do it = still conscious, not automated

## The Mental Program: Pre-Race Consistency

### Problem:
Most swimmers have inconsistent thoughts before races. Sometimes confident, sometimes anxious, sometimes distracted. **Inconsistent pictures = inconsistent performance.**

### Solution: Run a Mental Program

**Definition:** A series of thoughts that, when pictured, trigger the subconscious to perform the appropriate action.

**Why it works:**
- Occupies the Conscious Mind with YOUR pictures (not environment)
- An occupied Conscious Mind cannot choke
- Triggers automated subconscious performance

---

### Example Mental Program Structure (for 100m race)

**Phase 1: PREPARATION (5 min before race)**
1. Rehearse race plan (tempo, stroke count, splits)
2. Picture perfect start, underwater, breakout
3. Affirmation: "I am ready. This is like me."

**Phase 2: PERFORMANCE (on the blocks)**
1. Deep breath, shoulders down
2. Picture first stroke (water entry, catch)
3. Cue word: "Fast" or "Smooth"
4. **Let it happen** — trust subconscious

**Phase 3: POST-PERFORMANCE (immediately after)**
1. Reinforce good execution: "That's like me!"
2. If mistake: refocus immediately on next race/practice
3. Record in performance journal (see separate page)

**Key:** Same program, every race. Consistency in mental process = consistency in results.

## Building Self-Image: "It's Like Me"

### The Language of Champions

**Bad Self-Talk (imprints losing identity):**
- "I always choke in finals"
- "I'm not a sprinter"
- "I swim well in practice but not meets"

**Good Self-Talk (imprints winning identity):**
- "I am a fast finisher"
- "I race my best when it matters most"
- "I am a champion"

### Reinforcement Strategy

**After GOOD performances:**
- Say immediately: **"That's like me!"**
- Write it in performance journal
- Rehearse it again mentally that night

**After MISTAKES:**
- DO NOT replay the mistake
- DO NOT say "I always do that"
- Immediately refocus: "Next rep" or "Next race"
- Later: rehearse the CORRECT version 5 times

**Why:** You become what you think about and talk about. Every thought imprints. Control the imprint.

## Practical Application for Swim Coaches

### 1. **Eliminate Negative Commands**
❌ "Don't breathe into the wall"  
✅ "Long stroke to the finish"

❌ "Don't go out too fast"  
✅ "Hold your tempo through 50m"

❌ "Don't miss the podium again"  
✅ "You belong on the podium. This is your race."

---

### 2. **Teach Mental Programs (Not Just Physical Skills)**

**In practice:**
- Before each race-pace set: "Run your mental program"
- Rehearse start, underwaters, turn execution
- Practice reinforcement: "That's like me!" after good reps

**Before meets:**
- Write out mental program with athlete
- Practice it 10 times before race day
- Make it automatic (subconscious)

---

### 3. **Build Winning Self-Image Through Language**

**Team culture phrases:**
- "That's like us" (after good training)
- "Champions train like this"
- "We are fast finishers"
- "Pressure is a privilege"

**Individual affirmations:**
- Swimmer writes 5 copies of directive affirmation (see separate page)
- Reads + visualizes daily for 21 days
- Example: "I am the fastest 100 freestyler on this team. I train with intensity and race with confidence. I finish strong. That's like me."

---

### 4. **Performance Journaling (See Separate Page)**

**Three reasons athletes MUST keep a performance journal:**

1. **You can't manage what you don't measure** — Track progress, identify what works
2. **Coaching efficiency** — Coach knows what happened without guessing
3. **Self-Image building** — Writing imprints stronger than thinking

**What to record:**
- Workout details (times, stroke counts, rest intervals)
- What worked / what didn't
- Mental state (confident, anxious, focused, distracted)
- Reinforcement: "That's like me!" moments
- Directive affirmations
