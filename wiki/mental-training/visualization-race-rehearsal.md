# Visualization and Race Rehearsal for Swimmers

**Source:** *Mental Training for Peak Performance* by Dr. Steven Ungerleider  
**Core Principle:** "Your brain can't tell the difference between vivid imagination and real experience. Use that to your advantage."  
**Application:** Step-by-step protocols for mental race rehearsal, stroke visualization, and performance imagery

---

## Why Visualization Works: The Science

### Neural Pathway Activation

**What happens when you visualize swimming:**
- Motor cortex fires as if you're actually swimming
- Muscle memory patterns activate
- Heart rate can increase (if you imagine intense effort)
- Brain rehearses the sequence: dive → underwater → breakout → stroke → turn → finish

**Result:** When you do the real race, your brain says "I've done this before" → less anxiety, better execution.

**Research-backed example:**

**Dr. Jacqueline Golding & Dr. Steven Ungerleider** (survey of Olympic athletes):
> "Many athletes who trained without coaches had designed innovative mental practice strategies on their own and incorporated them into their daily training. Visualization is available to anyone who takes a drink from the spring."

**Translation:** You don't need a sport psychologist. You need a quiet space and 10 minutes.

---

## Visualization Technique #1: Full Race Rehearsal

### When to Use
- **In-season:** 2-3x per week (10-15 min)
- **Taper:** Daily (10-15 min)
- **Race day morning:** Before prelims/finals (5-10 min)

### The Protocol

**Step 1: Set the Scene (2 minutes)**
- Find quiet space (bedroom, car, locker room)
- Sit or lie down comfortably
- Close eyes
- Deep breathing: 4 seconds in, 6 seconds out (5 cycles)

**Step 2: Enter the Environment (1 minute)**
- **See:** The competition pool, the banners, the starting blocks
- **Hear:** Announcer, crowd buzz, splashing in warm-up
- **Feel:** Your racing suit, goggles on your head, heart beating steadily
- **Smell:** Chlorine

**Step 3: Walk Through Pre-Race Routine (2 minutes)**

**Warm-up pool:**
- See yourself doing your usual warm-up
- Feel the water temperature
- Notice your stroke feeling smooth, powerful

**Ready room:**
- Sitting calmly, breathing steady
- Your affirmation running through your head: "I'm ready. I own this race."
- Relaxed but focused

**Behind the blocks:**
- Stepping up
- Shaking out arms and legs
- Three deep breaths
- "I've done this a thousand times. Let's go."

**Step 4: The Race (5-10 minutes)**

**Start:**
- Announcer: "Take your mark..."
- Coiled on the blocks, ready to explode
- **BEEP** — Perfect entry, tight streamline, no splash
- Underwater: Explosive dolphin kicks, fast breakout

**First length:**
- Smooth, controlled, executing the plan
- Breathing rhythm locked in
- Seeing the lane line, feeling the water pressure on your forearms

**Turn 1:**
- Aggressive approach, tight flip/touch
- Fast feet to the wall, explosive push-off
- Underwater rocket, breakout at optimal depth

**Middle section:**
- Holding pace, staying loose
- Any fatigue? Acknowledge it, deflect it
- Affirmation: "Smooth and strong"

**Final turn:**
- "HERE WE GO—this is MY race"
- Best turn of the day, explosive push
- Maximum effort underwater

**Last 25:**
- Pain is there—irrelevant
- Laser-focused on technique: full strokes, fast tempo
- Hunting the wall
- One more stroke, one more stroke

**Finish:**
- **TOUCH** — look at the board
- See your goal time (or better)
- Feel the satisfaction, relief, pride
- Hear your coach/teammates cheering

**Step 5: Anchor the Feeling (1 minute)**
- Take a deep breath
- Lock in the confidence: "I just swam a perfect race"
- Slowly open eyes
- Carry that feeling into your day

## Visualization Technique #2: Stroke Correction Imagery

**Use case:** Fixing technique flaws through mental rehearsal

### Example: Summer Sanders (4 Olympic medals)

**Her problem:**
> "When I race, I always get into trouble in the last few meters. I tend to tighten up and not get good distance with my stroke. With my butterfly, I don't get my arms out of the water; I don't extend fully."

**Her solution:**
> "I try and visualize a full stroke—not a short, quick one, but a full, relaxed one. If I can do this in practice, then it will come naturally in competitive races."

**How to apply this:**

**Step 1: Identify the flaw**
- Short stroke under fatigue
- Weak underwater off turns
- Dropped elbow on breathing
- Tight shoulders at the finish

**Step 2: Visualize the CORRECT version**

**Instead of visualizing the problem (DON'T imagine your bad technique), see the SOLUTION:**

✅ **Full, extended stroke** (not short, choppy)  
✅ **Explosive underwater with 5-6 tight dolphin kicks** (not lazy 3-kick breakout)  
✅ **High elbow on breathing, quick head turn** (not lifting/dropping)  
✅ **Loose, powerful finish** (not tight, restricted)

**Step 3: Practice the correction 10 times in your mind**
- Close eyes
- Slow-motion mental rehearsal
- Feel the CORRECT movement
- See it from your own eyes (first person), then from above (third person)

**Step 4: Reinforce in practice**
- After visualizing, do 4-6 reps of the correct technique at easy pace
- "I just rehearsed this mentally—now let's FEEL it physically"

**Frequency:**
- Daily for 2 weeks to rewire the pattern
- Then 2-3x per week for maintenance

## Visualization Technique #3: Underwater Imagery

**Use case:** Perfecting underwaters (the hidden race within the race)

### Terry Laughlin (Total Immersion, USA Swimming technique specialist)

**On visual learning:**
> "There are people who learn best visually. If you don't provide them with some sort of visual feedback or visual lesson in what you want them to do, they're always handicapped in their ability to learn."

**His method:** Videotape swimmers above and below water, play it back in slow motion.

**Mental training version:**

**Step 1: Watch elite underwater footage (YouTube, meet videos)**
- Caeleb Dressel's underwaters
- Sarah Sjöström's dolphin kicks
- Ryan Murphy's backstroke breakout

**Step 2: Mental rehearsal (eyes closed, 5 minutes)**

**Visualize from FIRST PERSON (your eyes):**
- Perfect streamline: hands locked, biceps squeezed against ears, core tight
- Explosive dolphin kicks from the hips (not knees)
- Holding depth: staying at 1-1.5m below surface
- Breaking out at optimal moment (just before losing speed)
- Clean breakout: head steady, first stroke powerful

**Visualize from THIRD PERSON (drone camera above you):**
- See yourself rocketing off the wall
- See the tight streamline (no wiggle, no bend)
- See the dolphin kick ripple through your body
- See the breakout: smooth, fast, no wasted motion

**Step 3: Reinforce with physical reps**
- After mental rehearsal, do 6-8 underwater-focused turns in practice
- Match the FEELING to the VISUALIZATION

**Outcome:** Your brain has now "practiced" perfect underwaters 50+ times without getting in the pool. When you race, it's automatic.

## Visualization Technique #4: Pre-Competition Mental Walk-Through

**Use case:** Race morning or ready room prep

### The 5-Minute Race Day Protocol

**Timing:** 30-60 minutes before your race

**Step 1: Find a quiet corner**
- Locker room, hallway, outside the facility
- Earbuds + calm music (optional)

**Step 2: Quick relaxation (1 min)**
- Three deep breaths: 4 seconds in, 6 seconds out
- Shake out arms and legs
- Roll shoulders back

**Step 3: Mental race walk-through (3 min)**
- Close eyes
- **See:** Your lane, the blocks, the finish
- **Feel:** Goggles tight, suit fitting perfectly, muscles ready
- **Hear:** The beep, the splash, your breathing rhythm
- **Know:** "I've prepared for this. I'm ready."

**Quick version:**
- Dive → perfect entry
- Underwater → explosive
- First 50 → smooth, controlled
- Turns → aggressive
- Last 25 → attack mode
- Finish → touch, look, celebrate

**Step 4: Affirmation (30 sec)**
- "I'm fast. I'm ready. This is MY race."
- Repeat 3x

**Step 5: Physical anchor (30 sec)**
- Tap goggles twice (or your personal cue)
- "Let's go."

**Elite example: Rebecca Twigg (Olympic cycling bronze)**
> "I used to imagine winning, standing high on the podium. I would use that image to reinforce my confidence for my next race. In the Olympics, I carried with me a set of images from that precious moment, and those visuals usually enhanced the preparation for my next big race."

**Swimming version:** Carry your "victory snapshot" into the ready room. When nerves hit, recall it.

## Visualization Technique #5: Sensory Immersion Imagery

**Use case:** Building VIVID mental rehearsal (not just "thinking" about the race—EXPERIENCING it)

### The 5 Senses Framework

**Most people visualize with SIGHT only. Elite athletes use ALL FIVE SENSES.**

**Example: Mental rehearsal of a 200 IM**

**SIGHT:**
- Lane line rushing past
- Walls coming up fast
- Other swimmers in peripheral vision
- Scoreboard at the finish

**SOUND:**
- Splash of your breakout
- Breathing rhythm: sharp inhale, explosive exhale
- Crowd roar on the last 50
- Your coach yelling "GO, GO, GO!"

**FEEL:**
- Water resistance on your forearms (the "catch")
- Powerful push off the wall
- Goggles tight on your face
- Legs burning on the last 25

**TASTE:**
- Chlorine
- Adrenaline in your mouth

**SMELL:**
- Pool deck chlorine
- Your towel

**Why this matters:**

**Dr. Ungerleider:**
> "When you repeat your mantra with complete meditational focus, an athlete sends signals from the mind through the neural pathways to the muscles and organs, reminding them to stay cool and calm. The body gets shut down by delightful signals being passed along the central nervous system."

**Translation:** The more VIVID the mental rehearsal, the stronger the neural activation. Your brain literally "believes" you swam the race.

## When NOT to Visualize

### Justin Dumais (13 national championships, diving)

**His recipe for diving success:**
- ✅ "Run through your tape in your head" (during training)
- ✅ "Repeat a mantra: 'I'm the best'" (before competition)
- ❌ **"Don't visualize DURING competition, only during training"**

**Why?**

**Dumais:**
> "I have 'stupid smarts'—the ability to turn off my brain and let doubt disappear. I can win it if I let my mind go."

**Translation:**
- **Training/taper:** Visualization is REHEARSAL—you're programming the sequence
- **Competition:** Trust the work—EXECUTE, don't overthink

**Swimming application:**

✅ **Visualize:**
- The week before the meet (daily)
- Race morning (5-10 min)
- Ready room (2-3 min)

❌ **Don't visualize:**
- On the blocks (too late—trust your prep)
- During the race (stay PRESENT—feel the water, execute the plan)

**Exception:** Quick mental cue at the turn ("Attack this wall") is fine. Full mental movie mid-race = overthinking.

## Video Feedback + Mental Rehearsal (The Laughlin Protocol)

**Terry Laughlin's approach:**

**Step 1: Film yourself (above + below water)**
- Use GoPro, phone in waterproof case, or team videographer
- Capture starts, turns, stroke mechanics

**Step 2: Watch in slow motion**
- Identify ONE thing to improve (don't try to fix everything)
- Example: "My breakout is too shallow—I'm losing speed"

**Step 3: Watch elite footage of the CORRECT technique**
- YouTube: Search "Caeleb Dressel breakout" or "Katie Ledecky underwaters"
- Study it frame by frame

**Step 4: Mental rehearsal (eyes closed, 5 min)**
- Visualize YOURSELF doing the corrected version
- See it, feel it, lock it in

**Step 5: Physical practice (low volume, high focus)**
- 6-8 reps of the corrected technique
- Match the mental image to the physical execution

**Step 6: Film again, compare**
- Did the mental rehearsal translate to improved technique?
- If yes, keep reinforcing
- If no, adjust the mental image and repeat

**Frequency:** Once per week for ongoing technique refinement.

## Practical Implementation for Coaches

### Week 1: Teach Full Race Rehearsal
- Team meeting: Walk through the 5-step protocol
- Assign homework: "Practice this 3x this week, 10 min each"
- Debrief: "What did you notice?"

### Week 2-3: Add Stroke Correction Imagery
- Pick one common flaw (e.g., weak underwaters)
- Have athletes visualize the CORRECT version
- Follow with focused physical reps

### Week 4: Introduce Sensory Immersion
- "Don't just SEE the race—FEEL it, HEAR it, TASTE it"
- Practice adding layers: sight → sound → feel → taste → smell

### Taper: Daily Race Rehearsal
- 10-15 min every morning
- Lock in the race plan
- Build confidence through mental reps

### Race Day: 5-Minute Protocol
- Quiet space in ready room or outside
- Quick mental walk-through
- Affirmation → physical anchor → GO

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Visualizing failure
❌ "I see myself choking on the last 50"  
✅ "I see myself attacking the last 50 and finishing strong"

**Your brain doesn't know the difference between real and imagined—don't rehearse failure.**

### Mistake 2: Vague, low-detail imagery
❌ "I imagine I swim fast"  
✅ "I see my hand entering at the perfect angle, I feel the water pressure on my forearm, I hear the splash of my breakout"

**Specificity = neural activation.**

### Mistake 3: Only visualizing once before the meet
**Visualization is a SKILL. It requires daily practice, just like physical skills.**

### Mistake 4: Overthinking during the race
**Visualize beforehand. Execute during. Reflect afterward.**

## Measuring Progress

### Ask your athletes after 2-4 weeks:

**Mental clarity:**
- "Does the race feel more familiar when you swim it?"
- "Are you less anxious on race day?"

**Execution:**
- "Are you hitting your race plan more consistently?"
- "Are you making fewer technical errors under pressure?"

**Confidence:**
- "Do you BELIEVE you can execute your plan?"
- "When things go sideways (bad start, slow turn), can you recover faster?"

**The goal:** Athletes who have mentally swum the race 50+ times before they physically swim it once.
