# The Cleaner Mentality: Becoming Unstoppable
**Source:** *Relentless: From Good to Great to Unstoppable* by Tim S. Grover (2013)

## Core Philosophy

> "Don't think. You already know what you have to do, and you know how to do it. What's stopping you?"

Tim Grover (trainer to Michael Jordan, Kobe Bryant, Dwyane Wade) defines three types of competitors:

1. **Coolers** - Good performers who maintain things
2. **Closers** - Great performers who step up when needed  
3. **Cleaners** - Unstoppable performers who dominate relentlessly

**The Cleaner:** The most intense and driven competitor imaginable. Refuses limitations. Quietly and forcefully does whatever it takes to get what they want.

---

## The Three Competitor Types

### Coolers (Good)
- First to arrive at celebration, last to leave
- Wait to be told what to do
- Think about how hard the work will be
- Celebrate reaching the goal

### Closers (Great)
- Make appearances, then leave with own crew
- Step up in big moments
- Get the job done when needed
- Satisfied with reaching the goal

### Cleaners (Unstoppable)
- Just want to get back to work
- Trust instincts, never hesitate
- Work becomes irrelevant compared to results
- Already thinking about next goal before achieving current one

**For coaches:** Identify which swimmers are Coolers, Closers, or Cleaners. You can develop Coolers into Closers, and Closers into Cleaners - but only if they're willing to pay the price.

---

## Cleaner Characteristics

### 1. Never Satisfied
- Won in six? Disappointed you didn't win in five
- Benched 195? Why not 200?
- **Application:** After a great practice, don't celebrate - analyze what could have been better

### 2. Internal Pressure Over External
**Michael Jordan's mindset:** Standards he set for himself were higher than anyone else could set for him.

**For swimmers:** External motivation (parents, coach praise, rankings) fades. Internal drive (proving something to yourself) is infinite.

**Protocol:**
- Stop asking "Did coach like that?"
- Start asking "Was that my absolute best?"
- Judge yourself by YOUR standards, not others'

### 3. Addiction to Winning
> "Winning is a habit that inevitably becomes an addiction. You feel it in your gut, in the dull ache of your dark side begging for it."

**Kobe Bryant's response** when asked how many more years he'd play: "Until I get number six." (He had 3 rings at the time)

He didn't answer in years - he answered in rings.

**For swimmers:** Don't think "I want to swim through college." Think "I want three NCAA championships" or "I want to break 1:45 in the 200 free."

### 4. Dark Side Advantage
**Controversial truth:** Cleaners have a dark side that refuses to be taught to be good.

**What this means:**
- Killer instinct in competition
- Willingness to be "selfish" with training time
- Ability to shut out distractions (even loved ones)
- Comfort with being misunderstood

**Grover:** "Use it well and it can be your greatest gift."

**For coaches:** Don't try to make your most driven athletes "nicer" or "team-oriented" if it compromises their competitive edge. Channel that intensity, don't suppress it.

### 5. Relentless = Predatory
> "Truly relentless people are predators, with dark sides that refuse to be taught to be good."

**In swimming terms:**
- See weakness in lane next to you? Attack it
- Opponent takes stroke off the wall? You take none
- They're breathing every 3? You breathe every 5
- They ease into turns? You crush every wall

**Mental tactic:** In practice, identify the fastest swimmer. Your job every set: hunt them down.

## Done. Next.

### The Cleaner's Favorite Words

**After achieving goal:** "Done. Next."

- No celebration
- No satisfaction
- Immediately moving to next challenge

**Example:** Dwyane Wade winning 2012 championship, still had chronic leg injuries, immediately called Grover: "I'm not done yet."

That's a Cleaner.

### The Quiet Melancholy of Victory
> "A Cleaner knows the quiet melancholy of victory. While everyone else enjoys the win, he waits for the impending letdown, the unyielding reminder that the glory is already in the past."

**What this looks like:**
- Swimmer wins state championship
- Everyone celebrates
- Swimmer thinks: "I could have gone faster on the back half"
- Next morning: already planning next season

**Grover:** "For five minutes, he's completely exhilarated. For the next twenty-four hours, he's relatively happy. After that... what? Back to work."

## Practical Application for Coaches

### Creating Cleaners in Your Program

**1. Stop Praising Effort, Start Demanding Results**
- Don't say: "Great effort today!"
- Say: "You went 1:52. Your goal is 1:48. What are you changing tomorrow?"

**2. Teach Them to Trust Instincts**
> "The minute your mind thinks 'Done,' your instincts say 'Next.'"

**Drill:** Last 25 of practice set
- Don't give them target time
- Tell them: "Trust your body. Go when you're ready. Finish when you're done."
- Teaches internal regulation over external commands

**3. Create Uncomfortable Situations 3-4x Per Week**
Grover's tactic with elite athletes: practice being uncomfortable regularly

**Swimming applications:**
- Head-to-head racing when exhausted (last 15 min of practice)
- Stopping practice: "That wasn't good enough. We're starting over."
- Intense scrutiny: Get close to lane, demand higher standard
- Message (subtle but critical): Not "you must do better" but "You CAN do better. Let's go."

**4. Make Them Get Uncomfortable With Comfort**
**Grover's method:** After big win, immediately increase training intensity

**Why:** "You won a title, took it easy during off-season, and returned the next year feeling a little content and satisfied. Usually, only one thing can bring him back: the competitive spirit, the idea that someone else is about to take what's his."

**For coaches:** After championship season, don't ease up. Go harder. Someone else is training while you celebrate.

### 5. Rehearse Equipment/Situation Failures
**Michael Phelps example:** Grover intentionally stepped on Phelps' goggles before race in Australia. Goggles filled with water. Phelps couldn't see, reverted to stroke-count method.

**Result:** Beijing 2008, 200m butterfly final for 10th gold medal - goggles immediately fill with water. Phelps falls back on stroke-count practice. Wins + world record.

**Swimming protocol:**
- Practice with fogged goggles
- Practice counting strokes per lap (know exact number)
- Practice with goggle strap break mid-set
- Practice starting with incorrect starting position

**Grover:** "Rehearse equipment/situation failures so they don't derail performance."

## The Zero Limitation Mindset

### Core Belief
> "I truly believe I have zero limitations. You should believe the same about yourself."

**How to develop this:**

**1. Remove the word "can't"**
- Swimmer says: "I can't go under 1:50"
- Response: "You haven't YET. Different statement."

**2. Place No Limits on Yourself**
- Kobe says he wants six rings? Grover wants him to have seven
- Guy says he wants to come back from injury in 10 weeks? Grover gets him there in 8
- You want to drop thirty pounds? You'll drop thirty-four

**Grover:** "That's how you become unstoppable - by placing no limits on yourself."

**3. Demand More of Yourself Than Anyone Else Demands**
> "The greatest battles you will ever fight are with yourself, and you must always be your toughest opponent."

**Daily practice:** Before coach gives interval, decide YOUR interval. Make it harder than what coach will give.

## Learn. Adapt. Overcome.

### Grover's ACL Story (Relevant for Injured Athletes)

**Background:** Grover was a basketball player who tore ACL twice, had botched surgery, career ended

**Turning point:** Kid said to him, "I remember when you were good."

**Grover's response:**
- Didn't sulk or quit
- Realized if damaged body couldn't play basketball, he'd use what he learned to help others
- Became trainer to greatest athletes ever

**Lesson:** "You take what everyone else sees as a negative and turn it to your advantage."

**For injured swimmers:**
1. **Don't see injury as failure** - see it as recalibration
2. **Use the time** - mental training, visualization, core work, film study
3. **Come back stronger** - this is your advantage now, you know your body better than anyone

**Grover's motto:** "Don't try. Do."

## Practical Training Protocols

### The 48-Inch Jump Test
**Grover's standard for all athletes after knee/ankle/hip rehab:**

Jump down 48 inches to floor and back up. Must pass before returning to competition.

**Swimming equivalent:** Box jumps post-injury
- Start: 12 inches
- Progress: 24 inches
- Clear to compete: 36+ inches, pain-free

### Robbie Hummel Protocol (Double ACL Tear)
**Challenge:** Purdue basketball player, two ACL surgeries in 8 months

**Grover's approach:**
- 7 months rehab
- 5 days/week
- Twice per day
- First day: "Within an hour they had me puking in a trash can"

**Result:** Returned better than ever. First Team All-Big Ten.

**For coaches:** Severe injury = opportunity to rebuild athlete stronger than before. Not just rehab - REBUILD.

## The Performance Checklist

### Before Performance (Race Day)
✅ **Strategy decided weeks before** (not day of)  
✅ **Trust the preparation** (done thinking, now executing)  
✅ **Internal pressure only** (external noise is irrelevant)  
✅ **Dark side activated** (predatory mindset)  

### During Performance
✅ **Don't think - execute**  
✅ **Trust instincts** (you've done this thousands of times)  
✅ **Attack weakness** (yours and opponents')  

### After Performance
✅ **File away lessons** (what worked, what didn't)  
✅ **Don't celebrate** (or celebrate briefly, then back to work)  
✅ **Done. Next.** (already thinking about tomorrow)

## The Ultimate Lesson

### Churchill Quote Grover Loves
> "Success is the ability to go from one failure to another with no loss of enthusiasm."

**Grover's addition:** "That's also a pretty good definition of resilience."

### Make Training Harder Than Competition
**Final directive:**

> "Make your training so demanding, so uncomfortable, so pressure-filled that actual performance feels easier by comparison."

**Implementation:**
- Practice is harder than meets
- Training sets are faster than race pace
- Mental pressure in practice exceeds meet pressure
- Physical demands in practice exceed competition demands

**When meet day comes:** Swimmer thinks "This is easier than Tuesday."

That's when you know you've created a Cleaner.
