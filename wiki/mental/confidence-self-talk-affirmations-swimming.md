# Building Confidence Through Self-Talk and Affirmations

**How Olympic swimmers use positive self-talk, affirmations, and mental cues to build unshakeable confidence — and how your swimmers can master this skill.**

---

## What Is Self-Talk and Why It Matters

**Definition:** Self-talk is anytime you think about something — you are "talking to yourself."

**Affirmations:** A very specific and individualized type of self-talk. More than just "stay focused" — affirmations are **personal, positive statements** about your abilities, identity, and readiness.

**Key principle:** The frequency and content of your thoughts determine your performance.

**Research finding:** Athletes who use positive self-talk and affirmations are **more relaxed, better prepared, and more successful.**

---

## The Difference Between Self-Talk and Affirmations

### **Self-Talk (General)**

**Example:**
- "Stay focused"
- "Keep your pace"
- "Breathe"

**Purpose:** Simple reminders during activity.

---

### **Affirmations (Specific)**

**Example:**
- "I know I'm really good at this"
- "I am long and strong"
- "I belong in this heat"
- "I've put in the work. I'm ready."

**Purpose:** Build self-esteem, self-worth, and confidence.

**Quote — Sport psychologist Jean Williams, PhD:**

Affirmations help athletes:
- Correct bad habits
- Prepare for performance
- Focus attention
- Build confidence and competence
- Create a positive, constructive mood

---

## How Olympic Swimmers Use Self-Talk

### **Susie O'Neil (Australian Olympic Gold Medalist)**

**Challenge:** Had to "perpetually talk myself into believing that I was good."

**Her self-talk strategy:**

**Before race time:**
- "I am better than everyone else who jumped into the pool"
- "Stay calm"
- "Rid myself of the pregame nerves"
- "Concentrate on my game plan"

**During visualization:**
- "I am long and strong"
- No extra thoughts
- No distractions or worries

**Quote:**
> "Very simple. I did not want to have any other distractions or worries that would take me away from my game plan."

**Coaching lesson:** **Simplicity wins.** A few powerful phrases beat complex mental chatter.

---

### **Summer Sanders (4 Olympic Medals, 1992)**

**Philosophy:** Stay in the present, think positive thoughts.

**Her self-talk during competition:**
- "Stay with my game plan"
- "Full strokes"
- "Feel my pace"
- "Don't get outside of myself"

**What she avoided:**
- Focusing on negatives
- Racing someone else's pace (instinct trap)
- Doubting herself

**Quote:**
> "Swimming for me is to stay with my game plan, visualizing my full strokes and feeling my pace without getting outside of myself."

---

### **Janet Evans (4x Olympic Gold Medalist)**

**Her self-talk principle:** "I am FAST."

**Belief system:**
> "Half of everything you do is mental."

**Quote:**
> "I have never been to a competition, including the Olympic Games, where I didn't see myself win in my mental images before I got there."

**Key:** Her self-talk reinforced **identity** ("I am fast") + **expectation** ("I win").

## Self-Talk for Handling Adversity

### **Pete Sampras (Tennis Champion): Escaping Negative Spirals**

**Challenge:** As a child, Sampras let anger trap him → negative tension on court.

**Solution:** Positive self-talk to INTERRUPT the negative cycle.

**His phrases:**
- "I need to get out of this mind-set"
- "I need to let go of that last point and stay focused on the present"
- "I need to prepare for the next point"

**Result:** Stayed fresher, less easily distracted, more receptive to focusing on the game.

---

### **Swimming Application: Converting Bad Breaks**

**Scenario:** Bad start (belly flop off the blocks)

**❌ Quitter self-talk:**
- "Well, that's it. Race is over."
- "I screwed up. Why bother?"

**✅ Competitor self-talk:**
- "Okay, bad start. Make it up in the turns."
- "Focus. Execute the race plan."
- "Finish strong."

**Quote — Nancy Lopez (Professional Golfer):**
> "A competitor will find a way to win. Competitors take bad breaks and use them to drive themselves just that much harder. Quitters take bad breaks and use them as reasons to give up."

## The Building Blocks of Effective Self-Talk

### **1. Keep It Short and Simple**

**Principle:** Short, concise cue words are more effective than long sentences.

**Sport psychologist Jim Loehr's recommendation:**

For a young athlete learning tennis:
- **Forehand**
- **Backhand**
- **Crosscourt**

**Swimming examples:**
- **Long** (for stroke extension)
- **Strong** (for power)
- **Fast** (for tempo)
- **Smooth** (for rhythm)

**Why it works:** Over time, these reminders become **automatic and programmed** — you don't have to think about the placement of a shot or the timing of a stroke. It becomes **muscle memory.**

---

### **2. Match Self-Talk to Your Sport**

**Example:**

**Runner:** "Steady," "Keep your pace"

**Diver (Greg Louganis, 2x Olympic gold medalist):** Has the luxury of pausing, reflecting, and conducting mental talk and affirmation imagery before each dive.

**Swimmer (200 Free):** May have to use QUICK cues during the race:
- **First 50:** "Rhythm"
- **Turn:** "Tight"
- **Final 50:** "Drive"

**Figure skater (Nancy Kerrigan):** Needs DIFFERENT strategies because of the flow and timing of the performance.

**Coaching lesson:** **Sport-specific self-talk matters.** Concentration and control may complement certain sports, while others demand different strategies.

---

### **3. Use Self-Talk to Correct Technique**

**Example — Swimmer learning proper form:**

**Cue words for freestyle:**
- **"High elbow"** (during catch)
- **"Long reach"** (during entry)
- **"Rotate"** (body roll)
- **"Kick"** (legs engaged)

**Practice:**
1. Say the cue word BEFORE the stroke
2. Execute the movement
3. Repeat until automatic

**Goal:** The verbal reminder becomes **automatic and subconscious.**

## Affirmations: Building Unshakeable Confidence

### **What Makes an Effective Affirmation?**

**Criteria:**
1. **Personal** (specific to you)
2. **Positive** (not "don't mess up" — say "stay strong")
3. **Present tense** ("I am fast" not "I will be fast")
4. **Believable** (if you don't believe it, it won't work)

---

### **Examples of Swimming Affirmations**

**Identity-based:**
- "I am a FAST swimmer"
- "I am mentally tough"
- "I am a competitor"
- "I belong in this heat"

**Preparation-based:**
- "I've trained for this race"
- "I've put in the work"
- "I am READY"

**Process-based:**
- "My stroke is smooth and powerful"
- "My turns are explosive"
- "My finish is strong"

**Outcome-based:**
- "I am a champion"
- "I am a winner"
- "I achieve my goals"

---

### **How to Create Personal Affirmations**

**Step 1: Identify your goal or challenge**
- Example: "I want to improve my 100 Free time"

**Step 2: Write a positive, present-tense statement**
- ❌ "I will swim faster"
- ✅ "I swim with speed and power"

**Step 3: Make it personal and specific**
- ✅ "My underwater kicks are explosive"
- ✅ "I finish races strong"

**Step 4: Repeat it daily**
- Morning (before school/work)
- Before practice
- Before bed
- Before races

**Step 5: Believe it**
- If you don't believe it, rewrite it until you do

## Using Affirmations in Training and Competition

### **Summer Sanders: Post-Recovery Confidence Building**

**Challenge:** At age 14, struggled with self-esteem and swimming.

**Solution:** Worked with a psychologist, did relaxation training and imagery practice.

**Her affirmation strategy:**
- **Wrote goal times on notes**
- **Posted them around the house** (bathroom, closet, mirrors, hallway)
- **Result:** Constant visual cue + reminder that she could achieve her goals

**Quote:**
> "It was a good cue to remind me that I could get there and be successful while relaxed."

**Also practiced:**
- Self-talk before going to the pool
- Positive affirmations to build confidence
- **Visualized her family, friends, and support system** (relaxed her, reminded her how far she'd come)

---

### **Coach Harvey Glance: Building Confidence Through Notes**

**Background:** Harvey Glance (former Olympic sprinter, now head coach at University of Alabama) uses affirmations to build athlete confidence.

**His method:**
- **Writes notes to his athletes** congratulating them on workouts
- **Reminds them to stay positive and refreshed** when they train
- **Reinforces healthy, positive attitude** in daily workouts

**Goal:** Bring these images and affirmations to each race.

**Quote (from his coach, Stan Wright):**
> "I had a very sensitive eye to building last-minute confidence and self-image. I would see Harvey Glance with his head down before the Olympic Trials and know that he wasn't emotionally prepared to race. I would remind him of his other big races, remind him of his need for positive self-talk and positive images when he set up in the blocks. Most of all, I would tell Harvey to keep his head up and remind him that he was the best in the business and that he could get the job done."

## Self-Talk Strategies for Common Swimming Challenges

### **Challenge 1: Pre-Race Nerves**

**Negative self-talk:**
- "I'm so nervous. What if I mess up?"
- "Everyone is faster than me."
- "I'm going to lose."

**Positive affirmations:**
- "I am calm and ready."
- "I've trained for this moment."
- "Nerves mean I care. I channel this energy into speed."

---

### **Challenge 2: Mid-Race Fatigue**

**Negative self-talk:**
- "I'm so tired. I can't keep this pace."
- "My arms are dead."
- "I want to quit."

**Positive cues:**
- "Strong finish."
- "Drive to the wall."
- "One more stroke. One more stroke."

---

### **Challenge 3: Falling Behind in a Race**

**Negative self-talk:**
- "I'm losing. It's over."
- "I can't catch them."

**Competitor self-talk:**
- "Stay with my plan."
- "Finish strong."
- "Make up ground on the turn."

---

### **Challenge 4: Technical Breakdown**

**Negative self-talk:**
- "My stroke feels terrible."
- "I'm all over the place."

**Corrective cues:**
- "Long and smooth."
- "High elbow."
- "Feel the water."

## The Ultimate Goal: Automatic Performance

**Quote — Sport psychologist Jean Williams, PhD:**

> "The ultimate goal of teaching self-talk and positive affirmations is to have the athlete achieve a sense of mastery — a proficiency that becomes automatic."

**What this means:**
- Self-talk becomes SUBCONSCIOUS
- You don't have to THINK about it — your body just DOES it
- This takes TIME and PRACTICE

**How to get there:**
1. **Start with conscious cues** ("high elbow")
2. **Repeat in training** (thousands of reps)
3. **Integrate with visualization** (see + say + do)
4. **Practice in competition** (use cues in races)
5. **Trust the process** (eventually it becomes automatic)

## Coaching Swimmers: Teaching Self-Talk

### **Step 1: Teach the Concept**

**Message to swimmers:**
- "Your thoughts affect your performance."
- "Negative self-talk makes you slower. Positive self-talk makes you faster."
- "We're going to TRAIN your brain just like we train your body."

---

### **Step 2: Help Swimmers Identify Their Self-Talk**

**Exercise: Self-Talk Audit (5 minutes)**

**Ask swimmers:**
1. What do you say to yourself before a race?
2. What do you say during a race?
3. What do you say after a bad swim?

**Common responses:**
- Negative: "I'm going to lose," "I suck," "This hurts too much"
- Positive: "I'm ready," "I'm fast," "I can do this"

**Goal:** Increase awareness. You can't change what you don't notice.

---

### **Step 3: Replace Negative Self-Talk with Positive Cues**

**Method:**

**Negative thought:** "I'm so slow."  
**Replacement:** "I am getting faster every day."

**Negative thought:** "My turns suck."  
**Replacement:** "My turns are improving. I focus on tight streamlines."

**Negative thought:** "I'm going to lose."  
**Replacement:** "I stay focused on MY race."

**Practice:** Have swimmers write down their negative thoughts, then create positive replacements.

---

### **Step 4: Create Personal Affirmations**

**Team exercise (10 minutes):**

1. Each swimmer writes 3-5 personal affirmations
2. Share with the team (builds accountability)
3. Post affirmations on bathroom mirror, locker, phone wallpaper
4. Repeat daily (morning, before practice, before bed)

---

### **Step 5: Practice Self-Talk in Training**

**Integration:**
- **Warm-up:** Say affirmations while swimming easy
- **Drill work:** Use technique cues ("high elbow," "long reach")
- **Main set:** Use performance cues ("strong," "fast," "smooth")
- **Cool-down:** Reflect on what self-talk worked

---

### **Step 6: Use Self-Talk in Competition**

**Pre-race:** Affirmations ("I am ready. I am fast.")  
**Behind blocks:** Quick cue ("Let it flow.")  
**During race:** Process cues ("Drive," "Finish strong")  
**After race:** Positive reflection ("I gave my best. I'm proud.")

## Common Self-Talk Mistakes

### **❌ Mistake 1: Using Negative Language**

**Problem:** "Don't mess up," "Don't tighten up," "Don't get passed"

**Why it fails:** Your brain focuses on the negative image (messing up, tightening, getting passed).

**Solution:** Reframe positively.
- ❌ "Don't tighten up" → ✅ "Stay relaxed"
- ❌ "Don't get passed" → ✅ "Stay strong"
- ❌ "Don't miss the turn" → ✅ "Nail the turn"

---

### **❌ Mistake 2: Affirmations You Don't Believe**

**Problem:** "I'm the fastest swimmer in the world!"

**If you don't believe it, it creates cognitive dissonance** → undermines confidence.

**Solution:** Make affirmations **believable.**
- ✅ "I am getting faster every week"
- ✅ "I belong in this race"
- ✅ "I've trained hard. I'm ready."

---

### **❌ Mistake 3: Only Using Self-Talk During Competition**

**Problem:** Self-talk is a SKILL. It requires practice.

**Solution:** Practice self-talk DAILY in training.
- Morning affirmations
- Pre-practice cues
- During sets (stroke cues, pace reminders)
- Post-practice reflection

---

### **❌ Mistake 4: Too Many Cues**

**Problem:** "Remember: high elbow, long reach, rotate, kick, breathe every 3, stay relaxed, go fast..."

**Your brain OVERLOADS.**

**Solution:** ONE cue at a time.
- This set: Focus on "high elbow"
- Next set: Focus on "strong kick"
- Race: ONE cue ("fast and smooth")

**Susie O'Neil:** "Long and strong." That's it. Simple.

## Practical Self-Talk Exercises

### **Exercise 1: Morning Affirmation Routine (2 minutes)**

**When:** Every morning (before school/work)

**Steps:**
1. Stand in front of mirror
2. Say 3-5 personal affirmations OUT LOUD
3. Visualize yourself succeeding
4. Take 3 deep breaths
5. Start your day

**Example:**
- "I am a FAST swimmer."
- "I am mentally tough."
- "I train hard and smart."
- "I achieve my goals."
- "I am READY."

---

### **Exercise 2: Pre-Practice Cue Setting (1 minute)**

**When:** Before entering the water

**Steps:**
1. Identify the focus for today's practice (technique, speed, endurance)
2. Choose ONE cue word/phrase
3. Say it 3x
4. Use it during practice

**Example:**
- Today's focus: Stroke technique
- Cue: "High elbow"
- During drills: "High elbow, high elbow, high elbow"

---

### **Exercise 3: Post-Swim Reflection (2 minutes)**

**When:** After practice or race

**Steps:**
1. What self-talk did I use today?
2. What worked?
3. What didn't work?
4. What will I say differently next time?

**Goal:** Build self-awareness and improve self-talk over time.

---

### **Exercise 4: Reframing Negative Thoughts (5 minutes)**

**When:** Anytime you catch yourself thinking negatively

**Steps:**
1. **Notice the negative thought** ("I'm so slow")
2. **Pause**
3. **Reframe positively** ("I'm getting faster every day")
4. **Repeat the positive version 3x**

**Over time, this becomes automatic.**

## Key Takeaways

✅ **Self-talk = anytime you think about something** (it's constant)  
✅ **Affirmations = specific, personal, positive statements** (more powerful)  
✅ **Positive self-talk → better performance** (relaxed, prepared, confident)  
✅ **Keep it short and simple** (one cue at a time)  
✅ **Match self-talk to your sport** (swimming needs quick, mid-race cues)  
✅ **Use affirmations daily** (not just on race day)  
✅ **Replace negative self-talk with positive cues** (reframe)  
✅ **Practice self-talk in training** (it's a skill, not magic)  
✅ **Goal: automatic performance** (subconscious mastery)  
✅ **Simplicity wins** (Susie O'Neil: "Long and strong" — that's it)
