# Mental Management System (Lanny Bassham Framework)

**Source:** *With Winning in Mind* by Lanny Bassham (Olympic rifle shooting gold medalist)  
**Context:** Core mental training framework applicable to all high-performance sports  
**Swimming relevance:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (universal principles)

---

## The Three Mental Processes

**Performance = Conscious Mind + Subconscious Mind + Self-Image**

All three must be optimized simultaneously for peak performance.

### 1. Conscious Mind (10% of performance)

**What it is:** What you're thinking **right now**  
**Size:** Small — only one thought at a time  
**Control:** 100% under your control

**In swimming:**
- Race strategy ("negative split")
- Pre-race mental routine
- What you focus on during warm-up
- Your self-talk between sets

**Key principle:** If your conscious mind is occupied with the RIGHT thought, it can't be occupied with the WRONG one.

---

### 2. Subconscious Mind (Skill — up to 90% of performance)

**What it is:** Your physical technique, developed through training  
**Size:** Larger than conscious mind — can multitask (heart beating, lungs breathing, stroke mechanics)  
**Control:** Indirect — through training volume & quality

**How it grows:**
- **Quality:** Best coaching, technique work
- **Quantity:** Hours in the pool
- **Efficiency:** Training all 3 processes simultaneously

**In swimming:**
- Stroke technique
- Turns, starts, breakouts
- Pacing feel
- Race execution

**Bassham principle:** "You can imagine far more than you currently can achieve. If you consistently rehearse what you want to achieve, what you imagine can become reality."

**Example:** Bassham visualized shooting a perfect 400 for 2 months. Never done it in training. First competition after: **shot 400**.

---

### 3. Self-Image (The Thermostat)

**What it is:** How you see yourself — your "comfort zone"  
**Function:** Acts like a thermostat, keeping you within your zone  
**Size:** The REAL limiter of performance

**The Self-Image Problem:**
> "Your Self-Image 'makes you act like you.' It keeps you within your comfort zone. If you are below your zone, your Self-Image makes you uncomfortable and turns up your power until you are within the zone. Likewise, if you are above your zone, the Self-Image will cut your power, dropping you back within your zone."

**In swimming:**
- "I'm a 53-second 100 butterflyer" (comfort zone: 52.5-53.5)
- Splits 51.8 in prelims → nervous in finals, swims 53.2
- Or: Splits 54.2 in prelims → locks in for finals, swims 52.9

**The breakthrough:** To swim faster consistently, you must **change your Self-Image**, not just train harder.

---

## Principle #1: You Become What You Think About

> "It's not what you are that holds you back. It's what you think you are not."

**Bassham:** Early career, mediocre baseball player. Frustrated by failure, became obsessed with finding something he could master. That obsession → Olympic gold.

**Swimming application:**
- Stop dwelling on missed intervals
- Stop replaying bad races
- Start replaying — and **writing down** — great swims

**The rule:** Every time you think/talk/write about a performance, you imprint it deeper into your Self-Image.

---

## Principle #2: It Takes 21 Days to Change a Habit

**The mechanism:**
- Repetitive imprinting over 21 days changes Self-Image
- Then rest 9 days
- Repeat if needed

**Bassham's tool:** Directive Affirmation (see below)

---

## Principle #3: The Self-Image Cannot Tell the Difference Between Actual and Vividly Imagined Events

**This is HUGE.**

**Bassham example:**
- Visualized breaking the national record at 400 for 2 months
- Rehearsed the internal voice: *"That's OK, I do this all the time"*
- First competition: Shot 400, set record
- Heard that exact voice during the attempt

**Swimming application:**
- Rehearse breaking :50 in the 100 free
- Rehearse touching first at NCAAs
- Rehearse staying calm when the leader is ahead at the 150

**Study cited:** Positive imagery group improved performance, **negative imagery group got WORSE**.

## The Three Phases of Every Task

### 1. Anticipation Phase (Before)
**What you think about immediately before performance**

**Swimming:**
- Behind the blocks
- Pre-race routine
- Mental prep 30 minutes before race
- What you rehearse the night before

**Bassham lesson:** Plan for EVERY contingency. At 1976 Olympics, he was calm because he'd rehearsed every possible scenario.

---

### 2. Action Phase (During)
**What you think about AS you perform**

**Swimming:**
- Race execution
- What you focus on during each lap
- Your mental cues ("long," "tempo," "press")

**Bassham rule:** Let the Subconscious do the work. Conscious mind should run a **mental program** (see below).

---

### 3. Reinforcement Phase (After)
**What you think about immediately after performance**

**Bassham's teammates:**
- **Jack Writer:** Only talked about his HIGH scores, never low ones
- **Margaret Murdock:** Rarely talked, but praised others (which also reinforced her own success)
- **Lones Wigger:** "You will likely beat me four out of five times, but I plan to make my one time count."

**Swimming application:**
- After a bad 100 free: "That's not like me. Next time, I'll [specific fix]."
- After a great 100 free: **"That's like me!"** (write it down)

**The trap:** Reinforcing error by talking about it  
**The fix:** Feast or forget

## Running a Mental Program

**Definition:** A series of thoughts/images that occupy the Conscious Mind and trigger the Subconscious to perform automatically.

### Criteria for a Mental Program:
1. **Occupies the Conscious Mind** (keeps it busy, not overthinking)
2. **Transfers power to the Subconscious** (lets technique happen automatically)
3. **Duplicable** (same every time)
4. **Simple** (easy to remember)
5. **Performer-specific** (personalized)

### Bassham's 5-Step Program (Rifle Shooting):

1. **Point of Initiation** — Load rifle (triggers start of program)
2. **Point of Attitude** — Feel what it's like to shoot a 10
3. **Point of Direction** — See perfect sight alignment, imagine the 10
4. **Point of Control** — Check wind flags, think "slower, smaller"
5. **Point of Focus** — Center target in sight (last thought before trigger)

### Swimming Adaptation (Behind the Blocks):

**3-Step Program:**
1. **Point of Initiation** — Step onto blocks, take position
2. **Point of Direction** — Visualize perfect start + breakout
3. **Point of Focus** — Cue word ("explode" / "streamline" / "long")

**4-Step Program (Mid-Race Turn):**
1. **Initiation** — 3 strokes from wall
2. **Alignment** — Tighten core for rotation
3. **Direction** — See the breakout
4. **Focus** — Cue word ("push" / "snap")

**Key:** Same program every time = mental consistency = better performances.

## The Directive Affirmation (21-Day Self-Image Changer)

**Purpose:** Rewrite your Self-Image in 21 days

**Bassham's Olympic Affirmation (1976):**
> "1/1/76. I am a member of the 1976 U.S. Olympic Shooting Team. I have taken my next step toward the accomplishment of my lifelong goal, an Olympic Gold Medal. I enjoy the recognition as one of the best shooters in my country..."

### 5 Steps to Write One:

1. **Define the goal** — "I am a 1:42 200 freestyler"
2. **Set time limit** — 21 days from today
3. **List pay-value** — "I earn All-American status. I'm in the NCAA final. I prove to myself I can do hard things."
4. **Outline the plan** — "I hit every practice. I do my drills with focus. I rehearse my race every night. I read this affirmation daily."
5. **Write the affirmation** — First person, present tense, as if already accomplished

### Swimming Example:

> **4/27/26.** I am a 1:42 200 freestyler. I am an All-American. I swim in the NCAA final. I prove to myself I can do hard things. I hit every practice, do my drills with focus, rehearse my race every night, and read this affirmation daily. I am a 1:42 200 freestyler.

**How to use:**
- Write on 5 index cards
- Place in 5 locations (mirror, fridge, steering wheel, nightstand, locker)
- Every time you see it: Read it + **visualize it** (30 seconds)
- Do this for 21 days
- Rest 9 days
- Repeat if needed

**Bassham:** "Either you will become the person described, or you will stop reading the affirmation."

## Performance Analysis (The Journal)

**Bassham's rule:** "You cannot manage what you do not measure."

### What to Record (Daily):
1. **General Data** — Date, duration, location, what you did
2. **Solution Analysis** — Problems you solved today (or are looking for solutions to)
3. **Success Analysis** — What you did WELL (reinforce success)
4. **Goal Statement** — "I often break :50 in the 100 free" (first person, present tense)

**Key rule:** **NEVER** write about bad performances. Only solutions and successes.

**Why it works:**
- Thinking = weak imprint
- Talking = stronger imprint
- **Writing = strongest imprint**

**Bassham:** "We become what we write about."

## Training Guidelines (7 Principles)

### 1. Catch Yourself Doing Something Right
**Wrong:** "What am I doing wrong?"  
**Right:** "What did I do right in that rep?"

**Example:**
- Bad swimmer: "Why do I always do that?" (shrinks Self-Image)
- Champion: "That's a good one. What did I do right?" (grows Self-Image)

### 2. Train 4-5 Days a Week
- 1 day/week = worse than nothing
- 2-3 days = maintain
- 4-6 days = improve

### 3. Wherever You Are, Be All There
**Bassham:** "When you go from the office to the pool, leave the office thoughts in the office. When you go home, leave the bad swim at the pool."

### 4. Rehearse Match Day Often Within Training
- Treat some practices like meets
- Visualize competition conditions
- Practice pre-race routine

### 5. When You're Swimming Well, Swim a Lot
**Jack Writer:** "When I was shooting above my personal record, I kept shooting. When you're shooting well, shoot a lot."

**Application:** If you're crushing a set, keep going. If you're having a terrible day, **stop training**. Don't practice losing.

### 6. Train with People Better Than You
**Bassham:** "We raise ourselves to the standard we are around."

**Swimming:** Train with faster lanes. Watch elite swimmers. You won't be dragged down by coaching slower swimmers — your Self-Image separates who YOU are from who THEY are.

### 7. Plan Your Year
- Count training days available
- Budget for equipment, travel, meets
- Divide into quarters:
  - **Q1:** Evaluate, plan, test new ideas
  - **Q2:** Conditioning, hard volume
  - **Q3:** Competition phase, peak
  - **Q4:** Rest, reflect, recover

## The #1 Mental Problem: Over-Trying

**Bassham:** "95% of competitors are over-trying at least some of the time."

**The trap:** "I HAVE to hit this shot" → tension → missed shot

**Why we over-try:**
1. Afraid to trust the Subconscious (not enough training)
2. Thinking about **outcome** instead of **process**
3. Belief that "trying harder = better performance" (FALSE)

**Swimming examples:**
- "I HAVE to make this interval" → tight shoulders → slow split
- "This is my last chance to qualify" → anxiety → bad dive

**Bassham's advice:**
- Champions work hard in **training**, work easy in **competition**
- The best performances happen when demonstrating (not competing)
- Dial it back. Let it happen.

**The fix:** Care less about score/time, care MORE about executing process.

## Pressure = Anxiety + Tension

**Bassham:** "Pressure is neither positive nor negative. It's like air. Too much = hurricane. Too little = suffocation. The right amount = breath of life."

### Anxiety (Fear)
- Overcome by **experience**
- Lessens with exposure to pressure situations

### Tension (Excitement Level)
- Natural tension state varies by sport
- Shooting = low tension
- Basketball/football = high tension
- **Swimming = medium** (especially sprints/IM)

**If too nervous:**
1. **Recognize pressure is positive** — body saying "This is important, pay attention"
2. **Focus on what you WANT, not what stresses you** — Process, not outcome
3. **Use a recovery strategy:**
   - 3 deep breaths (pattern you control)
   - Relax neck/shoulders
   - Visualize being in control
4. **Fake a yawn** — releases relaxation chemicals

**Bassham:** "Coal under pressure produces a diamond. Competitors in control under pressure produce winning scores."

## Decisiveness

**Bassham definition:** "Having the power to determine an outcome beyond doubt."

**The look of decisiveness:**
- Shoulders set straighter
- Chin lifts
- Quiet focus in the eyes
- "This is going to be a great day"

**Why we lose decisiveness:**
1. **New to sport** — Solution: View first meets as learning, be decisive about what you DO know
2. **Ill-prepared** — Solution: Work the problem, stay strong in what you CAN do
3. **Overly cautious** — Solution: Caution → indecision → paralysis

**Bassham:** "Trying not to make a mistake is a sure way of making one."

## Building Self-Image: The 4-Step Process

### Step 1: Be Willing to Change
**Bassham:** "Our Self-Image does not respond to changes others want us to make. You must decide for yourself and you must choose to do it NOW."

### Step 2: Identify What Needs to Change
**Example:** "I'm looking for a solution to worrying about swimming below my average in the days before a big meet."

### Step 3: Identify the New Self-Image (Direct Conflict)
**New attitude:** "I only think about what I want to have happen concerning upcoming competitions."

### Step 4: Exchange Old for New Through Imprinting
**How to imprint:**
- Actual performance (environmental imprint)
- **Imagined performance** (rehearsal — just as powerful)
- Writing (strongest)
- Talking
- Thinking (weakest)

**The trap:** Worrying = negative rehearsal = imprinting failure

**The fix:** Only rehearse/write/talk about what you WANT to happen

## Actionable Takeaways for Swimmers

1. **Start a Performance Journal** — Record successes daily, never failures
2. **Write a Directive Affirmation** — 21 days to change one limiting belief
3. **Create a mental program** — Behind blocks, turns, finishes (same every time)
4. **Rehearse races daily** — 5 minutes before bed, visualizing success
5. **Reinforce good swims** — "That's like me!" after every great rep
6. **Feast or Forget** — Praise success, forget error immediately
7. **Train 4-6 days/week** — Consistency builds Subconscious skill
8. **When swimming well, swim more** — Capitalize on momentum
9. **Plan your year** — 4 quarters, objectives per quarter
10. **Stop over-trying** — Care about process, not outcome
