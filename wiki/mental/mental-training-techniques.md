# Mental Training Techniques

**Practical mental skills for competitive swimming — imagery, focus, pressure management, and the 7 C's of mental toughness.**

---

## The Foundation: 90% Mental

> "Baseball is 90% mental. The other half is physical."  
> — Yogi Berra

**The problem:** Most swimmers spend 0% of their time training the mental game.

**Questions to ask yourself:**
- How many books about sports psychology have you read?
- How many lessons have you taken from a "head" coach?
- How much time do you spend practicing mental skills?

**The reality:** The mind is like a VCR — it records sights and sounds, and the tape plays continuously. **The human body treats every vivid thought and image as if it is real and happening NOW.**

## The Art & Science of Swimming (Mental Edition)

### **Art (Feeling)**
- Being "at one with the water"
- Fluid movement (like an artist's brush)
- Graceful (like dance)
- Powerful (like musical harmony)

### **Science (Understanding)**
- Understanding how to produce the most propulsion with the least effort
- Using physics principles deliberately
- Thinking through movement as an equation

**Best practice:** Engage both body AND brain. Drills are useful because they encourage feeling + understanding.

## Mental Rehearsal & Imagery

**The head edge** = Using sports psychology (the science of success) to outperform athletes of equal physical ability.

**Research finding:** Take 20 athletes of equal ability. Give 10 mental training. **They will outperform the other 10 EVERY TIME.**

---

### **Study 1: Free Throw Shooting (College Basketball)**

**3 groups, 3 months:**
1. **Group A:** Shot free throws 1 hour/day (physical practice only)
2. **Group B:** Thought about shooting free throws 1 hour/day (mental practice only)
3. **Group C:** Shot free throws 30 min/day + visualized 30 min/day (combined)

**Results:**
- **Group C improved the most** (mental + physical)
- Imagery had AS MUCH IMPACT as physical shooting

**Takeaway:** Mental practice is as effective as physical practice.

---

### **Study 2: US Olympic Ski Team**

**Setup:**
- Two groups, matched for ski-racing ability
- **Group A:** Imagery training
- **Group B:** Control group (no mental training)

**Results:**
- **Group A improved so rapidly** that the coach called off the experiment
- He insisted ALL skiers receive imagery training

**Why it works:** Visualization builds neural pathways (brain → body connection) without physical wear.

## Pelé's Pre-Game Routine (The Mind Gym)

**Pelé's ritual (1 hour before every game):**

1. **Find private space** (locker room corner)
2. **Lie down** (one towel under head, one over eyes)
3. **Mental camera — Part 1:** Childhood memories
   - Playing soccer on the beaches of Brazil
   - Feel the gentle breeze, smell the salt air
   - Remember the FUN and LOVE for the game
4. **Mental camera — Part 2:** Greatest moments
   - Fast-forward to World Cup highlights
   - Relive the WINNING FEELING
5. **Mental camera — Part 3:** Upcoming game
   - Picture opponents
   - See himself dribbling through defenders, heading shots, scoring goals
6. **Physical prep:** Stretching exercises
7. **Result:** Physically AND mentally prepared

**Your turn:** Create your own mind gym (imaginary retreat for mental preparation).

## Bob Tewksbury's Mind Gym (MLB Pitcher Example)

**Problem:** Bob didn't have a great fastball. He relied on location + changes in speed.

**Solution:** Created an elaborate mental studio:
- **Bubble-like structure** (energy machine)
- **Ticker tape** (flashing positive affirmations)
- **State-of-the-art sound system**
- **Big-screen TV** (mounted overhead, playing highlight tape of himself)
- **Mind-gym bed** (for stretching out and mental practice)

**Result:** Bloomed into an All-Star with the Cardinals.

## Focus & Distraction Management

### **The Placekicker Problem**

**Story:** NFL kicker to mental coach: "I'm a great field goal kicker. But I just can't kick when Gene [Stallings, head coach] is watching me."

**Coach's response:** "Well, I think he's going to be at all the games."

**The issue:**
- Kicker was **self-conscious** and **coach-conscious**
- He was NOT **task-conscious**
- His mind was on his boss (negative thought)

**The fix:** **The mind can concentrate on only ONE thing at a time.**
- Rather than suppress what you DON'T want to happen…
- Replace it with a NEUTRAL or POSITIVE thought

---

### **Distraction Technique (Placekickers)**

**Example: Al Del Greco (Tennessee Titans, Super Bowl XXXIV)**

**His word:** "Birdie"

**Why it works:**
- Al is a scratch golfer (best golfer in the NFL)
- "Birdie" creates the FEELING of success
- Reminds him of the FUN he has on the golf course
- Blocks out negative thoughts, relieves tension

**Your turn:** Create your own focus word.

## Mind Games: Don't Look Where You Don't Want to Go

### **The Water Hole Problem (Golf)**

**Scenario:** Weekend golfer arrives at water hole.

**What he does:**
1. Fishes an old ball (water ball) out of bag
2. Steps to tee, tells himself: **"Don't hit it in the water."**

**What his mind hears:**
- **"Water, water, water."**
- Looking at water
- Thinking about water

**Law of dominant thought:** Your mind remembers the MOST DOMINANT THOUGHT.

**Result:** Ball goes in the water.

---

### **The Fix: Tell Your Mind WHAT TO DO (Not What NOT to Do)**

**Instead of:** "Don't hit it in the water"  
**Say:** "Land the ball 10 yards to the right of the pin."

**Swimming example:**

**DON'T say:**
- "Don't breathe too early"
- "Don't drop your elbow"
- "Don't kick from your knees"

**DO say:**
- "Breathe when hand enters"
- "High elbow recovery"
- "Kick from hips"

**Principle:** **Actions follow thoughts and images.** The mind works most effectively when you tell it what TO do.

## Managing Pressure

### **The Pressure Principle (Scott Hamilton)**

> "Under pressure you can perform 15% better or worse."  
> — Scott Hamilton (Olympic Gold Medalist, Figure Skating)

**What is pressure?**
- Lee Trevino (Golf): "Pressure is when you've got $35 riding on a 4-foot putt and you've only got $5 in your pocket."
- Bill Lee (Baseball): "Pressure is something you feel only when you don't know what you're doing."
- Charles Barkley (NBA): "Pressure is what you put in tires."

**The truth:** Pressure is REAL. Every athlete feels it.

---

### **Where Does Pressure Come From?**

**John Elway (NFL Hall of Fame QB):**
> "Most of that pressure came from within."

**Mark Messier (Hockey legend):**
> "The only pressure I'm under is the pressure I've put on myself."

**Key insight:** **Pressure is self-imposed.**

---

### **How the Body Reacts to Pressure**

**Jack Nicklaus (Golf):**
> "Pressure creates tension, and when you're tense, you want to get your task over and done with as fast as possible. The more you hurry in golf, the worse you probably will play, which leads to even heavier pressure and greater tension."

**Arthur Ashe (Tennis):**
> "When tension rises, two things happen: the feet can't move and the diaphragm collapses. It's automatic. It's in the genetic code."

**Physical symptoms:**
- Heart beats faster
- Breathing quickens
- Muscles tighten
- Decision-making slows

---

### **Pressure Can Bring Out the Best**

**Goose Gossage (MLB Closer):**
> "I'm not at my best until the situation is at its worst."

**How Gossage handled pressure:**
- Kept his job in perspective
- Thought of his home in the Rockies (relaxed him)
- Told himself: "The worst thing that could happen is that I'd be home fishing there tomorrow."

**Lesson:** Reframe pressure. Keep perspective.

---

### **Scott Hamilton's Approach: Refined Indifference**

**Strategy:**
1. **Train for years** to prepare for the moment
2. **When the spotlight comes on:** Let fate carry you through
3. **Tell yourself:** "The hard work is over. Now go out and enjoy."

**Sarah Hughes (2002 Winter Olympics, Figure Skating):**
- In 4th place after short program
- **Feeling:** "I have nothing to lose."
- **Approach:** Skated with abandon, "uninhibited joy"
- **Result:** Won GOLD while older, more experienced Olympians faltered

**Quote:** "I didn't hold back. It was my greatest skate ever."

---

### **The Zone: Letting Go of Outcomes**

**Research (Ken Ravizza):** Study of athletes during their "greatest moment" in sports.

**Finding:** More than 80% said:
- They felt **NO fear of failure**
- They weren't thinking about their performance
- They were **immersed in the activity**
- They were **in "the zone"**

**Principle:** **The probability of achieving the outcome you want increases when you let go of the need to have it.**

## The 7 C's of Mental Toughness

**Definition:** Mental toughness = a set of behaviors and beliefs about yourself, your work, your sport, and how you interact.

**Core belief:** A mentally tough person looks at competition as a **challenge to rise up to** (not a threat to back down from).

**Good news:** Like physical skills, mental toughness can be LEARNED through quality instruction and practice.

---

### **1. COMPETITIVE**

**Nancy Lopez (Golf):**
> "A competitor will find a way to win. Competitors take bad breaks and use them to drive themselves just that much harder. Quitters take bad breaks and use them as reasons to give up."

**Swimming application:**
- Bad start? Make up for it in the turns.
- Missed your pace? Adjust and finish strong.
- Don't let setbacks become excuses.

---

### **2. CONFIDENT**

**Tiger Woods:**
> "Every time I play, in my mind I'm the favorite."

**Michael Jordan:**
> "I went into every game believing I was the best player on the floor."

**Key:** Can-do attitude. Belief in yourself. Confident athletes almost never fall victim to self-defeating thoughts.

**Swimming application:**
- "I belong in this heat."
- "I've trained for this race."
- "I am FAST."

---

### **3. CONTROL**

**Definition:** Successful athletes are able to control their emotions and behavior.

**Examples:**
- Don't retaliate to bad officiating
- Stay composed after a bad flip turn
- Manage frustration when touched out

**Hockey/Basketball lesson:** The athlete who retaliates is usually the one who gets penalized.

**Motto:** **"Keep your cool when the heat is on."**

---

### **4. COMMITTED**

**John McEnroe:**
> "There are scores of players who can hit every shot in the book who never make it into a Grand Slam event. Those who make it are there because they are mentally tougher. They wanted it more."

**Swimming application:**
- Show up to every practice
- Focus time and energy on your goals
- Self-directed, highly motivated

**Andre Agassi example:** Free fell from top of tennis rankings → rededicated himself → worked hard → got back into shape.

---

### **5. COMPOSURE**

**Definition:** Mentally tough athletes know how to stay focused and deal with adversity.

**Bad call lesson (Tennis):**
- Expect 2-3 bad calls in every match
- How you manage your emotions determines whether you win or lose
- **Mentally tough response:** "OK, if I've got to beat the other guy AND the referee, then fine — I'll do that."

**Swimming application:**
- Goggles fill with water? Stay calm, finish the race.
- Competitor false starts? Don't let it rattle you.
- Rough water (open water)? Adjust, keep moving forward.

---

### **6. COURAGE**

**Paul Stoltz (*Adversity Quotient*):**
- Success is like a mountain
- **Climbers** get to the top
- **Campers** get partway up and decide to stay where they are
- Only climbers feel alive and proud

**Key:** Mentally tough athletes are willing to take a risk. That's what peak performers do.

**Swimming application:**
- Negative split a race (go out slower, finish faster)
- Try a new stroke technique in competition
- Move up an age group / race against faster swimmers

---

### **7. CONSISTENCY**

**Definition:** Mentally tough athletes possess an inner strength. They often play their best when they're feeling their worst.

**Key:** **They don't make excuses.**

**Swimming application:**
- Feeling tired? Still give your best effort.
- Didn't sleep well? Execute your race plan anyway.
- Travel meet? Perform regardless of conditions.

## Know Your Numbers (Optimal Arousal)

**Concept:** The Performance Curve (Inverted "U")

**How it works:**
- **X-axis (horizontal):** Stress/Arousal (0-10)
- **Y-axis (vertical):** Performance/Productivity (0-10)
- **Peak performance:** Top of the curve (optimal arousal number)

**Everyone has a different optimal number.**

**Examples:**
- **Marathon runner:** Lower arousal (calm, steady)
- **Sprinter:** Higher arousal (explosive, intense)
- **Distance swimmer (1500m):** 5-6 arousal
- **Sprint swimmer (50m):** 8-9 arousal

**Your job:** Know YOUR number. Learn how to manage your arousal to reach it.

---

### **How to Manage Your Number**

**Too low (under-aroused)?**
- Increase heart rate (jumping jacks, dynamic stretching)
- Use energizing self-talk ("Let's GO!", "Attack!")
- Listen to pump-up music

**Too high (over-aroused)?**
- Slow breathing (4 counts in, 6 counts out)
- Use calming self-talk ("Relax", "Smooth and fast")
- Visualize a calming place (Goose Gossage's fishing spot)

**Herbert Benson (Harvard cardiologist) technique:**
- Focus on your breathing
- Repeat the word "one" (lowers blood pressure and heart rate)

## Practical Exercises

### **Exercise 1: Create Your Mind Gym**

**Instructions:**
1. Close your eyes
2. Imagine your ideal mental retreat (studio, beach, mountain, spaceship, whatever)
3. Add details (sounds, smells, sights, feelings)
4. Create a highlight reel (big-screen TV playing your best swims)
5. Add positive affirmations (ticker tape, voice recording, mirror with words)

**Use it:** Before races, visualize yourself in your mind gym. Watch your highlight reel. Feel the confidence.

---

### **Exercise 2: Mental Rehearsal (Swim-Specific)**

**The night before a race:**
1. **Visualize the pool** (see the starting blocks, feel the water temperature)
2. **See your race strategy** (pacing, breathing pattern, turn execution)
3. **Feel your strokes** (smooth, powerful, efficient)
4. **See yourself finishing strong** (touching the wall, looking at the clock)
5. **Relive the winning feeling** (pride, joy, accomplishment)

**Carl Yastrzemski (Baseball):**
> "The night before a game, I visualize the pitcher and the pitches I'm going to see the next day. I hit the ball right on the button. I know what it's going to feel like."

---

### **Exercise 3: The Greatest Moment**

**Recall a time when you broke through** (your personal best swim, a race you won, a goal you achieved).

**Notice:**
- Were you relaxed or tense?
- Excited or anxious?
- Did you fear failure or feel a desire to win?
- Were you focused on the outcome or absorbed in the process?

**Most athletes in "the zone" say:**
- They felt NO fear of failure
- They weren't thinking about the result
- They were immersed in the PROCESS (stroke, rhythm, pace)
