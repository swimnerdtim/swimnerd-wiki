# Overcoming Adversity: The Michael Phelps Origin Story
**Source:** *Beneath the Surface* by Michael Phelps (2004)

## The Foundation: Early Trauma

### Christmas Eve Viral Infection (Age 2)

**The crisis:**
- Developed serious viral infection
- White blood cell count dropping
- Temperature: 104°F
- Required emergency transport to Franklin Square Hospital (45 min away)

**Father's response:** Fred Phelps (Maryland State Trooper) put Michael in police car, flashed lights, blared siren

**Doctor's assessment:** "If the infection hadn't been treated within a few hours, I probably wouldn't have survived"

**First lesson:** Life is fragile. You're here because someone fought for you.

### The Dog Attack (Age 3)

**The scene:** Family dog Thadeus (white German Shepherd) in pen

**What happened:**
- Thadeus used to play with baby dolls - shake them, toss them, chew them
- Mistook 3-year-old Michael for a doll  
- Bit him in back of neck and started chewing
- Tossed him from one end of pen to the other
- Hilary (age 10) screamed and tried to pull dog off
- Fred eventually pulled dog off

**Injuries:** Broken skin in several places (could have been killed)

**What happened to Thadeus:** Put down due to rabies risk (2 raccoons near house died from rabies/distemper)

**Phelps's memory:** "The day Thadeus mistook me for a rag doll was my first memory of anything"

**Lesson:** Trauma shapes memory. Your first memory sets your baseline for life.

### The Broken Collarbone (Age 7)

**How it happened:** Wrestling with friend Russell Fitzell on lawn

**The problem:** Phelps complained but couldn't articulate what hurt (dealing with parents' separation, complained about everything)

**Diagnosis:** Broken collarbone

**Result:** Wore thick back brace all summer

**The frustration:**
> "It was so hot and I wanted to get in the water and play around the pool with my friends, but instead I just sat there, because I had no choice."

**When brace came off:** "I was fired up to swim again"

**Lesson:** Forced absence creates hunger.

## The Divorce (Age 7)

### The Conversation

**The scene:** Walked into parents' room, Mom with sad look, sisters crying

**Phelps's reaction:**
> "You know how you walk into a room and realize you've interrupted a heavy conversation and you don't know how to make a graceful exit?"

**Words he'd never heard before:** "Separation" and "Divorce"

**What he didn't understand:**
> "Parents are always together. There is a mom and there is a dad and they will always be there to look after kids like me, to teach us, guide us, pat us on the back, have all the answers because parents just know these things."

### The Realization

**The video game moment:**
> "I used to bounce into our den and wait anxiously for my dad to get off the computer so I could play a game. Suddenly I didn't have to wait anymore. Maybe my dad really wasn't coming back."

**The questions:** "I'd ask my mom why and my sisters why, but they didn't offer an answer. Perhaps nobody really had one."

**Context:** Parents were high school sweethearts
- Met at Bruce High School (Westernport, MD - pop. 2,000)
- Both earned education degrees  
- Both athletes (Mom: cheerleader, Dad: football DB, almost made Redskins)
- Together since they were kids

### What It Changed

**Before:** Stable family, both parents present, traditional structure

**After:** 
- Single mom (Debbie) raising three kids
- Dad (Fred) away
- Uncertainty about future
- Processing grief while hyperactive and 7 years old

**The timing:** Same year Phelps started swimming seriously

**The connection:** Swimming became the stability when home life was unstable

---

## Fear of Water (Age 7)

### The First Swim Lesson

**Who taught him:** Cathy Lears (neighbor, family friend)

**Phelps's reaction:**
> "I hated it. We're talking screaming, kicking, fit-throwing, goggle-tossing hate."

**The problem:** "I was scared to get my face wet"

**The shallow end:** OK, because he could stand up

**Deep end:** Tense, rigid, unable to float, sinking

### The Compromise

**Cathy Lears:**
> "That's your problem, Michael Phelps. Your mom wants you to learn how to swim, so you're going to swim."

**Phelps:** "But I don't want to get my face wet." (Screaming stink)

**The solution:**
> "Well, you can start on your back and learn to swim that way, but you're going to learn one way or another."

**Why backstroke worked:** Couldn't see how deep the water was

**The progression:**
1. Float on back
2. Flail arms and legs (without sinking)
3. Flailing turns into swimming
4. Eventually turns over on stomach
5. Learns Australian crawl

**The transformation:**
> "Once I figured out how to swim, I felt so free. It was like I had this new toy that my sisters enjoyed all this time. I went to the pool every day and it usually took a while to get me to leave."

### The Irony

**Age 7:** Screaming, refusing to get face wet, terrified of water

**Age 19:** Eight Olympic medals, six gold, two world records

**The lesson:** Your biggest fear can become your greatest strength.

## Whitney's Injury: The Sister's Fight

### Whitney's Path

**Background:**
- Named outstanding swimmer in Maryland (1990-1993)
- Age 13: Qualified for world team (2nd place 200 fly at nationals)  
- Age 14: Only 14-year-old on US team at '94 Worlds in Rome (9th place)
- Expected to be "the next star of USA Swimming"

**Her personality:**
- If coach said do 10, she'd do 12
- If coach said arrive at noon, she'd be there at 11:30
- Never complained
- Mature beyond her years

**The buzz:** "Whitney Phelps will be the next star. Gold medals are a matter of time."

### The Back Injury

**Age 9-10:** Minor back soreness, recurring

**Early 1995:** Murray Stephens had Whitney swim with a monofin (training device adds resistance)

**What happened:**
- Didn't cause the problem, but triggered first serious strain
- Whitney didn't tell Murray
- Pain increased over time

**1995 Pan-Pacific Championships:**
- Won bronze medal for USA in 200 fly
- Left pool in serious pain  
- Every flip turn = nerves tugging at back
- Arms and legs would go numb when she bent over

### The Grind

**Whitney's training philosophy:**
> "If that extra lap was going to take an extra hundredth off her time, she'd plow through it, trying to gain an advantage. She wanted to be the fittest in order to be the best."

**The problem:** Doing sets Michael "never had to do"

**The reality:** 
- Constant pain
- Numbness in limbs  
- Couldn't stop - Olympics approaching
- Pressure mounting

### The 1996 Olympic Trials

**Whitney's goal:** Make the team

**The result:** Didn't qualify

**What Michael saw:** His sister, the best swimmer he knew, coming up short of her dream

**The lesson for young Michael:**
1. Talent isn't enough
2. Injuries can derail everything  
3. Swimming is brutally unforgiving
4. Your body can betray you

**What happened to Whitney:** Eventually had to manage pain for years, career never reached potential

## The ADHD Struggle

### The Dinner Table

**Behaviors:**
- Twirled pens, pencils in middle fingers
- If no pens available: salt shaker, steak knife
- Tried to twirl glasses of milk (disaster)
- After eating: played with food
- Mixed ketchup, mayo, sugar, potatoes, spinach into "casseroles"  
- Would eat the casserole if still hungry (with milk on top)

**Mom's job:** Keep Michael away from anything that could break, spill, cause harm, or overseason food

### The School Struggles

**The teacher's assessment:**
> "Michael just can't focus on anything. Well, maybe he's bored with what he's being taught. I just don't ever see him being able to focus on anything in his life."

**Mom's response:**
> "Mrs. Phelps, you're not insinuating that Michael is especially gifted, are you?"

**The implication:** You're delusional. Your kid is a problem, not a prodigy.

### The Ears

**The physical reality:** Big ears

**Phelps's strategy:** Wore a hat all the time to hide them

**The bullying:** 
- Kids flicked his ears
- Exposed his ears  
- Talked about his ears
- On school bus: older kids took off his hat, flung it onto driveway
- "References to Spock, the Vulcan character in Star Trek"

**The sensitivity:** "Other kids knew they could get under my skin"

**How it shaped him:**
> "I'm not sure if it was because of my big ears or short attention span, but I always appreciated people who were patient and supportive and I always identified with underdogs."

### The Speech Issues

**The problems:**
- Dropped Ls when talking fast
- Added Ss to words  
- Had a lisp
- When trying to say "I don't have a lisp" → would lisp the word "lisp"

**The social cost:** Kids mocked him, made him repeat himself

**The pattern:** Physical differences + ADHD behaviors = easy target

## The Ritalin Years

### The Diagnosis (Sixth Grade)

**Dr. Wax's assessment:** ADHD affects ~2 million elementary school kids

**Potential complications:**
- Learning problems (dyslexia)
- Antisocial behavior  
- Severe depression

**Phelps's luck:** "Those things didn't happen to me"

### The Protocol

**Medication:** Ritalin, 3× daily
1. Morning
2. Lunchtime (had to leave class - nurse's office)
3. Before dinner

**Weekend:** No Ritalin on Saturdays (swim meets = energy outlet)

**The embarrassment:**
> "Other kids used to ask me why I would just disappear like that and what my problem was that I had to go to the nurse's office all the time."

**What it did:** Calmed him down, less jumpy in class

**What it didn't solve:** Underlying hyperactivity at swim meets

### The Kickboard Chaos

**The system:** Parents placed kickboards in rows of 8 along deck

**How it worked:** Kids stood/sat on boards, moved up one spot after each heat

**Fastest heat:** Last in line

**What Phelps did:**
- Running off to play with other kids
- Banging kickboard against pool deck  
- Sneaking to front of line to jump into someone else's race

**The correction:** Parents/officials pulled him away from front, board sat unoccupied in back

**The constant refrain:** "No, Michael, you have to wait your turn"

---

## The Temper: Goggle Throwing

### The Pattern

**What triggered it:** Losing races, bad swims, frustration

**What he did:** Threw goggles

**How far:** "With an outfielder's throwing arm, I could get some pretty good distance from a very energetic windup"

**Frequency:** "I had a lot of practice"

### The Princeton Turning Point

**The race:** 200-yard freestyle at meet in Princeton, NJ

**Result:** Kid from Delaware beat him

**The urge:**
> "I could feel the goggle-tossing urge inside me. This could be a really good heave, I thought. I'm really mad about this."

**What he did instead:** 
> "I kept it inside, let it simmer and waited for it to boil over during the remaining races."

**The result:**
- 5 more events at meet
- Won each of them  
- Equaled a national age-group record

**The lesson:**
> "It was a good lesson that I could find more constructive outlets for my frustrations by keeping my mouth shut, getting back in the pool, and kicking everyone's butt. If I could only remember that all the time."

**The problem:** He couldn't remember it all the time (yet)

## The Support Systems

### Barbara Kines (Third-Grade Teacher)

**What she did differently:**
- Called it "active personality" not "problem behavior"
- Instead of telling Michael what to do, asked what he liked to do
- Invited to swim meets with husband Rodge

**Rodge Kines:** Legally blind

**What Michael learned:**
- Introduced himself: "Hi, Mr. Rodge, it's Michael"
- Told other kids to do the same  
- Learned empathy from someone honest about limitations

**After winning freestyle race at Towson State:**
- Tapped Rodge's arm before saying anything  
- Rodge: "Michael, that was great"
- Michael: "How did you know it was me and the result?"
- Rodge: "Didn't you hear them cheering after they said your name? Michael, the cheering was for you. You were great."

**The lesson:** People with limitations can still see what matters most.

### Debbie Phelps (Mom)

**Her background:**
- Home economics teacher
- 2× Maryland State Teacher of the Year  
- Got a "C" in parenting class at Fairmont State

**Michael's grade for her:** "A-plus"

**What she provided:**
- Unconditional support
- Irrefutable logic  
- Got him to swim for water safety (Dr. Wax's recommendation)
- Defended him to teachers
- Raised three kids as single parent
- Alarm clocks at 4 a.m. to get Hilary to practice

**Her teaching:**
> "Try to keep calm in the face of tension and excitement"

**Applied at medal ceremony:** Singing anthem through teeth, not losing composure

### Hilary Phelps (Sister)

**Her role:** Nurturing, modest, strong sense of right/wrong

**The car door incident:**
- Door banged garage while moving car  
- Nick barely noticeable
- Apologized as if "she had rolled over the house"
- Called Mom from kitchen  
- Called Dad at work
- Already punishing herself

**Michael's take:** "That's Hilary"

**What she gave Michael:** Model of integrity

### Whitney Phelps (Sister)

**Her role:** Best pal, daily caretaker, fierce competitor

**Morning routine:**
- Wake Michael up (zombie at 7 a.m.)
- Make egg sandwich (threw random ingredients on it - cinnamon, peppers)  
- Michael wouldn't notice (smothered in mayo)
- Pack his lunch
- Watch Bobby's World together  
- Walk him outside for bus
- Boyfriend Victor would pick Whitney up for high school

**What she gave Michael:**
1. Daily stability  
2. Model of work ethic
3. Understanding that talent + work can still fall short
4. Appreciation for sacrifices siblings make

## The Transformation: From Goof-Off to Champion

### Tom Himes (First Real Coach)

**What he did:**
- Showed up at pool in Santa Claus outfit (Christmas)
- When Hilary's group won unexpected Zone meet: Let her shave off his mustache (kept promise)
- Every Friday: took Whitney and Michael to TCBY for yogurt
- Always looked after Michael
- Told jokes when Michael went from hyper to moody

**The impact:** 
> "He was the perfect age-group coach who taught the basics well and encouraged kids to have fun"

**What Michael learned:** Swimming could be joyful, not just work

### The Calculation Obsession

**What Michael started doing:**
> "I'd start looking at the clock and calculating split times in my head. Let's see: If that guy wants to swim X, that means he has to hit the 50-yard mark in Y."

**When Mom knew it was serious:** Michael talked about "tenths of seconds and negative splitting"

**The irony:** Can't focus in math class, but calculates split times for fun

### The Sleepswimming

**What the family heard:** Michael sitting up in bed, middle of night, yelling "One, two, three... GO!"

**What it meant:** Swimming had infiltrated his subconscious

**The significance:** The thing he once feared (water) now dominated his dreams

## Lessons for Coaches: Working with Difficult Kids

### 1. "Problem Behaviors" Can Be Channeled

**The teacher saw:** Can't focus on anything  
**Bob Bowman saw:** Unlimited energy to channel into swimming

**Michael's ADHD:**
- Twirling salt shakers → Calculating splits
- Goggle throwing → Competitive fire  
- Can't sit still → Trains 6 hours without tiring

**Application:** Stop trying to "fix" the kid. Channel the energy.

### 2. Trauma Creates Champions

**Michael's foundation:**
- Near-death viral infection (age 2)
- Dog attack (age 3)  
- Parents' divorce (age 7)
- Bullying (big ears, lisp, ADHD)
- Sister's injury derailing her dreams

**What it built:**
- Resilience
- Appreciation for opportunity  
- Fear of regret (saw Whitney miss Olympics)
- Hunger to prove doubters wrong

**Don't coddle athletes.** Let them struggle. It builds them.

### 3. Support Doesn't Mean Lowering Standards

**Debbie Phelps:**
- Supported Michael unconditionally  
- But didn't make excuses for him
- Challenged teachers who wrote him off
- Got him help (ADHD diagnosis, Ritalin)
- Made him stick with swimming even when he hated getting face wet

**Cathy Lears:**
- Patient but firm  
- "Your mom wants you to learn to swim, so you're going to swim"
- Found compromise (backstroke first)
- Didn't let him quit

**The balance:** Support + High Standards = Growth

### 4. Let Athletes See Failure

**What Whitney's injury taught Michael:**
- Talent doesn't guarantee success  
- Bodies can betray you
- Timing matters (injuries at wrong time = missed Olympics)
- You might only get one shot

**Don't protect young athletes from seeing older athletes fail.** It teaches urgency.

### 5. Find the Third-Grade Teacher

**Barbara Kines' approach:**
- Didn't label Michael as "problem"  
- Called it "active personality"
- Asked what he liked instead of telling him what to do
- Invited him into her life (swim meets with blind husband)

**Every team needs one coach/teacher who:** Sees potential where others see problems

## Lessons for Athletes: Your Adversity Is Your Fuel

### 1. Early Trauma Isn't Disqualifying

**Phelps before age 8:**
- Nearly died from infection
- Attacked by dog  
- Parents divorced
- Afraid of water
- ADHD diagnosis  
- Bullied for ears/speech

**Most people would think:** "This kid is damaged"

**Reality:** That kid became the greatest Olympian ever

**Your version:** Whatever happened to you doesn't define what you can become

### 2. What You Fear Can Become Your Strength

**Age 7:** Screaming, terrified to get face wet

**Age 19:** Olympic champion in water

**The pattern:** 
1. Face the fear (with support)  
2. Small wins build confidence
3. Fear transforms into freedom  
4. Mastery replaces terror

**Application:** The thing you're avoiding might be your destiny

### 3. Your "Disability" Might Be Your Advantage

**ADHD gave Phelps:**
- Unlimited energy (didn't get tired)
- Hyperfocus on swimming (when engaged)  
- Competitive fire (channeled frustration)
- Obsessive calculation (split times)

**What looks like a weakness** can become the foundation of greatness

### 4. Watch Your Siblings/Teammates Struggle

**What Michael learned from Whitney:**
- Talent isn't enough  
- Injuries happen
- You might only get one chance  
- Work harder while you can

**Don't insulate yourself from others' failures.** Learn from them.

### 5. Find Your Stability When Home Is Chaos

**When parents divorced:**
- Home life = unstable  
- Swimming = stable

**The pool became:**
- Daily routine when everything else was changing
- Place where effort = results (unlike home situation)
- Community when family was fractured
- Control when everything felt out of control

**Your version:** Find the thing that's stable when life isn't

---

## The Bottom Line

**The schoolteacher was right:** Michael Phelps couldn't focus on anything.

**Until he found the one thing worth focusing on.**

**Then the kid who:**
- Nearly died at age 2
- Got attacked by a dog at age 3  
- Lost his stable family at age 7
- Was terrified of water  
- Couldn't sit still
- Got mocked for big ears and a lisp  
- Took Ritalin 3× daily
- Threw goggles when he lost

**Became the kid who:**
- Calculated split times for fun
- Swam in his sleep  
- Trained 6 hours a day without tiring
- Visualized races to the hundredth of a second  
- Won 8 Olympic medals before age 20
- Changed the sport forever

**The difference:** He found people who saw potential instead of problems.

And he found the pool - the place where none of his "problems" mattered.
