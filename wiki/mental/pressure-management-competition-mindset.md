# Pressure Management & Competition Mindset (Bassham)

**Source:** *With Winning in Mind* by Lanny Bassham (Olympic Gold Medalist)  
**Focus:** How to use pressure as a weapon, not a weakness  
**Key insight:** Pressure = Anxiety + Tension (both controllable)

---

## The Pressure Paradox

**Common belief:** "I need to avoid pressure."  
**Truth:** "Pressure is neither positive nor negative. You need it in the RIGHT AMOUNT."

**Bassham:** "Pressure is like air. Too much = hurricane. Too little = suffocation. Just right = breath of life."

**Elite performers:**
- Don't avoid pressure
- Don't fear pressure
- **USE pressure**

---

## What IS Pressure?

**Pressure = Anxiety + Tension**

### 1. Anxiety = Fear

**Fear of:**
- Not performing well
- Disappointing others
- Losing
- Being embarrassed

**Good news:** Fear decreases with EXPERIENCE.

**Bassham's example:**
- 1972 Olympics (first time): Terrified
- 1976 Olympics (second time): Confident, in control, zero fear
- **Why?** He'd been there before. Familiarity = reduced anxiety.

**Swimming application:**
- First big meet: Nervous
- 10th big meet: "Just another race"

**How to reduce anxiety:**
1. Compete often (exposure therapy)
2. Rehearse the meet in your mind (mental familiarity)
3. Trust your preparation ("I've done the work")

**Cross-reference:** [Mental Management — Rehearsal](./mental-management-system-bassham.md#rehearsal)

---

### 2. Tension = Excitement Level

**Definition:** Your arousal state (calm ↔ pumped)

**Key insight:** Different sports/tasks require different tension levels.

**Tension spectrum:**

```
LOW                    MEDIUM                    HIGH
│                        │                        │
Archery/Rifle          Golf/Swimming           Sprinting/Boxing
(calm, steady)       (focused, controlled)    (explosive, intense)
```

**Swimming is MEDIUM tension:**
- Not comatose (rifle shooting)
- Not jacked up (100m sprint)
- Controlled intensity

**Problem:** Most swimmers are TOO TENSE in big meets.

---

## The Natural Tension State Mismatch

**Some people are naturally:**
- High-energy (bouncing off walls)
- Low-energy (calm, steady)

**If your natural state ≠ your sport's optimal state → you must manage tension.**

**Example:**

### High-Energy Person in Swimming

**Natural state:** Jacked up, excitable  
**Optimal state:** Controlled intensity  
**Risk:** Over-arousal → tight muscles → slower times

**Solution:** Learn to dial it DOWN.

---

### Low-Energy Person in Swimming

**Natural state:** Very calm, almost too relaxed  
**Optimal state:** Controlled intensity  
**Risk:** Under-arousal → sluggish start → poor performance

**Solution:** Learn to dial it UP.

---

## How to Manage Tension

### If You're TOO TENSE (Most Common)

#### 1. Recognize Pressure is POSITIVE

**Wrong mindset:** "I'm so nervous. This is bad."  
**Right mindset:** "I feel something. That means this matters. Good."

**Bassham:** "Pressure is your body saying: 'This is important. Pay attention.'"

**Reframe:**
- Butterflies = excitement (not fear)
- Adrenaline = power boost (not panic)
- Tension = energy to use (not enemy to fight)

---

#### 2. Focus on PROCESS, Not OUTCOME

**Outcome thinking (raises tension):**
- "I NEED to make finals"
- "If I don't swim 1:45, I'm done"
- "What if I false start?"

**Process thinking (lowers tension):**
- "Execute my race plan"
- "Nail my underwater on every wall"
- "Trust my training"

**Why it works:**
- Process = controllable → confidence
- Outcome = uncontrollable → anxiety

**Bassham:** "Thinking about winning while performing = over-trying = slower."

**Champions think about PROCESS during the race, OUTCOME after.**

---

#### 3. Use a Planned Recovery Strategy

**When tension spikes mid-competition:**

**3-Step Recovery Protocol:**

1. **Breathe** (3 deep breaths in a specific pattern)
   - In for 4 counts
   - Hold for 4 counts
   - Out for 6 counts

2. **Relax muscles** (target specific areas)
   - Drop shoulders
   - Unclench jaw
   - Shake out hands

3. **Visualize success** (5-10 seconds)
   - Picture perfect execution
   - FEEL the power

**Why it works:**
- Conscious mind can only think ONE thing at a time
- While breathing/relaxing/visualizing → NOT worrying
- You control breathing/muscles/thoughts (builds confidence)

**Cross-reference:** [Performance Under Pressure](./performance-under-pressure.md) — Recovery techniques

---

#### 4. The Fake Yawn Trick

**Bassam's secret weapon:**

**How:**
- Catch yourself getting too tense
- FAKE a yawn (exaggerate it)
- Hold for 3-5 seconds

**What happens:**
- Same chemicals that relax you during a real yawn are released
- Muscles relax
- Tension drops

**When to use:**
- Behind the blocks
- Waiting in ready room
- Between heats

**Why it works:** Body doesn't know the difference between real and fake yawn.

**Watch Olympic footage — you'll see it constantly.**

---

### If You're TOO RELAXED (Less Common)

**Problem:** Under-aroused. Not sharp. Sluggish.

**Solutions:**

#### 1. Physical Activation

- Jump jacks
- High knees
- Explosive arm swings
- Slap thighs/arms

**Goal:** Wake up the nervous system.

---

#### 2. Aggressive Self-Talk

- "Let's GO!"
- "Time to ATTACK!"
- "I'm READY!"

**Goal:** Amp up the energy.

---

#### 3. Visualize Explosive Performance

- See yourself CRUSHING the start
- FEEL the power in your stroke
- Picture DOMINATING

**Goal:** Mental activation.

## The Over-Trying Epidemic

**The #1 mental error in competition**

**Definition:** Trying TOO hard (beyond optimal effort level).

**Signs:**
- Tight shoulders/neck
- Rushing tempo
- Conscious override ("I've GOT to make this turn!")
- Performance drops compared to practice

**Why it happens:**
1. Afraid to trust Subconscious skill
2. Thinking about OUTCOME instead of PROCESS
3. Belief that "trying harder = better results" (FALSE for most sports)

---

## The Effort Paradox

**Question:** When do you swim your best times?

**Most swimmers:** "When I'm relaxed and just flowing."

**NOT:** "When I'm trying my absolute hardest."

**Why?**

**Every task has an OPTIMAL effort level:**
- 1% below optimal = underperformance
- 1% above optimal = underperformance

**The sweet spot = "Trying just hard enough"**

---

## Champions Work EASY in Competition

**Bassham's observation:**

> "Champions work HARD in training and work EASY in competition."

**What "work easy" means:**
- Trust your training
- Let the Subconscious execute
- Flow, don't force

**What it DOESN'T mean:**
- Lazy
- Not caring
- Half-effort

**Example:**

### Michael Phelps (2008 Beijing 200 Fly Final)

**Before race:**
- Goggles filled with water (couldn't see)
- Swam the entire race BLIND
- Still won GOLD and set world record

**How?**
- Trusted his stroke (Subconscious)
- Counted strokes per lap (Process focus)
- Didn't panic (Easy, not hard)

**If he'd over-tried:** "Oh no! I can't see! Pull harder!" → Would have lost.

**He trusted. He flowed. He won.**

---

## The Three Reasons People Over-Try

### 1. Afraid to Trust Subconscious Skill

**Root cause:** Insufficient training OR lack of mental preparation.

**Solution:**
- Train more (build skill bank)
- Rehearse success (build Self-Image confidence)
- **Bassham:** "The key is to work hard ENOUGH in practice to just TRUST your Subconscious in competition."

---

### 2. Thinking About OUTCOME Instead of PROCESS

**If you're worried about:**
- Your time
- Your place
- Your competitors
- What happens if you fail

**Then you're NOT thinking about:**
- Your stroke
- Your race plan
- Your execution

**Conscious mind = one thing at a time.**

**Solution:** Redirect focus to process (stroke, walls, tempo).

---

### 3. Belief That "Trying Harder = Better Results"

**Where this comes from:**
- School: "Try your best!"
- Little League: "Give 110%!"
- PE class: "Run as fast as you can!"

**Where it works:**
- Powerlifting (max effort = max weight)
- 100m sprint (explosive all-out)

**Where it FAILS:**
- Swimming (over-trying = tight muscles = slow)
- Golf (over-swinging = slice)
- Shooting (over-controlling = miss)

**Solution:** Change your belief.

**New belief:** "Optimal effort = optimal results. Not maximum effort."

---

## Dial It Back: The 90% Rule

**If you swim worse in meets than in practice → you're over-trying.**

**Experiment:**

### The 90% Effort Test

**Next meet:**
- Swim at **90% effort**
- Focus on SMOOTH, not HARD
- Let it flow

**Many swimmers discover:**
- 90% effort = FASTER times
- Why? Tension drops, stroke stays long, rhythm intact

**Bassham:** "The best performances seem effortless."

---

## Process vs. Outcome: The Mental Shift

**Outcome goals (SET these in training):**
- "I want to swim 1:45 in the 200 free"
- "I want to make Olympic Trials"
- "I want to win conference"

**Process goals (FOCUS on these in competition):**
- "Execute my race plan"
- "Nail every underwater"
- "Finish the last 50 strong"

**Why the shift?**
- Outcome = uncontrollable (depends on competitors, conditions, luck)
- Process = 100% controllable (you decide how you swim)

**Ben Crane example (PGA Tour):**
- 2010: Set goal to "make the process primary" (not "win tournaments")
- Won 2 tournaments that year (first wins in years)
- After winning: Had NO IDEA he'd won until his wife ran onto the green
- **He was so focused on PROCESS, he didn't even know the OUTCOME**

**Cross-reference:** [Bob Bowman — Mastery and Motivation](../coaches/bob-bowman/mastery-and-motivation.md) — "Process is Primary"

## The Winning Performance vs. Winning the Event

**Key distinction:**

### Winning Performance

**Definition:** Executing YOUR best race, regardless of outcome.

**Characteristics:**
- You controlled what you could control
- You executed your race plan
- You trusted your training
- You left it all in the pool

**Result:** You might finish 1st... or 5th. Doesn't matter.

---

### Winning the Event

**Definition:** Finishing first on the scoreboard.

**Characteristics:**
- You had the fastest time that day
- You earned the medal/trophy

**Result:** Doesn't always = best performance (sometimes you win on a bad day due to conditions, others' failures, etc.)

---

## Why "Winning Performance" Matters More

**Bassham's argument:**

**You can have a winning performance and NOT win the event:**
- Late start time in bad weather
- Someone else has the race of their life
- Equipment malfunction (goggles, suit)

**You can win the event and NOT have a winning performance:**
- Everyone else swims terribly
- You get lucky
- You win on a time that's slower than your practice bests

**Goal:** Have a winning performance EVERY time.

**Side effect:** You'll win more events.

---

## When to Think About Winning

**Training:** ✅ Think about winning CONSTANTLY
- "I'm going to win NCAAs"
- "I'm going to make the Olympic team"
- Visualize standing on the podium

**Why?**
- Builds Self-Image ("It's like me to win")
- Prepares you mentally for success
- Creates familiarity with winning

**Competition:** ❌ DON'T think about winning
- Focus on execution
- Trust your process
- Let the outcome happen

**Why?**
- Thinking about winning = outcome focus = over-trying
- Over-trying = slower

**Bassham's rule:**  
> "Think about winning in TRAINING. Keep your Conscious Mind on PERFORMANCE in the big event."

---

## The Three Phases of a Race (Mental Preparation)

### 1. Anticipation Phase (Before the Race)

**What you think about in the 10 minutes before you dive in.**

**Champion's checklist:**

✅ **Run your Mental Program**
- Visualize perfect start
- See your race plan
- FEEL yourself executing

✅ **Positive self-talk**
- "I'm ready"
- "I've done the work"
- "This is my race"

✅ **Control what you can control**
- Warm-up routine
- Breathing pattern
- Mental cues

❌ **Avoid:**
- Thinking about competitors
- Worrying about time
- Replaying past failures

**Bassham's Munich 1972 prep:**
- Rifle damaged before competition
- Had to borrow another shooter's rifle
- Said: "Wouldn't it be special to win with a borrowed rifle. I'm going to do it."
- **Set world record.**

**Lesson:** Circumstances don't matter. Mindset matters.

---

### 2. Action Phase (During the Race)

**What you think about WHILE racing.**

**Champions think:**
- Very little (mostly silence)
- Process cues ("Drive the walls")
- Trust

**Not:**
- "Am I ahead?"
- "Is she catching me?"
- "I hope I make finals"

**Bassham's Thun 1974 World Championships:**
- Competition in Switzerland
- Snowing so hard he couldn't see the target
- Said: "I'm the best snow shooter here. I love this."
- **Won the match.**

**Quote:** "Sometimes when your best is not good enough, you have to do what is REQUIRED to win."

**Swimming example:**

### Adapting Mid-Race

**Problem:** Feel terrible at 100m of 200 free  
**Wrong response:** "I'm dying. This sucks. I'm going to lose."  
**Right response:** "Okay. This is hard. Execute anyway."

**Process focus keeps you in the race.**

---

### 3. Reinforcement Phase (After the Race)

**What you think about in the 5 minutes after you finish.**

**Champions:**
- ✅ Reinforce what went WELL
- ✅ "My turns were perfect"
- ✅ "I finished strong"

**Average swimmers:**
- ❌ "I screwed up my start AGAIN"
- ❌ "Why do I always breathe into the wall?"

**Why reinforcement matters:**
- Every time you THINK about an error → imprint it again
- Every time you TALK about an error → imprint it deeper
- You become what you reinforce

**Jack Writer (Olympic Champion):**
- NEVER talked about bad performances
- Only discussed his HIGH scores
- "I don't want to give you an edge"

**Margaret Murdock (Olympic Silver Medalist):**
- Rarely talked about herself
- Only praised others
- **Every time she praised others → built her OWN Self-Image**

## The Mental Program for Racing

**Bassham's 5-step program (adapted for swimming):**

### Step 1: Point of Initiation

**Physical trigger to start mental routine.**

**Examples:**
- Step onto the block
- Pull goggles down
- Grab the starting handle (backstroke)

**Purpose:** Signal to brain: "It's time. Focus NOW."

---

### Step 2: Point of Attitude

**Picture what it FEELS like to execute perfectly.**

**Visualize:**
- The feeling of an explosive start
- The sensation of perfect streamline
- The power in your stroke

**NOT the outcome (time, place) — the FEELING of performance.**

---

### Step 3: Point of Direction

**Rehearse the action.**

**See:**
- Driving off the blocks
- Hitting the water clean
- 5-6 dolphin kicks
- Breakout strong

**Brief (5-10 seconds), vivid, positive.**

---

### Step 4: Point of Control

**Center on the most critical part of the race.**

**Examples:**
- "My underwater"
- "My tempo"
- "My finish"

**Think:** "Smooth. Controlled. Powerful."

---

### Step 5: Point of Focus

**Last thought before the beep.**

**One word or phrase:**
- "GO"
- "ATTACK"
- "TRUST"

**Or:** Silence. Let the Subconscious take over.

**Then:** DIVE.

**Champions who use pressure:**

### Malcolm Cooper (Olympic Shooting Champion)

**Situation:**
- Favored to win
- Discovered rifle was DAMAGED before match
- Had to borrow another shooter's rifle

**Response:**
- "Wouldn't it be something to win with a borrowed rifle. I'm going to do it!"
- **Set world record.**

**Lesson:** Turned adversity into opportunity. Embraced the challenge.

---

### Darrell Pace (Olympic Archery Gold, Age 19)

**Mindset:**
- Referred to the gold as "MY medal"
- "I wonder where they're keeping MY medal"
- "Everyone's after MY medal, but they can't take it"

**Had already won in his mind.**

**Bassham:** "He helped reinforce many of my own attitudes on winning."

**Both won gold in 1976.**

---

### Lanny Bassham (1976 Olympics)

**Before the Games:**
- Rehearsed EVERY possible thing that could go wrong
- Planned responses to every contingency
- Reduced stress by being PREPARED

**During the Games:**
- Calm
- Confident
- Executed

**Result:** GOLD medal.

**Quote:** "I was very calm because I had rehearsed over and over every possible thing that could happen and how I should respond."

## Handling the Unexpected

**Swimming scenarios:**

### Goggles Fall Off

**Wrong response:** Panic. Stop. Lose.

**Right response:**
- "Okay. Swim blind. Count strokes."
- (Michael Phelps did this. Won gold.)

---

### False Start (By Someone Else)

**Wrong response:** "Ugh. Now I have to get up again. This is annoying."

**Right response:** "Extra chance to rehearse my start. Good."

---

### Awful Prelim Swim

**Wrong response:** "I suck. I'm going to bomb finals too."

**Right response:**
- "That's done. What did I learn?"
- "Finals is a new race. Execute."

**Bassham:** "Do not reinforce a bad performance by getting angry or talking about it."

## Decisiveness: The Hidden Edge

**Decisiveness = Having the power to determine an outcome beyond doubt.**

**What it looks like:**
- Shoulders set a little straighter
- Chin lifts slightly
- Eyes have quiet focus
- Posture radiates confidence

**Where it comes from:**
- Preparation (you've done the work)
- Trust (you believe in your training)
- Decision (you DECIDE to perform)

**You can't fake it. You can't buy it. You can't earn it.**

**You can only DECIDE it.**

---

## Three Reasons for Indecisiveness

### 1. New to the Sport

**Solution:**
- View first meets as LEARNING experiences
- Be decisive about what you KNOW you can do well
- Build confidence in small areas first

---

### 2. Ill-Prepared

**Solution:**
- Train more
- Rehearse more
- Trust will come with preparation

---

### 3. Overly Cautious

**Solution:**
- Stop hedging
- Commit to the race plan
- "I'm doing THIS. Period."

**Bassham:** "Indecision = trying not to make a mistake = guaranteed to make one."

## Practical Implementation for Coaches

### 1. Teach the Mental Program

**Every swimmer should have:**
- Point of Initiation (physical cue)
- Point of Direction (visualize execution)
- Point of Focus (final thought before GO)

**Practice it:** Every rep in practice.

---

### 2. Process-Focused Meet Talks

**Before the meet:**
- ✅ "Execute your race plan"
- ✅ "Trust your training"
- ❌ "You need to swim 1:45 to make finals"

**Outcome goals belong in TRAINING.**  
**Process goals belong in COMPETITION.**

---

### 3. Post-Race Reinforcement Protocol

**After EVERY race:**

**Coach asks:** "What did you do WELL?"

**Swimmer lists:** 3 things (turns, tempo, finish)

**Coach praises:** Specific successes

**NO dwelling on errors** (save that for practice).

---

### 4. Tension Management Drills

**In practice:**
- Teach fake yawn trick
- Practice 3-step recovery (breathe, relax, visualize)
- Simulate meet pressure (race pace sets with announcements, cheering)

**Build mental muscle in training.**

---

## Bottom Line

**Pressure is not the enemy.**

**Over-trying is the enemy.**

**Solution:**
1. Trust your training
2. Focus on process (not outcome)
3. Work easy in competition
4. Use pressure as fuel (not fear)

**Result:** You perform BETTER under pressure than in practice.

**Because your Self-Image believes:** "Big moments = my moments."
