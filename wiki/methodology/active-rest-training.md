# Active Rest Training: Increasing Anaerobic Threshold

**Source:** *Janet Evans' Total Swimming* (2007), Chapter 7  
**Context:** Evans' favorite training method for building "easy speed"  
**Audience:** Coaches & competitive swimmers wanting to swim faster for longer

---

## What is Active Rest?

> "Allowing the heart rate to drop (the rest part) while you're swimming (the active part), instead of while you're at the wall."

**The concept:**
- **Traditional rest:** Stop at wall → heart rate drops → push off
- **Active rest:** Keep swimming → vary effort → heart rate fluctuates → no stopping

**Result:** More time spent training at race-relevant intensities without full recovery.

---

## The Physiological Target: Anaerobic Threshold

### Definition
**Anaerobic threshold (AT):** The absolute maximum level of effort at which the body can continue to transport enough oxygen TO muscles and lactic acid FROM muscles.

**The AT Window:** Effort between aerobic (sustainable forever) and anaerobic (sustainable ~60 seconds).

| **Workout Type** | **Effort Level** | **What it trains** | **Sustainable duration** |
|-----------------|------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Aerobic (Base) | ES 4-6, 70-85% MHR | Cardiovascular endurance | Hours |
| **AT (Active Rest)** | **ES 4-8, 70-90% MHR** | **Anaerobic threshold + "easy speed"** | **Minutes to 30+ min** |
| Anaerobic (Speed) | ES 9-10, 95-100% MHR | Raw speed/power | ~60 seconds |

**Why AT training matters:**
> "It's one thing to be able to swim at ES 2 indefinitely. It's quite another to be able to swim at ES 7 indefinitely. The higher your anaerobic threshold, the less difficult it will feel to go faster."

---

## "Easy Speed" — The Holy Grail

**What is easy speed?**
- Ability to hold fast paces without feeling like you're sprinting
- Swimming at ES 7 feels like ES 5
- Race pace feels sustainable, not desperate

**How AT training builds it:**
1. Train body to clear lactate faster
2. Improve oxygen delivery efficiency
3. Raise the ceiling on "comfortable fast"
4. Make speed feel easier over time

**Evans' perspective:**
> "I consider anaerobic threshold workouts, also called 'active rest,' to be the most enjoyable."

**Why she loved them:**
- Effort levels change often → workouts never stale
- Fast swimming → heart rate up to ~90% MHR
- Slower swimming → chance to recover before next fast swim
- Develops ability to distinguish between varying effort levels

## Active Rest Set Structures

### Classic Active Rest Pattern

**Format:** Fast swim → easy swim → repeat

**Example 1:** Workout #1 (1,200 yards)
```
1 × 100 free on 2:20 (ES 4) ← EASY
4 × 75 free on 1:20 (ES 8)  ← FAST
Repeat 3 times
```

**How it works:**
- ES 8 on 75s → heart rate climbs to ~90% MHR
- ES 4 on 100 → heart rate drops to ~70% MHR while still swimming
- Short rest between reps, but enough to sustain effort through 3 rounds

**Example 2:** Workout #2 (1,200 yards)
```
1 × 50 free on 1:05 (ES 4)  ← EASY
1 × 100 free on 1:45 (ES 8) ← FAST
Repeat 8 times
```

**Key principle:** 50% or more of distance is ES 4 rest swimming, allowing you to maintain pace on the ES 8 swims.

---

### Descending Interval Active Rest

**Format:** Effort stays high (ES 6-8), but interval gets faster as set progresses.

**Example:** Workout #3 (1,500 yards)
```
15 × 100 free
  #s 1-5 on 1:55 (ES 6)
  #s 6-10 on 1:50 (ES 7)
  #s 11-15 on 1:45 (ES 8)
Repeat 5 times
```

**What makes this AT training (not pure speed):**
- No easy swimming built in
- But intervals allow 7-10 seconds rest → heart rate stays 80-90% MHR
- Teaches pacing + effort recognition
- Develops ability to hold pace with decreasing rest

**Evans:**
> "This is the kind of set that develops easy speed."

---

### Pyramid / Ladder Active Rest

**Format:** Alternate fast swims with easy recovery swims, varying distances.

**Example:** Workout #5 (1,800 yards)
```
1 × 300 free on 5:45 (ES 6) ← EASY
1 × 200 free on 3:40 (ES 7) ← FASTER
1 × 100 free on 1:45 (ES 8) ← FASTEST
Repeat 3 times
```

**Descending interval twist:** Base per 100 gets 5 seconds faster each repeat.

**Challenge:** 300s are "easy" but still work → you're tired by the time you hit 100s → must demonstrate endurance to repeat miniset 3 times.

---

### Build-Up Active Rest

**Format:** Gradually increase # of fast swims, with easy swims between.

**Example:** Workout #8 (2,000 yards)
```
1 × 100 free on 2:10 (ES 4)
1 × 100 free on 1:45 (ES 8)

1 × 100 free on 2:10 (ES 4)
2 × 100 free on 1:45 (ES 8)

1 × 100 free on 2:10 (ES 4)
3 × 100 free on 1:45 (ES 8)

1 × 100 free on 2:10 (ES 4)
4 × 100 free on 1:45 (ES 8)

1 × 100 free on 2:10 (ES 4)
5 × 100 free on 1:45 (ES 8)
```

**Strategy:**
- Each group of ES 8 100s = its own miniset
- Easy 100s = recover + prep for next group
- Start at strong (not sprint) pace that's sustainable
- Maintain pace throughout → ES 8 intervals don't get faster

**Evans' coaching:**
> "Just make sure to use the easy 100s to rest."

## Programming Active Rest Workouts

### Structure Components

**Every AT workout includes:**

1. **Warm-up** (400-600 yards, ES 1-2)
   - Easy swimming
   - Mix in drills/strokes
   - Get heart rate up gradually

2. **Drill set** (300-600 yards, ES 3-4)
   - Freestyle drills (One-Arm, 3-3-3, Hip-Shoulder-Forehead, Fist)
   - Focus on technique refinement
   - Prepare body for main set

3. **Pull set** (400-800 yards, ES 5-6)
   - Pull buoy + paddles
   - Long, stretched-out swimming
   - Shoulder warm-up before intensity

4. **Main set** (1,200-2,500 yards, ES 4-8)
   - Active rest structure
   - Mix of easy and hard efforts
   - Heart rate stays 70-90% MHR

5. **Cool-down** (150-300 yards, ES 1)
   - Easy swimming
   - Mix strokes/drills
   - Heart rate returns to baseline

### Distance Progression

**Evans' workout series:**
- Workouts 1-2: 2,500 yards (~50 min)
- Workouts 3-4: 3,000 yards (~60 min)
- Workouts 5-6: 3,500 yards (~70 min)
- Workouts 7-8: 4,000 yards (~80 min)
- Workouts 9-12: 4,500-5,000 yards (~90-100 min)

**Principle:** Increase by 500 yards every 2 workouts = gradual progression.

## Interval Guidelines for Active Rest

### How Much Rest to Build In

**Target:** Heart rate should NOT return to normal between reps.

> "The point of the rest between repeats isn't to allow your heart rate to return to normal. In fact, the converse is true. Rest should be kept to a minimum during most sets so that the heart rate stays somewhat consistent."

**Recommended rest by distance:**
- **50 yards:** 5 seconds minimum
- **100 yards:** 7-8 seconds
- **150 yards:** 10 seconds
- **200 yards:** 15 seconds
- **250 yards:** 17-18 seconds
- **300 yards:** 20 seconds

**For easy (ES 4) swims in active rest:** Can add 5-10 extra seconds to allow more recovery.

**Too much rest = defeated purpose:** If heart rate drops below 70% MHR between reps, you're no longer training AT.

---

## Effort Scale Recognition

**Active rest training is THE best way to learn effort recognition.**

**Why:** You're constantly shifting between ES 4 (moderate) and ES 8 (hard) within the same set.

**What swimmers learn:**
- **ES 4 feel:** Moderate effort, can hold conversation in short bursts, 70% MHR
- **ES 6 feel:** Definite effort, breathing harder, 80% MHR
- **ES 7 feel:** Serious effort, focused breathing, 85% MHR
- **ES 8 feel:** Very serious effort, controlled hard swimming, 90% MHR

**Application:** After mastering AT workouts, swimmers can self-regulate effort in any set.

## Common Mistakes & Fixes

### Mistake 1: Swimming Fast Swims Too Easy

**Problem:** Swimmer treats ES 8 swims like ES 6 → no AT training happening.

**Fix:**
- Check heart rate immediately after fast swim → should be ~90% MHR
- If it's only 80%, swimmer needs to go harder
- Coach: Give specific time standards or have them descend 3-5 seconds from warm-up pace

### Mistake 2: Not Using Easy Swims to Recover

**Problem:** Swimmer treats ES 4 swims like ES 7 → heart rate stays at 90% → can't sustain set.

**Fix:**
- Emphasize "long and stretched out" on easy swims
- Give SLOWER interval for easy swims (e.g., 100 on 2:20 vs. typical 2:00)
- Teach swimmer to feel heart rate dropping during easy swim

### Mistake 3: Taking Extra Rest at Wall

**Problem:** Swimmer takes 5-10 extra seconds beyond interval → heart rate drops below 70%.

**Fix:**
- Use pace clock discipline
- Explain that extra rest defeats AT purpose
- If swimmer NEEDS more rest, lower the base for easy swims

### Mistake 4: Starting Fast Swims Too Hard

**Problem:** Swimmer sprints first few reps → can't maintain pace through set.

**Fix:**
> "The key is to go out at a moderate pace on the [early reps] so you have something left for the [later reps]."
- Teach negative splitting within set
- Give specific time ranges (e.g., "hold 1:35-1:38 on all ES 8 100s")
- Reward consistent pacing, not fast early times

## Sample Active Rest Workouts (Evans-Style)

### Beginner AT Workout (2,500 yards)

```
Warm-up: 400 free easy
Drill: 6 × 50 on 1:15 (25 drill, 25 swim)
Pull: 400 free with buoy/paddles

MAIN SET (1,200 yards):
1 × 100 free on 2:20 (ES 4)
4 × 75 free on 1:20 (ES 8)
Repeat 3 times

Cool-down: 200 free easy
```

**Total: 2,500 yards, ~50 minutes**

---

### Intermediate AT Workout (3,500 yards)

```
Warm-up: 600 free easy with backstroke/breast mixed in
Pull: 4 × 200 free on 4:20 with buoy/paddles

MAIN SET (1,800 yards):
1 × 300 free on 5:45 (ES 6)
1 × 200 free on 3:40 (ES 7)
1 × 100 free on 1:45 (ES 8)
Repeat 3 times

Cool-down: 3 × 100 free easy with 10 sec rest
```

**Total: 3,500 yards, ~70 minutes**

---

### Advanced AT Workout (4,500 yards)

```
Warm-up: 600 free (mix drills every 4th 50)
Kick: 12 × 50 on 1:45 (odd = free, even = fly/breast) with board
200 free easy to loosen arms

MAIN SET (1,950 yards):
1 × 50 free on 1:05 (ES 4)
2 × 100 free on 1:50 (ES 8)
1 × 50 free on 1:05 (ES 4)
4 × 100 free on 1:50 (ES 8)
1 × 50 free on 1:05 (ES 4)
6 × 100 free on 1:50 (ES 8)

Pull: 6 × 100 free on 2:10 with buoy/paddles
Cool-down: 300 free easy
```

**Total: 4,500 yards, ~90 minutes**

## When to Use Active Rest Training

### Training Cycle Placement

**Best used:**
- **Mid-season** (after base phase, before taper)
- **2-4 weeks before target race** (builds race-specific fitness)
- **Year-round maintenance** (1-2x/week alongside base + speed work)

**Not ideal:**
- **Early season** (need base endurance first)
- **Taper week** (too fatiguing)
- **Recovery weeks** (defeats recovery purpose)

### Frequency

**Evans' recommendation:** 2-3 AT workouts per week during build phase.

**Sample week:**
- Monday: Active rest workout
- Tuesday: Base/endurance workout
- Wednesday: Drills + easy swim
- Thursday: Active rest workout
- Friday: Speed/sprint workout
- Saturday: Long base workout
- Sunday: Rest or easy swim

## Measuring Progress

### Performance Markers

**You know AT training is working when:**
1. Times on fast swims stay consistent through entire set (no drop-off)
2. Heart rate recovers faster during easy swims
3. ES 8 pace starts to feel like old ES 7 pace
4. You can hold race pace for longer distances in practice
5. Racing feels easier at same speed

**Evans' test:**
- **Week 1:** 10 × 100 free on 1:45 (ES 8) → hold 1:35-1:38
- **Week 6:** Same set → hold 1:32-1:35 at same ES 8 feel

**Result:** "Easy speed" — you're swimming 3-6 seconds faster per 100 at the same perceived effort.

---

## Key Takeaway

**Active rest training = the bridge between endurance and speed.**

**What it is:** Varying effort levels (ES 4-8) within a single set to keep heart rate in the 70-90% MHR "sweet spot" — the anaerobic threshold zone.

**Why it works:** Trains the body to clear lactate, deliver oxygen efficiently, and sustain fast paces longer → "easy speed."

**How to structure it:** Fast swim (ES 7-8) → easy swim (ES 4) → repeat, OR descending intervals at ES 6-8.

**Evans' verdict:** "The most enjoyable workouts" — varied enough to stay interesting, effective enough to produce results.

**Proof:** Evans used AT training throughout her 10+ year Olympic career, winning 4 golds and setting multiple world records in distance events (400/800/1500 free).
