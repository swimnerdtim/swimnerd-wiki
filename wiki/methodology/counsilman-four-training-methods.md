# Counsilman's Four Training Methods

**Source:** *The Complete Book of Swimming* (1977) by Dr. James E. "Doc" Counsilman  
**Context:** Indiana University coach for 33 years, coached Mark Spitz to 7 Olympic golds, revolutionized training methodology  
**Key Insight:** Modern swimmers use FOUR methods, not just the two (overdistance + sprint) that dominated the 1920s-1940s

---

## Why This Matters

The improvement in competitive swimming over 30 years (1947-1977) was due **primarily to training method evolution**, not just talent. Thirteen-year-olds in 1977 swam faster than 1920s Olympic champions Johnny Weissmuller and Buster Crabbe at their peak.

**The revolution:** Adding **interval training** and **repetition training** to the traditional overdistance and sprint methods.

---

## Historical Context: The "Old" Two Methods (1920s-1940s)

### Typical Workout (Weissmuller/Crabbe Era)
1. **1 mile continuous** (1760 yards) — slow to moderate
2. **½ mile kick** (880 yards) — moderate speed
3. **½ mile pull** (880 yards) — with flotation device
4. **2 × 50 all-out sprints** + **2 × 25 all-out sprints**

**Total:** ~3,000 yards

**Modern comparison:** Today's high-performance swimmers train **5,000-12,000 yards/day**, with some peaking at 15,000 yards.

---

## The Four Modern Training Methods

### 1. Overdistance Training

**Definition:** Swim farther than race distance at slower speed than race pace.

**Purpose:** Build **cardiorespiratory endurance** (heart-lung efficiency)

**Target heart rate during overdistance:** 130-150 bpm (can spike to 180-200 on max efforts)

**Why it works:**  
- Heart becomes more efficient at pumping blood  
- Lungs increase size and oxygen absorption capacity  
- Muscles develop more **myoglobin** (oxygen carrier)  
- More **mitochondria** (cellular energy factories)  
- More functional **capillaries** open in muscles  
- Body adapts to sustain elevated heart rate for long periods

**Counsilman's monitoring rule:** Stop swimmers mid-set and check pulse. If below 130 bpm → "You're not working hard enough."

#### Five Ways to Swim Overdistance

**A. Without Timing (Not Recommended)**  
- Low motivation  
- Easy to swim too slowly  
- Use only when extremely tired and need active recovery

**B. Out Slow — Back Faster** ⭐  
Used by: Gary Hall, Mark Spitz, John Kinsella, Shane Gould

**Example:**  
- 800 yards total  
- First 400: 5:00 (75 pace)  
- Second 400: 4:40 (70 pace)  
- **Total: 9:40**

**C. Holding Steady Pace** (Most Popular)  
**Example:**  
- 800 yards in 9:20 target  
- Each 100 must be 1:10  
- Check pace clock every 100

**D. Speed Play (Fartlek)** ⭐  
Borrowed from track training (Swedish: "speed play")

**Example:**  
- Swim 30 minutes continuous  
- Vary speed: slow → moderate → fast  
- Within that time, hit timed segments:  
  - 2 × 200 (on pace clock)  
  - 4 × 100  
  - 4 × 50  
- Fill gaps with easy swimming

**Counsilman's Masters workout:** This is his personal favorite.

**E. Overdistance Interval Training**  
**Example:** 3 × 800 with 30 seconds rest  
- Total: 2,400 yards  
- **Advantage over continuous 2400:** More challenge, faster pace possible, better motivation

### 2. Interval Training

**Definition:** Series of repeat swims with **controlled short rest intervals**.

**Origin:** Introduced to competitive swimming in late 1940s — **this is when training science changed forever**.

**Key concept:** The "interval" refers to controlling the **rest period**, not the swim itself.

**Purpose:**  
- Build **endurance** (when rest is short)  
- Maintain elevated heart rate across multiple efforts  
- Train body to recover quickly while still working

#### The Four Factors You Must Determine

When designing interval training, answer these four questions:

**1. What distance will each repeat be?**

**General rule:**  
- **Longer repeats** → more endurance, less speed emphasis  
- **Shorter repeats** → more speed, less endurance emphasis

**Example:** Training for 200 yards?  
Use variety: 50s, 75s, 100s, 150s, 200s  
**Why variety?** Builds both qualities + prevents boredom

**2. How many repeats?**

**Guideline from elite swimmers (1970s):**  
- 40-70% of total workout should be interval/repetition sets  
- Learn from experience what your body can handle  
- Don't exceed recovery capacity

**3. What rest interval?**

**Critical principle:** **Shorter rest = more endurance emphasis**

**Why:**  
- 5-10 second rest → pulse drops from 180 to only 130-160  
- Heart rate stays elevated → cardiovascular stress continues  
- Partial oxygen debt recovery → you swim next repeat already tired

**vs. longer rest:**  
- 5 minute rest → pulse drops from 180 to under 100  
- Full recovery → next repeat is fresh → **this becomes repetition training** (see below)

**4. How fast should you swim each repeat?**

**Depends on the first three factors:**  
- Shorter distance + fewer reps + longer rest = **swim faster**  
- Longer distance + more reps + shorter rest = **swim slower**

**No exact formula exists.** Learn from experience by:  
1. Swim the set once  
2. Record average time  
3. Repeat same set in a few days  
4. Try to improve average  
5. Find your plateau (peak average)  
6. Improvement = better conditioning or stroke efficiency

**Swimmer culture:** Champions keep logbooks with best averages for every set:  
- "Gary averaged 1:26.5 for 10 × 150 fly"  
- "John averaged 4:38 for his 500s"

#### Real Example: John Kinsella (Former 1500m World Record Holder)

**Personal best 100 free:** 46.1

| Set | Rest | Avg Time | Pulse After | Pulse Before Next |
|-----|------|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **A. 30 × 100** | 5 sec (DP 60s) | **55.2** | 174 | 163 |
| **B. 10 × 100** | 20 sec (DP 1:30) | **50.4** | 181 | 124 |
| **C. 4 × 100** | 5 min (DP 6:00) | **48.2** | 184 | 88 |

**Analysis:**  
- **Set A:** Short rest interval training → endurance focus, heart rate stays high  
- **Set B:** Medium rest → blends speed + endurance  
- **Set C:** Long rest → **this is repetition training** (see below)

**Note on Departure Times (DP):**  
Most swimmers use **departure times** instead of exact rest intervals because the math is simpler:  
- DP 1:00 = leave every minute  
- DP 1:30 = leave every 90 seconds  
- If you swim 58, you get 32 seconds rest  
- If you swim 1:03, you get 27 seconds rest

**Why DP is better:** Different swimmers in the same lane can share departure times even at different speeds.

### 3. Repetition Training

**Definition:** High-quality, long-rest intermittent work where heart rate returns **close to normal** (under 100 bpm) between efforts.

**Key difference from interval training:** Full recovery between repeats.

**Purpose:**  
- Build **anaerobic capacity** (work without oxygen)  
- Train the body to handle **oxygen debt**  
- Simulate race-pace intensity  
- Strengthen respiratory muscles  
- Increase mitochondria at cellular level

**Effort level:** Almost maximum (90-95% effort)

**Physical experience:** "You literally **pant** after the effort is completed."

**Why this matters:**  
The specific stress of **oxygen debt** triggers different physiological adaptations than distance training:  
- **Quick energy release** systems improve  
- Heart sustains greater **pumping capacity** under stress  
- Body becomes efficient at anaerobic work

**Law of Specificity:** The body makes specific adaptations to specific stresses. Since 100m/200m/400m races create oxygen debt, you must train this system.

#### Usage Guidelines

**Frequency:** 2-3 times per week maximum

**Warning:** Too much high-quality repetition training → excessive fatigue → inability to recover by next day.

**Balance rule:**  
- Add one stressful factor (higher speed) → remove another (short rest)  
- Short rest → reduce speed  
- If you don't balance, you'll overtrain

**Who needs it:**  
ALL swimmers — sprinters, middle distance, AND distance swimmers. Even distance swimmers need repetition training for 200m/400m speed.

---

### 4. Sprint Training

**Definition:** All-out maximum effort swims (typically 25-50 yards).

**Purpose:**  
- Build **muscular strength** (muscles work against higher resistance at speed)  
- Train **neuromuscular coordination** at race speeds  
- Prevent feeling "uncoordinated" when trying to sprint

**Frequency:** Can be integrated into nearly every practice once peak training season is reached.

**Distance swimmer note:** Even distance swimmers need some sprint work for 200m events (now an "extended sprint").

**Why sprinting is different:**  
- Greater resistance at high speeds → muscles adapt by getting stronger  
- Nervous system learns to fire muscles in correct sequence at speed  
- "A swimmer who does nothing but slow, easy swimming will feel uncoordinated when he tries to sprint." — Counsilman

## Integrated Workout Example

**No single method is complete training.** You should use **3-4 methods in one workout**.

**Two purposes of integration:**  
1. **Variety** → prevent boredom  
2. **Different stresses** → develop both speed and endurance

### Sample Integrated Workout (Indiana University, Peak Indoor Season)

**Total: ~8,000-9,000 yards**

| Component | Method | Distance | Purpose |
|-----------|--------|----------|---------|
| **Warm-up** | Overdistance | 800 yds easy swim, 400 kick | Prepare body, elevate heart rate gradually |
| **Main Set 1** | Interval Training | 20 × 100 on 1:20 (DP) | Endurance, moderate speed, sustained heart rate |
| **Main Set 2** | Repetition Training | 8 × 100 on 5:00 rest | High quality, anaerobic work, oxygen debt |
| **Speed Work** | Sprint Training | 6 × 25 all-out (full recovery) | Neuromuscular coordination, max power |
| **Overdistance** | Active recovery | 400 easy pull | Heart rate stays elevated but muscles recover |
| **Cool-down** | Overdistance | 400 easy | Remove metabolic waste, gradual heart rate drop |

**Physiological changes from this workout:**  
- Myoglobin increase (warm-up/overdistance)  
- Mitochondria increase (interval + repetition)  
- Capillary density increase (overdistance)  
- Anaerobic enzyme activity (repetition)  
- Neuromuscular firing patterns (sprint)  
- Muscle strength (sprint + repetition)

## Key Principles of Adaptation

### The Concept of Stress-Adaptation

**Counsilman's explanation:**  
"The human body is a very adaptable organism that has the important trait of being able to change itself when a stress is placed on it so as to better handle this particular stress."

**Classic example:** Immunization  
- Stress: Introduction of weakened bacteria  
- Adaptation: Production of antibodies

**Athletic example:** "Getting into shape"  
- Stress: Conditioning program  
- Adaptation: Heart pumps more blood, lungs absorb more oxygen, muscles use oxygen more efficiently

**Critical insight:** These adaptations happen to **everyone** who trains, not just elite athletes. This is why swimming is one of the best forms of exercise.

### Specificity of Training

Different stresses → different adaptations:

| Training Method | Primary Stress | Primary Adaptation |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Overdistance** | Sustained elevated HR | Cardiorespiratory efficiency, capillary density |
| **Short-rest interval** | Partial recovery work | Aerobic capacity, HR recovery speed |
| **Repetition** | Oxygen debt | Anaerobic capacity, quick energy systems |
| **Sprint** | High resistance at speed | Muscular strength, neuromuscular coordination |

**Implication for coaches:** You can't build a complete swimmer with just one method. **All four are necessary.**

## How to Track Progress

### The Logbook Method ⭐

**What champions do:**  
- Record all practice times in a logbook  
- Track "personal bests" for every repeat set  
- Know their best averages: "20 × 100", "10 × 200", etc.

**Post-practice conversations:**  
- "Did you hear what Gary went for 10 × 150 fly?"  
- "Yes, 1:26.5 average — he should break the American record now."  
- "John averaged 4:38 for his 500s."

**Why this works:**  
- Practice times predict race times  
- Improving practice averages = improving fitness  
- Objective measure of progress  
- Builds intrinsic motivation

**Coach's job:** Memorize these averages and understand their significance for each swimmer.

## Practical Application for Coaches

### Designing a Training Week (Counsilman Framework)

**Monday:** Overdistance emphasis (6,000-8,000 yds)  
**Tuesday:** Interval training focus (7,000-9,000 yds)  
**Wednesday:** Repetition + sprint (6,000 yds, high quality)  
**Thursday:** Interval training (7,000-9,000 yds)  
**Friday:** Overdistance + sprint (6,000 yds, moderate)  
**Saturday:** Repetition training (5,000-6,000 yds, race simulation)  
**Sunday:** Active recovery or rest (2,000-4,000 yds easy, or off)

**Ratio guidelines:**  
- 40-70% interval/repetition work  
- 30-50% overdistance  
- 5-10% sprint (integrated into other sessions)

### Pulse Rate Monitoring (Counsilman's Innovation)

**What he did:**  
Stopped swimmers mid-workout to check pulse rate.

**Targets:**  
- Overdistance: minimum 130 bpm, ideally 130-150  
- Interval training (short rest): 130-180 bpm  
- Repetition training: 160-200 bpm during effort, drops to <100 between

**If below target:** "You're not working hard enough — pick up the pace."

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Using only one or two methods**  
→ Creates incomplete swimmers (either slow but durable, or fast but no endurance)

**2. Too much repetition training**  
→ Overtraining, inability to recover, injury risk  
→ **Solution:** Limit to 2-3x/week

**3. Swimming overdistance too slowly**  
→ Insufficient cardiovascular stress  
→ **Solution:** Monitor heart rate, use pace clock

**4. Not varying repeat distances**  
→ Boredom, limited adaptation  
→ **Solution:** Mix 50s, 100s, 200s even when training for same event

**5. Inconsistent rest intervals**  
→ Can't track progress, inconsistent stress  
→ **Solution:** Use departure times (DP)

**6. Ignoring sprint work for distance swimmers**  
→ Lack of speed for 200m/400m events  
→ **Solution:** Add 2-3 sprint sets per week

## Why This Changed Swimming Forever

**Before interval/repetition training (pre-1940s):**  
- Swimmers did 3,000 yards/day  
- Only two methods: overdistance + sprint  
- Limited physiological stress variety  
- Slower race times

**After interval/repetition training (1950s onward):**  
- Swimmers did 8,000-12,000 yards/day  
- Four methods with integrated workouts  
- Multiple stress pathways → more complete adaptations  
- **13-year-olds swimming faster than 1920s Olympic champions**

**Counsilman's contribution:**  
He didn't invent these methods, but he **systematized them** and taught coaches **why they work** at a physiological level. This created a generation of coaches who could design intelligent training programs, not just copy what others did.
