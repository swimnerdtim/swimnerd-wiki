# Drill Fundamentals: The Art & Science of Practice

**Source:** *The 100 Best Swimming Drills* by Blythe Lucero  
**Context:** Core philosophy for using drills to develop efficient, economic swimming  
**Audience:** Coaches & swimmers at all levels

---

## Core Philosophy: Efficient Swimming = Art + Science

### The Art of Swimming
**"Feeling" effective movement through the water.**

- **Fishlike** — Fluid, graceful movement
- **Balance, rhythm, posture** — Like dance
- **Harmony** — Combined actions producing greater results than parts alone
- **"At one with the water"** — Not fighting it

### The Science of Swimming
**"Understanding" movement through the water.**

> "Water, brains, and guts." — Doc Counsilman, "Father of Modern Swimming"

- **Physics at work** — Study movement as an equation
- **Cause & effect** — Understand what produces propulsion with least effort
- **Applying principles** — Learn to use the science in the water

**Best practice engages both body AND brain.**

---

## Economic Swimming vs. Efficient Swimming

### Economic Swimming (Step 1)
**Managing energy resources.**

Like gas in a tank:
- Swimming requires effort
- Effort uses energy
- Energy is not unlimited
- **Less effort = more distance/speed from same tank**

**Foundation of economic swimming:**
1. **Minimize drag** — Save energy
2. **Develop "feel" for the water** — Avoid wasted effort/extra strokes
3. **Access potential power** — Engage muscle groups best suited to move forward with least effort

### Efficient Swimming (Step 2)
**Applying conserved energy to sustaining speed over time.**

**Bridge from economic → efficient:**
1. Ongoing practice of economic swimming through good technique
2. Develop conditioning & strength to apply solid technique stroke after stroke

## What Is a Drill?

**Definition:** A structured method of practice used to acquire a skill, procedure, or sequence.

### Swimming Drill Specifics
- Activity designed to develop and practice good stroke habits
- Repetitive exercise for improving and perfecting technique
- Way of isolating part of a complex stroke sequence to learn and perform it correctly

### Types of Drills

**1. Progression-Based Drills**
- Build stroke one piece at a time
- Start with basic skill
- Add parts until all connected
- Feel and understand in context of coordinated stroke

**2. Part Practice**
- Isolate particular action when stroke flaw identified
- Repetitive practice of just that part
- Relearn weak area
- Implement new correct technique in full stroke

**3. Contrast Drills**
- Perform skill incorrectly first
- Immediately repeat correctly
- Notice better results from correct technique
- **Controversial but effective when followed by correct practice**

**4. X Drills (eXaggeration)**
- Use eXtreme to demonstrate effect of technique
- Take skill to its full range
- Feel desired technique more clearly
- Discover how to balance various stroke actions

## Seven Aspects Drills Address

### 1. Body Position
**Goal:** Master horizontal balance & core stability.

- Reduce drag
- Improve feel for water
- Increase access to power
- Achieve "swimming downhill" feeling

**Key:** Spine and core strength are crucial (like standing erect on land).

### 2. Kick
**Goal:** Provide forward momentum, rhythm, counterbalance.

**Challenge:** Large leg muscles demand great oxygen.

**Develop:**
- **Productive kick** — Relieves upper body workload
- **Sustainable kick** — Economical energy expenditure

**Requirements:**
- Relaxed feet & ankles
- Fluid motion
- Feet connected to water

**Important:** Kick for forward motion, NOT to correct floating problems.

### 3. Arm Stroke (Power Phase)
**Three dimensions:** Length, width, depth

**Three basic functions:**
1. **Maximize stroke length** — Longer strokes = fewer strokes = less energy
2. **Move body forward in straightest path** — Sculling/sweeping motions that accelerate
3. **Position hands to feel the water** — Use water like a handle; anchor and advance past it

### 4. Recovery
**Goal:** Return arm to starting point; rest momentarily (except breaststroke).

**Three keys:**
- **Relax** — Important for economic swimming
- **Align** — Misaligned recovery → misaligned stroke → constant correcting
- **Balance** — Connected to other stroke actions; must complement forward motion

**Bonus:** Aligned recovery prevents shoulder pain/injury.

### 5. Breathing
**Goal:** Replenish oxygen within the line of the stroke.

**Two-part timing:**
- **Inhale** — During recovery
- **Exhale** — During power phase (summons more power, like other sports)

**Rhythm theory:** Some believe breathing rhythm should determine stroke rate, pace, and strategy.

**Applies to all distances:** 50 sprint to mile.

### 6. Leverage
**Core-centered power.**

> "Like a ceiling fan — the motor in the middle propels the blades."

**Examples in other sports:**
- Baseball pitcher — hips & shoulders snap forward
- Batter, golfer, boxer, ice skater — all use core

**In swimming:**
- Stabilize joints (elbows, knees)
- Fish don't have elbows/knees — neither should we
- Don't let joints collapse and weaken levers

### 7. Coordination
**Goal:** Fit all pieces together for fluid, graceful, powerful forward motion.

**Key principles:**
- Use kick WITH arms, not just in addition
- Breathe within line of stroke (don't interrupt forward motion)
- Use body position to access leverage
- Time each action to complement others
- Develop weakest actions
- Make most of strongest actions

**Single goal:** Move through water with less effort → faster & longer.

## When to Use Drills

### Every Swim Session
- **Warm-up** — Establish foundation
- **Within sets** — Remind of correct technique when working hard
- **Between sets** — Refocus on swimming efficiently

### Competition Preparation
- **Taper** — Quality swimming, less intensive, maintain feel while recovering
- **Race day warm-up** — Practice ideal stroke actions before racing

### Off-Season
- Use drills almost exclusively
- Maintain conditioning
- Practice stroke economy
- Improve efficiency for next season

## How to Use Drills: The Rules

### Rule 1: Know the Purpose
**Just performing a drill is not enough.**

- Doing drill without understanding = empty swimming
- Must think about AND feel what is supposed to happen
- Must notice what IS happening

**Be fully engaged.**

### Rule 2: Focus on the Point
**Certain drills target very specific parts.**

Example: Kicking drill with arms at sides
- Breathing will be difficult
- Breathing technique won't match full stroke
- **BUT breathing is not the point — ignore it**
- Focus on kicking or you defeat the purpose

### Rule 3: Follow the Correct Sequence
**Perform each step correctly to feel full effect.**

- Skipping steps reinforces incorrect technique
- Can interfere with desired outcome
- Skipped step might be the very problem area

### Rule 4: Start Slowly
**Many drills seem awkward at first.**

- Like any new thing, gets better with practice
- Spend time on each drill
- Identify problems
- Make needed modifications
- Practice until you do it right

### Rule 5: Know the Speed
**Some drills = swim speed. Some drills = not.**

- Some are done stationary
- **Concern = quality, NOT yardage**
- Once you can do drill correctly, alternate laps of drill & swim
- New technique works in context of full stroke

### Rule 6: No Rush
**The process is equally rewarding as the goal.**

- Each positive change = revelation
- Each new skill = puzzle piece discovered after long search
- Every improvement empowers you to pursue another

## Turning Workout into Practice

### The Problem: One-Sided Approach
**Common mistake:**
1. Learn to produce forward motion
2. Leave stroke technique behind
3. Focus on conditioning only
4. Get rapid improvement (for a while)
5. Hit wall when conditioning alone stops working

**Missing:** Workout is only HALF the formula.

### The Solution: Both Sides Simultaneously
**Regular attention to technique:**
- Develops swimming that requires less effort
- Energy saved → applied to building better swimming through efficient strokes
- Long-term improvement from technique side AND conditioning side

**Transform workout → practice:**
- Not just opportunity to swim more
- Opportunity to practice efficient swimming

### How Drills Transform Practice
**Strategic incorporation into any swim session:**
- Specific practice (drills) rather than just workout
- Build efficient swimming from both sides
- Experience long-term improvement

## Practice Doesn't Make Perfect

> "Practice doesn't make perfect; **perfect practice** makes perfect. Practicing the wrong action over and over doesn't improve your stroke or speed. Practicing the right technique will."

### The Fatigue Problem
**When tired, we revert to old habits.**
- Lose stroke, form, technique
- Practicing flawed technique = perfecting flawed technique

### Start Short, Add Distance Gradually
**Best way to learn:**
1. Practice new technique for SHORT distances first
2. Even experienced swimmers start short when learning new technique
3. Gradually add distance once technique is solid
4. Ingrain new technique before adding fatigue

**Goal:** Learn to hold your stroke for whatever distance you're swimming.
- Sprinter: hold for 50-100
- Distance: hold for 1500+
- Triathlete: hold for up to 2 miles

## History of Swimming Drills (Highlights)

### Ancient Times
- Swimming depicted in Egyptian/Assyrian art (2000 B.C.)
- Used for survival, military, leisure
- Bible mentions it
- Romans: important part of education
- Prophet Mohammed: "the highest sport"
- Medieval knights: one of the original "agilities"

### 1500s-1700s
- First books on swimming technique (European breaststroke)
- **First drills:** Diagrams for study and emulation
- **Land practice:** Moving arms/legs through air (failed — no floatation experience!)
- Explorers see alternating over-arm stroke in New World — superior in speed
- Europeans don't adopt it (grace > speed in their view)

### 1800s-1900s
- Swimming becomes official sport — speed becomes goal
- Trial & error → new strokes (sidestroke, trudgen, crawl)
- Early coaches = "professors of swimming" with secret techniques
- 1920s: Filming best swimmers as training tools
- 1950s: Four distinct competitive strokes established with rules

### 1960s: Doc Counsilman Revolution
**Changed focus from speed → efficiency.**

- Used science to explain swimming propulsion
- Focused on technique of efficient swimming
- Contributed concepts still used today:
  - Sculling
  - Leverage

**Last 40 years:**
- Training methods develop rapidly
- **Drills remain part of training at all levels**

### Modern Examples
- **Alexander Popov:** Freestyle with dolphin kick
- **Inge de Bruijn:** Hours of single-leg kicking
- **Natalie Coughlin:** Rose to world-class with no arm work training
- **USA Swimming:** "A skill done 99% correctly is 100% wrong"

## Actionable Takeaways for Coaches

✅ **Drills = thinking AND feeling approach** — Engage body & brain  
✅ **Economic first, efficient second** — Minimize drag/waste → apply saved energy to speed  
✅ **Know the purpose** — Empty practice = no improvement  
✅ **Focus on the point** — Ignore aspects not being targeted  
✅ **Start short** — When tired, we revert to old habits  
✅ **Perfect practice makes perfect** — Practicing wrong = perfecting wrong  
✅ **Every session should include drills** — Warm-up, sets, between sets, taper, race day  
✅ **Seven aspects to address:** Body position, kick, arm stroke, recovery, breathing, leverage, coordination
