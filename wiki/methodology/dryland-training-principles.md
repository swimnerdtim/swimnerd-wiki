# Dryland Training Principles for Swimmers

**Source:** *Swimming Anatomy* by Ian McLeod  
**Category:** Methodology | Strength & Conditioning | Program Design  
**For:** Coaches designing swim-specific dryland programs

## Core Philosophy

**Swimming itself is the best way to become a better swimmer**, but dryland training:
- Corrects muscle imbalances inherent to swimming
- Strengthens stabilizers that prevent injury
- Builds strength in stroke-specific movement patterns
- Enhances power transfer through the kinetic chain

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. Transference vs. Isolation

**Transference:** Choosing exercises because they transfer to swimming performance

**Direct Transference:**
- Movements mimic stroke components
- Example: Physioball prone streamline (mimics streamline position off starts/walls)

**Indirect Transference:**
- Target muscles used in strokes, but movement differs
- Example: Lat pull-downs (strengthen lats = primary mover in all strokes)

**Isolation:**
- Target specific muscle/group for:
  1. Correcting imbalances (strengthen underdeveloped areas)
  2. Injury prevention (stabilizers)
  3. Addressing weakness identified in stroke analysis

### 2. The Kinetic Chain

**Concept:** Body = chain linking hands → feet
- Power generated by arms transfers through torso → legs
- **Weak link = power loss, uncoordinated movement, injury risk**

**Implication:** Strong core is the foundation linking upper/lower extremities

### 3. Creating Your Own Base of Support

Unlike land athletes (stable surface to push from), swimmers must:
- Generate base of support in fluid environment
- Link upper/lower extremity movement through **core stability**
- Think: "Core = foundation. Even a strong house deteriorates if foundation is weak."

## Addressing Swimmer-Specific Muscle Imbalances

### Problem Areas

| Body Region | Overdeveloped | Underdeveloped | Risk |
|-------------|---------------|----------------|------|
| **Upper body** | Lats, pecs | Scapular stabilizers (mid/lower traps, rhomboids) | Rounded shoulders, impingement |
| **Lower body** | Quads, hip flexors | Hamstrings, glutes | Postural imbalance, knee/hip injury |
| **Shoulder** | Internal rotators | External rotators (rotator cuff) | Shoulder instability, injury |

### Solution Framework
1. **Flexibility component** (especially dynamic stretches pre-workout)
2. **Strengthen stabilizers** (scapular, core, rotator cuff)
3. **Balance push/pull exercises** (2:1 ratio for swim-specific needs)
4. **Address tight muscles** (static stretching post-workout)

## Program Design Principles

### Warm-Up (Dynamic, Not Static)

**Old way:** Static stretching before lifting  
**New way:** Dynamic movements + stretches

**Benefits:**
- Effective low-intensity warm-up
- Address flexibility in movement patterns relevant to swimming
- Prepare neuromuscular system for work

**Static stretching** → Reserve for END of dryland session

---

### Exercise Order

**Optimal sequence:**
1. **Dynamic warm-up** (5-10 min)
2. **Core stabilization** (set foundation early)
3. **Multi-joint, total-body exercises** (combine upper + lower extremities)
4. **Multi-joint upper-body exercises** (compound movements)
5. **Isolation exercises** (single-joint, single muscle group)
6. **Additional core work** (optional)
7. **Static stretching + flexibility**

**Why?** Avoid fatiguing single muscle group early → compromises compound lifts later

**Swimming analogy:** "Don't do an all-out 50 fly at the start of a workout"

**Example Session:**
1. Dynamic warm-up
2. Planks + Russian twists (core)
3. Streamline step-ups (total body)
4. Lat pull-downs (multi-joint upper)
5. Lawn mower pulls (multi-joint upper)
6. Dumbbell kickbacks (isolation: triceps)
7. Static stretching

---

### Push vs. Pull Balance

**Pushing exercises:** Pecs + triceps (push-ups, bench press)  
**Pulling exercises:** Lats + biceps (pull-ups, rows)

**Ratio for swimmers:** More pulling than pushing
- Swimming already emphasizes internal rotation, pec/lat development
- Need to balance with scapular stabilizers + external rotators

**Benefit:** Exercises "mirror each other" → one group recovers while other works

---

### Sets, Reps, and Intensity

**Volume** = Total # of reps performed  
**Intensity** = Effort exerted per rep

**Trade-off:** ↑ reps = ↓ intensity (and vice versa)

#### Rep Ranges by Goal

| Goal | Reps | Sets | Rest | Weight | Example Stroke Benefit |
|------|------|------|------|--------|----------------------|
| **Muscular endurance** | 13-15+ | 2-3 | 30-60s | Light | Distance swimmers, sustained stroke power |
| **Hypertrophy** (muscle size) | 8-12 | 3-4 | 60-90s | Moderate | Sprinters, middle-distance |
| **Strength** | 4-6 | 4-5 | 2-3 min | Heavy | Power off starts/walls, stroke power |
| **Power** | 1-3 | 3-5 | 3-5 min | Explosive | Starts, breakouts, sprint finishes |

**For age-group swimmers:** Start with endurance (13-15 reps), progress to strength as they mature

---

### Traditional vs. Circuit Training

**Traditional weight training:**
- Perform X sets of exercise A → rest → move to exercise B
- Better for: Older/advanced swimmers focusing on strength/power

**Circuit training:**
- Rotate through multiple exercises with minimal rest
- Better for: Younger swimmers, building base endurance, time-efficient sessions

**Hybrid approach:** Combine both (circuits for endurance work, traditional for power lifts)

## Exercise Selection Guidelines

### 1. **Prioritize Stabilizers**

**Scapular stabilizers:**
- Scapular push-ups (serratus anterior)
- T-Y-A exercises (trapezius, rhomboids)
- Scapular dips (lower trapezius)

**Rotator cuff:**
- Internal rotation with tubing (subscapularis)
- External rotation with tubing (infraspinatus, teres minor)

**Core stabilizers:**
- Planks (transversus abdominis, rectus abdominis)
- Russian twists (obliques)
- Hollow holds (lower abs, hip flexors)

**Why first?** These are your injury prevention foundation

---

### 2. **Choose Exercises by Stroke**

**Freestyle/Backstroke:**
- Lat pull-downs (latissimus dorsi)
- Cable rows (scapular stabilizers + lats)
- Core rotation exercises (body roll mechanics)
- Streamline flutter kicks (core + hip flexors)

**Butterfly:**
- Triceps pushdowns (forceful elbow extension at finish)
- Superman holds (paraspinal muscles for undulation)
- Physioball crunches (abdominals for torso wave)
- Dolphin kick planks (total-body undulation)

**Breaststroke:**
- Adductor strengthening (power phase of kick!)
- Triceps work (recovery phase extension)
- Hip flexibility drills (outsweep positioning)
- Physioball crunches with rotation (torso lift)

**All Strokes:**
- Physioball prone streamline (starts/walls)
- Core stabilization (foundation for all movement)
- Rotator cuff + scapular work (injury prevention)

---

### 3. **Target Weak Links**

**How to identify:**
- Video analysis (collapsing elbow = weak biceps/catch muscles)
- Fatigue patterns (fading back half = weak triceps/finish)
- Imbalances (rounded shoulders = weak scapular stabilizers)

**Then:** Prescribe isolation exercises to address weakness

---

## Setting the Foundation: Shoulder Blades + Core

### Before Every Exercise:

**Set the shoulder blades:**
1. Pinch shoulder blades BACK and DOWN
2. Like putting them in your back pockets
3. **Avoid** shrugging upward (shifts focus from lower traps → upper traps, already overdeveloped)

**Set the core:**
1. Simultaneously contract: abs, low back, glutes
2. Like wearing a corset around midsection
3. Creates foundation for force transfer, stabilizes low back

**Why?** Without stable base, forces dissipate instead of transferring through kinetic chain

## Age-Appropriate Progressions

| Age Group | Focus | Exercise Examples | Weight | Coaching Cues |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|--------|---------------|
| **7 & younger** | Introduce basics, little/no weight | Bodyweight squats, push-ups, planks | Bodyweight | Fun, movement patterns, posture |
| **8-10** | Movement quality, light resistance | Resistance bands, light dumbbells, physioball | Very light | Technique over load |
| **11-13** | Build base endurance | Circuit training, 2-3 sets × 13-15 reps | Light-moderate | Endurance, consistency |
| **14-15** | Introduce strength focus | Mix endurance + strength (8-12 reps) | Moderate | Proper form, progressive overload |
| **16-18** | Sport-specific strength/power | Traditional lifting, 4-6 reps for power | Moderate-heavy | Explosiveness, transfer to pool |
| **19+** | Individualized (strength, power, or maintenance) | Tailored to event/weakness | Varies | Performance optimization |

**Key principle:** Progress conservatively. Master movement before adding load.

## Sample Session Structures

### Age Group (11-13): Circuit Endurance

**Format:** 3 rounds, 30-45s rest between exercises
1. Dynamic warm-up (5 min)
2. Plank hold (30s)
3. Physioball squats (15 reps)
4. Resistance band rows (15 reps)
5. Push-ups (10-12 reps)
6. Russian twists (20 total)
7. Streamline flutter kicks (30s)
8. Static stretch (5 min)

---

### Senior (16+): Strength & Power

**Format:** Traditional sets with rest
1. Dynamic warm-up (5 min)
2. **Core:** Hollow holds (3 × 30s)
3. **Power:** Box jumps into streamline (4 × 5)
4. **Strength:** Lat pull-downs (4 × 6-8)
5. **Strength:** Bench pulls (4 × 6-8)
6. **Accessory:** Triceps pushdowns (3 × 10)
7. **Accessory:** External rotation with tubing (3 × 12)
8. **Core:** Cable crunches (3 × 12)
9. Static stretch + foam rolling (10 min)

---

### Sprint-Specific

**Focus:** Power + explosiveness
1. Dynamic warm-up
2. Core activation
3. **Plyometrics:** Broad jumps, medicine ball throws
4. **Heavy compound lifts:** Squats, deadlifts, pull-ups (low reps, high intensity)
5. **Stroke-specific:** Fast triceps pushdowns, explosive lat pulls
6. Cool down

---

### Distance-Specific

**Focus:** Muscular endurance
1. Dynamic warm-up
2. Core stabilization (longer holds)
3. **Circuit format:** 12-15 reps, minimal rest
4. **Exercises:** Rows, lat pulls, step-ups, lunges, push-ups
5. **Finisher:** Long core hold or high-rep core circuit
6. Cool down

## Injury Prevention Focus

### Shoulder Health (Priority #1 for Swimmers)

**Problem:** Repetitive overhead motion → impingement, instability

**Solutions:**
1. **Scapular stabilization** (serratus anterior, lower traps, rhomboids)
2. **Rotator cuff balance** (external rotation to counter internal rotation bias)
3. **Postural correction** (combat rounded shoulders)

**Must-include exercises:**
- Scapular push-ups
- T-Y-A holds
- External rotation with tubing
- Scapular dips

---

### Core Stability (Foundation)

**Why critical:**
- Links upper/lower extremities
- Prevents energy leaks in kinetic chain
- Stabilizes spine during rotation (freestyle/backstroke)
- Powers undulation (butterfly/breaststroke)

**Must-include exercises:**
- Planks (front, side)
- Hollow holds
- Russian twists
- Physioball exercises

---

### Lower Body Balance

**Problem:** Quad/hip flexor dominance → weak hamstrings/glutes

**Solutions:**
- Hamstring curls
- Romanian deadlifts
- Glute bridges
- Single-leg stability work

## Practical Coaching Tips

### 1. **Quality > Quantity**
- Perfect 10 reps > sloppy 15 reps
- Fatigue = technique breakdown = injury risk

### 2. **Progressive Overload**
- Increase load gradually (5-10% per week max)
- Change ONE variable at a time (volume OR intensity, not both)

### 3. **Listen to Swimmers**
- Soreness = normal
- Pain = stop
- Chronic fatigue = reduce volume

### 4. **Integrate with Pool Training**
- Heavy dryland days = easier pool days (or vice versa)
- Taper dryland with pool taper
- In-season: Maintain strength (lower volume)
- Off-season: Build strength (higher volume)

### 5. **Test & Reassess**
- Retest exercises every 4-6 weeks
- Track progress (weight, reps, time)
- Adjust program based on results

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Too much weight, too soon** → Injury, poor form  
❌ **Neglecting stabilizers** → Imbalances worsen  
❌ **Static stretching before lifting** → Reduced power output  
❌ **Isolation exercises first** → Fatigues muscles needed for compounds  
❌ **Same program year-round** → Plateau, overtraining  
❌ **Copying other sports' programs** → Not swim-specific
