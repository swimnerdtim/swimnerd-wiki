# Energy Systems for Swimming

**The Three Fuel Tanks — How Swimmers Power Every Stroke, From 50m Sprints to 1500m Distance**

---

## The Three Energy Systems

Energy is the capacity to perform work. Work is muscle contraction. For swimmers, **work = force applied per stroke**.

The body replenishes energy (ATP) using **three energy systems**, depending on the intensity and duration of the swim:

1. **Anaerobic Alactic (ATP-CP)** — Explosive power, 0-10 seconds
2. **Anaerobic Lactic** — High-intensity sustained speed, 10-70 seconds
3. **Aerobic** — Endurance, 2 minutes to 2+ hours

**Key insight:** The three systems are **not independent**. They collaborate based on the event's physiological demands. Smart training develops the **dominant system** while supporting the others.

---

## System 1: Anaerobic Alactic (ATP-CP)

**Fuel source:** ATP (adenosine triphosphate) + CP (creatine phosphate) stored in muscle  
**Duration:** 0-10 seconds  
**Intensity:** Maximum effort (>95% max speed)  
**By-product:** None (no lactic acid)  
**Recovery time:** 2-5 minutes for full restoration

### How It Works
- Muscles store only a small amount of ATP (enough for 6-10 seconds of max effort)
- When ATP depletes, **creatine phosphate (CP)** breaks down to resynthesize ATP instantly
- **No oxygen required** — this system is immediate and explosive

### Swimming Events Using ATP-CP System
- **50m sprint** (all strokes) — First 10-12 seconds of the race
- **Starts** — Explosive power off the blocks
- **Turns** — Reactive power pushing off the wall
- **Underwaters** — First 3-5 dolphin kicks
- **Breakouts** — Explosive stroke cycles 1-3

### Training the ATP-CP System

**Goal:** Increase maximum speed, fast propulsion, quick reaction

**Training Zone 1 (Alactic):**
- **Duration:** 4-12 seconds per rep
- **Reps:** 10-30 per session
- **Rest:** 2-5 minutes (full recovery)
- **Intensity:** >95% max speed
- **Lactic acid:** 1-2 millimoles (minimal)

**Example workouts:**

**Sprint workout (50 specialists):**
- 20 × 12.5m all-out sprints (any stroke)
- Rest: 3 minutes between reps
- **Goal:** Maximum velocity, zero fatigue between reps

**Starts/turns workout:**
- 10 × explosive starts (dive to 15m)
- Rest: 4 minutes
- **Focus:** Reaction time, breakout speed

**Underwater kicks:**
- 15 × 5 dolphin kicks max effort (underwater)
- Rest: 2-3 minutes
- **Focus:** Explosive power, not endurance

**Strength training for ATP-CP:**
- Maximum strength: 5 sets × 4 reps @ 88% 1RM (bench press, lat pulls)
- Power: 6 sets × 6 explosive medicine ball slams
- Plyometrics: 8 × 3 reactive box jumps (rest 3 min)

**Why rest matters:** If rest is too short, CP won't fully restore → anaerobic lactic system kicks in → lactic acid builds → speed drops. **You're no longer training alactic power.**

**Common mistake:** Doing "sprint sets" with 30-second rest = NOT alactic training. That's lactic acid tolerance training (Zone 2).

## System 2: Anaerobic Lactic (Glycolysis)

**Fuel source:** Glycogen (stored carbohydrate in muscles)  
**Duration:** 10-70 seconds  
**Intensity:** 85-95% max effort  
**By-product:** Lactic acid (causes burning sensation)  
**Recovery time:** 1-5 minutes

### How It Works
- After 10 seconds of max effort, ATP-CP depletes → body breaks down **glycogen** for energy
- Glycogen breakdown creates **lactic acid** as a by-product
- Lactic acid accumulation causes muscle fatigue, burning sensation, and eventually forces you to slow down
- **No oxygen required** — but lactic acid limits duration

### Swimming Events Using Lactic System
- **100m** (all strokes) — Final 50-75m of the race
- **200m** (all strokes) — Middle 100m (after initial speed, before aerobic endurance kicks in)
- **100m IM** — Sustained high-intensity effort

### Training the Lactic System

**Goal:** Increase lactic acid tolerance, buffer acidosis, improve lactic acid clearance

**Training Zone 2 (Lactic Acid Tolerance):**
- **Duration:** 20-90 seconds per rep
- **Reps:** 6-10 per session
- **Rest:** 1-5 minutes (partial recovery)
- **Intensity:** 85-95% max speed
- **Lactic acid:** 12-18 millimoles (very high — painful!)

**Two training variations:**

#### **Variation 1: Long reps, long rest (High lactic production)**
**Goal:** Adapt to extreme acidosis

**Example:**
- 6 × 100m @ 90% max effort (any stroke)
- Rest: 4 minutes
- **Result:** Lactic acid peaks at 15+ millimoles — brutal, but effective

**Physiological benefit:** Increases number of lactate transporters in muscles → faster lactic acid clearance → delay fatigue

#### **Variation 2: Short reps, short rest (Cumulative lactic buildup)**
**Goal:** Train under conditions of extreme acidosis (race-specific)

**Example:**
- 10 × 50m @ 85% max effort
- Rest: 90 seconds (incomplete recovery)
- **Result:** Lactic acid accumulates across reps → trains tolerance

**Example (200m race-specific):**
- 8 × 25m @ 95% race pace
- Rest: 45 seconds
- **Result:** Simulates back-half of 200m race (high lactic acid, must maintain speed)

**Strength training for lactic system:**
- Power-endurance: 4 sets × 12 reps bench press @ 70% 1RM, 90s rest
- Circuit training: 3 rounds (8-12 reps per exercise, 1-2 min rest between rounds)

**When to use Zone 2 training:**
- **Pre-competition phase** (4-8 weeks before championships)
- **Frequency:** 2-3x per week max (very fatiguing)
- **Warning:** Overdoing lactic training = overtraining, burnout, slow recovery

**Psychological benefit:** Pushes you beyond the pain threshold → mental toughness for racing

## System 3: Aerobic (Oxidative)

**Fuel source:** Glycogen, fats, proteins (with oxygen)  
**Duration:** 2 minutes to 2+ hours  
**Intensity:** 40-80% max effort  
**By-product:** Carbon dioxide + water (expelled via breathing/sweating)  
**Recovery time:** Minimal (system can sustain for hours)

### How It Works
- **Requires oxygen** — heart rate and breathing increase to transport O₂ to muscle cells
- Glycogen is primary fuel for 2 min to 3 hours
- Beyond 3 hours, body breaks down **fats** and **proteins** for energy
- **No lactic acid buildup** (or minimal) → can sustain work for long periods

### Swimming Events Using Aerobic System
- **400m** (all strokes) — Second half of race (after lactic phase)
- **800m / 1500m freestyle** — Entire race
- **Distance events** (open water 5K, 10K)
- **Recovery swimming** (warm-up, cool-down)

### Training the Aerobic System

The aerobic system has **four training zones** for swimmers:

---

### **Training Zone 3: Maximum Oxygen Consumption (VO₂ max)**

**Goal:** Increase oxygen transport and usage

**Duration:** 3-5 minutes per rep  
**Reps:** 8-12 per session  
**Rest:** 2-3 minutes  
**Intensity:** 80-85% max effort  
**Lactic acid:** 6-12 millimoles  
**Heart rate:** ~170-180 bpm

**Example workouts:**

**400m/800m swimmers:**
- 10 × 400m @ 82% max effort (any stroke)
- Rest: 2:30
- **Goal:** Sustained high heart rate, oxygen system maxed out

**200m swimmers:**
- 12 × 200m @ 85% max effort
- Rest: 2:00
- **Goal:** Train VO₂ max with shorter, faster reps

**Team sports equivalent:** Hockey players alternating high-intensity shifts with rest (mimics Zone 3)

---

### **Training Zone 4: Anaerobic Threshold**

**Goal:** Push the threshold where lactic acid accumulation begins (train body to tolerate higher speeds without acidosis)

**Duration:** 2-7 minutes per rep  
**Reps:** 4-8 per session  
**Rest:** <5 minutes  
**Intensity:** 65-80% max effort  
**Lactic acid:** 4-6 millimoles (mild distress)  
**Heart rate:** ~160-170 bpm

**Example workouts:**

**Mid-distance swimmers (200-400m):**
- 6 × 500m @ 75% max effort
- Rest: 3:00
- **Goal:** Train just below lactic threshold → improve clearance

**Distance swimmers (800-1500m):**
- 5 × 1000m @ 70% max effort
- Rest: 4:00
- **Goal:** High-volume aerobic work with minimal lactic buildup

**Key insight:** Zone 4 is the "sweet spot" for endurance swimmers — hard enough to stimulate adaptation, sustainable enough to avoid burnout.

---

### **Training Zone 5: Aerobic Threshold**

**Goal:** Build aerobic base, improve fat oxidation, increase capillary density

**Duration:** 10 min to 2 hours  
**Reps:** 1-6 per session  
**Rest:** 2-3 minutes  
**Intensity:** ~60% max effort  
**Lactic acid:** 2-3 millimoles  
**Heart rate:** 130-150 bpm

**Example workouts:**

**Base-building phase (early season):**
- 1 × 3000m continuous swim @ 60% effort
- **Goal:** Aerobic capacity, stroke efficiency, mental endurance

**Active recovery session:**
- 2000m easy swim (any stroke mix)
- **Goal:** Flush metabolites, active regeneration

**Hydration critical:** Long aerobic work = heavy sweating → dehydration reduces performance. Drink water before, during (if >45 min), and after.

**When to use Zone 5:**
- **Preparatory phase** (Sep-Nov for NCAA) — build aerobic base
- **In-season maintenance** — 1-2x per week to support recovery
- **Active recovery days** — after hard lactic or sprint sessions

---

### **Training Zone 6: Aerobic Compensation (Recovery)**

**Goal:** Speed up recovery, eliminate metabolites, restore immune function

**Duration:** 45 min to 2 hours  
**Reps:** 1-2 per session  
**Rest:** 2-5 minutes between reps  
**Intensity:** 40-50% max effort  
**Lactic acid:** 2-3 millimoles  
**Heart rate:** 120-130 bpm

**Example workouts:**

**Post-competition recovery:**
- 20-30 min easy swim (mix strokes, kick, drill)
- **Goal:** Active recovery, flush lactic acid

**Post-hard workout:**
- 1500m @ 40% effort (very relaxed)
- **Goal:** Aid regeneration, prevent stiffness

**Why active recovery beats rest:**
- **Study (Wigernaes et al., 2001):** Active recovery (cycling/running @ 50% max) after strenuous training:
  - ✅ Prevented drop in white blood cell count
  - ✅ Restored neutrophil and monocyte levels (immune function)
  - ✅ Eliminated spike in plasma free fatty acids
  - ✅ Faster regeneration than static rest (lying down)

**Bottom line:** 15-20 minutes of easy swimming after a hard session = faster recovery, stronger immune system, better next workout.

**When to use Zone 6:**
- After **hard lactic sessions** (Zone 2)
- After **VO₂ max workouts** (Zone 3)
- Following **competitions** or **time trials**
- During **taper week** (keep moving, but very light)

## Energy System Breakdown by Swimming Event

| Event | Alactic (%) | Lactic (%) | Aerobic (%) | Dominant System | Strength Training Focus |
|-------|-------------|------------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------------|
| **50m** | 70 | 25 | 5 | ATP-CP | Maximum Strength → Power |
| **100m** | 30 | 50 | 20 | Lactic | Maximum Strength → Power |
| **200m** | 10 | 40 | 50 | Lactic + Aerobic | Maximum Strength → Power-Endurance |
| **400m** | 5 | 25 | 70 | Aerobic + Lactic | Maximum Strength → Muscular Endurance (short) |
| **800m** | <5 | 15 | 80 | Aerobic | Maximum Strength → Muscular Endurance (medium) |
| **1500m** | <5 | 10 | 85 | Aerobic | Maximum Strength → Muscular Endurance (long) |

**Key insight:** Even the 1500m uses some lactic system (final sprint). Even the 50m uses some aerobic system (recovery between heats). Train the dominant system, support the others.

## Integrating Energy Systems + Strength Training

**The mistake:** Separating pool work and dryland work.  
**The solution:** Match strength training to the energy system you're targeting in the pool.

### **Example Weekly Plan (200m Swimmer, Pre-Competition Phase)**

| Day | Pool Focus | Energy Zone | Dryland Focus |
|-----|------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Monday** | 8 × 200m @ 85% (rest 2:00) | Zone 2 (Lactic) | Power-Endurance (4×12 @ 70% 1RM) |
| **Tuesday** | 10 × 50m sprint (rest 3:00) | Zone 1 (Alactic) | Maximum Strength (5×5 @ 85% 1RM) |
| **Wednesday** | 6 × 500m @ 75% (rest 3:00) | Zone 4 (Threshold) | Aerobic Compensation (light circuit) |
| **Thursday** | 6 × 100m @ 90% (rest 4:00) | Zone 2 (Lactic) | Power (6×8 explosive lifts @ 75% 1RM) |
| **Friday** | 3000m steady @ 65% | Zone 5 (Aerobic) | Muscular Endurance (circuit: 3 rounds × 15 reps) |
| **Saturday** | Race-pace time trial | Zone 1+2+4 | Recovery (15 min easy swim/stretch) |
| **Sunday** | Off or 20 min easy swim | Zone 6 (Recovery) | Off |

**Why this works:**
- **Monday:** Lactic pool work + lactic strength work = matched recovery time
- **Tuesday:** Alactic sprints + max strength = both use ATP-CP system
- **Wednesday:** Threshold work + light circuit = aerobic system recovery
- **Friday:** Aerobic swim + muscular endurance circuit = both use aerobic system

**Principle:** Match the energy system in the pool to the energy system in the weight room → faster recovery, better adaptation.

## Common Mistakes

### ❌ **Mistake 1: Training Alactic System With Short Rest**
**Problem:** 10 × 25m sprint with 30s rest = NOT alactic training. That's lactic training.  
**Fix:** Alactic = >2 min rest. If rest is shorter, you're training a different system.

### ❌ **Mistake 2: Ignoring Aerobic Base for Sprinters**
**Problem:** "I'm a 50 swimmer, I don't need aerobic work."  
**Reality:** Aerobic base aids recovery between heats, supports training volume, prevents injury.  
**Fix:** Sprinters still do Zone 5 work in preparatory phase (Sep-Oct).

### ❌ **Mistake 3: Overdoing Lactic Training**
**Problem:** 4-5 lactic sessions per week = overtraining, burnout, slow recovery  
**Fix:** Max 2-3 lactic sessions per week. Use Zone 6 (recovery) between hard days.

### ❌ **Mistake 4: Long Slow Distance for 200m Swimmers**
**Problem:** 200m is 40% lactic, 50% aerobic. Too much Zone 5 (easy aerobic) = slow racing.  
**Fix:** Focus on Zone 4 (threshold) and Zone 2 (lactic) for 200m events.

### ❌ **Mistake 5: No Active Recovery**
**Problem:** Hard workout → sit on couch → stiff, sore, slow next session  
**Fix:** 15-20 min easy swim after hard sessions = faster recovery, better immune function

## Key Takeaways

✅ **Three energy systems fuel swimming** — ATP-CP (0-10s), Lactic (10-70s), Aerobic (2 min+)  
✅ **Train the dominant system** — but support the others (even sprinters need aerobic base)  
✅ **Rest intervals matter** — alactic needs 2-5 min rest, lactic needs 1-5 min, aerobic needs minimal  
✅ **Match pool + dryland** — lactic pool work + power-endurance lifting = same recovery timeline  
✅ **Active recovery works** — 15-20 min easy swimming after hard sessions speeds healing  
✅ **Six intensity zones** — use Zones 1-6 strategically based on event and training phase
