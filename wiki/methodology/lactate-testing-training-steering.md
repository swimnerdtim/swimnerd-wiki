# Training Steering with Lactate Testing
**Source:** *The Science of Winning* by Jan Olbrecht

## Core Concept

**Training steering** = continuous loop of planning → executing → measuring → adjusting.

Lactate testing provides **objective feedback** to verify training effects and optimize the program for each swimmer's individual response.

---

## The Steering Cycle

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 1. Analyze Current Performance          │
│    (Lactate test + competition results) │
└──────────────┬──────────────────────────┘
               ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 2. Determine Training Objectives        │
│    (What needs work? What's priority?)  │
└──────────────┬──────────────────────────┘
               ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 3. Design Training Structure             │
│    (5-15 week plan)                      │
└──────────────┬──────────────────────────┘
               ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 4. Execute Training                      │
│    (Document everything)                 │
└──────────────┬──────────────────────────┘
               ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 5. Evaluate Training Effect              │
│    (Repeat lactate test)                 │
└──────────────┬──────────────────────────┘
               ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 6. Adjust Plan for Next Period           │
│    (Adapt based on actual response)      │
└──────────────┬──────────────────────────┘
               ↓
         (Return to step 1)
```

**Key Insight:** Identical training plans produce different results in different swimmers. You MUST measure to optimize.

## Why Lactate Testing?

### The Problem with Non-Specific Tests

**Holmer 1974 Study:**
- Elite swimmers tested on treadmill AND in pool over 6 years
- Treadmill VO2max: **unchanged**
- Pool VO2max: **clearly improved**

**Conclusion:** Testing swimmers on a bicycle or treadmill is like "taking temperature with a barometer" - both relate to weather but measure different things.

**Rule:** Tests must be swim-specific, stroke-specific, AND distance-specific.

---

## Lactate Test Protocol

### Standard Setup

**Test Structure:**
1. **Submaximal 400m freestyle** (establish aerobic endurance baseline)
2. **Complete rest** (10-20 minutes)
3. **100m or 200m all-out in personal stroke** (assess anaerobic capacity)

**Measurements:**
- **V4-400:** Speed at 4 mmol/L lactate on 400m freestyle (aerobic endurance)
- **Lamax:** Highest lactate concentration after all-out swim (anaerobic performance)

**Consistency:** Same protocol every time for same swimmer = comparable data.

---

## Key Metrics

### 1. Aerobic Endurance (V4-400)
**What it measures:** Speed at 4 mmol/L lactate threshold  
**What it tells you:** Aerobic conditioning base  
**Training response:** Improves slowly (weeks), shows benefit ~1 month AFTER volume increase  

### 2. Anaerobic Capacity (Lamax)  
**What it measures:** Peak lactate after all-out effort  
**What it tells you:** Glycolytic capacity  
**Training response:** Can drop during high aerobic volume phases if not maintained

## Real-World Example: Training Analysis Case Study

### The Setup
Young promising swimmers preparing for national winter championships (October → March).

**Testing Schedule:**
- October 12 (baseline)
- December 2 (after Period I)
- January 13 (after Period II)
- March 23 (after Period III)

### Period I: High Volume Phase

**Training Completed:**
- Duration: 8 weeks
- Volume: 32.5 km/week average
- Intensive work: 10.8% of volume (3.5 km/week)
- Load frequency: 
  - 1.4 blocks/week at 1.5-2.5 mmol/L
  - 0.9 blocks/week at 2.5-5 mmol/L
  - 0.9 blocks/week >5 mmol/L
- Plus: 3x25m sprints 2-3x/week

**Results:**
- V4-400: **+12 seconds** (huge aerobic gain)
- Lamax: **-1.2 mmol/L** (anaerobic capacity decreased!)

**Analysis:**
- ✅ Volume successfully built aerobic base
- ❌ Not enough intensive work to maintain anaerobic capacity
- ❌ 2 intensive blocks/week was insufficient for this group

**Adjustment for Period II:**
- Increase intensive training frequency
- Use fall break to further boost volume
- Add more workouts >1.5 mmol/L lactate

### Period II: Adjusted Plan

**Training Completed:**
- Duration: ~8 weeks
- Volume: 43.6 km/week (+11.1 km/week vs Period I)
- Intensive work: 6.8% of volume (3.0 km/week)
- Load frequency:
  - 1.8 blocks/week at 1.5-2.5 mmol/L
  - 0.9 blocks/week at 2.5-5 mmol/L  
  - 1.4 blocks/week >5 mmol/L
- Total intensive volume: 920m/week (vs 580m/week Period I)

**Results:**
- V4-400: **Maintained** (slightly improved but too soon after volume increase to see full effect)
- Lamax: **Restored** to October baseline

**Analysis:**
- ✅ Aerobic base preserved despite more intensive work
- ✅ Anaerobic capacity recovered with proper dosing
- ✅ Key finding: ~2 intensive blocks/week minimum needed

**Adjustment for Period III:**
- Focus on power and frequency work
- Maintain aerobic base with regeneration-focused training
- Specific competition preparation

### Period III: Competition Phase

**Training Completed:**
- Lower volume, much higher intensity
- Extended peak form period with 1 small high-volume regeneration mesocycle after Flemish Championships

**Results:**
- V4-400: **Well maintained** despite lower volume + competitions
- Lamax: **Slightly increased**

---

## Key Training Insights from Study

### Finding #1: Volume vs. Anaerobic Capacity
**Increasing training volume improves aerobic endurance BUT may depress maximum lactate if not properly compensated.**

**Solution:** Maintain 2+ intensive blocks/week during high-volume phases.

### Finding #2: Intensive Block Frequency
**On average, 2 intensive blocks per week will develop anaerobic conditioning.**

**But:** Dosing is highly individual - requires evaluation for each swimmer.

### Finding #3: Volume Matters More Than Total Intensive Volume
**Improvement of anaerobic conditioning relates more to weekly NUMBER of intensive blocks than total volume of intensive work.**

**Example:** 2 blocks of 100m intensive = better adaptation than 1 block of 200m intensive.

### Finding #4: Aerobic Maintenance  
**Aerobic endurance can be maintained despite intensive training/competitions IF compensated with lots of regeneration training.**

### Finding #5: Short Intensive Bouts in Aerobic Work
**Improvement of aerobic capacity is stimulated by short intensive parts within aerobic training.**

**Practical:** "Spice" long aerobic sets with occasional fast 25s or 50s.

## Training Documentation Requirements

To make steering work, you MUST document:

### Daily Training Log
- Volume (total distance)
- Intensity (% in each zone: <1.5, 1.5-2.5, 2.5-5, >5 mmol/L lactate)
- Load frequency (# of intensive blocks/week)
- Load density (how quickly intensive sessions follow each other)
- Attendance (did swimmer complete full workout?)
- Health status (illness, injury, fatigue level)
- External factors (exams, stress, sleep quality)

### Weekly Summary
- Total km
- % distribution by intensity zone
- # intensive blocks by type
- Competitions

### Mesocycle Summary  
- Compare actual vs. planned training
- Competition results  
- Lactate test results
- Subjective athlete feedback

**Without documentation, steering is impossible.**

---

## Adjustments You Can Make

Based on test results, adjust:

1. **Training Intensity** (faster/slower intervals)
2. **Training Volume** (more/less total distance)  
3. **Distribution** (% in each intensity zone)
4. **Composition** (types of exercises)
5. **Frequency** (# workouts/week, # intensive blocks/week)
6. **Density** (spacing between hard sessions)

## When Tests Show Unexpected Results

### Scenario: Aerobic improved but swimmer slower in competition
**Possible causes:**
- Insufficient anaerobic capacity
- Technical breakdown under fatigue  
- Insufficient power work
- Mental/tactical issues

**Solution:** Look beyond lactate - analyze technique, stroke rate, race execution.

### Scenario: Lactate suggests improvement but times stagnant
**Possible causes:**
- Test environment different from competition
- Pacing issues
- Starts/turns deteriorated
- Technical regression in main stroke

**Solution:** Add technique assessment, video analysis, race simulation sets.

### Scenario: Both metrics improved but swimmer reports high fatigue
**Possible causes:**  
- Insufficient regeneration
- Accumulated stress (school, life)
- Early overtraining signs
- Illness developing

**Solution:** Insert recovery microcycle, reduce load density, check non-training stressors.

## Practical Guidelines

### Testing Frequency
- **Base period:** Every 4-6 weeks
- **Competition period:** Every 2-3 weeks or after major adaptation stimulus
- **Transition period:** Not needed (recovery focus)

### Interpreting Changes

**Aerobic Endurance (V4-400):**
- +5-10 sec improvement = good response
- +10-15 sec = excellent response  
- No change after 6+ weeks = insufficient stimulus or poor recovery
- Decrease = overtraining or illness

**Anaerobic Capacity (Lamax):**
- 10-14 mmol/L = well-trained
- <10 mmol/L = needs anaerobic work or recovery
- >14 mmol/L = strong glycolytic capacity
- Drop >1 mmol/L during volume phase = add intensive work

### Age Group Swimmers
- Expect slower adaptations (developing systems)
- Focus more on V4-400 than Lamax
- Don't chase high lactate numbers - chase speed at low lactate

### Elite Swimmers  
- Smaller margins of improvement
- More sensitive to small training changes
- Need more precise steering
- Mental/tactical factors matter more

---

## Common Steering Mistakes

❌ **Testing but not adjusting** - Data without action is wasted  
❌ **Changing too much at once** - Can't identify what worked  
❌ **Ignoring individual response** - Group plans need individual tweaks  
❌ **Not documenting training** - Can't link results to stimulus  
❌ **Testing in wrong stroke/distance** - Not specific to competition goal
