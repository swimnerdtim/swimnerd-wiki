# Training Periodization: Macrocycle Structure
**Source:** *The Science of Winning* by Jan Olbrecht

## Core Concept

A **macrocycle** is the complete training period between major competitions, divided into three distinct phases:

1. **Base Training Period** (capacity building)
2. **Competition Training Period** (power & fine-tuning)  
3. **Transition Period** (recovery)

The goal: systematically build conditioning, peak at the right time, and avoid overtraining.

---

## The Three Periods

### 1. Base Training Period

**Purpose:** Build foundation for peak performance

**Duration:** 9-15 weeks (longest phase)

**Focus:**
- Aerobic capacity (high volume, low-moderate intensity)
- Anaerobic capacity 
- Maximal force development
- Technical refinement

**Structure:** 3 mesocycles (3-6 weeks each)

#### Mesocycle I: General Conditioning
- Approach previous year's performance level at this time
- Non-specific training, low-moderate intensity
- Perfect time for:
  - Other sports for general conditioning
  - Intensive strength training (non-swim-specific OK)
  - Technique work (acquiring, correcting, automating)

#### Mesocycle II: Specific Basic Abilities
- Training becomes stroke-specific
- High volume, moderate intensity (endurance training)
- Continue maximal force or endurance strength work
- Insert short anaerobic capacity work 1-2x/week
- Add sprints every 2-3 training units
- **Risk:** High volume can suppress anaerobic/sprint capacity

#### Mesocycle III: Competition-Specific Abilities  
- Maintain/stabilize acquired conditioning
- Begin frequency (stroke rate) training
- Automate technique at competition speed
- Slightly decrease endurance volume
- Increase intensity significantly (2-4x/week anaerobic capacity for sprinters)
- Aerobic power workouts for distance swimmers
- 1-2 weeks relative rest from dryland before increasing explosive/endurance strength

---

### 2. Competition Training Period

**Purpose:** Fine-tune and balance conditioning components

**Key Insight:** Building conditioning is easy and fast. Making it **stable and repeatable** requires proper base period length.

**Duration:** 5-10 weeks

**Focus:** High specificity, high intensity, lower volume

#### Types:

**Simple Competition Training Period** (1 major meet)
- Only pre-competition (peak form inducing) period
- Includes taper phase

**Complex Competition Training Period** (multiple consecutive meets)
- Pre-competition period (peak form inducing)
- In-between period (peak form maintaining)
- Multiple cycles

#### Pre-Competition Training Guidelines

**Distance Swimmers (400m+):**
- Long working phase: 3-6 weeks
- Short taper: 4-14 days
- Need 4-6 weeks of aerobic power work to optimize
- **Risk:** 4-6 consecutive weeks of aerobic power = overtraining risk
- **Solution:** 2 mesocycles with short taper between

**Sprinters/Middle-Distance (50-200m):**
- Short working phase: 1-5 weeks  
- Long taper: 2-4 weeks
- Need 3-5 weeks anaerobic power work
- **Risk:** >3 weeks anaerobic power = basic endurance deteriorates
- **Solution:** Either:
  - 1 long mesocycle (7-8 weeks) with 2 x 2-week anaerobic power blocks separated by 3-5 days aerobic capacity
  - 3 short mesocycles: anaerobic power (cycle 1) → aerobic refresh (cycle 2, max 10 days) → anaerobic power (cycle 3)

#### Peak Form Maintaining Period (between competitions)

**Goal:** Keep swimmer fit, fresh, vigorous, powerful

**Guidelines:**
- May include short high-volume or single high-intensity workout
- **Must guarantee super-compensation time**
- Brief intense work OK, but LOW VOLUME
- Can increase volume but only extensive (low intensity) + rapid regeneration compensation
- Each workout must show effects within 2-3 days

**Between-Meet Training:**
- >9 days between meets: 1 short microcycle (max 3 days) to refresh aerobic/anaerobic capacity (NOT power!)
- <9 days between meets: Maximum regeneration + possibly 1 aerobic capacity workout

**Peak Form Duration:** ~3 weeks maximum. For 4 consecutive weekend meets, only compete in 3.

---

### 3. Transition Period

**Purpose:** Physical and mental regeneration

**Duration:** 2-4 weeks

**Characteristics:**
- No competitions
- No intensive training
- Very little stroke-specific work
- Other non-swim sports activities
- Recovery activities: sauna, massage, hydrotherapy

**Why Critical:** Sharply decreases overtraining risk in systematic training progression.

---

## Practical Application: Yearly Plan Structure

**2 Peaks/Year** (recommended for age group swimmers):
- Macrocycle 1: ~24 weeks (base + competition + transition)
- Macrocycle 2: ~24 weeks (base + competition + transition)

**4 Peaks/Year** (elite swimmers only):
- Requires high conditioning base
- At expense of form stability
- More risk of overtraining

**Example Timeline** (Long Course Summer Peak):
- **Months 1-3:** Base training (capacity)
- **Month 4:** Competition training (power)
- **Month 5:** Taper + major meet + transition
- **Repeat for next macrocycle**

---

## Training Volume Distribution

**Base Period:**
- 85-95% aerobic training (<2.5 mmol/L lactate)
- 5-10% intensive work (>2.5 mmol/L lactate)
- 10-15% increase in volume across mesocycles

**Competition Period:**
- 60-75% aerobic training
- 25-40% intensive work
- Volume decreases as intensity increases
- Large contrast in intensities (very easy vs. very hard)

---

## Key Principles

1. **Aerobic capacity before aerobic power**
2. **Anaerobic capacity before anaerobic power**  
3. **Longer base period = more stable peak form**
4. **Fast conditioning gains = unstable (deteriorate quickly)**
5. **Peak form lasts ~3 weeks maximum**

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Too many peaks per year** → insufficient base building  
❌ **Insufficient base period** → unstable peak form  
❌ **Too long in peak form training** → burnout/overtraining  
❌ **Skipping transition period** → accumulated fatigue  
❌ **Not respecting super-compensation timing** → chronic fatigue
