# Physics of Swimming Speed

**Source:** *Fundamentals of Fast Swimming* by Gary Hall Sr. & Devin Murphy (2020)

## The Fundamental Equation

Swimming velocity is determined by **Newton's laws applied to fluid mechanics**:

```
Velocity = (Propulsion - Drag) / Mass
```

Or more practically:

```
Swimming Speed = Propulsion Force ÷ Drag Force
```

> **Hall's Insight:** "In a sport that often results in differences between winning or not winning being measured in hundredths of seconds, we would be negligent to think that technique does not matter. The old philosophy of working your way to an Olympic gold medal just doesn't float any longer."

**Example:** Gary Hall Jr. and Anthony Ervin won Olympic golds by a combined **0.02 seconds** across three events. One small technical mistake = loss of gold medal.

## The Four Sciences of Swimming

### 1. Physics (Newtonian & Fluid Mechanics)
- **Forces:** Drag, propulsion, lift
- **Law of Inertia:** Body in motion stays in motion
- **Vortices:** Flow patterns around the swimmer's body
- **Application:** Quantifying frontal drag (active and passive), understanding how body position affects propulsion

> **Hall's Observation:** "Of the four basic sciences, the application of physics is perhaps the least understood or studied in the sport of swimming."

---

### 2. Physiology
- **Energy systems:** ATP, aerobic, anaerobic pathways
- **Muscle composition:** Fast-twitch vs. slow-twitch recruitment
- **Oxygen delivery:** VO2 max, lactate threshold
- **Application:** Training methods that improve energy delivery and sustain muscle contraction

> "Physiology relates to our ability to provide sufficient energy, and from the right systems, to enable the muscles involved in the ideal biomechanical swimming motions to generate propulsion."

**Key Point:** The best coaches in advanced swimming nations have mastered physiology through conditioning. The gap is now in **physics and technique**.

---

### 3. Kinesiology
- **Biomechanics:** Joint angles, force vectors, muscle activation
- **Ideal motions:** Movements that maximize velocity for event duration
- **Application:** Understanding which body movements produce the most propulsion with the least drag

> "Kinesiology relates to our ability to understand the ideal motions that maximize a swimmer's velocity for the duration of the event."

**Important:** Ideal biomechanics are **inextricably tied to physics** — you can't separate technique from drag and propulsion.

---

### 4. Neural, Cognitive, & Behavioral Sciences
- **Mind-body connection:** How the brain controls athletic performance
- **Neuromuscular adaptation:** Training the nervous system for explosive power
- **Mental toughness:** Psychology of training and competition

> "Neural, Cognitive, and Behavioral Sciences are critical in understanding how the mind controls athletic performances in training and in competition. Virtually every cognitive function we perform is controlled by our minds. Swimming is no exception."

## Propulsion: How Swimmers Generate Forward Force

### Propulsion Mechanics

Swimmers generate propulsion through:
1. **Backward hand/foot pressure** — Pushing water backward creates forward thrust (Newton's 3rd law)
2. **Lift forces** — Sculling motions create lift (similar to airplane wings)
3. **Vortex generation** — Controlled turbulence enhances propulsion

### Key Principle: Early Backward Pressure

> **Hall's Teaching Point (Butterfly example):** "The key to improving the pulling propulsion is to apply backward pressure to the hands early and maintain that pressure on the hands all the way back to the full extension of the elbows."

**Actionable:**
- Get hands into propulsive position **quickly** (high elbow catch)
- Apply force **backward, not downward**
- Maintain pressure through full extension

### Propulsion Measurement: The Race Club Technology

Gary Hall Sr. developed custom tools:
- **Velocity Meter (VM)** — Measures instantaneous swimming speed
- **Pressure Meter (PM)** — Measures force on hands throughout pull
- **Propulsion/Drag Meter** — Quantifies net force production

**Finding:** Most swimmers have **two pressure peaks** during butterfly pull:
1. **First peak:** Hands moving backward past shoulders, synchronized with shoulder lift
2. **Second peak:** Hands releasing upward (no propulsive value — force is upward, not backward)

**Lesson:** Only backward pressure = propulsion. Vertical pressure = wasted effort.

## Drag: The Enemy of Speed

### Types of Drag

1. **Frontal Drag** — Resistance from pushing water forward with your body
2. **Surface Drag** — Friction along the body surface
3. **Wave Drag** — Energy lost creating waves on the pool surface

**Frontal drag** is the dominant force in swimming — far more significant than surface or wave drag.

---

### Reducing Frontal Drag

#### Body Position
- **Streamline position** — Hands stacked, head neutral, tight body line
- **Horizontal body position** — Hips at surface, minimal "sitting" position
- **Rotation vs. flat swimming** — Depends on stroke (see [Three Styles of Freestyle](../technique/freestyle/three-styles-of-freestyle.md))

#### Head Position
- **Eyes down** — Reduces frontal drag by keeping hips high
- **Breathing technique** — Minimize head lift (see [Coupling Motions](../technique/coupling-motions.md))

#### Underwater Phase
- **Dolphin kicks off the wall** — Fastest part of the race (no wave drag, minimal frontal drag)
- **Timing:** Immediate kick vs. short glide vs. long glide

> **Hall's Research Finding:** "With all four swimmers, the fastest time to 5 meters was with a delayed dolphin kick using a short glide. When the dolphin kick was initiated immediately off the wall, the time to 5 meters was slower. With a long glide, the time to 5 meters was slower."

**Actionable:**  
✅ **Short glide (0.5-1 second) then dolphin kick** = fastest  
❌ Immediate kick = slower (body not streamlined yet)  
❌ Long glide = slower (deceleration too great)

## Stroke Rate vs. Distance Per Stroke (DPS)

### The Trade-Off

```
Velocity = Stroke Rate × Distance Per Stroke
```

But it's not linear:
- **Increasing stroke rate** → Often decreases DPS (shorter strokes)
- **Increasing DPS** → Often decreases stroke rate (longer glide)

**Optimal swimming** = Finding the rate/DPS combination that maximizes velocity for your event.

---

### By Event Distance

| Event | Stroke Rate Priority | DPS Priority |
|-------|---------------------|--------------|
| 50m | High (max turnover) | Low (power > glide) |
| 100m | High-moderate | Moderate |
| 200m | Moderate | Moderate-high |
| 400m+ | Lower | High (efficiency) |

---

### Measuring & Coaching

**Tools:**
- **Tempo trainer** — Lock in specific stroke rate
- **Stroke count** — Measure DPS consistency
- **Video analysis** — Identify wasted motion

**Drill:**  
Swim same pace at different stroke rates:
- Example: 1:00 per 100m at 50 SPM, 55 SPM, 60 SPM
- Find which rate feels most powerful and sustainable
- Train that rate

## Vortices: The Hidden Propulsion Tool

### What Are Vortices?

**Vortex** = A swirling flow of water created by hand/foot motion.

> **Hall's Definition:** "The fluid dynamics and flow (vortices) of a swimmer affect the ability to generate propulsion."

### How Vortices Help

When you scull your hand through the water:
1. You create **bound vortices** around your hand
2. These vortices create **lift force** (similar to airplane wing)
3. Combined with drag force (pushing water back), this produces propulsion

**Key Insight:** Elite swimmers don't just "pull" water backward — they **sculpt and control vortices** to maximize propulsive force.

---

### Training Vortex Control

**Drills:**
- **Sculling drills** — Figure-8 patterns, feel pressure on hands
- **Fist swimming** — Remove hand propulsion, feel forearm pressure
- **Single-arm drills** — Isolate hand path, experiment with angles

**Coaching Cue:**  
> "Feel for 'grip' on the water — not just pushing, but holding and accelerating through the pull."

---

## Active vs. Passive Drag

### Passive Drag
**Definition:** Drag measured while body is held still in water (towed by rope or streamlined glide).

**Factors:**
- Body size and shape
- Streamline position quality
- Surface texture (shaved vs. unshaved)

---

### Active Drag
**Definition:** Drag measured while swimming (includes propulsive movements).

**Key Difference:** Active drag is **higher** than passive drag because:
- Arms and legs create turbulence
- Body position changes throughout stroke cycle
- Recovery movements add frontal surface area

**Hall's Tech Insight:**  
"Our ability to quantitate frontal drag in both active (while swimming) and passive (fixed positions) forms largely influences the swimming techniques we should teach."

---

## Applying Physics to Stroke Technique

### Example: Backstroke vs. Freestyle Pull Depth

> **Hall's Observation:** "In freestyle, the hand is at least two feet under water before the propulsion phase begins. In backstroke, propulsion can start at a depth of eight to ten inches."

**Why?**
- **Backstroke:** Shoulders already in extended position (biomechanically stronger)
- **Freestyle:** Need depth to avoid "negative angle" (elbow higher than hand)

**Coaching Application:**  
Don't coach backstroke pull like freestyle pull — physics demands different paths.

---

### Example: Butterfly Hand Path

**Two approaches:**
1. **Small outsweep, high elbow** (Milorad Cavic) — Reduces frontal drag
2. **Wider outsweep, more inward motion** (Roland Schoeman) — Generates more lift

**Hall's Measurement (using Pressure Meter):**
- Both swimmers show **two pressure peaks**
- First peak = propulsive (backward force)
- Second peak = non-propulsive (upward force at release)

**Lesson:** Wide vs. narrow pull can both work — what matters is **backward pressure** during propulsive phase.

## Arm Recovery: High-Octane vs. Low-Octane

### High-Octane Recovery (Freestyle)

**Technique:**
- **Straight arm** driven forward aggressively
- **High shoulder elevation** (shoulder nearly touches chin)
- **Fast, powerful recovery**

**Physics:**
- Creates **centrifugal force** pulling shoulder joint apart (temporarily)
- Generates **coupling motion** — hip rotation drives shoulder recovery
- Increases **angular momentum** for next stroke

**Trade-Off:**
- More strain on shoulder joint
- Requires excellent shoulder stability
- Higher injury risk if done incorrectly

> **Hall's Warning:** "The faster the swimmer drives the arm down on the recovery, the greater the force pulling the shoulder joint apart."

---

### Low-Octane Recovery

**Technique:**
- **Bent elbow** recovery (high elbow above water)
- **Relaxed, controlled motion**
- **Lower shoulder elevation**

**Physics:**
- Less angular momentum
- Reduced shoulder strain
- More time for opposite arm to finish pull

**Best For:**
- Distance swimmers
- Swimmers with shoulder issues
- Developing proper timing

---

### Coaching Decision

**Use high-octane when:**
- Sprinting (50m, 100m)
- Swimmer has strong, stable shoulders
- Maximizing stroke rate and power

**Use low-octane when:**
- Distance events (800m, 1500m)
- Shoulder injury history
- Teaching beginners

## The Technology Gap in Swimming

> **Hall's Mission Statement:** "Relative to other sports or businesses, and with our current state of knowledge and technology, the swimming world is a relatively low-tech world. I hope to see that change."

### Current State
- **Physiology:** Well-developed (heart rate monitors, lactate testing, VO2 max)
- **Psychology:** Growing (sports psych, visualization)
- **Physics:** Underdeveloped (few tools measure drag, propulsion, vortices in real-time)

### The Race Club's Innovation
Gary Hall Sr. developed:
- **Velocity Meter** — Real-time speed tracking
- **Pressure Meter** — Force measurement on hands
- **Propulsion/Drag Meter** — Net propulsive force

**Impact:** Quantifying technique allows coaches to **measure** improvements, not just "feel" them.

---

## Actionable Coaching Applications

### 1. Measure Frontal Drag
- **Passive drag test:** Tow swimmer in streamline, measure resistance
- **Underwater video:** Identify body position errors (hips low, head high)
- **Fix:** Core strengthening, head position drills

### 2. Measure Stroke Rate & DPS
- **Tempo trainer:** Lock in optimal stroke rate for each event
- **Stroke count sets:** Maintain consistent DPS under fatigue
- **Test:** 6×100 at race pace, track stroke count per 25m

### 3. Optimize Underwater Phase
- **Test dolphin kick timing:** Immediate, short glide, long glide
- **Measure:** Time to 5m, 10m, 15m off each wall
- **Find:** Individual optimal glide length

### 4. Improve Propulsive Efficiency
- **Sculling drills:** Build feel for hand pressure
- **Fist swimming:** Develop forearm propulsion
- **Pressure-focus sets:** Swim with focus on "pressing" water backward

### 5. Balance Stroke Rate & DPS
- **Descending stroke rate set:** 4×100 @ same pace, descending stroke count
- **Ascending stroke rate set:** 4×100 @ same pace, ascending stroke rate
- **Find:** The rate/DPS combo that feels most powerful

## Key Takeaways

1. **Physics is under-coached** — Most focus on physiology, not drag and propulsion
2. **Measure, don't guess** — Use tempo trainers, video, stroke counts
3. **Backward pressure = propulsion** — Vertical or upward pressure is wasted
4. **Frontal drag is the enemy** — Body position matters more than surface area
5. **Optimal rate/DPS varies by event** — Sprinters need higher rate, distance swimmers need higher DPS
6. **Technology closes gaps** — 0.02 seconds = gold vs. silver; technique matters enormously

**Bottom Line:** Swimming fast is a physics problem. Coaches who understand drag, propulsion, and vortices can make measurable, race-winning improvements that training volume alone cannot deliver.
