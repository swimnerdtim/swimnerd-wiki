# Physics of Swimming Speed

**Source:** *Fundamentals of Fast Swimming* by Gary Hall Sr. & Devin Murphy (2020)

## Overview

Swimming speed is governed by four basic sciences: **Physics, Physiology, Kinesiology (Biomechanics), and Neural/Cognitive Sciences**. Of these, physics is the least understood and most under-applied in coaching.

> **Hall's Assessment:** "Of the four basic sciences, the application of physics is perhaps the least understood or studied in the sport of swimming."

Understanding physics reveals why **ideal biomechanics often involve compromise** between propulsion and drag, not maximum propulsion alone.

---

## The Four Sciences of Swimming

### 1. Physics
**What it governs:** Bodies in motion through fluid  
**Key concepts:** Drag, propulsion, lift, inertia, flow dynamics (vortices)  
**Swimming relevance:** Newtonian mechanics + Fluid mechanics  

**Why it's under-applied:**
- Difficult to measure accurately in water
- Counterintuitive findings (more propulsion ≠ always faster)
- Requires specialized equipment (velocity meters, pressure sensors)

**Hall's focus:** Physics determines ideal technique, not tradition or observation.

### 2. Physiology
**What it governs:** Energy systems, muscle contraction, oxygen delivery  
**Key concepts:** Aerobic/anaerobic capacity, lactate threshold, VO2max, muscle fiber types  
**Swimming relevance:** Training methods, breathing, energy availability  

**Coaching strength:** Most advanced swimming nations excel at physiological training.

### 3. Kinesiology (Biomechanics)
**What it governs:** Body movements, muscle recruitment, range of motion  
**Key concepts:** Joint angles, force vectors, stroke mechanics, flexibility  
**Swimming relevance:** How to move through water efficiently  

**Coaching strength:** Coaches have good handle on biomechanics ("high elbow," "streamline," etc.)

### 4. Neural/Cognitive/Behavioral Sciences
**What it governs:** Mind control of athletic performance  
**Key concepts:** Neuromuscular adaptation, mental toughness, focus, stress response  
**Swimming relevance:** Training adaptation, race-day performance, motor learning  

**Coaching gap:** Mental training is still in infancy compared to physical training.

> **Hall's Belief:** "I believe that there is a great opportunity for improvement in these sciences" — particularly physics and neural sciences.

## The Drag vs. Propulsion Compromise

### Why Maximum Propulsion ≠ Maximum Speed

> **Critical Insight:** "The biomechanics that generate the most propulsion are often the ones that cause excessive amounts of frontal drag. Therefore, to reach a maximum sustainable velocity, the ideal biomechanics of a swimmer's technique involves compromise between these two forces of propulsion and drag."

**Example: Straight-Arm vs. Bent-Arm Recovery**

| Recovery Style | Propulsion Effect | Drag Effect | Net Result |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------|------------|
| Straight arm (high) | No change | +20% frontal drag | **Slower** |
| Bent arm (low) | No change | Minimal drag increase | **Faster** |

**Why bent arm wins:** Same propulsion, far less drag = higher net speed.

**Example: Wide Pull vs. Narrow Pull**

| Pull Width | Propulsion | Drag | Net Result |
|------------|-----------|------|------------|
| Wide pull (past shoulders) | +15% hand surface area | +25% frontal drag | **Slower** |
| Narrow pull (under shoulders) | Baseline | Baseline | **Faster** |

**Physics lesson:** More hand surface engaging water ≠ better if body drag increases more.

## The Three Forces in Swimming

### 1. Propulsion (Forward Force)

**Sources:**
- Hands pushing water backward
- Feet pushing water backward
- Small contribution from forearms/shins

> **Hall's Data:** "Nearly all of the propulsion of a swimmer comes from the surface of the hands and feet moving through the water."

**Propulsive mechanics:**
- **Newton's Third Law:** For every action, equal and opposite reaction
- Push water backward → body moves forward
- Magnitude depends on surface area × velocity² of hand/foot movement

**Maximizing propulsion:**
- High hand velocity through water
- Early vertical forearm (EVF) — more hand surface perpendicular to flow
- Strong kick
- **But:** Must not increase drag excessively

### 2. Drag (Resistance Force)

**Types of drag:**

#### A. Frontal Drag (Form Drag)
- Resistance from body profile pushing through water
- Increases with cross-sectional area
- **Dominant drag source** in swimming

**Factors:**
- Head position (lifting head = huge drag increase)
- Body position (hips dropping = drag)
- Arm recovery (straight arm recovery = drag)
- Shoulder width (wider = more drag)
- Swimming suit (tech suits reduce drag ~2-3%)

#### B. Surface Drag (Skin Friction)
- Resistance from water flowing over skin surface
- Minimal compared to frontal drag
- **Why shaving works:** Reduces surface drag slightly (~1-2%)

#### C. Wave Drag
- Energy lost creating surface waves
- Increases with speed (proportional to velocity³)
- Mostly relevant for surface swimming, less for underwater

**Drag formula (simplified):**
```
Drag Force = 0.5 × Water Density × Velocity² × Drag Coefficient × Frontal Area
```

**Key insight:** Drag increases with **square of velocity** — doubling speed = 4x drag.

### 3. Lift (Perpendicular Force)

**What it is:**
- Force perpendicular to hand/foot movement direction
- Created by pressure differential (like airplane wing)

**Swimming application:**
- **Sculling motions** — Hands create lift force
- **S-curve pull** (historical) — Attempted to use lift
- **Modern straight pull** — Minimizes lift, maximizes drag-based propulsion

> **Hall's Position:** Lift force is secondary to drag-based propulsion in swimming. Focus on pushing water directly backward.

## Flow Dynamics: Vortices

### What Are Vortices?

**Definition:** Rotating water flow created by hand/foot movement

**Types:**
- **Attached vortices:** Rotate with hand during pull
- **Shed vortices:** Released when hand changes direction

**Why they matter:**
- Vortices indicate energy transfer from hand to water
- Visible proof of propulsion
- Studying vortex patterns reveals propulsive efficiency

### The Race Club's Vortex Studies

**Method:** Underwater cameras capture vortex formation during stroke

**Findings:**
- **High-elbow catch:** Creates strong, attached vortex (good propulsion)
- **Dropped elbow:** Vortex detaches early (weak propulsion)
- **Fast hand entry:** Disrupts vortex formation (lost propulsion)
- **Smooth acceleration:** Maintains vortex attachment (sustained propulsion)

**Coaching application:** Teach swimmers to "hold water" = maintain attached vortex throughout pull.

## Anatomical Constraints

> **Hall's Reality Check:** "The human body was not designed or engineered very well to move fast through water."

### The Tools Required for Fast Swimming

**Swimmers need extraordinary:**

#### 1. Ankle Flexibility
- **Plantar flexion** — Foot points in line with shin (ballet foot)
- **Why:** Reduces drag from feet, increases kick propulsion
- **Limitation:** Most humans have limited ankle flexibility

#### 2. Knee Hyperextension
- **Requirement:** Knees extend beyond straight (slight backward bend)
- **Why:** Streamline position, powerful kick finish
- **Limitation:** Not all swimmers can achieve this

#### 3. Shoulder Extension
- **Requirement:** Extreme range of motion (180°+ overhead)
- **Why:** Streamline, long stroke, recovery clearance
- **Limitation:** Shoulder impingement common

#### 4. Hip Internal Rotation (Breaststroke)
- **Requirement:** Knees together, feet turned out
- **Why:** Breaststroke whip kick mechanics
- **Limitation:** Hip anatomy varies widely

#### 5. Lower Back Extension
- **Requirement:** Hyperextension (arch)
- **Why:** Butterfly undulation, streamline off walls
- **Limitation:** Back injuries common

### Strength in Unusual Muscles

**Swimmers must develop:**
- **Scapular stabilizers** — Control shoulder blade position (high elbow)
- **Hip flexors** — Drive kick recovery
- **Hip extensors** — Power kick propulsion
- **Serratus anterior** — Protract shoulder for reach
- **Rotator cuff** — Stabilize shoulder under load

> **Hall's Point:** "Humans need to become strong in muscles that are not normally very strong for purposes on land, such as the four primary muscles of the scapula and other muscles involved in high-elbow pulling motion, hip and knee extensors and hip flexors."

**Implication:** Dryland training must target swimming-specific muscles, not just general strength.

## Velocity, Drag, and Power Relationship

### The Cubic Relationship

**Formula:**
```
Power Required = Drag Force × Velocity
Drag Force ≈ Velocity²

Therefore:
Power Required ≈ Velocity³
```

**What this means:**
- Doubling speed requires **8x more power** (2³ = 8)
- Going from 1.0 m/s → 2.0 m/s needs 8x energy
- Tiny speed increases at high velocity are extremely costly

### Sprint vs. Distance Energy Costs

**50m sprint (2.0 m/s):**
- High power output
- Massive energy expenditure
- Sustainable for ~25 seconds

**1500m distance (1.5 m/s):**
- 75% of sprint speed
- Only **42% of sprint power** (0.75³ = 0.42)
- Sustainable for ~15 minutes

**Coaching implication:** Distance swimmers should prioritize technique efficiency (reduce drag) over raw power. Sprinters need both.

## Practical Applications: Reducing Drag

### Head Position

**Physics:**
- Lifting head 2cm = +15-25% frontal drag
- Looking forward = +20-30% drag
- Head tilt 5° = +10% drag

**Optimal:**
- Crown of head pointing forward (not up)
- Eyes looking down (45-90° angle)
- Chin tucked slightly
- Waterline at hairline (freestyle/fly)

**Hall's breathing cue:** Breathe in trough of bow wave, not above it.

### Body Position

**Physics:**
- Hips dropping 10cm below shoulders = +30-40% drag
- Body angle 5° from horizontal = +15% drag
- Legs too wide = +10-15% drag

**Optimal:**
- Horizontal body alignment (head to toe)
- Hips at surface
- Legs inside shoulder width "tube"
- Core engaged (prevent sag)

### Arm Recovery

**Physics:**
- Straight-arm recovery = +15-20% drag (arm acts like sail)
- High recovery over centerline = +5-10% drag
- Swinging across body = +10% drag

**Optimal:**
- Bent elbow recovery (lower profile)
- Recovery over shoulder line (not across)
- Relaxed, fluid motion (no splashing entry)

### Kick Width

**Physics:**
- Legs outside shoulder width = +10-15% drag
- Scissor kick (legs opposing) = +20% drag
- Knees bending >90° = +15% drag

**Optimal:**
- Legs inside "tube" defined by shoulders
- Kick from hips, not knees
- Small, fast kick (less drag than large, slow kick)

## Propulsion Mechanics

### Hand Path: Straight vs. S-Curve

**Historical (1970s-1990s):**
- **S-curve pull** — Hand sweeps in/out to generate lift
- Based on misapplication of Bernoulli's principle
- Theory: Lift force propels swimmer forward

**Modern (2000s+):**
- **Straight pull** — Hand moves directly backward
- Maximizes Newton's Third Law (push water back → go forward)
- Minimizes lateral movement (less drag)

> **Hall's Position:** "Our ability to understand how the fluid dynamics and flow (vortices) of a swimmer affect the ability to generate propulsion largely influence the swimming techniques we should teach."

**Science wins:** Velocity meters and pressure meters prove straight pull is faster.

### Early Vertical Forearm (EVF)

**Physics:**
- **Goal:** Get hand perpendicular to flow as early as possible
- Maximizes hand surface area pushing water
- Creates stronger vortex attachment

**Technique:**
- High elbow position during catch
- Fingertips pointing down toward pool bottom
- Elbow higher than hand throughout pull

**Result:**
- +20-30% hand pressure (measured by pressure meter)
- More propulsion from same muscular effort
- Foundation of modern freestyle/backstroke technique

### The Catch-Up Fallacy

**Myth:** "Catch-up freestyle" (one arm waits in front until other finishes) is efficient

**Physics reality:**
- Long glide phase = velocity drop (no propulsion)
- Swimmer must re-accelerate after glide (costs energy)
- Total time per stroke cycle increases

**Optimal:** Front quadrant swimming (one hand starts pull before other finishes)
- Minimizes velocity fluctuation
- Maintains momentum
- Propulsion nearly continuous

> **Hall's Rule:** "With hip-driven freestyle, the pulling hand should be in the early propulsive phase of the pull by the time the recovering hand strikes the water... There is no place for a catch-up freestyle technique in freestyle races."

## Stroke Rate vs. Distance Per Stroke (DPS)

### The Equation

**Velocity = Stroke Rate × Distance Per Stroke**

**Example:**
- Swimmer A: 60 SPM × 2.0 m/stroke = **2.0 m/s**
- Swimmer B: 75 SPM × 1.6 m/stroke = **2.0 m/s**

Both swimmers go the same speed, different techniques.

### The Trade-Off

**Increasing stroke rate:**
- ✅ More propulsive phases per second
- ✅ Less time spent gliding (velocity drop)
- ❌ Higher energy cost (more muscle contractions)
- ❌ Can decrease DPS if technique breaks down

**Increasing DPS:**
- ✅ More efficient (fewer strokes per lap)
- ✅ Lower energy cost per stroke
- ❌ Longer glide = velocity fluctuation
- ❌ May not be fast enough for sprints

### Optimal Strategy by Event

| Event | Priority | Stroke Rate | DPS |
|-------|----------|-------------|-----|
| 50m | Max speed | Very high (85-100 SPM) | Moderate |
| 100m | Speed + efficiency | High (75-90 SPM) | Moderate-High |
| 200m | Efficiency + speed | Moderate-High (65-80 SPM) | High |
| 400m+ | Max efficiency | Moderate (55-70 SPM) | Very High |

**Hall's insight:** Hip-driven (distance) uses low stroke rate + high DPS. Shoulder-driven (sprint) uses high stroke rate + moderate DPS.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Ideal technique = propulsion/drag compromise** — Not maximum propulsion alone
2. **Drag increases with velocity²** — Small technique errors cost more at high speeds
3. **Frontal drag dominates** — Head position, body alignment, recovery technique matter most
4. **Hands/feet generate ~100% of propulsion** — Focus on hand path and kick mechanics
5. **Vortices reveal efficiency** — Strong, attached vortex = good technique
6. **Anatomical constraints limit swimmers** — Flexibility training is essential
7. **Power required ≈ velocity³** — Tiny speed gains at elite level require massive energy increases
8. **Straight pull > S-curve** — Modern technique backed by physics
9. **Front quadrant swimming > catch-up** — Continuous propulsion beats gliding
10. **Stroke rate × DPS = speed** — Optimize both based on event distance

**Bottom Line:** Physics governs swimming speed more than any other science, yet it's the least studied by coaches. Understanding drag reduction, propulsive mechanics, and the velocity/power relationship reveals why certain techniques work and others don't. Measure with technology when possible; apply physics principles always.
