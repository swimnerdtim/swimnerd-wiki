# Race Club Technology: Measuring Swimming Performance

**Source:** *Fundamentals of Fast Swimming* by Gary Hall Sr. & Devin Murphy (2020)

## Overview

The Race Club pioneered the use of advanced measurement technology to quantify swimming technique with scientific precision. Founded by Gary Hall Sr. and Gary Hall Jr. after retirement, The Race Club transitioned from an elite training environment to a **teaching organization** focused on evidence-based technique coaching.

> **Hall's Mission:** "I am particularly dedicated to teaching based on good data, research and intelligent analysis, not just tradition, history, observation or theory."

**The Three Technologies:**
1. **Velocity Meter** — Real-time speed tracking throughout stroke cycle
2. **Pressure Meter** — Quantifies hand/foot pressure (propulsive force)
3. **Propulsion/Drag Meter** — Separates propulsive forces from drag forces

---

## Why Technology Matters in Swimming

### The 0.02-Second Margin

> **Hall's Story:** "My son, Gary Hall Jr., and his training partner, Anthony Ervin, have each won 2 Olympic gold medals in the 50-meter sprint by a combined total of .02 seconds in three events."

**Olympic Gold Margins:**
- 2000 Sydney: Ervin & Hall Jr. tied for gold in 50 free (21.98)
- 2004 Athens: Hall Jr. won by 0.01 seconds
- 2016 Rio: Ervin won by 0.01 seconds

**The takeaway:** At the elite level, one small technique mistake = losing gold.

> "In a sport that often results in differences between winning or not winning being measured in hundredths of seconds, we would be negligent to think that technique does not matter."

### The Low-Tech Problem

**Hall's observation:**
> "Swimming is a sport that takes place, for the most part, at the interface between water and air. When it comes to making mistakes, water is not very forgiving. One could easily consider that swimming is the most technique-sensitive sport in the world. Yet, relative to other sports or businesses, and with our current state of knowledge and technology, the swimming world is a relatively low-tech world."

**The solution:** Bring data-driven measurement to swimming technique coaching.

## The Three Technologies

### 1. Velocity Meter (Acquired 2015)

**What it measures:**
- Instantaneous swimming velocity throughout the stroke cycle
- Speed fluctuations during different stroke phases
- Acceleration and deceleration patterns
- Turns, starts, and underwater performance

**Developer:** Nunzio Lanotte (APLab Italy)

**How it works:**
- Tether-based system connects swimmer to measurement device
- Records velocity 50-100+ times per second
- Generates velocity curves for each stroke cycle
- Synced with video for visual analysis

**Key insights from velocity meter:**

#### Velocity Curve Analysis
- **Hip-driven freestyle:** Smoother velocity curve, less fluctuation
- **Shoulder-driven freestyle:** More velocity variation, higher peaks
- **Breaststroke:** Massive velocity fluctuations (surge during kick, deceleration during recovery)
- **Butterfly:** Two velocity peaks per stroke (one per kick)

#### Propulsive Phases
- Identifies exactly **when** hands/feet generate propulsion
- Reveals "dead spots" where velocity drops
- Shows coupling motion effects on speed

#### Turns & Starts
- Measures wall push-off velocity
- Tracks underwater velocity decay
- Identifies optimal breakout timing

**Coaching application:**
- Compare "before" and "after" technique changes
- Quantify improvements in hundredths/tenths of a second
- Identify which stroke phases need work

---

### 2. Pressure Meter

**What it measures:**
- Hand and foot pressure against the water
- Propulsive force magnitude
- Pressure distribution across palm/fingers
- Timing of peak pressure during stroke

**Developer:** Paolo Villanis (BioMovie)

**How it works:**
- Pressure sensors attached to hands/feet
- Real-time pressure mapping
- Color-coded pressure visualization
- Synced with video for biomechanical analysis

**Key insights from pressure meter:**

#### Hand Pressure Patterns

**High-elbow catch:**
- Pressure concentrated on palm and fingertips
- Early pressure generation (front quadrant)
- Sustained pressure through mid-pull

**Dropped elbow:**
- Pressure shifts to back of hand (non-propulsive)
- Delayed pressure onset
- Weak mid-pull pressure

#### Coupling Effect Validation

> **Critical finding:** "When coupling timing is correct, hand pressure increases measurably without increased arm strength."

**Experiment:**
- Swimmers perform pull with and without coupling motions
- Pressure meter shows 10-20% pressure increase with proper coupling
- No change in muscular effort (EMG studies)

**Proof:** Coupling motions work.

#### Foot Pressure (Kick Analysis)

**Flutter kick:**
- Measures downward pressure (propulsion)
- Identifies "scissor kick" (opposing forces cancel out)
- Reveals ankle flexibility limitations

**Dolphin kick:**
- Two pressure peaks per kick cycle (down and up phases)
- Quantifies core contribution to kick
- Shows timing with arm pull

**Breaststroke kick:**
- Peak pressure during outward "whip"
- Measures recovery drag
- Reveals timing with arm pull

### 3. Propulsion/Drag Meter

**What it measures:**
- Separates propulsive forces from drag forces
- Net force (propulsion minus drag)
- Active drag (drag while swimming, not static)
- Power output (force × velocity)

**Developer:** Paolo Villanis (BioMovie)

**How it works:**
- Force sensors measure all directional forces
- Algorithm separates forward propulsion from backward drag
- Calculates net propulsive efficiency
- Synced with velocity and pressure data

**Key insights from propulsion/drag meter:**

#### Propulsive Efficiency

**Formula:**
```
Propulsive Efficiency = Net Forward Force / Total Propulsive Force
```

**Why it matters:**
- Swimmer A: 100N propulsion, 40N drag = 60N net force (60% efficient)
- Swimmer B: 120N propulsion, 80N drag = 40N net force (33% efficient)
- **Swimmer A is faster despite less propulsion** — technique creates less drag

> **Hall's Conclusion:** "Achieving the ideal biomechanics of any swimmer involves compromise between these two forces of propulsion and drag."

#### Active Drag vs. Passive Drag

**Passive drag:** Measured in streamline position (fixed)
**Active drag:** Measured while swimming (dynamic)

**Finding:** Active drag is often 2-3x higher than passive drag
- Arm recovery lifts body (drag)
- Head position shifts (drag)
- Leg kick widens profile (drag)

**Application:** Focus technique coaching on minimizing active drag during stroke.

#### Stroke-Specific Findings

**Freestyle:**
- Straight-arm recovery = 15-20% more active drag than bent arm
- Crossover recovery = 10-15% more drag than shoulder-width entry
- Head lift during breathing = 20-30% drag increase

**Breaststroke:**
- Recovery drag can equal propulsive force (net force near zero)
- Narrow recovery = 25% less drag than wide recovery
- Streamline timing critical (glide phase has lowest drag)

**Butterfly:**
- Over-the-water recovery = lower drag than underwater recovery
- Head position variance = 15-25% drag fluctuation
- Hip undulation can increase or decrease drag (technique-dependent)

## Synchronizing Technology with Video

> **Hall's Breakthrough:** "Third, analyzing the data and images simultaneously is essential (synchronizing video to the technology) and, if done carefully, can lead to accurate conclusions."

### The Integration Process

**Step 1: Capture**
- Record underwater and above-water video
- Simultaneously capture velocity, pressure, and/or drag data
- Use waterproof markers for alignment

**Step 2: Synchronize**
- Align video timeline with data timeline
- Mark key stroke events (entry, catch, finish, recovery)
- Create split-screen view (video + data overlay)

**Step 3: Analyze**
- Identify when velocity drops → look at video to see why
- Check pressure data at low-velocity moments → diagnose hand position
- Correlate drag spikes with body position errors

**Step 4: Adjust & Re-Test**
- Make one technique change at a time
- Re-test with same technology setup
- Compare before/after data quantitatively

**Result:** Evidence-based technique coaching, not guesswork.

## Key Findings from Race Club Technology

### 1. All Swimmers Make Mistakes

> **Hall's First Conclusion:** "All swimmers, including Olympians, are making mistakes. The poor swimmers just make more of them."

**Olympic-level mistakes discovered:**
- Dropped elbow during catch (low pressure)
- Excessive head lift on breathing (velocity drop)
- Late coupling timing (missed pressure amplification)
- Inefficient kick (high effort, low propulsion)

**The good news:** Every swimmer can improve.

### 2. The Data Is Reliable

> **Hall's Second Conclusion:** "The data from this technology is very good (not perfect) and reproducible."

**Testing protocol:**
- Repeat same swim 3-5 times
- Data variance < 5% for elite swimmers
- Consistent patterns emerge across trials
- Technology is trustworthy for coaching decisions

### 3. Swimmers Can Learn

> **Hall's Fourth Conclusion:** "Swimmers can learn from the knowledge. In other words, they can apply the technical changes that they have been taught, some better than others, and swim faster. Some swim much faster."

**Case study example:**
- Swimmer with dropped elbow (pressure meter shows low hand pressure)
- Coached to high-elbow catch position
- Re-tested: 18% pressure increase, 0.4 sec faster per 100m
- **Total coaching time:** 30 minutes

**Proof:** Data-driven coaching works.

## Becoming a Swimming Technique Coach

> **Hall's Vision:** "For the future of our sport, I hope to push the envelope of swimming technology. In the process, a new breed of swimming coach is arising, the swimming technique coach. I am proud and excited to be one of those new breeds of coaches."

### The New Coaching Model

**Old model:**
- "Swim more yards"
- Observation-based corrections
- Trial and error
- Traditional wisdom ("this is how we've always done it")

**New model:**
- **Measure** technique with technology
- **Analyze** data synchronized with video
- **Adjust** one variable at a time
- **Re-test** to confirm improvement
- **Iterate** until optimal

### Required Skills

**Swimming technique coach must:**
1. Understand physics of swimming (drag, propulsion, Newton's laws)
2. Interpret velocity, pressure, and drag data
3. Synchronize video with data
4. Communicate findings to swimmers (kinesthetic cues)
5. Design technique-focused workouts (not just conditioning)

**Training needed:**
- Biomechanics coursework
- Technology platform training (APLab, BioMovie)
- Video analysis certification
- Physics/fluid mechanics basics

## Practical Applications for Coaches

### What You Can Do Without Technology

**Use video analysis:**
- 240fps slow-motion cameras (smartphones work)
- Underwater housing ($50-500)
- Free software (Coaches Eye, Hudl Technique)

**What to measure manually:**
- Stroke count per 25m (distance per stroke)
- Stroke rate with tempo trainer
- Split times at 5m intervals (velocity curve approximation)
- Breathing pattern consistency

**How to approximate technology findings:**
- **Velocity drops:** Watch for slowdowns in video → correlate with stroke phase
- **Hand pressure:** Look for high elbow position, EVF (early vertical forearm)
- **Drag increases:** Check head position, body alignment, recovery technique

### Sets That Leverage Technology Insights

**Set 1: Velocity Curve Smoothing**
```
8 × 50 freestyle on 1:00
Goal: Minimize velocity fluctuation within each stroke
Cue: "Constant speed, no surging or gliding"
```

**Set 2: Coupling Timing (from pressure meter findings)**
```
6 × 75 on 1:30
25 - Exaggerate body rotation (feel coupling)
25 - Normal stroke
25 - Fast rotation, fast recovery
Goal: Feel pressure increase in pulling hand
```

**Set 3: Active Drag Reduction**
```
10 × 25 on :45
Odd: Normal technique
Even: Focus on streamline during recovery
Goal: Match or beat odd-25 times with less effort
```

## The Future of Swimming Technology

### Emerging Tools

**Already available:**
- **Wearable IMU sensors** — Track body rotation, acceleration (TritonWear, FORM)
- **Underwater pace clocks** — Real-time split feedback (Swimnerd Live)
- **AI video analysis** — Automated stroke analysis (SwimAI, MotionMetrics)
- **Power meters** — Similar to cycling, measure propulsive power

**Coming soon:**
- **Real-time coaching displays** — Swimmer sees data during practice
- **VR technique training** — Practice stroke in virtual environment
- **Genetic/physiological profiling** — Personalized technique recommendations based on muscle fiber type, limb length, flexibility

### Hall's Hope

> "For the future of our sport, I hope to push the envelope of swimming technology... We can make a difference with swimmers, if we use and interpret the technology properly."

**The goal:** Make swimming as data-driven as cycling, rowing, and running already are.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Technology reveals hidden mistakes** — Even Olympians have technique flaws detectable by velocity/pressure/drag meters
2. **Data is reproducible** — Technology findings are trustworthy for coaching decisions
3. **Synchronize video with data** — Visual + quantitative analysis together = accurate diagnosis
4. **One change at a time** — Isolate variables, re-test, confirm improvement before next adjustment
5. **Swimmers can learn quickly** — Data-driven coaching can yield 0.2-0.5 sec improvements in 30-60 minutes
6. **New breed of coach emerging** — Swimming technique coach role requires biomechanics knowledge and tech interpretation skills
7. **You don't need $50K equipment** — Video analysis + stroke counting + tempo trainers approximate many technology insights

**Bottom Line:** Swimming is entering a data-driven era. Coaches who embrace measurement technology and evidence-based technique adjustments will produce faster swimmers. The guesswork is over — now we can measure, analyze, and optimize with precision.
