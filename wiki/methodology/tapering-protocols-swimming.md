# Tapering Protocols for Swimming

**Real-World Taper Plans From Elite Swimming Coaches — Bob Bowman, Shannon Rollason, and Research-Based Examples**

## Overview

This page compiles **proven tapering protocols** from world-class swimming coaches who have produced Olympic and World Champions. These are NOT theoretical — they are **actual taper plans** used at the highest level of the sport.

**Featured coaches:**
1. **Bob Bowman** — Michael Phelps (14 Olympic Gold Medals), Peter Vanderkaay, Erik Vendt

## Bob Bowman's Taper Philosophy (Michael Phelps' Coach)

### Core Principles

**"The main challenge when designing a peaking plan is maintaining control of the various elements that interact with each other during the taper process. It is unwise to reduce both volume and intensity simultaneously."**

**Bowman's Golden Rules:**

1. **Reduce volume first, maintain intensity** throughout the bulk of the taper
2. **Begin taper 21 days before first event** (formal taper)
3. **Start pre-taper reduction 6 weeks out** (reduce frequency by 1 session/week)
4. **Reduce intensity ONLY in final days** (when volume is at lowest)
5. **Mental preparation is the key** to outstanding performance

---

### Bowman's Volume Reduction Strategy

**Pre-taper training load (Michael Phelps, midseason):**
- Morning: 6,000m
- Afternoon: 7,000m
- **Total:** 13,000m/day

**Taper progression:**
- **6 weeks out:** Drop from 4 morning sessions/week → 3 morning sessions/week (automatic 20% volume reduction while maintaining intensity)
- **20 days out:** Formal taper begins (gradual volume reduction continues)
- **Final days:** Volume at lowest, intensity drops to maintenance

**Key insight:** Reducing **frequency** early (6 weeks out) is Bowman's "extra step" that ensures consistent volume reduction without compromising intensity during the critical 3-week taper.

### Bowman's Key Insights

#### 1. Mental Preparation (Starts 6 Weeks Out)

**"Mental preparation is the key to outstanding performance in world-level competition."**

**Bowman's mental training protocol:**
- **6 weeks before competition:** Begin daily visualization
- **Specificity is critical:** "See the race unfold the way you want it to"
- **Focus on race segments:** Start, turns, pace splits, finish (impossible to replicate entire race in training, so break it down)
- **Visualize during taper workouts:** While performing race-pace sets, imagine swimming in the actual competition

**Example:** During 4×50m @ race pace set, visualize the first 50m of the 200 IM at World Championships (underwater off start, breakout, stroke rate, breathing pattern).

---

#### 2. Day-to-Day Feelings Don't Matter

**"We do not put a lot of emphasis on how each swimmer feels on a day-to-day basis. We focus on stroke technique and times swum at certain distances. You do not have to feel good to swim fast."**

**Example from 2007 Worlds:**
- 3 days before competition, Michael Phelps had "one of his worst workouts of the year"
- Bowman's response: "How he feels 3 days before the event is not important. He was well prepared for the year preceding, and one practice would not affect his performance."
- **Result:** 7 gold medals, 5 world records

**Key lesson:** **Trust the taper plan, not the feelings.** Temporary setbacks (sluggish workout, heavy legs, stroke feeling "off") are NORMAL during taper. The adaptations will emerge on race day.

---

#### 3. Individualization Within Group Training

**Even swimmers on the same relay need different tapers:**

**2007 World Championships 4×200m Freestyle Relay (World Record):**
- **Michael Phelps:** Standard taper (as shown above)
- **Peter Vanderkaay:** "Slightly less volume because of his muscular build and residual fatigue during sharpening"
- **Klete Keller:** "More aerobic mileage as normal course with a swimmer who is not particularly muscled and comes from distance training background"

**Principle:** **Age, muscle mass, training history, and event specialty all affect taper needs.** Coaches must adjust individual plans within the group framework.

---

#### 4. Peaking Is Not Magic

**"The peaking period is not a magic time when coaches create miracles. The peaking period is a natural outgrowth of the entire training year."**

**Bowman's philosophy:**
- If preparation during hard training is well-planned and executed, **the taper will be successful**
- If training is poorly planned, **no taper can fix it**
- The coach must "sell" the plan to athletes and refine it over years to meet individual needs

**Bottom line:** **Taper reveals fitness; it doesn't create it.** The work is done in the months before. Taper simply removes the fatigue masking that fitness.

## Shannon Rollason's Taper Philosophy (Jodie Henry, Alice Mills)

### Core Principles

**"In the last 21 days before competition starts, it is important that you and your swimmers are not trying to find speed; if you let it, it will find you."**

**Rollason's Golden Rules:**

1. **Two primary taper models:** Linear and Exponential (both produce world-class results)
2. **Swim submaximal and easy** ("easy-fast," not "hard-fast")
3. **Monitor stroke length** (not kick intensity)
4. **Use 1×200m heart rate check** (30-40 bpm below max) to track taper progress
5. **Never talk about fitness during taper** — emphasize efficiency and balance (arms vs. legs)
6. **Psychology is crucial** — rooming assignments, pre-race routines, managing emotions

---

### Rollason's Taper Models

#### Model 1: Exponential Taper (Bigger Volume Reduction)

**Weekly volume progression (as % of peak training):**
- **Week 1:** 68% of season preparation volume
- **Week 2:** 48%
- **Week 3:** 33%

**Example (peak training = 40km/week):**
- Week 1: 27.2km
- Week 2: 19.2km
- Week 3: 13.2km
- **Total reduction:** 67% by final week

**When to use:** "Uninterrupted preparation" (no illness, injury, or training disruptions). Allows for aggressive recovery.

---

#### Model 2: Linear Taper (More Conservative)

**Weekly volume progression (as % of peak training):**
- **Week 1:** 75%
- **Week 2:** 50%
- **Week 3:** 25%

**Example (peak training = 40km/week):**
- Week 1: 30km
- Week 2: 20km
- Week 3: 10km
- **Total reduction:** 75% by final week

**When to use:** "Interrupted preparation" (illness, travel disruptions). Provides more gradual recovery when training block was compromised.

### Jodie Henry's 2004 Olympic Games Taper (Gold Medal, World Record)

**Context:**
- **Pre-Olympic trials performance:** 53.77s in 100m freestyle (world-class)
- **Preparation:** 15 weeks, avg 38.5km/week, peak 48km/week
- **Disruption:** Illness 6 weeks out (only 15km that week)
- **Taper model selected:** **Linear taper** (due to illness disruption)

**Result:**
- **Olympic Trials:** Split of 52.95s in 4×100m relay (fastest in history at that time)
- **Olympic Final:** Won 100m freestyle individual gold

### Rollason's Monitoring Tool: Heart Rate Check (HRC)

**Protocol:**
- **Distance:** 1×200m
- **Target heart rate:** 30-40 bpm below maximum HR
- **Measurement:** Record time and HR

**Purpose:**
- "Gives me an easy indication of where they are during the taper"
- If HR is lower but time is faster → fitness improving, fatigue dissipating (IDEAL)
- If HR is higher and time is slower → incomplete recovery, need more rest

**Example from Jodie Henry's taper:**
- **Mon Jul 26 (3 weeks out):** 2:08.80 @ 168 bpm (30 bpm below max of ~198)
- **Fri Jul 30 (2.5 weeks out):** 2:11.80 @ 152 bpm (HR dropped 16 bpm despite similar effort)
- **Interpretation:** Fatigue clearing, aerobic efficiency improving, taper progressing well

**Practical tip:** Use this weekly during taper to objectively track recovery (removes guesswork from "how do you feel?").

### Rollason's Psychological Strategies

#### 1. Rooming Assignments During Championships

**Bad match:**
- Two "highly excitable" swimmers (amplify each other's nerves)
- Swimmers finishing on different days (e.g., one finishes day 4, other swims days 7-8 → first swimmer's excitement disrupts second swimmer's focus)

**Good match:**
- One calm swimmer + one excitable swimmer (balances energy)
- Swimmers with similar competition schedules

**50m sprint swimmers:** "Performance can be compromised if swimmers have been overstimulated by fast swimming by their peers during the meet." Keep sprint swimmers away from mid-meet excitement if they compete late in the meet.

---

#### 2. Pre-Race Routine (Jodie Henry's 2004 Olympic 100m Final)

**Context:** After Jodie's 52.95 relay split, "there was a lot of talk among swimmers that she was the woman who would win. Jodie got off track a little."

**Rollason's intervention:**
- **No discussion of the race itself** (no splits, no strategy, no opponents)
- **Focus only on the 10-15 minutes before the gun:** "What to do on pool deck"
- **Instructions:** Stay in the moment, "what will be, will be"

**Key quote:** "Too many swimmers swim their race before the gun goes off."

**Rollason's philosophy:**
- **Don't talk about the opposition** ("I didn't talk to Jodie about what the opposition would do, like go out in under 26s, because they didn't!")
- **Timing is everything:** "Don't say the right thing at the wrong time"
- **What you DON'T say is just as important as what you DO say**

**Result:** Jodie executed perfectly. Gold medal.

---

#### 3. Tactic from 2003 World Championships (Alice Mills)

**Situation:** Alice Mills, young swimmer, first major international meet

**Rollason's approach:** Same as Jodie Henry (focus on pool deck routine, not race tactics)

**Result:** Alice Mills won **two individual silver medals** (massive breakthrough)

**Key insight:** Young or nervous swimmers benefit from **external focus** (what to do on deck) rather than **internal focus** (splits, opponents, outcome). Reduces cognitive load and anxiety.

## Research-Based Taper Protocols (Mujika's Meta-Analysis)

### Protocol 1: Optimal Exponential Taper (2-3 Weeks)

**Best for:** Middle-distance and distance swimmers (200m-1500m)

**Duration:** 14-21 days

**Volume reduction:**
- **Week 1:** 30-40% reduction (daily volume = 60-70% of pre-taper)
- **Week 2:** 50-60% reduction (daily volume = 40-50% of pre-taper)
- **Week 3 (if used):** 60-70% reduction (daily volume = 30-40% of pre-taper)

**Intensity:**
- **Maintain at 90-100%** of pre-taper intensity throughout
- **Increase proportion of race-pace work** each week (from 15% to 30% of weekly volume)

**Frequency:**
- **Reduce by ≤20%** (e.g., 10 sessions/week → 8 sessions/week)

**Expected performance gain:** 2.0-4.0% (research average)

---

### Protocol 2: Sprint Swimmer Taper (1-2 Weeks)

**Best for:** 50m and 100m sprinters

**Duration:** 7-14 days (shorter than distance swimmers)

**Volume reduction:**
- **Week 1:** 40-50% reduction
- **Week 2:** 60-70% reduction

**Intensity:**
- **Maintain 100%** of race-pace and sprint work
- **Emphasize neuromuscular power:** Starts, turns, underwater dolphin kicks, breakouts
- **Short rest intervals** (keep nervous system sharp)

**Frequency:**
- **Maintain 90-100%** of pre-taper frequency (8-10 sessions/week)
- Sprinters detrain FASTER than distance swimmers → need more frequent stimulation

**Expected performance gain:** 1.5-3.0%

---

### Protocol 3: Multiple-Peak Season (Dual Tapers)

**Best for:** Swimmers competing at both Conference Championships AND NCAA/National Championships (2-4 weeks apart)

**Taper 1 (Conference Championships):**
- **Duration:** 7-10 days (short taper)
- **Volume reduction:** 40-50%
- **Intensity:** Maintained
- **Goal:** Peak for conference, but NOT fully rested

**Recovery Week (Between Meets):**
- **Days 1-3 post-conference:** Active recovery (3,000-4,000m/day, easy aerobic)
- **Days 4-7:** Resume moderate training (6,000-7,000m/day, some race-pace work)

**Taper 2 (Nationals):**
- **Duration:** 10-14 days (longer taper)
- **Volume reduction:** 60-70%
- **Intensity:** Maintained + increased race-pace rehearsal
- **Goal:** Full peak for championship meet

**Key insight:** First taper is "practice taper" (test protocols, identify what works). Second taper is "championship taper" (execute refined plan).

## Common Questions & Answers

### Q1: "My swimmer feels sluggish during the first week of taper. Is this normal?"

**A:** **YES.** This is glycogen supercompensation (muscles storing extra fuel + water). Bob Bowman: "You do not have to feel good to swim fast." Shannon Rollason: "At no time during the taper do I talk about fitness; I emphasize efficiency." The sluggishness will disappear by week 2-3.

**Fix:** Trust the process. Monitor stroke length and technique (not feelings). Use HRC to objectively track progress.

---

### Q2: "Should I reduce intensity to 'save energy' for the meet?"

**A:** **NO.** Bob Bowman: "It is unwise to reduce both volume and intensity simultaneously." Research shows reducing intensity = detraining (VO₂ max declines, performance plateaus or declines).

**Fix:** **Volume down, intensity maintained.** Only reduce intensity in final 1-2 days before competition (and only slightly).

---

### Q3: "My swimmer had a bad workout 3 days before the meet. Should I panic?"

**A:** **NO.** Michael Phelps had "one of his worst workouts of the year" 3 days before the 2007 World Championships. Result: 7 golds, 5 world records.

**Fix:** "The athlete and coach must have faith in the plan and not panic when temporary setbacks occur." (Bob Bowman)

---

### Q4: "Linear or exponential taper — which is better?"

**A:** **Exponential is slightly better** according to research (Banister et al. 1999: 4.0% improvement with exponential vs. 0% with step taper). BUT **both work** at elite level:
- Shannon Rollason uses BOTH (exponential for uninterrupted prep, linear for interrupted prep)
- Jodie Henry won Olympic gold with LINEAR taper

**Fix:** **Start with exponential** (science says it's optimal). If it doesn't feel right after 1-2 trials, switch to linear.

---

### Q5: "How do I decide between 2-week vs. 3-week taper?"

**A:**
- **Sprinters (50m-100m):** 7-14 days (shorter taper)
- **Middle-distance (200m-400m):** 14-21 days (longer taper)
- **Distance (800m-1500m):** 14-21 days (longer taper)
- **Older athletes (30+ years):** Add 3-5 days to standard taper
- **Overtrained athletes:** Add 5-7 days to standard taper

**General rule:** If baseline fatigue is HIGH (overreaching, chronic soreness), go LONGER. If fitness is good and fatigue is moderate, go SHORTER.

---

### Q6: "My swimmer wants to add extra yardage because they 'feel good.' Should I let them?"

**A:** **NO.** Shannon Rollason: "In the last 21 days, it is important that you are not trying to find speed; if you let it, it will find you."

**Fix:** Excess energy = sign that taper is working. Redirect energy into mental preparation (visualization, race strategy, sleep hygiene). Adding volume now = sabotaging the taper.

## Key Takeaways

✅ **Bob Bowman's philosophy:** Reduce volume, maintain intensity, begin 21 days out, extend pre-taper prep to 6 weeks (frequency reduction)  
✅ **Michael Phelps' 2007 Worlds taper:** 20-day exponential taper, 73% volume reduction, race-pace work maintained, mental prep critical  
✅ **Shannon Rollason's philosophy:** "Speed will find you," monitor stroke length & HRC, psychology is crucial  
✅ **Jodie Henry's 2004 Olympic taper:** Linear taper (due to illness), 75% volume reduction, pre-race routine focused on pool deck (not race tactics)  
✅ **Research protocols:** Exponential > linear > step; 2-3 weeks for distance, 1-2 weeks for sprinters; 41-60% volume reduction optimal  
✅ **Mental game:** Feelings don't predict performance, trust the plan, manage rooming assignments, focus on process (not outcome)  
✅ **Monitoring tools:** HRC (1×200m @ submaximal HR), stroke length, technique quality (NOT "how do you feel?")
