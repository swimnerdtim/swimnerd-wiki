# Freestyle Technique for Open Water

**Source:** *Open Water Swimming* by Steven Munatones (2011)  
**Context:** Stroke modifications and technical adaptations specific to open water conditions  
**Audience:** Coaches teaching freestyle technique for open water competition

---

## Core Principle: Symmetry Over Speed

**In the pool:**
- Walls provide orientation
- Black line guides direction
- Lane lines prevent drift
- Asymmetry is corrected by structure

**In open water:**
- No walls, no lines, no anchors
- Asymmetrical stroke = swimming in circles
- Symmetry = straight swimming = fewer sightings = faster overall
- **Symmetry is speed in open water**

---

## Body Position & Rotation

### The Central Axis Concept

**Ideal position:**
- Spine is the fixed axis (imagine a rod through head to hips)
- Shoulders, torso, hips rotate smoothly around this axis
- Minimal lateral movement of head, torso, legs
- Left arm + left leg stay on left side of axis
- Right arm + right leg stay on right side of axis

**Why it matters:**
- Streamlined position reduces drag
- Symmetrical rotation = straight swimming
- Easier to navigate without constant sighting

### Body Rotation Adaptations

**Pool swimming:** 60-90 degree rotation (maximum efficiency)

**Open water swimming:** 45-60 degree rotation (stability + efficiency)

**Reasons for reduced rotation:**
- More stable in rough water
- Less susceptible to being rolled by waves
- Easier to sight without disrupting stroke
- Better balance when contact occurs

**How to practice:**
```
Drill: Controlled Rotation
- 8 × 100 @ :20 rest
- Focus on 45-60 degree rotation (not maximum)
- Flatter body position
- Stronger core engagement to maintain position
- Video analysis: check rotation angle
```

## Arm Stroke Modifications

### 1. Stroke Length vs. Stroke Rate

**Pool reality:**
- Longer stroke = more efficient (Distance Per Stroke focus)
- Extended glide phase maximizes propulsion
- Typical: 12-16 strokes per 25 yards

**Open water reality:**
- Waves disrupt glide phase
- Continuous propulsion needed against elements
- Higher turnover = better balance
- Typical: 16-22 strokes per 25 yards (in rough water)

### When to Adjust Stroke Rate

**Increase turnover when:**
- Rough water/surface chop (waves kill glide)
- Fighting headwinds or current
- Contact/pack swimming (maintain position)
- Final sprint (last 200 yards)

**Maintain longer stroke when:**
- Calm conditions
- Swimming with current
- Drafting (conserving energy)
- Ultra-long swims (marathon distance)

**Training for variable stroke rate:**
```
Set: Stroke Rate Ladder
5 × 400 @ :30 rest
- 400 #1: 16 strokes per 25 (long, smooth)
- 400 #2: 18 strokes per 25
- 400 #3: 20 strokes per 25
- 400 #4: 22 strokes per 25 (quick turnover)
- 400 #5: Return to 16 strokes per 25

Goal: Maintain same pace despite changing stroke count
Practice shifting gears mid-swim
```

### 2. The Catch in Open Water

**Middle part of stroke = most effective in rough water**

**Why:**
- Front of stroke (entry/extension): Hand slips in waves
- End of stroke (finish/follow-through): Less effective in turbulence
- **Middle** (when hand is under chest/hips): Maximum propulsion

**Technical focus:**
- Quick entry, no over-reaching
- High elbow catch position
- Powerful pull through middle phase
- Clean exit (don't drag hand through recovery)

**Key difference from pool:**
- Pool: Full stroke completion, long finish
- Open water: Abbreviated finish, focus on catch and middle pull

## Kick: The Variable Propulsion Tool

### Kick Intensity Based on Situation

**Standard pool kick:** 2-beat or 4-beat (energy conservation)

**Open water requires flexibility:**

| Situation | Kick Intensity | Purpose |
|-----------|---------------|----------|
| Calm water, drafting | 2-beat (minimal) | Energy conservation |
| Rough water | 4-beat (moderate) | Stability and balance |
| Fighting current | 6-beat (strong) | Additional propulsion |
| Sprint finish | Continuous flutter | Maximum speed |
| Mass start chaos | Strong kick | Position establishment |
| Riding wave/swell | Sustained kick | Maintain wave momentum |

### Training Variable Kick Intensity

**Goal:** Shift kick intensity mid-swim without losing rhythm

```
Set 1: Kick Gears
8 × 400 @ :30 rest
- 400 #1: 2-beat kick (minimal)
- 400 #2: 4-beat kick (moderate)
- 400 #3: 6-beat kick (strong)
- 400 #4: Continuous flutter (sprint)
- Repeat 2x
Maintain stroke rate, change only kick

Set 2: Situational Kicking
10 × 200 @ :20 rest
- First 100: Minimal kick (drafting simulation)
- Second 100: Strong kick (sprint/current simulation)
- Practice the transition mid-200

Set 3: No-Push Kick Strength
6 × 100 @ 1:30
- Do NOT push off walls
- Flip turn, resume stroking immediately
- Forces strong kick to maintain momentum
```

### Leg Position for Streamlining

**Even with minimal kick:**
- Keep feet pointed (NOT flexed)
- Hips riding high in water
- Small amplitude kick (narrow cylinder)
- Avoid "dropped hips" position

**Problem:** Many swimmers stop kicking entirely → hips drop → massive drag

**Solution:** Always maintain *some* kick, even if minimal 2-beat

## Breathing Adaptations

### 1. Bilateral Breathing (Mandatory)

**Why it's non-negotiable in open water:**
- Single-side breathing = blind spot = danger
- Can't see competitors, boats, buoys on one side
- Pulling stronger on breathing side = swimming in circles
- Asymmetry leads to drift (requires more sighting)

**How to implement:**

**Beginner:** 3-stroke breathing pattern (alternate sides)
**Intermediate:** 5-stroke pattern
**Advanced:** Variable (3-3-5-5 or adapt to conditions)

### 2. Sheltered-Side Breathing

**The tactic:** Breathe away from waves/wind

**Example scenarios:**
- Waves coming from left → breathe right
- Wind from right → breathe left
- Following motorboat → breathe away from exhaust

**How to practice:**
```
Drill: Directional Breathing
- Coach creates "waves" by splashing from one side
- Swimmer adapts breathing to opposite side
- Practice switching sides mid-swim
- Goal: Comfort breathing either direction
```

### 3. Mouth Management

**Pool habit:** Mouth often open when face in water

**Open water requirement:** Mouth CLOSED except when exhaling

**Why:**
- Waves will fill mouth with water if open
- Swallowing water → nausea, seasickness
- Close mouth = only open to exhale/inhale

**Breathing technique:**
1. Mouth closed when face submerged
2. Exhale through nose (or mouth) moments before turning to breathe
3. Turn head, mouth opens, inhale
4. Face returns to water, mouth closes immediately

**How to practice:**
```
Drill: Closed-Mouth Swimming
- 10 × 100 @ :20 rest
- Mouth completely closed except when breathing
- Exhale through nose only
- Builds habit for open water
```

### 4. Breathing Position in Rough Water

**Standard pool breathing:** Head rotates 90 degrees, mouth at surface

**Rough water adaptation:** Breathe farther back

**Technique:**
- Rotate head slightly more backward
- Position mouth under recovering arm/armpit
- Creates sheltered "pocket" of air
- Avoids wave directly hitting mouth

**Practice:**
- Film swimmers from side
- Check breathing position: is mouth forward or back?
- Adjust to more rearward position

## Sighting Integration

### The Two-Step: Sight + Breathe

**Incorrect:** Sight forward, then turn and breathe (2 separate head lifts)

**Correct:** Sight forward, breathe to side in ONE motion

**Technique:**
1. Begin normal breath cycle
2. Lift head slightly forward (sight)
3. Immediately rotate to side and breathe
4. Return to streamline

**Result:** One head lift accomplishes both tasks

### Minimizing Sighting Disruption

**Goal:** Sight without killing momentum

**Crocodile eyes technique:**
- Eyes just above waterline (minimal head lift)
- Maintain arm stroke rhythm
- See target, breathe to side, return to streamline
- <0.5 second total disruption

**Water polo technique:**
- Head and chest lift higher (more disruption)
- Use only when critical (turn buoys, mass start, rough water)
- Better visibility but slower

**When to use each:**
- Calm water, clear landmarks: Crocodile eyes
- Rough water, critical navigation: Water polo style
- First 400 yards of race: Water polo (frequent sighting)
- Mid-race, on-course: Crocodile eyes (minimize disruption)

## Symmetry Training

### Testing for Asymmetry

**Pool test:**
1. Swim 100 freestyle, eyes closed (lifeguard watching)
2. See if you hit lane line or cross into another lane
3. If yes → asymmetrical stroke

**Which side is stronger?**
- Usually: Breathing side pulls weaker, non-breathing side pulls stronger
- Result: Drift toward breathing side

### Fixing Asymmetry

**Drill 1: One-Side Days**
```
Monday practice: Breathe LEFT only (entire practice)
Wednesday practice: Breathe RIGHT only (entire practice)
Friday practice: Bilateral (alternating)
Goal: Equal comfort and speed both sides
```

**Drill 2: Stroke Count Matching**
```
10 × 50 @ :15 rest
- Odd 50s: Breathe left, count strokes
- Even 50s: Breathe right, count strokes
- Goal: Same stroke count both ways
If different → weaker side needs work
```

**Drill 3: Single-Arm Freestyle**
```
12 × 50 @ :15 rest
- 25 left arm only (right at side)
- 25 right arm only (left at side)
- Builds isolated strength on each side
- Evens out asymmetries
```

**Drill 4: Eyes-Closed Swimming**
```
4 × 100 @ :20 rest (with lifeguard supervision)
- Eyes closed entire 100
- Goal: Stay in lane, swim straight
- If you drift, your stroke is asymmetrical
- Repeat until you can swim straight
```

## Core Strength & Stability

### Why Core Matters More in Open Water

**Pool swimming:**
- Push off walls every 25-50 yards
- Walls reset body position
- Less reliance on core for stability

**Open water swimming:**
- No walls for hundreds/thousands of yards
- Continuous core engagement
- Waves try to roll/pitch your body
- Core = stability = efficiency

### Open Water Core Training

**In the pool:**

```
Set 1: No-Wall Push-Offs
10 × 100 @ 1:30
- Flip turn at wall, do NOT push off
- Resume stroking immediately
- Forces core-driven momentum
- Builds open water-specific strength

Set 2: Vertical Kicking
5 × 1:00 vertical kick (hands above water)
:30 rest between reps
- Builds core stability
- Mimics maintaining position in rough water

Set 3: Single-Arm + Core Rotation
8 × 75 @ :20 rest
- 25 left arm only
- 25 right arm only
- 25 full stroke
- Isolates core rotation
```

**On deck:**
- Planks (front, side)
- Russian twists
- Medicine ball rotations
- Stability ball work

## Wetsuit Considerations (When Legal)

### Stroke Modifications in Wetsuit

**Body position changes:**
- Hips ride higher (wetsuit buoyancy)
- Less kick needed
- More horizontal position

**Stroke adjustments:**
- Shorter, quicker arm recovery (wetsuit restricts shoulder)
- Focus on high elbow catch (shoulder mobility limited)
- Increase arm turnover slightly
- Reduce kick intensity (wetsuit provides lift)

**Training in wetsuit:**
- Practice at least 3-5 times before race
- Do race-pace sets to feel stroke changes
- Practice sighting in wetsuit (body position different)
- Test chafing points (neck, underarms)

## Adapting to Conditions

### Rough Water Stroke

**Modifications:**
- Shorter, quicker strokes (18-22 per 25)
- Higher stroke rate
- Stronger kick for stability
- Less glide, more continuous propulsion
- Breathe in wave troughs, not on crests

**Practice:**
```
Rough Water Simulation Set
8 × 200 @ :20 rest
- High stroke rate (20+ per 25)
- 4-beat or 6-beat kick
- Sight every 6 strokes
- Bilateral breathing
- Simulate instability by varying intensity
```

### Current Swimming

**With current:**
- Lengthen stroke (back to 14-16 per 25)
- Reduce turnover (let current help)
- Extend glide phase
- Minimal kick

**Against current:**
- Shorten stroke (18-20 per 25)
- Increase turnover
- Stronger kick
- Stay streamlined (minimize frontal drag)

## Mental Technique: Swimming Straight

### Landmarks Over Buoys

**Priority order for navigation:**
1. **Onshore landmarks** (buildings, trees, mountains)
   - More stable than buoys
   - Easier to see from distance
   - Less affected by waves
   
2. **Turn buoys** (close range only)
   - Can disappear in wave troughs
   - Use for final approach (last 200 yards)
   
3. **Other swimmers** (only if confident in their navigation)
   - Risky: they might be off course
   - Useful: If they're known good navigators

### The "Gun Sight" Method

**Technique:**
1. Identify two landmarks in line (e.g., building + tree)
2. Swim toward them keeping them aligned
3. If they separate, you've drifted
4. Correct course until they realign

**This method is more accurate than sighting single objects**

## Integration: The Complete Open Water Stroke

**Starting position (first 200-400 yards):**
- High stroke rate (20+ per 25)
- Strong kick
- Frequent sighting (every 4-6 strokes)
- Water polo style sighting
- Expect contact, maintain balance

**Settling phase (after initial chaos):**
- Moderate stroke rate (16-18 per 25)
- Moderate kick (4-beat)
- Less frequent sighting (every 8-10 strokes)
- Crocodile eyes sighting
- Find draft if possible

**Mid-race (steady state):**
- Adjust to conditions:
  - Rough water: Higher rate, stronger kick
  - Calm water: Longer stroke, less kick
  - Current: Adapt accordingly
- Bilateral breathing
- Minimal sighting (every 10-15 strokes if on course)

**Final 400 yards (sprint):**
- Increase stroke rate
- Increase kick intensity
- Sight frequently (every 4-6 strokes)
- Maintain form despite fatigue
- Finish strong

## Key Technique Principles

✅ **Symmetry = straight swimming** → fewer sightings, faster times  
✅ **Bilateral breathing is mandatory** → safety and navigation  
✅ **Stroke rate is variable** → adapt to conditions  
✅ **Middle pull is most effective** → focus power there  
✅ **Kick changes by situation** → practice shifting gears  
✅ **Core strength = stability** → train it systematically  
✅ **Sight efficiently** → integrate with breathing  
✅ **Practice in conditions** → pool can't replicate everything

## Common Technical Mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Single-side breathing only | Blind spot, swimming in circles | Mandatory bilateral from day 1 |
| Over-reaching on entry | Slipping in rough water, shoulder strain | Shorter entry, quick catch |
| Dropped hips (no kick) | Massive drag, slow swimming | Always maintain minimal kick |
| Mouth open underwater | Swallowing water, nausea | Close mouth, exhale before breathing |
| Excessive glide in rough water | Momentum lost to waves | Higher turnover, continuous propulsion |
| Lifting head too high to sight | Speed loss, stroke disruption | Crocodile eyes technique |
| Asymmetrical pull | Drifting off course | Single-arm drills, stroke count matching |

## Training Progression: 8-Week Technical Focus

**Weeks 1-2: Bilateral Breathing**
- All freestyle sets: 3-stroke breathing
- Goal: Equal comfort both sides

**Weeks 3-4: Sighting Integration**
- Add sighting every 10 strokes to all sets
- Goal: <2 seconds per 100 time penalty

**Weeks 5-6: Variable Stroke Rate**
- Stroke rate ladders (16-22 strokes per 25)
- Goal: Shift gears mid-swim

**Weeks 7-8: Full Integration**
- Race simulation sets
- Combine all skills: bilateral + sighting + variable rate
- Goal: Automatic execution under fatigue
