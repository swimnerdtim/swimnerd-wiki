# Open Water Hydration & Nutrition Strategy

**Source:** *Open Water Swimming* by Penny Lee Dean, EdD (13 world records, including English Channel 7:40)  
**Context:** Feeding protocols for OW swims & triathlons — what works, what fails, real-world examples  
**Audience:** Coaches, swimmers, triathletes preparing for races >30 minutes

---

## The Brutal Truth About Dehydration

> "Paula Newby-Fraser, 7-time Ironman world champion, collapsed 400 meters from the finish line in 1995. She didn't drink fluids during the 2.4-mile swim. No one did. She crawled on her knees for 20 minutes before finishing 4th. She returned in 1996, hydrated properly, and won."

**What happened:** Dehydration began in the swim, compounded on the bike, destroyed her on the run.

**The lesson:** Dehydration starts early. By the time you feel thirsty, you're already compromised.

---

## Dehydration: Early Warning Signs (Don't Ignore These)

### Phase 1: Early Dehydration (Still Recoverable)
- Dizziness
- Fatigue (more than normal race fatigue)
- Headache
- Thirst (by the time you feel this, you're behind)
- Loss of appetite
- Flushed skin
- Impatience, irritability
- Weakness

### Phase 2: Moderate Dehydration (Performance Failing)
- Blurred vision
- Difficulty hearing (ears feel "full")
- Difficulty swallowing
- Dry skin (despite being in water)
- Rapid pulse
- Shortness of breath beyond exertion level
- No saliva (mouth feels like cotton)
- Inability to stand easily post-swim

### Phase 3: Severe Dehydration (Medical Emergency)
- Confusion, disorientation
- Unable to answer simple questions
- Collapse
- Requires immediate medical intervention

**Critical:** Dehydration is one of the first symptoms of hypothermia. Cold water + dehydration = extremely dangerous combination.

## Hydration Protocol: Frequency & Volume

### For Swims/Races UNDER 2 Hours

**Every 10-15 minutes: 8 ounces (1 cup) of fluid**

**Conditions that require MORE frequent hydration (every 5-10 min):**
- Water temp >75°F (hot water)
- Water temp <60°F (cold water — yes, you still dehydrate!)
- Air temp >85°F
- High sun exposure
- High exertion (race pace vs. training pace)

**Fluid type:**
- **Under 2 hours:** Water is adequate
- **Over 2 hours:** Electrolyte replacement required (see below)

### For Swims/Races OVER 2 Hours

**Every 10-15 minutes: 8 ounces electrolyte drink**

**Why electrolytes matter:**
- Sweat contains sodium, potassium, magnesium (yes, you sweat in water)
- Water alone dilutes blood sodium levels (hyponatremia risk)
- Electrolytes maintain muscle function, nerve signals, hydration balance

**Types of electrolyte drinks:**
- Sports drinks: Gatorade, Powerade (6-8% carb solution)
- Purpose-built: ERG (mentioned by Dean), Tailwind, Scratch Labs
- Homemade: Water + pinch salt + honey/sugar

## Nutrition Strategy: What to Eat & When

### Swims Under 1 Hour
**Pre-swim only** (2-3 hours before)
- Light carbs: banana, toast with honey, oatmeal
- Moderate protein: yogurt, eggs
- Hydrate fully (16-20 oz water)

**During swim:** Water only (no food needed)

### Swims 1-2 Hours
**Pre-swim** (same as above)

**During swim (optional, every 30 min):**
- Gels (if tolerated)
- Honey or maple syrup (small squeeze)
- Diluted sports drink with carbs

### Swims 2+ Hours (Marathon Swims)

**Pre-swim (3-4 hours before):**
- Full meal: pasta, rice, potatoes (complex carbs)
- Moderate protein: chicken, fish
- Avoid: heavy fats, dairy (if sensitive), high fiber (GI distress)

**During swim (every 20-30 minutes):**

**Solid foods** (tested in training, never try new food in race)
- Bananas
- Peeled oranges (natural sugar + hydration)
- Cookies (simple carbs, easy to digest)
- Energy bars (cut into small pieces)
- Penny Lee Dean mentions: **"Chicken works for some swimmers"** (protein for ultra-distance)

**Liquid foods** (easier to consume while swimming)
- Honey (straight from squeeze bottle)
- Maple syrup
- Liquid carb drinks (Carbo-Pro, maltodextrin)
- Electrolyte drinks (Gatorade, ERG, etc.)

**Feeding mechanics:**
1. Swimmer stops (treads water or floats)
2. Paddler/boat hands food in cup or bottle
3. Swimmer eats/drinks quickly (30-60 seconds max)
4. Swimmer resumes

**Key rule:** NEVER try new foods during a race. Test everything in training 3-5 times minimum.

## The Support Crew's Role (Often Overlooked)

### Crew Members Dehydrate Too

**The danger:**
- Paddlers, boat crew, coaches sit in sun for hours
- Focused on swimmer, forget to drink themselves
- Dehydrated crew = poor decision-making = swimmer in danger

**Protocol:**
- Crew drinks on same schedule as swimmer (every 10-15 min)
- **NO alcohol during swim** (causes dehydration, impairs judgment)
- Sunscreen, hats, sunglasses mandatory
- Cool fluids preferred (easier to digest)

**Penny Lee Dean's rule:** "If the support crew isn't operating at 100%, the swimmer is in danger."

## Temperature-Specific Hydration Adjustments

### Cold Water (<66°F / 19°C)

**Common myth:** "I'm cold, so I'm not dehydrated."

**Reality:**
- Cold water triggers diuresis (increased urination)
- Body shunts blood from extremities to core (fluid loss)
- Vasoconstriction makes you FEEL like you don't need fluids
- **You're still losing fluids, possibly faster than in warm water**

**Protocol:**
- Warm fluids (85-90°F) during feed
- Easier to digest, helps maintain core temp
- Every 10 min in water <60°F

### Warm Water (>75°F / 24°C)

**Problem:**
- Increased sweat rate
- Core temp rising
- Higher exertion to maintain pace

**Protocol:**
- Cool fluids (easier to drink, helps cool core)
- Every 5-10 min in water >78°F
- Consider electrolyte-enhanced water even for swims <2 hours

## Case Study: John York — Hypothermia from Dehydration

**Context:** 1977 Catalina Channel double crossing attempt (42 miles)

**What happened:**
- First leg: 8:31 (world record), but vomited throughout (seasickness)
- Rested 3 min, ate chicken + cookies, drank ERG
- Second leg: Continued vomiting, got colder
- Stroke rate dropped from 88 SPM → 66 SPM
- Swam 8 strokes without breathing
- Pulled unconscious from water
- Body temp: 88°F (severe hypothermia)
- Hospitalized 2 days, nearly died

**Root cause:**
- Seasickness prevented hydration/nutrition
- Dehydration accelerated hypothermia
- Crew didn't pull him soon enough (too focused on goal)

**1978 return:**
- Conquered the channel (no seasickness)
- Proper hydration protocol
- Broke overall double crossing record by 3 hours

**Lesson:** Dehydration + cold water = life-threatening. No goal is worth dying for.

## Feeding Logistics: How to Deliver Food/Fluids

### Equipment Needed

**For paddler-supported swims:**
- Insulated cooler (keeps fluids cool/warm as needed)
- Squeeze bottles (easier to hand off than cups)
- Pre-portioned food in baggies/containers
- Bungee cords to secure cooler to board/kayak
- Backup supplies (swimmer may reject certain foods mid-race)

**For boat-supported swims:**
- Larger cooler on boat
- Extendable pole or net to hand food (faster than swimmer swimming to boat)
- Labeled containers (1st feed, 2nd feed, etc.)
- Backup bottles/cups (things fall in water)

### Feeding Mechanics (Practice This!)

**Bad method:**
1. Swimmer stops
2. Paddler fumbles with cooler
3. Swimmer waits 2-3 minutes (getting cold, losing rhythm)
4. Food finally delivered
5. Paddler falls behind, struggles to catch up

**Good method (practiced in training):**
1. Swimmer signals 50 yards before feed (or scheduled time)
2. Paddler prepares bottle/food while swimmer approaches
3. Swimmer stops, treads water
4. Food handed immediately (10 seconds)
5. Swimmer eats/drinks (30-60 seconds)
6. Swimmer resumes, paddler stows gear while keeping pace

**Key:** Paddler must be able to keep up even with feeding delays. If paddler can't match swimmer's pace, need faster paddler or boat support.

## Common Nutrition/Hydration Mistakes

### Mistake #1: "I'll drink when I feel thirsty"
**Wrong.** Thirst = already 1-2% dehydrated. Performance drops at 2% dehydration.

### Mistake #2: "Water is enough for any swim"
**Wrong.** Swims >2 hours require electrolytes. Water-only = hyponatremia risk.

### Mistake #3: "I don't need to hydrate in cold water"
**Wrong.** Cold water actually increases fluid loss (diuresis). Warm fluids every 10-15 min.

### Mistake #4: "I'll try this new energy gel during the race"
**Wrong.** NEVER try new foods/drinks in competition. GI distress = DNF.

### Mistake #5: "The crew doesn't need to drink — they're not swimming"
**Wrong.** Dehydrated crew = poor decisions = danger to swimmer.

### Mistake #6: "I can skip feeding to stay ahead"
**Wrong.** Short-term gain, catastrophic long-term failure. See Paula Newby-Fraser story.

## Sample Feeding Schedule: 10K Open Water Race (2 hours)

**Pre-race (2-3 hours before start):**
- Breakfast: Oatmeal + banana + honey
- Hydrate: 16-20 oz water
- 30 min before start: 8 oz sports drink

**During race:**
- **15 min:** 8 oz electrolyte drink
- **30 min:** 8 oz electrolyte drink
- **45 min:** 8 oz electrolyte drink + energy gel (if tolerated)
- **60 min:** 8 oz electrolyte drink
- **75 min:** 8 oz electrolyte drink
- **90 min:** 8 oz electrolyte drink + gel or banana piece
- **105 min:** 8 oz electrolyte drink
- **Finish:** Immediate hydration + carbs (banana, bar, drink)

**Post-race:**
- Continue hydrating (don't stop just because race is over)
- 16-20 oz fluid within 30 min
- Light meal within 1 hour (carbs + protein)

## Sample Feeding Schedule: English Channel (10-14 hours)

**Pre-swim (3-4 hours before):**
- Full meal: Pasta, chicken, bread
- Hydrate: 20-24 oz water

**During swim (every 20-30 minutes):**

**Alternating pattern:**
- **Feed 1 (0:20):** Warm electrolyte drink (8 oz) + honey
- **Feed 2 (0:40):** Banana + warm drink
- **Feed 3 (1:00):** Warm drink + cookies
- **Feed 4 (1:20):** Orange slices + warm drink
- **Feed 5 (1:40):** Warm drink + energy bar piece
- **Feed 6 (2:00):** Chicken piece + warm drink (yes, really — protein for ultra-distance)
- **Repeat cycle**

**Adjust based on:**
- Stomach tolerance (if nauseous, liquids only)
- Water temp (colder = warmer fluids)
- Swimmer preference (some want more solid food, some prefer all liquid)

**Final 2 hours:**
- Increase frequency to every 15 min (fatigue + cold = need more support)
- Lighter foods (stomach may be sensitive)
- Warm sweet drinks (psychological boost + energy)

## Hydration for Triathletes: Swim Leg Specifics

### The Unique Challenge

**Problem:**
- Can't feed during most tri swims (too short, not allowed)
- Dehydration begins in swim
- Compounds on bike/run
- Many triathletes skip swim hydration entirely (huge mistake)

### Solution: Pre-Race Hydration

**3-4 hours before start:**
- Full meal (race-day tested)
- 20-24 oz water

**2 hours before:**
- 16 oz sports drink

**1 hour before:**
- 8-12 oz water (sip, don't chug)

**30 min before:**
- Last 8 oz sports drink
- **Stop drinking 20 min before start** (avoid needing to pee during swim)

**Immediately post-swim (T1 transition):**
- 8-12 oz sports drink before getting on bike
- Sets you up for bike leg hydration

**On bike:**
- Every 10-15 min: 8 oz (Paula's mistake was not drinking on bike after skipping swim hydration)

- ☐ Backup supplies (extra bottles, cups, food)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Hydrate BEFORE you feel thirsty** — Thirst = already dehydrated
2. **Every 10-15 min: 8 oz fluid** — Non-negotiable for swims >30 min
3. **After 2 hours: Electrolytes required** — Water alone = hyponatremia risk
4. **Cold water ≠ no dehydration** — Actually increases fluid loss
5. **Test all foods 3-5x in training** — NEVER try new food on race day
6. **Support crew hydrates too** — Dehydrated crew = danger to swimmer
7. **Feeding stops should be <60 seconds** — Practice logistics in training

**Paula Newby-Fraser's lesson:** Skipping hydration in the swim cost her an 8th consecutive Ironman title. Don't let dehydration steal your race.
