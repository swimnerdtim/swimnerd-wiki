# Open Water Skills for Pool Coaches

**Source:** *Open Water Swimming Manual* by Lynne Cox  
**Context:** Technical skills pool coaches need to teach swimmers transitioning to open water  
**Audience:** Pool coaches preparing athletes for triathlons, open water events, or summer training

---

## Why Pool Coaches Need Open Water Skills

**The reality:**
- 2.5 million+ triathletes in the U.S. (2010 data)
- 900+ competitive open water swims annually across U.S.
- Olympic sport since 2008
- Many college/university coaches now incorporate open water in winter training

**The problem:**
- Pool swimming ≠ open water swimming
- Athletes entering open water unprepared = high-risk situation
- Lack of spatial awareness, navigation, safety protocols

**Your role:**
- Teach transitional skills in the pool
- Prepare athletes for environment without lane lines, walls, or clear visibility
- Ensure safety competence before open water entry

## Technical Skills: What Changes in Open Water

### 1. Breathing Pattern

**Pool reality:**
- Most swimmers breathe to one side only
- Perfectly acceptable in pool with lane lines

**Open water problem:**
- **Blind on one side = dangerous**
- Can't see boats, other swimmers, hazards
- Will swim in circles (pulling stronger on breathing side)
- No spatial awareness

**Solution: Bilateral Breathing**

**How to teach:**
1. Start with 3-stroke breathing pattern (breathe every 3rd stroke = alternating sides)
2. Practice during warm-up sets (low intensity)
3. Progress to 5-stroke or 7-stroke patterns for advanced swimmers
4. Make it mandatory for all freestyle sets

**Drill: Blind Side Awareness**
- Swimmer breathes right side only for 50 yards
- Coach stands on left side of pool
- Swimmer must identify what coach is holding up (fingers, color)
- Proves they can't see their blind side
- **Then repeat with bilateral breathing — problem solved**

---

### 2. Sighting (Navigating Without Lane Lines)

**The skill:**
- Lifting head forward mid-stroke to see where you're going
- Without sighting, swimmers drift off course (up to 50+ yards in a mile)

**How to teach in pool:**

**Drill 1: Crocodile Eyes**
- Swim freestyle, lift eyes just above waterline (like a crocodile)
- Look forward, spot target (wall clock, flag, cone)
- Minimize disruption to stroke
- Breathe normally to side after sighting
- Practice every 4-6 strokes

**Drill 2: Water Polo Style**
- Lift head higher (chest out of water)
- Spot target, then return to streamline
- More disruptive but useful in choppy water
- Practice every 10 strokes initially

**Drill 3: Straight Line Swimming**
- Remove lane lines (or swim diagonal across pool)
- Place target cone at far end
- Swimmer must swim straight without lane line guidance
- Coach watches from side — are they drifting?

**Frequency:**
- Sight every 6-10 strokes in open water
- In rough conditions or near finish, every 3-4 strokes

---

### 3. Head Position in Water

**Pool habit:**
- Head down, looking at bottom (good for streamline)

**Open water adjustment:**
- Need to see forward periodically
- Balance streamline with awareness

**Teaching cue:**
> "Swimming is like flying an airplane. You can't fly looking down at your instruments all the time. You need to look forward to see where you're headed."  
> — Lynne Cox

**How to practice:**
- Alternate: 4 strokes head down → 1 stroke sight forward
- Don't drop hips when lifting head (maintain streamline)
- Quick look, back to normal position

---

### 4. Feeling the Water (Proprioception)

**Why it matters:**
- Open water has currents, chop, swells
- Swimmers need to FEEL water movement, not just see it
- Adjustments happen by feel, not vision

**Pool drill: Blind Swimming**
- Close eyes for 25-50 yards
- Focus on feeling water pressure on hands
- Feel body position, balance, rotation
- Develops kinesthetic awareness

**Advanced: Varied Tempo**
- Swim through turbulence created by other swimmers
- Practice maintaining stroke in disrupted water
- Simulates open water conditions

---

### 5. Entry & Exit (Shore/Surf Skills)

**Pool swimmers are not prepared for:**
- Walking into water (uneven bottom, rocks, marine life)
- Body surfing out through small waves
- Timing entry between wave sets
- Exiting through shore break

**The Stingray Shuffle (coastal safety):**
- Don't lift feet when walking in shallow water
- Shuffle feet along bottom
- Pushes stingrays away (vs. stepping on them)
- Avoids stingray barbs

**Shore Break Safety:**
- **Always look back before entering/exiting surf**
- Watch for dark blue "bump" (incoming wave)
- Time entry between sets
- Dive under small waves, don't let them crash on you

**How to prepare (pool simulation):**
- Practice running entries (start on deck, run to pool, dive in)
- Quick transitions (simulates beach start)
- Can't fully replicate surf, but builds confidence

## Open Water-Specific Pool Sets

### Set 1: Bilateral Breathing Focus
```
Warm-up: 400 easy, alternating 25 right-side breathing / 25 left-side breathing

Main:
8 × 100 freestyle on 1:30
  - #1-2: Breathe every 3 (bilateral)
  - #3-4: Breathe every 5
  - #5-6: Breathe every 3
  - #7-8: Breathe every 7 (advanced)

Goal: Comfortable breathing both sides at race pace
```

### Set 2: Sighting + Navigation
```
4 × 200 freestyle on 3:00
  - Sight forward every 6 strokes (use wall clock as target)
  - Focus on minimal head lift (crocodile eyes)
  - Maintain streamline

4 × 50 on 1:00
  - Water polo style sighting every 4 strokes
  - Higher head lift, practice spotting object across pool
```

### Set 3: Open Water Pace Simulation
```
Continuous swim (no walls for rest):
20-30 minutes non-stop freestyle

Rules:
  - Flip turns only (no stopping)
  - Bilateral breathing mandatory
  - Sight every 50 yards (spot clock)
  - Focus on sustainable pace (not sprinting)

Simulates: Long-distance open water pacing
```

### Set 4: Blind Swimming (Proprioception)
```
8 × 25 on :45
  - Eyes closed entire 25
  - Focus on feel of water
  - Coach watches — are they swimming straight?

Cool-down: 200 easy with eyes open
```

## What Pool Coaches MUST Teach Before Open Water

**Minimum competencies:**

**1. Safety awareness**
- Never swim alone
- Buddy system
- Distress signals (vertical position, waving arm)
- Hypothermia signs (shivering, confusion)

**2. Environmental knowledge**
- Check water temp, weather, currents BEFORE swimming
- Fog = dangerous (don't swim)
- Boat traffic = wear bright cap
- Shore break = look back before entering/exiting

**3. Technical skills**
- Bilateral breathing (non-negotiable)
- Sighting every 6-10 strokes
- Swimming straight without lane lines
- Maintaining pace without walls/intervals

**4. Equipment**
- Bright swim cap (orange, yellow, red — NOT black or blue)
- Goggles that fit (test before race day)
- Wetsuit optional (check race rules)

**5. Support crew basics**
- Know what a kayaker/support boat does
- How to communicate mid-swim
- When to ask for help

## Common Pool Swimmer Mistakes in Open Water

**Mistake 1: Breathing to one side only**
- **Result:** Swims in circles, blind to hazards
- **Fix:** Bilateral breathing drills mandatory

**Mistake 2: Never looking forward**
- **Result:** Swims 50+ yards off course, wastes energy
- **Fix:** Sighting drills, straight-line swimming without lane lines

**Mistake 3: Sprinting the start**
- **Result:** Exhausted within 5 minutes, can't sustain pace
- **Fix:** Practice continuous swims (20-30 min), teach pacing

**Mistake 4: Panic in open water**
- **Result:** Can't see bottom, no walls, gets disoriented
- **Fix:** Gradual progression (pool → calm lake → ocean)

**Mistake 5: Poor equipment choices**
- **Result:** Dark cap (invisible), leaky goggles, wrong wetsuit
- **Fix:** Test all equipment in pool during training

## Preparing Athletes: The Progression

**Step 1: Pool Mastery (3-6 months)**
- Bilateral breathing comfortable
- Can sight without breaking stroke
- Swims straight without lane lines
- 30+ min continuous swim at target pace

**Step 2: Controlled Open Water (2-4 sessions)**
- Calm lake or protected cove
- 100-300 yards from shore
- Kayak support
- Practice sighting, bilateral breathing, navigation

**Step 3: Race Simulation (1-2 sessions)**
- Full distance in open water
- Support crew in place
- Practice mass start (if relevant)
- Test nutrition, pacing, equipment

**Step 4: Race Day**
- Confident, competent, prepared
- Knows the environment
- Has safety plan
- Enjoys the experience

## Coach's Role: Gatekeeping

**You decide when an athlete is ready for open water.**

**NOT ready if:**
- Can't bilateral breathe comfortably
- Swims crooked without lane lines
- Panics without visual references
- Doesn't understand safety protocols
- Hasn't trained for distance/duration

**Ready if:**
- Bilateral breathing is second nature
- Can sight and navigate
- Comfortable with distance
- Understands risks and safety
- Has practiced in pool simulations

**Your responsibility:** Don't send unprepared athletes into open water. It's dangerous.

## Actionable Takeaways

✅ **Teach bilateral breathing** — Make it mandatory for all freestyle sets  
✅ **Practice sighting in pool** — Crocodile eyes drill, straight-line swimming  
✅ **No lane lines = navigation skills** — Remove visual cues during training  
✅ **Continuous swims build endurance** — 20-30 min non-stop prepares for open water pacing  
✅ **Safety first** — Educate on hazards, never-swim-alone rule, distress signals  
✅ **Test equipment in pool** — Bright caps, fitting goggles, wetsuits (if used)  
✅ **Gradual progression** — Pool mastery → calm open water → race conditions  
✅ **You are the gatekeeper** — Only send prepared athletes into open water
