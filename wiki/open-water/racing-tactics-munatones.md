# Open Water Racing Tactics & Strategy

**Source:** *Open Water Swimming* by Steven Munatones (2011)  
**Context:** Tactical racing strategies from mass starts to finish sprints  
**Audience:** Coaches preparing athletes for competitive open water events (5K, 10K, marathon)

---

## Mass Start Positioning

### Pre-Start Strategy

**Know your strength:**
- **Strong starter?** → Front row, center or slightly outside
- **Steady pacer?** → 2nd-3rd row, avoid initial chaos
- **Slower starter?** → Outside position, clean water priority

**Key principles:**
1. Position yourself 200-400 yards BEFORE the start based on your race plan
2. Identify the strongest swimmers — decide if you'll draft them or avoid contact
3. Scout the first turn buoy location during warm-up
4. Know the optimal line from start to first buoy

### First 200 Yards: Survival Mode

**What happens:**
- Maximum chaos and contact
- Swimmers fighting for position
- Arms, legs, goggles at risk
- Adrenaline overload

**How to handle it:**

**Aggressive approach:**
- Sprint hard first 50-100 yards
- Establish position in lead pack
- Accept contact as part of racing
- Keep head up, sight frequently

**Conservative approach:**
- Start wide or slightly back
- Let the chaos settle (30-60 seconds)
- Find clean water, then accelerate
- Bridge to the pack once clear

**Pro tip:** Protect your goggles with one hand during the first 10-15 seconds if you're in the scrum.

## Pack Swimming & Drafting

### The Physics of Drafting

**Energy savings:** 10-20% reduction in effort when drafting properly

**Three drafting positions:**

1. **Directly behind (ideal):** Maximum draft, swim in swimmer's "wake"
   - Position: Fingertips 6-12 inches from feet
   - Risk: Can't see ahead, must trust leader's navigation
   
2. **Hip position (tactical):** Moderate draft, better visibility
   - Position: Head near swimmer's hip
   - Benefit: Can see turn buoys and monitor the pack
   
3. **Shoulder position (offensive):** Minimal draft, maximum visibility
   - Position: Shoulder-to-shoulder
   - Use: When preparing to pass or navigate independently

### Pack Dynamics

**Understanding pack shapes:**

- **Single-file line:** Occurs in narrow channels or strong currents
- **Wide spread:** Happens when swimmers take different navigational lines
- **Tight pack:** Most common in championship racing (3-wide, 4-wide, 5-wide)

**Moving within the pack:**

**To advance position:**
- Speed up 200-400 yards before a turn buoy
- Move wide, then cut inside at the turn
- Use a surge (10-15 hard strokes) to break free
- Time your move when others are fatigued

**To escape the middle of a pack:**
- **Technique 1:** Swim over competitor's legs (corkscrew move)
- **Technique 2:** Slow down 3-4 strokes, let them pass, find gap
- **Technique 3:** Sprint hard 50-100 yards, shoot the gap

**Practice in pool:**
- Swim 3-wide in one lane during practice
- Practice "corkscrew" moves (roll over lane lines near wall)
- Do pack simulation: 4-6 swimmers in close proximity for 200-400 yards

## Turn Buoy Tactics

### The Critical Moment

**Turn buoys = opportunity to gain/lose significant position**

Think of turn buoys like the wall in pool swimming — the "fifth stroke"

### Setting Up the Turn (200 Yards Out)

**Ideal inside position:**
- Shortest distance around buoy
- Highest risk of contact
- Most competitive position

**When to take inside:**
- You're strong out of turns
- Next leg favors your position (e.g., left-shoulder turn followed by right-shoulder turn)
- You can handle contact/chaos

**When to take outside:**
- Next turn is in opposite direction (inside becomes outside)
- Angle is sharp (>120 degrees) — outside may be faster line
- You want cleaner water coming out of turn

### Executing the Turn

**Standard turn:**
1. Sight the buoy 25-30 yards out
2. Maintain speed into the turn (don't slow down)
3. Tight turn: use twisting motion (freestyle + backstroke arm combo)
4. Protect your head with one arm if necessary
5. Accelerate OUT of the turn (10-15 hard strokes)

**Advanced: Underwater turn (when boxed in):**
1. Approach buoy, take deep breath
2. Dive under 3-4 feet
3. Swim around buoy rope underwater (DO NOT pull on it — penalty)
4. Twist body around the rope
5. Surface on the other side, resume stroke

**Practice in pool:**
- Set up buoy/kickboard in deep end
- Practice tight turns around it
- Combine freestyle + backstroke strokes to pivot quickly
- Time how fast you can execute vs. wide turn

## Navigation & Sighting

### Minimize Head Lifts, Maximize Efficiency

**Goal:** Sight as infrequently as possible while staying on course

**Sighting frequency:**
- **Calm water, straight line:** Every 10-15 strokes
- **Rough water or poor visibility:** Every 6-8 strokes
- **Near turn buoys:** Every 3-5 strokes
- **First 400 yards (finding position):** Constantly (every 4-6 strokes)

### Two Sighting Techniques

**1. Crocodile Eyes (minimal disruption)**
- Lift eyes just above waterline
- See forward while maintaining stroke rhythm
- Breathe normally to side after sighting
- Best for: Calm water, visible landmarks

**2. Water Polo Style (maximum visibility)**
- Lift head and chest out of water
- Spot target, then return to streamline
- More disruptive but necessary in rough water
- Best for: Choppy conditions, critical navigation moments

### Sighting Landmarks

**Prioritize permanent landmarks over buoys:**

**On shore:**
- Buildings, trees, towers, mountains
- Less affected by waves/movement
- Easier to spot from distance

**In water:**
- Turn buoys (close range only)
- Escort boats (can move!)
- Other swimmers (only if you know they navigate well)

**Pro tactic:**
- Use two landmarks in line (like a gun sight)
- If they separate, you've drifted off course

## Feeding Tactics (Middle & Marathon Distance)

### Four-Step Feeding Process

**1. SEEK AND SPOT**
- Lift head 50-75 yards from feeding station
- Locate your coach/feeder
- Account for swells, chop, position in pack

**2. REACH AND ROLL**
- Reach for bottle/cup with one arm
- Roll to side slightly for stability
- Maintain kick to stay in position

**3. GULP AND GO**
- Drink/consume quickly (3-5 seconds max)
- Don't stop swimming — slow to survival stroke
- Keep kicking to maintain position

**4. TOSS AND TURN**
- Discard bottle/cup cleanly
- Immediately return to race pace
- Sight ahead, resume full stroke

### Feeding Strategy

**Frequency:**
- **10K race:** 2-3 feeds (at 3K, 6K, 8K marks typically)
- **25K race:** 5-8 feeds (every 3-4K)
- **Marathon swim:** Every 20-30 minutes

**What to consume:**
- Gels, sports drinks, water
- Keep it simple — stick with what works in training
- Never experiment on race day

**Practice in pool:**
- Set up feeding station at wall
- Practice reaching, drinking, turning without stopping
- Time yourself — aim for <5 seconds from reach to full-speed swim

## Finish Tactics

### The Last 400 Yards

**Mental preparation:**
- Start thinking about finish at 600 yards out
- Identify your competitors' positions
- Assess your energy reserves

**At 400 yards:**
- Increase stroke rate slightly
- Check position of nearest competitors
- Sight frequently (every 4-6 strokes)

**At 200 yards:**
- Begin building speed gradually
- Move to outside if you have a strong finish
- If drafting, prepare to swing wide and sprint

### The Sprint (Final 100-200 Yards)

**When to initiate:**
- **Strong finisher:** Start at 200 yards out
- **Moderate finisher:** Start at 100 yards out
- **Weak finisher:** Maintain pace, don't blow up

**Sprint technique changes:**
- Increase arm turnover (higher stroke rate)
- Increase kick significantly (6-beat or more)
- Sight every 2-3 strokes
- Maintain streamline despite fatigue

**Passing on the finish:**
- Swing wide to go around
- Use clean water advantage
- Time your pass for final 50 yards
- Don't celebrate until you've touched the finish

### Touch Protocol

**Different finish types:**

**Beach finish:**
- Swim until hands touch sand
- Stand and run through finish line
- Don't stand too early (swimming is faster in 2+ feet of water)

**Floating finish:**
- Touch the finish structure with your hand
- Slap it clearly — referees must see contact
- Don't slow down until after touch

## Race-Day Conditions Adaptations

### Rough Water

**Stroke modifications:**
- Shorter, quicker strokes
- Higher elbow position for stability
- More bilateral breathing (switch sides based on wave direction)
- Stronger kick for balance

**Tactical adjustments:**
- Draft more aggressively (energy savings more valuable)
- Sight more frequently
- Breathe on sheltered side (away from waves)

### Current Swimming

**With current:**
- Lengthen stroke, reduce turnover
- Sight less frequently
- Use current to extend glide phase

**Against current:**
- Shorten stroke, increase turnover
- Stay streamlined, minimize frontal resistance
- Consider swimming closer to shore (weaker current)

### Sun Glare

**Problem:** Can't see turn buoys or landmarks

**Solutions:**
- Tinted goggles (test in training first!)
- Sight to side instead of straight ahead
- Use onshore landmarks instead of buoys
- Draft someone who navigates well

## Advanced Tactics: The Chess Game

### Think Several Moves Ahead

**Like chess, anticipate opponents' moves:**

1. **Identify the navigators** — Who sights frequently and swims straight?
2. **Identify the sprinters** — Who will you need to drop before the finish?
3. **Identify the drafters** — Who is sitting on your feet conserving energy?

### Tactical Maneuvers

**Breaking a draft:**
- Sudden surge for 10-15 strokes
- Sharp angle change (zig-zag)
- Slow down abruptly, then accelerate after they pass

**Forcing a mistake:**
- Take an aggressive inside line at turn buoy
- Force opponent wide or into contact
- Exit turn with better position

**The false surge:**
- Accelerate hard for 50 yards mid-race
- Drop back to normal pace
- Opponent may blow up trying to match
- Repeat 200 yards later when they're recovering

## Practice Integration

### Pool Sets for Open Water Racing

**Pack simulation:**
- 8 × 200 free, 3-4 swimmers per lane
- Intentional contact, position changes
- Practice sighting, surging, recovering

**Turn buoy practice:**
- Place kickboard/buoy in deep end
- 10 × 50: swim to buoy, tight turn, sprint back
- Focus on acceleration out of turn

**Sighting intervals:**
- 10 × 100 free, sight every 6 strokes (head-up freestyle)
- Time difference vs. normal 100 pace
- Goal: <2 seconds slower per 100

**Drafting practice:**
- Pairs: 6 × 400, swap lead every 100
- Maintain 6-12 inch gap when drafting
- Feel the energy savings

## Mental Preparation

### Pre-Race Visualization

**Walk through the race mentally:**
1. Start positioning and first 200 yards
2. Settling into pace, finding draft
3. Each turn buoy approach and execution
4. Feeding stations (if applicable)
5. Final 400 yards and sprint

**Prepare for adversity:**
- "What if I get hit in the face?" → Stay calm, regroup
- "What if I lose my pack?" → Bridge back or find new draft
- "What if I go off course?" → Stop, sight, correct, resume

## Key Takeaways

✅ **Position yourself strategically from the start** — know your strengths  
✅ **Draft intelligently** — save 10-20% energy for the finish  
✅ **Turn buoys are opportunities** — set up 200 yards in advance  
✅ **Sight efficiently** — minimize head lifts, use landmarks  
✅ **Practice feeding** — 5 seconds or less from reach to full speed  
✅ **Finish strong** — plan your sprint, execute with conviction  
✅ **Think like a chess player** — anticipate moves several steps ahead
