# Open Water Technique Adaptations

**Source:** *Open Water Swimming* by Penny Lee Dean, EdD (13 world records, English Channel 7:40 1978)  
**Context:** Stroke modifications for pool swimmers transitioning to open water  
**Audience:** Coaches preparing athletes for triathlons, OW races, or marathon swims

---

## Core Principle: Efficiency Over Pool Form

> "Open water swimming requires stroke adaptations pool coaches never teach. The difference between a 10-minute mile and a 12-minute mile in open water often comes down to technique, not fitness."  
> — Penny Lee Dean

**The reality:**
- No walls = continuous swimming requires sustainable mechanics
- No lane lines = need for bilateral awareness and sighting
- Conditions change = stroke must adapt to waves, chop, currents
- Equipment differs = goggles fog, suits chafe, cold affects technique

---

## Critical Skill #1: Bilateral Breathing

### Why It Matters (Life-and-Death)

**Pool swimmers breathe to one side only:**
- Comfortable
- Established habit
- Works perfectly in lanes

**Open water problem:**
- **Blind on one side = DANGEROUS**
- Can't see boats, kayaks, other swimmers
- Will swim in circles (stronger pull on breathing side)
- Miss navigation landmarks
- Get hit by support craft

**Solution:** MANDATE bilateral breathing for ALL open water preparation

### How to Teach

**Week 1-2: Introduction (Low Intensity)**
- Warm-up only: 400-800 breathing every 3rd stroke (alternating sides)
- Focus: comfort, not speed
- Expect complaints — this is normal

**Week 3-4: Integration**
- Add to aerobic sets: 10 x 100 @ moderate pace, breathe every 3
- Drill: 25 right-side only / 25 left-side only / 50 bilateral (repeat 4x)
- Goal: Equal comfort both sides

**Week 5+: Racing Pattern**
- All freestyle = bilateral (except sprints)
- Advanced: 5-stroke or 7-stroke breathing patterns
- Race simulation: Breathe opposite side on turns (mimics OW sighting check)

**Drill: Blind Side Awareness Test**
1. Swimmer breathes right-side only for 50
2. Coach stands on left side of pool holding up 1-5 fingers
3. Swimmer finishes — "How many fingers?"
4. Swimmer can't answer (blind side!)
5. Repeat with bilateral breathing — problem solved

## Critical Skill #2: Sighting (Navigation)

### The Problem

**In the pool:**
- Lane lines guide you
- Black line on bottom
- Walls every 25-50 yards
- You never look where you're going

**In open water:**
- NO visual guidance
- Drift happens instantly
- Swimming 50 yards off-course in a 1-mile swim = 200+ extra yards
- **Can mean 2-4 minutes lost to a competitor who sights properly**

### Sighting Technique #1: "Crocodile Eyes"

**When to use:** Calm to moderate conditions

**How it works:**
1. Mid-stroke, as lead hand enters water
2. Lift eyes ONLY (not whole head) — like crocodile surfacing
3. Eyes just break waterline
4. Spot target (buoy, landmark, boat)
5. Return to normal stroke
6. Breathe to side on NEXT stroke (not same stroke as sighting)

**Training progression:**
- **Week 1:** Sight every 10 strokes (clock, pool sign, cone on deck)
- **Week 2:** Sight every 6 strokes
- **Week 3-4:** Sight every 4 strokes (race frequency)
- **Week 5+:** Integrate with bilateral breathing: sight-stroke-breathe-stroke pattern

**Key coaching cue:**
- "Eyes up, NOT head up"
- "You're a submarine periscope, not a breaststroker"
- Minimize stroke disruption — hips should NOT drop

### Sighting Technique #2: "Water Polo Head"

**When to use:** Choppy water, large swells, crowded starts

**How it works:**
1. More aggressive head lift
2. Chest comes partially out of water
3. Clear view over waves/swimmers
4. More disruptive to stroke (use sparingly)
5. Return to streamline quickly

**When to teach:**
- After athlete masters crocodile eyes (4-6 weeks)
- Necessary skill for triathlon mass starts
- Useful in ocean swells

**Drill: Sighting + Breathing Combo**
- 6 x 50 freestyle
- Sight every 4 strokes (crocodile eyes)
- Breathe bilaterally between sightings
- Pattern: **sight-stroke-breathe-stroke** (repeat)
- Call out target each time (coach holds up colors/numbers)

## Critical Skill #3: Open Water Body Position

### The Difference

**Pool swimming:**
- High hips via wall push-offs
- Tight streamline off every wall
- Core engagement resets every 25-50

**Open water swimming:**
- No wall push-offs = hips sink if core weak
- Continuous swimming for 15-60+ minutes
- Fatigue causes form breakdown

### Adaptation: Sustainable Core Engagement

**The fix:**
- Slightly higher head position than pool (aids sighting, reduces neck strain)
- More active kick to maintain hip height (vs. minimal kick in distance pool swimming)
- Core braced continuously (no "rest" like in pool turns)

**Drills:**

**1. Continuous 400s (No Walls)**
- 4 x 400 freestyle @ moderate pace
- NO touching walls — open turns only (flip turn but don't push off)
- Forces sustainable body position
- First one will feel terrible — that's the point

**2. Head-Up Freestyle (HUFF)**
- 4 x 50 with head above water entire length
- Eyes forward, like water polo player
- Builds neck strength for sighting
- Strengthens core to counter drag
- **Brutal but effective** — expect complaints

**3. OW Simulation Set**
- 800 continuous (20-30 min depending on pace)
- Sight every 4 strokes (pick target on deck)
- Breathe bilaterally
- No stopping (including walls)
- Mental + physical simulation of race conditions

## Critical Skill #4: Stroke Rate Management

### Why Cadence Matters

**Pool distance swimmers:**
- Often slow cadence (55-65 strokes per minute)
- Long glide, maximizing DPS (distance per stroke)
- Works in calm, controlled water

**Open water reality:**
- **Slow cadence + choppy water = disaster**
- Hand gets stuck at top of waves
- Rhythm disrupted
- Forward momentum lost

**Solution:** Slightly faster cadence in OW (65-75 SPM for most athletes)

### How to Train Cadence

**Tempo Trainer Protocol:**
1. Establish pool SPM baseline (count strokes for 1 minute)
2. Set tempo trainer 5-10 beats faster
3. Practice sets with trainer (beeps = stroke entry)
4. Gradually adjust to faster turnover
5. Maintain in OW conditions

**Sets:**
- 10 x 100 @ T-pace, tempo trainer set to target SPM
- 5 x 200 building tempo each 50 (60 → 65 → 70 → 75 SPM)

## Critical Skill #5: Efficient Kicking

### The Pool-to-OW Disconnect

**Pool kicking:**
- Powerful 6-beat kick for sprints
- Minimal 2-beat for distance (energy conservation)

**OW + Triathlon kicking:**
- 2-beat kick too slow (hips drop, especially in chop)
- 6-beat kick too taxing (legs needed for bike/run in tri)
- **Solution: 4-beat crossover kick** (moderate propulsion, sustainable)

### Teaching the 4-Beat Kick

**Mechanics:**
- 4 kicks per stroke cycle (2 per arm)
- Moderate depth (12-18 inches)
- Loose ankles, knees relatively straight
- Enough to stabilize hips, not exhaust legs

**Drill: Kick Variation Set**
- 4 x 100 as:
  - 100 #1: 2-beat kick (minimal)
  - 100 #2: 4-beat kick (moderate)
  - 100 #3: 6-beat kick (aggressive)
  - 100 #4: Choose best for you (usually 4-beat)
- Notice speed + effort trade-off

## Chafing Prevention (The Thing Nobody Talks About)

### The Brutal Reality

> "I finished my Catalina Channel crossing with my right eye swollen shut. Petroleum jelly got on my goggle seal from a fish going down my suit. The salt water leaked in for 9 hours. It was excruciating."  
> — Penny Lee Dean (on her 1976 world record swim)

**Problem areas:**
- **Women:** Underarms, suit straps on neck/shoulders, back of neck (from cap)
- **Men:** Groin, face/neck (stubble from shaving), underarms
- **Both:** Goggle seals, any skin-on-skin or suit-on-skin friction

### Solutions

**Pre-swim prevention:**
1. **Apply petroleum jelly (Vaseline) liberally** to all potential rub zones
   - Underarms
   - Neck (especially where straps sit)
   - Groin area
   - Any previous chafe scars
2. **Use rubber gloves to apply** — if grease gets on hands → gets on goggles → goggles leak → eye pain
3. **Men: Shave morning of swim** (stubble = razors on your shoulders after 30+ min)
4. **Test suit straps in practice** — thin straps often chafe less than thick
5. **Body Glide as alternative** — less messy, works well for shorter swims

**During swim:**
- If chafing starts, adjust suit straps slightly
- Rinse with fresh water if available (salt makes it worse)

**Post-swim:**
- Remove suit immediately
- Fresh water rinse
- Clean with soap
- Apply antibiotic ointment to any cuts
- **DO NOT train on chafe wounds** — they take weeks/months to heal in salt water

## Goggles: The Most Important Equipment Decision

### Why Goggles Matter More in OW

**Pool swimming:**
- Goggles leak → stop at wall, adjust, continue
- Lose goggles → borrow from teammate
- Minor annoyance

**Open water swimming:**
- Goggles leak → entire swim compromised
- Salt water in eyes → pain, swelling, can't see
- Stopping to adjust → lose position, get cold, break rhythm
- **Can literally end your race/swim**

### Goggle Selection Criteria

**Fit testing (do this in the store):**
1. Press goggles to face WITHOUT strap
2. Should suction to face for 3-5 seconds
3. No gaps, no pressure points
4. If they fall off instantly → wrong shape for your face

**Types:**
- **Clear lens:** Night swimming, early morning, fog
- **Dark/mirrored lens:** Sunny days, reduce glare
- **Blue/amber lens:** Overcast, fog (enhances contrast)

**Brands that fit different face shapes:**
- Speedo Vanquisher: Wider faces
- TYR Socket Rockets: Narrow faces, deep eye sockets
- Aqua Sphere Kaiman: Larger gasket, more comfortable for long swims
- Arena Cobra Ultra: Low profile, competitive racing

**Race day protocol:**
1. Primary pair (tested in 5+ practices)
2. Backup pair #1 (identical to primary)
3. Backup pair #2 (different style, in case primary type fails)
- Mark each pair (1, 2, 3) with tape
- Store in labeled bag
- Coach/crew holds backups

### Anti-Fog Solutions

**The problem:** Goggles fog = can't see = can't sight = swim off course

**Solutions:**
1. **Spit** (old school, works)
2. **Anti-fog spray** (buy at swim shop, apply 30 min pre-swim)
3. **Baby shampoo** (thin layer, rinse lightly)
4. **DO NOT touch inside of lenses** (skin oils cause fogging)

## Cold Water Acclimatization

### The Physiology

**What happens in cold water (under 66°F / 19°C):**
- Blood vessels constrict (vasoconstriction)
- Blood shunted away from extremities to core
- Breathing becomes labored
- Stroke rate drops unconsciously
- Mental function impaired
- Hypothermia risk increases

### Progressive Acclimatization Protocol

**Week 1: Initial Exposure (5-10 minutes)**
- Enter water SLOWLY (do not dive/jump)
- Dip hands first
- Splash face
- Wade in gradually
- Swim easy 5-10 minutes
- Exit when uncomfortable
- **Key:** Your body WILL adapt, but it takes time

**Week 2: Increase Duration (10-20 minutes)**
- Same gradual entry
- Swim 10-20 minutes
- Monitor for shivering, numbness, confusion
- Warm up immediately after (hot shower, warm clothes, warm fluids)

**Week 3-4: Build to Race Distance**
- Add 5-10 minutes every 2-3 swims
- Stop if: severe shivering, can't answer questions, gray/blue skin, mental fog
- **Golden rule:** If you're not warming up within 30-60 min post-swim, DO NOT add time next session

**Week 5+: Maintenance**
- Swim race distance in race temperature 2-3x before race
- Simulate race conditions (time of day, feeding protocol)

### Safety Protocols (Non-Negotiable)

**During swim:**
- Swim cap (20% of body heat lost through head)
- Earplugs (prevent heat loss + inner ear cold exposure)
- Coach/paddler within 10 yards at all times
- Warm fluids every 10-15 min if swim >30 min

**Post-swim:**
- Remove suit IMMEDIATELY (wet suit traps cold water)
- Towel off vigorously (stimulates circulation)
- Warm clothes (multiple layers)
- Warm fluids (not hot — can cause shock)
- If still cold after 1 hour → see medical personnel

**Do NOT:**
- Dive into cold water (can cause heart attack, especially >40 years old)
- Train alone in water <60°F
- Push through severe shivering (sign of hypothermia)
- Drink alcohol post-swim (causes vasodilation = more heat loss)

**Minimum (every session):**
- ☐ 1 suit (tested, fits well)
- ☐ 2 pairs goggles (primary + backup)
- ☐ 2 caps (bright color for visibility)
- ☐ 1 set earplugs (if water <70°F)
- ☐ Petroleum jelly (chafe prevention)
- ☐ Towel
- ☐ Warm clothes (post-swim)

**Cold water (<66°F) additions:**
- ☐ Neoprene cap (if legal for your race)
- ☐ Wet suit (if legal/allowed)
- ☐ Extra towel
- ☐ Thermos with warm fluid
- ☐ Emergency contact info

- ☐ Ice chest with food/fluids
- ☐ Warm clothes for post-race
- ☐ Medical info card

## Technique Summary: Pool vs. Open Water

| Element | Pool Swimming | Open Water Adaptation |
|---------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Breathing** | Unilateral (one side) OK | MUST be bilateral |
| **Sighting** | Never needed (lane lines, black line) | Every 4-6 strokes (crocodile eyes) |
| **Body position** | High hips from push-offs | Sustainable core engagement |
| **Stroke rate** | 55-65 SPM (distance) | 65-75 SPM (compensate for conditions) |
| **Kick** | 2-beat (energy conservation) | 4-beat (stability in chop) |
| **Head position** | Low (streamline) | Slightly higher (aids sighting) |
| **Equipment focus** | Speed/comfort | Durability/visibility/safety |

## What Pool Coaches Get Wrong

### Mistake #1: "Just swim your pool stroke"
**Wrong.** OW requires sighting, bilateral breathing, modified cadence.

### Mistake #2: "Distance swimmers don't need to kick"
**Wrong.** In chop/waves, 2-beat kick = hips sink = massive drag.

### Mistake #3: "One pair of goggles is fine"
**Wrong.** Goggles fail. Always have 2-3 backups on race day.

### Mistake #4: "You'll adjust to cold water during the race"
**Wrong.** Without acclimatization, hypothermia = DNF or medical emergency.

### Mistake #5: "Grease/petroleum jelly isn't necessary"
**Wrong.** Chafing in salt water for 30-90+ min = open wounds that take months to heal.

## Progressive Training Plan: Pool to Open Water (8 Weeks)

**Weeks 1-2: Foundation (Pool)**
- Bilateral breathing all warm-ups (400-800)
- Sighting drill 2x/week: 6 x 50 @ easy, sight every 10 strokes
- Head-up freestyle: 4 x 25 (neck strength)

**Weeks 3-4: Integration (Pool)**
- Bilateral breathing on ALL freestyle (except sprints)
- Sighting every 6 strokes (increase frequency)
- Add continuous 400s (no push-offs)
- Tempo trainer work (establish OW cadence)

**Weeks 5-6: Transition (Pool + OW)**
- OW swim #1: 10-15 min (calm water, protected area)
- Practice sighting on real landmarks (buoys, buildings)
- Test goggles, suit, anti-fog protocol
- Pool: Maintain bilateral breathing, sighting drills

**Weeks 7-8: Race Prep (Primarily OW)**
- OW swim 2-3x/week (build to race distance)
- Cold water acclimatization (if applicable)
- Practice race start in waves/chop
- Feeding protocol (if race >1 hour)
- Final equipment testing (goggles, suit, grease)

---

## Key Takeaways for Pool Coaches

1. **Bilateral breathing is non-negotiable** — Start in week 1, enforce religiously
2. **Sighting takes 4-6 weeks to master** — Integrate into every practice
3. **Goggles matter more than you think** — Test, backup, test backup
4. **Cold water requires acclimatization** — 3-4 weeks minimum, progressive exposure
5. **Chafing is brutal** — Petroleum jelly everywhere, rubber gloves to apply
6. **Stroke modifications are necessary** — Higher cadence, 4-beat kick, sustainable form

**Bottom line:** Pool swimming ≠ open water swimming. Coaches who ignore these adaptations send athletes into dangerous, uncompetitive situations. Start preparing 8 weeks minimum before any OW race.
