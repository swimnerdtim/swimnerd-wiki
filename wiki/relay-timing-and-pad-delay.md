# Relay Timing and Pad Delay: Critical Details for Coaches

**Source:** Superior Swim Timing Manual (2019)  
**Category:** Meet Operations / Relays  
**For:** Coaches training relay teams and managing relay exchanges

---

## Why Pad Delay Matters for Relays

The single most important timing setting for relays is **pad delay** - and most coaches don't know it exists.

**What it is:** The time period after a touchpad is hit when it's temporarily disabled and won't register new touches.

**Why it exists:** To prevent relay swimmers from accidentally triggering the pad as they exit the pool after their leg.

**Standard setting: 17 seconds**

---

## How Pad Delay Works

### The Sequence

1. **Incoming swimmer touches pad** → Timer records split/finish time
2. **Pad delay activates immediately** → Pad is "dead" for 17 seconds
3. **Swimmer exits pool** → Any touches during this window are ignored
4. **Pad delay expires** → Pad becomes "live" again for next swimmer
5. **Next swimmer touches** → Timer records their split/finish

### Visual Timeline

```
Touch #1 → [---- 17 second PAD DELAY ----] → Touch #2 can register
            ↑                                  ↑
         Swimmer 1 finishes              Swimmer 2 can touch
         Swimmer 1 exits pool            without false trigger
```

---

## Why 17 Seconds?

**The calculation:**
- Average swimmer takes 5-10 seconds to exit pool after touching
- Need buffer for slower swimmers or difficult exits
- Must allow enough time but not so much that next swimmer is delayed

**17 seconds is the sweet spot** used by most timing systems as default.

### When Pad Delay Needs Adjustment

**Increase to 20-25 seconds when:**
- Young age group swimmers (slower exits)
- Swimmers with disabilities
- Shallow warm-up pool (harder to exit)
- Bulkhead configuration makes exit difficult

**Decrease to 12-15 seconds when:**
- Elite swimmers with quick exits
- Easy exit configuration (stairs, wide gutters)
- Fast-moving relay where next swimmer arriving quickly

**Coach tip:** Timing operators can adjust pad delay DURING a race if needed. If your swimmer is struggling to exit, ask timing to extend the delay before the next leg starts.

## Common Relay Timing Problems

### Problem 1: "Early Touch" Before Pad Delay Expires

**What happens:**
- Incoming swimmer touches pad
- Outgoing swimmer HASN'T exited yet
- Pad delay still active (less than 17 seconds elapsed)
- Touch doesn't register

**Result:** System doesn't record the split time

**Prevention:**
- ✅ Train swimmers on quick, clean exits
- ✅ Practice relay exchanges with timing (use pace clock for 17-second drills)
- ✅ Position next swimmer farther back on blocks if necessary

### Problem 2: Swimmer Exits Too Slowly, Triggers Pad Multiple Times

**What happens:**
- Swimmer touches to finish their leg
- Takes too long to exit (>17 seconds)
- Pad delay expires while they're still touching wall
- Accidental touch registers as false finish

**Result:** Invalid split time recorded, needs manual correction

**Prevention:**
- ✅ Train FAST EXITS as part of relay practice
- ✅ Use "touch and GO" drills
- ✅ Ask timing operator to increase pad delay if needed

### Problem 3: Relay Exchange Happens During Pad Delay

**What happens:**
- Incoming swimmer touches pad
- Outgoing swimmer dives before pad delay expires
- Outgoing swimmer touches wall to turn or finish
- Touch doesn't register because pad still disabled

**Why this is bad:**
- If it's the final leg, finish time may not record
- If it's a turn, lap count will be wrong
- System may require manual correction

**Prevention:**
- ✅ Time relay exchanges so outgoing swimmer doesn't arrive until pad is live
- ✅ Practice exchanges with pace clock (17-second marker)
- ✅ Timing operator can use "ARM" button to force pad live if needed

## Training Relay Exits: The 17-Second Drill

### Setup

1. Swimmer 1 touches wall (simulates finish)
2. Coach starts stopwatch
3. Swimmer 1 exits pool as fast as possible
4. Swimmer 2 waits on blocks
5. At 17 seconds, coach signals
6. Swimmer 2 dives and swims

### Goal

- Swimmer 1 should be OUT of pool before 17-second mark
- Swimmer 2 should be READY to dive at 17-second mark
- Builds timing awareness for both swimmers

### Common Mistakes to Fix

❌ **Swimmer exits too slowly** → Train explosive exit, use arms to push up
❌ **Swimmer exits wrong direction** → Exit opposite side from next swimmer's lane
❌ **Swimmer hangs on wall** → "Touch and GO" mindset
❌ **Next swimmer dives too early** → Wait for clear visual that pad is ready

## Manual Override: ARM and F ARM Buttons

Timing operators have emergency controls to override pad delay:

### ARM Button
**What it does:** Forces pad to become "live" immediately, ignoring pad delay
**When used:**
- Incoming swimmer exited quickly and cleanly
- Next swimmer is ready to go
- Don't want to wait full 17 seconds
- Speeds up meet

**Coach scenario:** Your relay swimmer exits in 8 seconds. Timing operator can ARM the pad immediately so next swimmer can touch without waiting another 9 seconds.

### F ARM Button
**What it does:** Forces the NEXT touch to be the finish for that lane
**When used:**
- Lap count is wrong
- Pad delay caused system to miss expected touch
- Emergency correction needed

**Coach scenario:** Your swimmer's final touch didn't register because they touched during pad delay. Operator uses F ARM to make the next touch (even if lap count is off) finish the race.

## Relay Rules vs. Timing System Reality

### USA Swimming Rules

**Rule 101.7 - Relay Takeoffs:**
- Outgoing swimmer's feet must leave block BEFORE incoming swimmer touches wall
- Early takeoff = disqualification
- Timing pads do NOT enforce this rule automatically

**Important:** Pad delay has NOTHING to do with legal relay exchanges. Pad delay is about timing equipment, not rule enforcement.

### What Timing Systems CAN'T Do

❌ Detect early takeoffs (that's the referee's job)  
❌ Measure reaction time on relay exchanges  
❌ Automatically DQ illegal relays  

### What Timing Systems CAN Do

✅ Record accurate split times when pad is functional  
✅ Provide backup times via buttons if pad fails  
✅ Display splits on scoreboard in real-time  
✅ Send relay data to meet management software

## Backup Button Timing for Relays

### When Backup Buttons Activate

**Key fact:** Backup buttons become active AFTER the initial pad delay expires (not just on final lap).

**Timeline:**
1. Race starts
2. Pad delay period elapses (~17 seconds)
3. Backup buttons now active for entire race
4. Operator can press button for ANY split, not just finish

**Why this matters for relays:**
- If pad fails during a relay leg, operator can hit backup button
- Backup time can be used as official split time
- Multiple backup operators can cover different legs

### Backup Button Strategy for Big Meets

**Best practice:**
- One backup button operator per 2-3 lanes
- Focus on watching incoming swimmers' touches
- Be ready to hit button if pad doesn't register
- Note any delayed exits that might cause timing issues

## Coaching the Perfect Relay Exit

### The 5-Second Exit

Elite relay swimmers should exit in **5 seconds or less** after touching.

**Technique breakdown:**
1. **Touch:** Full hand contact, don't pull away immediately (ensures pad registers)
2. **Pull:** Use both arms to pull body toward wall (builds momentum)
3. **Push:** Explosive push UP and OUT of water (core + arms)
4. **Rotate:** Turn body away from next swimmer's lane (clears space)
5. **Exit:** Fastest path to deck, don't linger in gutter

### Common Exit Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Gentle tap, quick pullaway | Full palm press, wait for light touch confirmation |
| Lazy climb out | Explosive push-up from wall |
| Exit on same side as next swimmer | Exit opposite side, clear the lane |
| Hang on gutter/lane line | Touch wall, immediately push away |
| No sense of urgency | Practice with timer, build exit speed awareness |

### Practice Drill: Exit + Exchange

**Setup:** Full relay, but focus on exit timing
1. Swimmer 1 finishes their leg, exits ASAP
2. Coach times exit (should be <8 seconds)
3. Swimmer 2 waits until timing confirms pad is live
4. Swimmer 2 dives
5. Repeat for all relay legs

**Goal:** Build muscle memory for fast exits + awareness of pad timing

## Meet Day: What to Tell Your Relay Teams

### Pre-Race Briefing

✅ "Touch the pad with full hand pressure - don't just tap it"  
✅ "Exit the pool in under 10 seconds - fast and explosive"  
✅ "Exit away from the next swimmer's lane"  
✅ "Next swimmer: don't dive until you see their head clear the water"  
✅ "If something feels wrong, keep swimming - timing will sort it out"  

### What NOT to Say

❌ "Just touch lightly and get out" → Touch must register on pad  
❌ "Dive as soon as they touch" → Pad delay makes this impossible  
❌ "If pad doesn't light up, do it again" → Keep swimming, don't re-touch

## Troubleshooting Relay Timing Issues

### "My swimmer touched but time didn't show"

**Possible causes:**
1. Pad delay was still active (less than 17 sec since previous touch)
2. Touch was too light to register
3. Pad malfunction

**What to do:**
- Alert timing operator immediately
- Note the clock time when touch occurred
- Ask about backup button time
- Meet referee determines official time

### "Wrong lap count showing"

**Possible causes:**
1. False touch during pad delay
2. Swimmer touched twice (once during delay, once after)
3. Equipment malfunction

**What to do:**
- Timing operator can manually adjust lap count
- "Touch +/-" buttons can add/remove touches
- F ARM button can force next touch to be finish

### "Split times not showing on scoreboard"

**Possible causes:**
1. Scoreboard not configured to display splits
2. Network delay between timing system and scoreboard
3. Meet management software not processing splits in real-time

**What to do:**
- Usually not a timing error, just display delay
- Times are still being recorded
- Results will appear in official meet results

## Key Takeaways

1. **Pad delay = 17 seconds (default)** - swimmers must exit in less than this time
2. **Train fast exits** - 5-8 seconds is ideal, under 10 is minimum
3. **Touch with purpose** - full hand contact ensures pad registers
4. **Exit opposite side** - clear space for next swimmer
5. **Timing operators can adjust** - pad delay can be changed during race if needed
6. **Backup buttons activate early** - not just on final lap, but after first pad delay
7. **ARM button speeds things up** - operator can force pad live before 17 seconds
8. **Pad delay ≠ relay rules** - timing system doesn't enforce early takeoff rules

## Practice Integration

### Relay Practice Must Include Exit Training

**DON'T just practice exchanges. Practice:**
- ✅ Full-speed touch with immediate exit
- ✅ Timed exits (coach with stopwatch)
- ✅ Realistic exchange spacing (wait for pad delay to expire)
- ✅ Communication between swimmers
- ✅ Backup plan if pad doesn't register

**Sample set:**
```
4 x Relay Exchanges (each swimmer goes twice)
- Focus: FAST EXITS
- Coach times exit for each swimmer
- Goal: All exits under 10 seconds
- Next swimmer waits 17 seconds before diving
- Track improvement over multiple practices
```
