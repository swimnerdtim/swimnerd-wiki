# How We Built a 500-Swimmer Program (From 20 Athletes): Landing the Plane

**Coach:** Ron Aitken (Sandpipers of Nevada)  
**Category:** Coaching, Methodology, Program Building  
**Source:** YouTube Clinic Talk  
**Link:** https://youtu.be/BYARqjsNV1Q

---

## Overview

Ron Aitken shares how he built Sandpipers of Nevada from 20 athletes in 1994 to 500 swimmers, focusing on coaching philosophy, season planning, and the critical concept of "landing the plane" — peaking athletes for their biggest meet of the year.

---

## Career Journey & Philosophy

### Staying at One Program

**Timeline:** With Sandpipers since 1994 (30+ years)

**Why he stayed instead of climbing the ladder:**
- Multiple opportunities to move to "bigger" programs
- Every time he looked around: "I have a pretty good program here"
- Other programs had issues (starting over, board-run dysfunction, poor budgeting)
- Found it tough to find anything better than his position

### College Coaching Reality Check

**Why Ron passed on college:**
- "Those guys are not really coaching as much as you think they are"
- "Just on the phone recruiting all the time"
- Not getting to do what coaches love most: working with kids
- Too much administrative burden, not enough actual coaching

**The takeaway:** Don't assume college coaching is the pinnacle — stay where you can actually coach.

---

## The 80/20 Rule of Coaching

**From Ron's father:**
> "If you can take 80% of the heartbreak and enjoy the 20% of the good stuff that comes along with it, you'll be fine."

**Reality check:**
- You put a lot of work in
- Lots of heartbreak goes along with this job
- You work really hard for kids
- Parents and athletes often don't appreciate it
- **If you love it, you'll do really well. If you don't, find another place.**

**Brutal truth:** "This is not an easy job. This is not an easy job."

## Building From 20 to 500 Athletes

### The 8-and-Under Lesson (1994)

**What happened:**
- Ron came in coaching eight-and-unders
- Expected them to act like elite-level athletes (all he knew was college swimming)
- **They didn't show up unless they were having fun**

**The turning point:**
> "Our athletes need to have fun. Even though we're one of the hardest working teams in the country, they have to still be having fun while they're working hard."

**Key principle:** Find a way to get the work done AND let them have fun.

---

### The Feeder Program Decision

**Critical question to parents (early days):**
> "Do you want to be the feeder program, or do you want to challenge the big teams?"

**Ron's mindset:**
- No ego about working with seniors vs. eight-and-unders
- Just wanted to know: **What is my job?**
- If the job was getting 13-year-olds to Junior Nationals and handing them off, fine — just tell me

**What parents wanted:**
- To become the team that challenged the big team

**The strategy:**
- Stick together
- Build from the bottom up (not top-down)
- Those eight-and-unders who set state records → stayed together for 10 years
- Seven years later: **won the entire state championship**

**Philosophy:** Focus on the future, not just the top end. Build the program from young talent up.

## Training Philosophy: Everything, Every Week

### Traditional Periodization (What Ron DOESN'T Do)

**Common approach:**
1. 3 months: aerobic base building
2. 3 months: anaerobic/threshold phase
3. 3 months: speed work + taper

**Ron's approach:**
> "I've taken everything they would do traditionally over a 12-week to 18-week spread and put it all into 1 week."

**The system:**
- Touch every energy system every week
- Aerobic, anaerobic, threshold, speed, stand-up/sit-up swims — all in one week
- Sometimes stretched to 2-week cycles
- 10-11 practices/week when going full volume

**Why it works:**
- Athletes are always touching something fast
- Always touching aerobic work
- Always touching anaerobic work
- **Nothing changes at taper except volume drops**

---

### Speed Throughout the Season

**Early mistake (learned from Dr. G):**
- Swam too much aerobic work
- Not enough speed work
- Only swam fast at end of season

**The fix:**
> "Once we started peppering in enough speed work throughout the season, we swim pretty fast all the time now. And we still drop time at the big meets."

**Result:** Fast times year-round, still peak at championship meets.

## Landing the Plane: Season Planning

### What is Your Team's Super Bowl?

**Critical question:**
> "Where are you going with the majority of your team? Where's that meet that means the most to you?"

**The goal:**
- Have a plan for the entire season
- Ultimate goal: race as fast as they can at the biggest meet of the year
- Don't worry as much about other meets during the season
- But still race fast enough to build confidence and speed

**Planning elements:**
1. Volume progression
2. Peak weeks
3. **Landing the plane** (taper strategy)

**Philosophy:** Not just surviving the season — having a plan to peak at the right time.

---

## Tracking & Progression

### You Can't Fly Blind

**Why tracking matters:**
> "If you're flying blind, you don't have any track record of what you're doing, you're not going to progress kids along."

**What to track:**
- What your top kid did
- What the slowest kid in your group did
- Build on each season, each block, each quad

**The compounding effect:** Each season builds on the last. Without records, you can't improve systematically.

---

## The Slowest Kid Principle

### You're Only as Good as Your Slowest Swimmer

**Ron's philosophy:**
> "You're only as good as the slowest kid in your group. If you're not taking care of the slowest kid in your group, you're not doing a good job of coaching."

**What it means:**
- Give the slowest kid as much attention as the top kid
- Work just as hard to get the most out of them
- Not about ego or only coaching fast swimmers

**The litmus test:**
> "If you're only taking care of the fast kid and not taking care of the slow kid, you're not a very good coach."

**Why it matters:** This grounds you. This keeps you focused on the real work of coaching — developing every athlete, not just collecting talent.

---

## Key Training Sets (Mentioned)

### UL RPT (Ultra-Long Race Pace Training)

**Concept:**
- Traditional approach: build up from 20x25 at race pace → 3 rounds of 20x25
- **Ron's approach:** Go the other direction
  - Start with 300s at race pace
  - Then 200s
  - Then 100s
  - Even 50s
- Work your way DOWN in distance while holding race pace

**Why:** Distance version of race pace training — building speed endurance from longer intervals down.

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Fun + Hard Work Coexist** — You can't have one without the other at scale (500 swimmers)
2. **Build From the Bottom Up** — Invest in young talent, don't just chase seniors
3. **Touch Everything Every Week** — Don't silo energy systems into separate blocks
4. **Speed Year-Round** — Pepper in speed work consistently, not just at the end
5. **Know Your Super Bowl** — Plan the whole season around one peak meet
6. **Track Everything** — You can't improve what you don't measure
7. **Coach the Slowest Kid** — That's the real test of your program and philosophy
8. **80/20 Rule** — Take the heartbreak with the wins. If you can't, find another job.

---

## Bottom Line

Ron Aitken built one of the largest and most successful club programs in the country by:
- Staying loyal to one program and building it from scratch
- Prioritizing fun alongside hard work
- Training all energy systems every week (not traditional periodization)
- Planning entire seasons around peaking for the biggest meet
- Giving as much attention to the slowest kid as the fastest
- Tracking everything to ensure systematic progression

**Core philosophy:** "If you love it, you'll do really, really well. If you don't, try to find some other place. This is not an easy job."
