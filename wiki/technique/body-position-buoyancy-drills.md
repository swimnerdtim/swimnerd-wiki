# Body Position & Buoyancy Drills: The Foundation Layer

**Source:** *The Swimming Drill Book* (Chapter 1) by Ruben J. Guzman  
**Context:** First-stage drills that establish proper alignment and water confidence  
**Application:** Essential foundation for all swimmers, from beginners to elite

---

## Overview: Why Body Position Comes First

**The fundamental principle:** You can't have good propulsion with bad body position.

**The physics:**
- Poor position = increased drag = wasted energy
- Proper streamline = minimal drag = efficient swimming
- **Drag increases exponentially** with body position errors

**Example:**
- **Streamlined swimmer:** Drag coefficient ≈ 0.4-0.5
- **Head-up swimmer:** Drag coefficient ≈ 0.8-1.0  
  → **2x more drag** = swimming with parking brake engaged

**Coaching implication:** Spend 2-3 weeks on body position drills **before** teaching full stroke. This investment pays lifelong dividends.

## The Body Position Drill Sequence

### Drill 1: Standing Streamline (Dry)

**Environment:** Deck (dry drill)  
**Category:** Body position awareness  
**Skill level:** All (especially beginners)

**How to perform:**
1. Stand with feet together, arms extended overhead
2. Lock thumbs or clasp hands (hands stacked, one on top of other)
3. Squeeze arms against ears (biceps touching ears, not behind)
4. Press hands slightly **back** (not straight up — creates slight shoulder extension)
5. Engage core (slight posterior pelvic tilt, ribs down)
6. Hold for 10-30 seconds

**What it teaches:**
- **Proper streamline shape** — Narrow profile, arms locked
- **Shoulder flexibility** — Many swimmers can't get arms fully overhead
- **Body awareness** — Feel the position without water distraction

**Coaching cues:**
- "Ears between your arms" (not arms behind ears)
- "Make yourself as skinny as possible"
- "Squeeze like you're trying to fit through a narrow tube"
- "Belly button pulled in, ribs down"

**Common errors:**
- ❌ Arms too wide (elbows out)
- ❌ Head forward (looking up instead of neutral)
- ❌ Arched back (ribs flared, butt sticking out)
- ❌ Feet apart (wider than hip-width)

**Progression:**
1. Hold standing streamline 10 seconds (static awareness)
2. Walk in streamline position (dynamic awareness)
3. Jump in streamline position (explosive power + position)
4. Transfer to water (next drill)

**Sample sets:**
```
Deck warm-up:
  10×10-second standing streamline holds
  5×walk 10 yards in streamline
  10×streamline jumps (vertical jump, maintain position)
```

### Drill 2: Cannonball Float

**Environment:** Shallow water  
**Category:** Buoyancy awareness  
**Skill level:** Beginners, water-fearful swimmers

**How to perform:**
1. Stand in chest-deep water
2. Take a breath and hold
3. Pull knees to chest (hug your knees)
4. Tuck chin to chest (ball shape)
5. **Float** — Your body should naturally rise and bob

**What it teaches:**
- **Positive buoyancy** — Humans float (with air in lungs)
- **Water confidence** — Feel the water supporting you
- **Breathing awareness** — Full lungs = more buoyancy

**Why this matters:**
Many beginner swimmers **fight** the water because they don't trust that they'll float. This drill proves they will.

**Coaching cues:**
- "Take a big breath and hold it"
- "Hug your knees tight"
- "Let the water push you up — don't fight it"
- "You're a beach ball — the water wants you on top"

**Common errors:**
- ❌ Exhaling (sinks immediately)
- ❌ Not fully tucking (legs drag down)
- ❌ Tensing up (relaxation helps buoyancy)

**Progression:**
1. Cannonball float 10 seconds with wall support
2. Cannonball float 10 seconds without wall
3. Cannonball float 30+ seconds (confidence test)
4. Transition to face-down float (next drill)

**Sample sets:**
```
Water confidence warm-up:
  5×cannonball float (10 seconds each, :20R between)
  3×cannonball float (30 seconds each, :30R between)
  Challenge: Who can float longest in cannonball? (game format)
```

### Drill 3: Dead Swimmer's Float (Jellyfish Float)

**Environment:** Shallow water  
**Category:** Buoyancy & relaxation  
**Skill level:** Beginners (prerequisite for face-down swimming)

**How to perform:**
1. Stand in chest-deep water
2. Take a big breath
3. Bend forward at waist, putting face in water
4. Let arms hang down loosely (like jellyfish tentacles)
5. Relax completely — back and butt should rise to surface
6. Hold 10-30 seconds
7. Stand up when you need air

**What it teaches:**
- **Face-down buoyancy** — Critical for all swimming strokes
- **Relaxation in water** — Tension = sinking
- **Breathing rhythm** — Can't hold breath forever, must breathe rhythmically
- **Horizontal position** — Body naturally wants to float horizontally (not vertical)

**Coaching cues:**
- "Let your arms dangle like a jellyfish"
- "Relax your neck — let your head hang"
- "Your back should pop up to the surface"
- "Don't kick or move — just float"

**Common errors:**
- ❌ Holding head up (prevents body from floating flat)
- ❌ Tensing arms/legs (sinks lower body)
- ❌ Kicking to stay up (defeats purpose — it's a **float**, not a swim)
- ❌ Panicking and standing up too soon (needs more time to trust water)

**Progression:**
1. Dead swimmer's float 5 seconds (build confidence)
2. Dead swimmer's float 10 seconds
3. Dead swimmer's float 20-30 seconds (full lung cycle)
4. Transition to streamline float (next drill)

**Breathing pattern practice:**
- Float 10 seconds → Stand, breathe → Float 10 seconds → Repeat 5-10 times
- Builds rhythm: **Breathe in → Hold → Float → Stand → Breathe out → Repeat**

**Sample sets:**
```
Beginner confidence drill:
  10×dead swimmer's float (10 seconds each, stand and breathe between)
  5×dead swimmer's float (20 seconds each)
  3×dead swimmer's float (hold as long as comfortable)
```

### Drill 4: Streamline Float

**Environment:** Pool (push-off from wall)  
**Category:** Body position + buoyancy  
**Skill level:** All levels

**How to perform:**
1. Stand with back against wall, feet on wall
2. Take a breath
3. Enter streamline position (hands locked, arms against ears, core tight)
4. **Push off wall** with feet
5. Glide in streamline position (no kick, no arm movement)
6. Hold streamline until you slow down or need air
7. Stand up

**What it teaches:**
- **Streamline under motion** — Harder than static streamline (water pressure tests your position)
- **Glide efficiency** — How far can you go without propulsion?
- **Body alignment** — Any misalignment = you veer left/right or sink
- **The power of streamline** — Good push-off + streamline = 5-10 yards effortlessly

**Coaching cues:**
- "Tight streamline before you push"
- "Push hard off the wall — let your streamline carry you"
- "If you veer left/right, your body isn't symmetrical"
- "If your legs sink, squeeze your core tighter"

**Common errors:**
- ❌ Loose streamline (arms apart, head up)
- ❌ Weak push-off (need power to test streamline)
- ❌ Holding breath with empty lungs (need full lungs for buoyancy)
- ❌ Kicking immediately (defeats purpose — this is a glide drill)

**Assessment criteria:**
- **Beginner goal:** Glide 3-5 yards
- **Intermediate goal:** Glide 5-7 yards
- **Advanced goal:** Glide 8-10 yards
- **Elite goal:** Glide 10+ yards (some can reach 15 yards)

**Why distance matters:**
Every yard of glide = less swimming required. In a 50-yard race, better streamline off start/turns saves 2-5 seconds.

**Sample sets:**
```
Streamline development:
  10×streamline float (focus: maximum distance)
  Record best distance each week (progress tracking)
  
Competition format:
  "Streamline wars" — Who can glide farthest? (motivates maximum effort)
  Team challenge: Total combined distance (encourages peer coaching)
```

### Drill 5: Pencil Float

**Environment:** Deep water  
**Category:** Vertical buoyancy  
**Skill level:** Intermediate water confidence

**How to perform:**
1. Stand in deep water (or shallow if deep isn't available)
2. Take a breath
3. Submerge vertically, arms at sides, body straight (like a pencil)
4. Relax and let yourself float upward
5. Your head should pop back to surface naturally
6. Repeat

**What it teaches:**
- **Vertical buoyancy** — Proves you'll always come back up
- **Relaxation under water** — Don't panic, water will return you to surface
- **Breath control** — Holding breath for short submersion

**Coaching application:**
- **Fear reduction:** "Even if you sink, you'll pop right back up — watch."
- **Open water prep:** Treading water and vertical position skills
- **Confidence building:** Comfort with face in water and submersion

**Progression:**
1. Pencil float in shallow water (can touch bottom)
2. Pencil float in deep water (trust the rise)
3. Pencil drop (next drill — active submersion)

---

### Drill 6: Pencil Drop

**Environment:** Deep water  
**Category:** Vertical control  
**Skill level:** Intermediate

**How to perform:**
1. Tread water or float in deep water
2. Take a breath
3. **Exhale** and let yourself sink vertically (straight body, arms at sides)
4. Sink 3-5 feet
5. **Kick or scull** to return to surface
6. Breathe and repeat

**What it teaches:**
- **Buoyancy control** — Empty lungs = negative buoyancy (you sink)
- **Vertical propulsion** — Kicking/sculling to ascend
- **Depth comfort** — Not all swimming is on the surface (underwater work, starts, turns)

**Open water application:**
- **Treading water rest position** — Slight sink allows relaxation
- **Underwater navigation** — Diving under obstacles (waves, other swimmers)

**Sample sets:**
```
Vertical control practice:
  5×pencil drop (3 feet depth)
  3×pencil drop (5 feet depth)
  2×pencil drop + hold at bottom for 3 seconds before ascending
```

### Drill 7: Sliding (Front)

**Environment:** Pool (push-off from wall)  
**Category:** Body position + glide  
**Skill level:** All

**How to perform:**
1. Push off wall in streamline position (face-down)
2. Glide 5+ yards without kicking or stroking
3. **Focus:** Maintain perfect body alignment
   - Head neutral (look at bottom, not forward)
   - Hips at surface (squeeze core/glutes)
   - Toes pointed
4. Let yourself glide to a stop
5. Stand or swim to wall

**What it teaches:**
- **Glide efficiency** — How much distance per push-off?
- **Head position** — Looking forward = hips sink
- **Core engagement** — Relaxed core = hips drop

**Coaching cues:**
- "Look at the bottom of the pool, not forward"
- "Squeeze your belly button to your spine"
- "Point your toes — pretend you're a torpedo"
- "Don't let your legs sink — hold the streamline"

**Common errors:**
- ❌ Head up (hips sink immediately)
- ❌ Arms apart (increased drag)
- ❌ Kicking (this is a glide drill — save kicking for next stage)

**Troubleshooting:**
- **Problem:** Legs sink no matter what  
- **Fix 1:** Check head position (must look down, not forward)  
- **Fix 2:** Full breath before push-off (empty lungs = less buoyancy)  
- **Fix 3:** Stronger core engagement (plank position practice on deck)

**Sample sets:**
```
Glide development:
  10×sliding on front (measure distance each rep)
  Goal: Increase distance by 1 yard per week
  
Contrast drill:
  1×sliding with head up (feel the drag)
  1×sliding with head neutral (feel the difference)
  Repeat 5 times
```

### Drill 8: Sliding on Back

**Environment:** Pool (push-off from wall)  
**Category:** Body position + glide (backstroke prep)  
**Skill level:** All

**How to perform:**
1. Push off wall in streamline position (**on back**, face up)
2. Glide 5+ yards without kicking
3. **Focus:** Maintain alignment
   - Ears in water (head neutral, not lifted)
   - Belly button toward ceiling (slight posterior pelvic tilt)
   - Hips at surface
4. Glide to a stop

**What it teaches:**
- **Backstroke body position** — Foundation for backstroke stroke
- **Balance on back** — Many swimmers struggle with back position
- **Water in ears comfort** — Psychological barrier for many beginners

**Coaching cues:**
- "Water should cover your ears"
- "Push your belly button toward the ceiling"
- "Don't sit — stay flat on the surface"
- "Look straight up, not at your feet"

**Common errors:**
- ❌ Head lifted (trying to keep face dry → hips sink)
- ❌ Sitting position (hips low, chest high)
- ❌ Hands apart (arms not locked in streamline)

**Psychological note:**
Many beginners fear water in their ears. **Practice on back is essential** for backstroke development.

**Progression:**
1. Sliding on back with coach hand-support under hips (build trust)
2. Sliding on back independent (3-5 yards)
3. Sliding on back with head fully neutral (ears submerged)
4. Add kick (transition to streamline back kick)

**Sample sets:**
```
Backstroke foundation:
  5×sliding on back (focus: ears in water)
  5×sliding on back (focus: hips at surface)
  3×contrast: sliding on front, then sliding on back (feel the difference)
```

## The Complete Body Position Progression

### Week 1: Awareness & Confidence
**Drills:** Standing Streamline (deck) → Cannonball Float → Dead Swimmer's Float

**Goal:** Understand buoyancy and trust the water

**Daily practice:**
```
10×standing streamline (10 seconds each)
5×cannonball float (10-30 seconds each)
5×dead swimmer's float (10-20 seconds each)
```

---

### Week 2: Horizontal Glide
**Drills:** Streamline Float → Sliding (front)

**Goal:** Glide 5+ yards in streamline

**Daily practice:**
```
Deck: 5×standing streamline
Pool: 10×streamline float (measure distance)
Pool: 10×sliding on front (head position focus)
```

---

### Week 3: Back Position & Vertical Control
**Drills:** Sliding (back) → Pencil Float → Pencil Drop

**Goal:** Comfort on back, vertical buoyancy control

**Daily practice:**
```
Pool: 5×sliding on back (ears in water)
Pool: 5×pencil float (deep water)
Pool: 5×pencil drop (3-5 feet depth)
```

---

### Week 4: Assessment & Integration
**Test:** All body position drills at mastery level

**Assessment checklist:**
- [ ] Standing streamline 30 seconds (proper form)
- [ ] Dead swimmer's float 30 seconds (relaxed)
- [ ] Streamline glide 5+ yards (no kick)
- [ ] Sliding on back 5+ yards (ears in water)
- [ ] Pencil drop 5 feet (controlled ascent)

**If passing all:** Ready for Stage 2 (Breathing & Kicking)  
**If failing any:** Repeat Week 3-4 with focus on weak areas

## Common Coaching Challenges (and Solutions)

### Challenge 1: "My legs sink no matter what I do"

**Root causes:**
1. **Head position** — Looking forward lifts chest, drops hips
2. **Empty lungs** — Exhaling completely removes buoyancy
3. **Tense legs** — Tension in quads/calves makes legs denser
4. **Low body fat** — Lean/muscular swimmers have less natural buoyancy (especially legs)

**Solutions:**
- **Fix head:** Look at bottom of pool (videotape from side to confirm)
- **Full breath:** Inhale completely before streamline (lungs = floatation device)
- **Relax legs:** Point toes, let legs float (not stiff/flexed)
- **Core engagement:** Slight posterior pelvic tilt (tuck tailbone under)
- **Accept reality:** Some swimmers need stronger kick to compensate for leg sink

**Drill prescription:**
```
2 weeks of:
  Daily: 10×sliding on front (videotape 1-2 per week for feedback)
  Daily: 20×standing streamline (muscle memory)
  Every other day: Plank holds on deck (1 min, 3 sets) to build core strength
```

---

### Challenge 2: "I can't get my arms behind my head"

**Root cause:** Tight shoulders (common in non-swimmers, weight lifters, desk workers)

**Solution:** Shoulder flexibility work

**Stretches:**
- **Wall angels:** Back against wall, slide arms overhead (hold 30 sec, repeat 5×)
- **Doorway chest stretch:** Arm on doorframe, rotate body away (30 sec each side, 3×)
- **Shoulder dislocates:** PVC pipe or band, arms wide, rotate overhead and behind back (10 reps, 3×)

**Timeline:** 2-4 weeks of daily stretching to gain overhead range

**Coaching note:** Don't wait for perfect flexibility to teach swimming. Modify streamline (arms as close to ears as possible) and continue flexibility work in parallel.

---

### Challenge 3: "I panic when my face is in the water"

**Root cause:** Fear of drowning, breath control anxiety

**Solution:** Gradual desensitization

**Progression:**
1. **Week 1:** Blow bubbles in water (just mouth, not full face)
2. **Week 2:** Submerge face for 3 seconds (in very shallow water)
3. **Week 3:** Dead swimmer's float 5 seconds
4. **Week 4:** Dead swimmer's float 10 seconds
5. **Week 5:** Streamline float off wall

**Key principle:** Never force. Let them progress at their own pace. Forcing creates trauma.

---

### Challenge 4: "My team finds body position drills boring"

**Root cause:** Drills lack competitive/game element

**Solution:** Gamify the drills

**Games:**
- **Streamline wars:** Who can glide farthest? (Winner gets to pick cool-down stroke)
- **Float challenge:** Who can hold dead swimmer's float longest?
- **Accuracy test:** Glide to a target (hula hoop on bottom of pool) — points for hitting target
- **Relay:** Streamline glide relay (each person glides, next person goes when previous touches wall)

**Result:** Same drills, more engagement.

## Body Position Drills for Advanced Swimmers

**Myth:** "Elite swimmers don't need body position drills."  
**Truth:** Elite swimmers benefit from **daily reminders** of optimal position.

**Advanced applications:**

### Streamline Hold (Underwater)
Push off wall, streamline glide underwater, see how far you can go before surfacing.

**Elite benchmark:** 15+ yards underwater streamline (no kick)

---

### Resistance Streamline
Streamline glide with drag shorts, t-shirt, or parachute.

**Purpose:** Exaggerates drag → Reveals position flaws → Fix and retest without resistance

---

### Streamline from Dive Start
Perfect streamline **immediately** after dive entry.

**Common elite error:** Diving deep, then streamlining (wasted distance/time)  
**Fix:** Enter streamline during flight → Maintain through entry and glide

---

### Contrast Drill: Bad vs. Good Streamline
1. Glide with head up, arms apart (feel the drag)
2. Glide with perfect streamline (feel the difference)
3. Repeat 5× (builds sensory awareness)

**Result:** Reminds elite swimmer how much bad position costs.

---

## Key Takeaways for Coaches

1. **Body position is the foundation** — Don't skip to stroke before position is solid

2. **Use dry drills** — Standing streamline on deck before streamline in water

3. **Be patient** — 2-3 weeks of body position drills pays lifelong dividends

4. **Assess objectively** — Video feedback reveals what verbal feedback misses

5. **Gamify for engagement** — Competitions and challenges make drills fun

6. **Don't abandon with advanced swimmers** — Elite swimmers benefit from daily streamline reminders

7. **Fix the root cause** — If legs sink, fix head position (not "kick harder")
