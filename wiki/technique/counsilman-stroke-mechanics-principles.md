# Counsilman's Stroke Mechanics Principles

**Source:** *The Complete Book of Swimming* (1977) by Dr. James E. "Doc" Counsilman  
**Context:** PhD in physiology, Indiana University coach for 33 years, revolutionized swimming by applying **physics and biomechanics** to stroke analysis  
**Key Innovation:** First coach to use **underwater filming** to prove what swimmers were actually doing (vs. what they thought they were doing)

---

## Why This Matters

Before Counsilman, swimming instruction was based on **what coaches saw above water**. He went underwater with cameras and discovered:

**What coaches thought swimmers were doing:** Pulling straight back with straight arms  
**What champions were actually doing:** Elliptical pull patterns with bent elbows

**This changed everything.**

Understanding **why** certain mechanics work lets you:  
1. Learn faster  
2. Self-correct more effectively  
3. Apply principles across all four strokes  
4. Coach with confidence, not guesswork

---

## The Six Fundamental Principles

### 1. Streamlining (Minimize Resistance)

**Definition:** Present as little surface area to forward progress as possible.

**Body position goal:** Flat/horizontal, not angled.

**Why it matters:**  
Water is **800 times denser** than air. Every degree of body angle creates exponentially more drag.

#### Common Streamlining Errors

**Error: Head too high**  
→ Legs drop → body swims at angle → massive drag increase  
→ **Fix:** Eyes look down 45° (freestyle), not forward

**Error: Hips/legs wiggle sideways**  
→ Destroys streamlining → creates additional resistance  
→ **Fix:** Core engagement, minimize lateral movement

**The swimming position vs. duck analogy:**  
- **Duck:** Swims ON TOP of water (designed for it)  
- **Dog:** Swims WITH only small part out of water (like humans)  
- **Swimmer:** Should be horizontal AT the surface, not trying to climb on top

**Planing effect:**  
As you go faster, your body naturally elevates slightly due to resistance on the bottom side.

**Critical mistake:** Trying to "climb on top" of water by arching back or lifting too high.  
**Correct approach:** Achieve lift through **speed from propulsion**, not by fighting body position.

---

### 2. Buoyancy (Work With It, Don't Fight It)

**Key fact:** Most people with **partially inflated lungs** are **slightly buoyant** — lighter than water.

**Implication:** You'll float just at the surface with very little of your body out of water.

**What NOT to do:**  
- Don't try to elevate yourself  
- Don't try to swim "on top" of water  
- Don't arch your back to stay high

**What TO do:**  
- Accept your natural buoyancy  
- Stay horizontal  
- Let increased speed create natural lift (planing effect)

**Breathing tip:**  
Lung inflation affects buoyancy. Full lungs = more buoyant. This is why **timing of breathing** matters in all strokes.

---

### 3. Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction)

**Statement:** For every action there is an **equal and opposite reaction**.

**Swimming application:** To move forward, you must push water backward.

**BUT HERE'S THE PARADOX:**  
You should **never push directly backward in a straight line**.

**Why not?** (This is where Counsilman's brilliance shows)

#### The Runner vs. Swimmer Problem

**Runner:**  
- Pushes ground backward with feet  
- Ground remains stationary  
- Runner moves forward  
- **Works great!**

**Swimmer (if they push straight back):**  
- Pushes water backward with hands  
- Water **ALSO moves backward**  
- Swimmer continues pushing **against water that's already moving backward**  
- Once hand speed = water speed → **no more propulsion**  
- **Doesn't work!**

**Counsilman's solution:** **Elliptical pull pattern** (see Principle #5)

---

### 4. The Elliptical Pull Pattern ⭐

**Revolutionary discovery:** ALL great swimmers pull in **single or double elliptical patterns**, NOT straight back.

**Why elliptical pulls work:**  
You constantly "get away from" water that you've already started backward and encounter **stationary water**.

**Counsilman's principle:**  
> "Maximum efficiency for propulsion in water is achieved by moving **a lot of water a short distance** versus moving **a little water a great distance**."

#### The Old vs. New Coaching Cue

**OLD (wrong):** "Grab a handful of water and keep that same handful, pushing it straight back."

**NEW (correct):** "Constantly find new water by moving your hand in an S-curve or hourglass pattern."

#### Elliptical Pattern Examples (All Four Strokes)

**Butterfly:** Double hourglass  
- Out-down-in-back (first loop)  
- Out and finish (second loop)

**Freestyle:** S-curve  
- Out-down-in-back-out  
- Single continuous ellipse

**Breaststroke:** Heart shape  
- Out-around-in-forward  
- Circular recovery integrated

**Backstroke:** Inverted S  
- Similar to freestyle, upside down  
- Out-down-in-back pattern

**Key insight:** The exact amount of "zigzag" varies by swimmer, but **all champions follow elliptical patterns**. Straight-back pulls don't exist at the elite level.

---

### 5. The Straight-Bent-Straight Pull ⭐

**Question:** Should elbows be straight or bent during the pull?

**Answer from above-water observation (WRONG):**  
Arms enter straight, exit straight, appear to pull straight → **straight arm pull**

**Answer from underwater filming (CORRECT):**  
All great swimmers use **bent elbow pulls**.

#### Why Bent Elbows Are Essential

**Straight arm pull problems:**  
1. Applies force in large semicircular sweeping movement  
2. Reaction pushes body in circular pattern  
3. **Test it yourself:** Lie on back, pull backstroke with straight arm → you swim in a circle

**Bent elbow advantages:**  
1. Directs propulsive force backward at desirable angle  
2. Still permits elliptical pattern  
3. Mechanically efficient leverage

#### The Progression: Straight → Bent → Straight

**Phase 1: Entry**  
- Elbow **straight** (or nearly so)

**Phase 2: First half of pull**  
- Elbow starts to bend  
- Reaches **maximum flexion of ~90°** at mid-pull

**Phase 3: Second half of pull**  
- Elbow bend decreases  
- Arm becomes **almost fully extended** as hand exits water

**This is true for ALL FOUR COMPETITIVE STROKES.**

**Memory cue:** "Straight-Bent-Straight" pull

---

### 6. The High Elbow Position ⭐

**Definition:** During the **first half of the pull**, the elbow must be held **higher than the hand**.

**Counsilman's research method:**  
Compared underwater films of poor swimmers vs. champions (Mark Spitz, Tim Shaw, etc.)

**Noticeable difference:**  
- **Great swimmers:** Carry elbows high during first half of pull  
- **Poor swimmers:** Drop elbows OR keep arms straight

#### How to Achieve High Elbow

**Two actions required:**

**1. Bend the elbow** (as described in Principle #5)

**2. Medially (inwardly) rotate the upper arm**  
This is the secret. The upper arm rotates inward at the shoulder joint, keeping the elbow pointing out/up while the hand presses down/back.

**Butterfly specific:** Medial rotation is **continued throughout the first half of the pull** (see figures in original text).

#### Why High Elbow Matters

**Mechanical advantage:**  
- Better leverage  
- More water displaced per stroke  
- Propulsive force directed backward (not downward)

**When elbow drops:**  
- Force pushes body UP, not forward  
- Less efficient propulsion  
- Wasted energy

**Visual test:**  
Film yourself underwater. If your elbow is below your hand during the first half of the pull, you're losing power.

## Hand Position & Pitch

### Entry Angle

**All strokes (except breaststroke):**  
Hands enter with palms facing **diagonally outward at ~45°**.

**Why 45°?**  
- Slices into water (less resistance)  
- Immediately begins elliptical pattern  
- Sets up high elbow position

### Pitch Changes During Pull

**Freestyle/Backstroke/Butterfly:**

**Entry:** Palms facing diagonally outward (45°)  
**Outward phase:** Palms remain at 45°  
**Inward phase:** Palms change to facing slightly inward  
**Finish:** Palms face almost directly backward

**Breaststroke (different pattern):**

**Start:** Palms facing outward  
**Mid-pull:** Palms facing inward/downward  
**Recovery:** Palms facing together (prayer position)  
**Extension:** Palms pitch outward again for next stroke

**Key principle:** Hand pitch **constantly changes** throughout the pull to maintain optimal angle against water flow.

## Practical Application: How to Learn From These Principles

### Self-Analysis Checklist

**Streamlining:**  
□ Is my head position causing leg drop?  
□ Are my hips wiggling sideways?  
□ Am I trying to "climb on top" instead of staying horizontal?

**Elliptical Pull:**  
□ Am I pulling in an S-curve or straight back?  
□ Am I constantly finding new water?  
□ Does my pull have an outward phase and inward phase?

**Bent Elbow:**  
□ Does my elbow start straight?  
□ Does it bend to ~90° at mid-pull?  
□ Does it extend again at the finish?

**High Elbow:**  
□ Is my elbow higher than my hand in the first half?  
□ Am I rotating my upper arm inward?  
□ Or am I dropping my elbow?

### Drills to Reinforce Principles

**High Elbow Catch (Freestyle/Backstroke):**  
- Swim with fists closed  
- Forces you to use forearm for propulsion  
- Automatically raises elbow

**Sculling Drills:**  
- Practice elliptical hand movements while stationary  
- Feel the pressure on your palms as you "find new water"  
- Builds kinesthetic awareness

**Single Arm Drill:**  
- Swim with one arm, other at side  
- Watch the pulling arm's path underwater  
- Check: Does it go straight or in an S-curve?

**Underwater Video Analysis:**  
- Film yourself from below (GoPro mounted on pool bottom)  
- Compare to Counsilman's diagrams  
- Check elbow position, pull pattern, body angle

## Common Coaching Mistakes (Based on Above-Water Observation)

### Mistake #1: Teaching Straight-Back Pulls

**Why coaches taught this:**  
Above water, it **looks** like arms enter and exit in a straight line backward.

**Why it's wrong:**  
Underwater filming proves champions use elliptical patterns.

**Counsilman's warning:**  
"If you do not understand why the swimmer should bend his elbow during the pull, you may consciously try to pull with a straight elbow and might recommend the technique to other people."

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Hand Pitch

**Why it happens:**  
Coaches focus on arm path, not hand orientation.

**Why it matters:**  
Hand pitch determines **angle of attack** against water. Wrong pitch = wasted energy.

**Solution:**  
Teach swimmers to **feel** water pressure on palms. If they don't feel resistance, their pitch is wrong.

### Mistake #3: Allowing Dropped Elbows

**Why it happens:**  
Swimmers naturally want to pull straight down (feels powerful).

**Why it's wrong:**  
Downward force lifts body UP, not forward.

**Solution:**  
High elbow drills + underwater video feedback.

## The Biomechanics Behind the Principles

### Why Elliptical Pulls Create More Propulsion

**Fluid dynamics principle:** **Bernoulli's Effect** + **Vortex Shedding**

When your hand moves through water in an elliptical path:  
1. Creates **vortices** (spinning water) behind hand  
2. Vortices generate **lift force** (perpendicular to hand movement)  
3. Combined with **drag force** (parallel to hand movement)  
4. **Resultant force** vector points backward → propels you forward

**Straight-back pull:**  
Only generates drag force, no lift component.

**Elliptical pull:**  
Generates BOTH drag and lift → **greater total propulsion**.

### Why Bent Elbows Are Mechanically Superior

**Lever arm analysis:**

**Straight arm:**  
- Long lever = high force required at shoulder  
- Mechanical disadvantage  
- Tires shoulder muscles quickly

**Bent elbow (~90°):**  
- Short lever = less force required  
- Mechanical advantage  
- Can sustain effort longer  
- Allows forearm to act as additional propulsive surface

**Counsilman's insight:**  
The forearm + hand together create a **larger paddle** than hand alone. Bent elbow keeps this paddle perpendicular to water flow.

## How This Applies to All Four Strokes

### Freestyle

**Streamlining:** Head down 45°, body rotation on long axis  
**Elliptical pull:** S-curve (out-down-in-back-out)  
**Bent elbow:** Straight → 90° → straight  
**High elbow:** Critical during catch and mid-pull  
**Hand pitch:** 45° entry → inward mid-pull → backward finish

### Backstroke

**Streamlining:** Head back, eyes up, hips near surface  
**Elliptical pull:** Inverted S (similar to freestyle)  
**Bent elbow:** Straight → 90° → straight  
**High elbow:** Elbow higher than hand (upside-down high elbow)  
**Hand pitch:** Pinky enters first, palm faces outward → backward

### Butterfly

**Streamlining:** Undulation (sine wave), not up-and-down bobbing  
**Elliptical pull:** Double hourglass (keyhole pattern)  
**Bent elbow:** Straight → 90° → straight  
**High elbow:** **Critical** — medial rotation throughout first half  
**Hand pitch:** 45° entry → inward → backward finish

### Breaststroke

**Streamlining:** Glide position, minimize head lift  
**Elliptical pull:** Heart shape (out-around-in)  
**Bent elbow:** Straight → 90° → straight (recovery is different)  
**High elbow:** Elbows stay high, don't pull past shoulders  
**Hand pitch:** Outward → downward/inward → together (prayer)

**Breaststroke note:** Recovery is forward (arms extend) vs. over-water in other strokes. Same principles apply to pulling phase.

## The Legacy: Why This Still Matters in 2026

**Counsilman published this in 1977.** Nearly 50 years later:

✅ **Still true:** Elliptical pulls, bent elbows, high elbows  
✅ **Still taught:** Every elite coach uses these principles  
✅ **Still effective:** No revolutionary technique has replaced them

**What's changed:**  
- Better underwater video (4K, slow-mo)  
- More precise biomechanical analysis  
- Refinements to hand pitch and timing

**What hasn't changed:**  
The fundamental physics of propulsion through water.

**Counsilman's contribution:**  
He took swimming from **art** (coached by feel and tradition) to **science** (coached by physics and evidence).

**For modern coaches:**  
These principles are your **foundation**. Build on them, don't ignore them.
