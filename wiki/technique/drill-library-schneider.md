# Drill Library: Terri Schneider's Core Drills

**Source:** *The Swimmer's Workout Handbook* by Terri Schneider  
**Context:** Essential drill definitions used across 100 structured workouts  
**Audience:** Coaches writing workouts, swimmers learning proper technique

---

## Overview: Why These Drills Matter

Schneider's 100 workouts reference **9 core drills** repeatedly. These aren't exotic — they're **fundamental building blocks** that every coach should know and every swimmer should master.

**Key principle:** These drills are designed to:
1. **Isolate** specific stroke mechanics
2. **Exaggerate** movement to build awareness
3. **Progress** from simple → complex → full stroke

**Coaching note:** Drills should be done at **L2 intensity** (conversational pace) unless otherwise specified. The goal is **technical mastery**, not fitness.

---

## The 9 Core Drills

### 1. Catch-Up Drill

**What it is:** Recovery arm 'catches up' with the extended arm at the top of the stroke before the extended arm pulls through.

**Stroke:** Freestyle

**How to perform:**
1. Push off, one arm extended in front (streamline position)
2. Pull with extended arm through full stroke cycle
3. As pulling arm recovers, keep other arm extended in front
4. **Wait** until recovering arm "catches up" and touches extended hand
5. Now pull with opposite arm
6. Repeat

**What it teaches:**
- **Front quadrant swimming** — Always have one arm out front
- **Patient hand entry** — Don't rush the stroke
- **Body rotation timing** — Rotate with the pull, stabilize during recovery
- **Streamline position** — Extended arm holds streamline while other arm works

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ Not waiting for catch-up (just regular freestyle)
- ❌ Dropping extended arm while waiting
- ❌ Holding breath (breathe normally every stroke or every other)
- ❌ Rushing through the drill

**Progression:**
1. **Week 1:** Catch-up with pause (full stop between strokes)
2. **Week 2:** Catch-up with touch (just touch hands, no pause)
3. **Week 3:** Near catch-up (hands get close but don't touch)
4. **Week 4:** Front quadrant freestyle (no catch-up, but always one arm forward)

**Sample sets:**
```
100 catch-up drill
4×50: 25 catch-up drill/25 freestyle w/:15R
2×100: 50 catch-up drill/50 freestyle w/:15R
```

**Cross-reference:** See [Drill Fundamentals: Lucero](drill-fundamentals-lucero.md) for progression-based drill theory.

### 2. 6-3-6 Drill

**What it is:** 6-beat kick on left side with left arm extended, 3 strokes, 6-beat kick on right side with right arm extended. Repeat.

**Stroke:** Freestyle

**How to perform:**
1. Push off on left side, left arm extended forward, right arm at side
2. **6-beat kick** on left side (count: 1-2-3-4-5-6)
3. Take **3 full freestyle strokes** (pull-breathe-recover)
4. After 3rd stroke, rotate to right side
5. **6-beat kick** on right side (right arm extended, left at side)
6. Take **3 strokes** back to left side
7. Repeat

**What it teaches:**
- **Body rotation** — Practice rotating from side to side
- **Streamlined kick** — Kick in streamline position
- **Balance** — Hold position on side without rolling over
- **Breathing pattern** — Breathe during 3-stroke segment
- **Side-to-side symmetry** — Equal time/effort on both sides

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ Only kicking 3-4 beats instead of 6 (count out loud if needed)
- ❌ Not rotating fully to side (should be 90° on side, not flat)
- ❌ Head up out of water (one goggle in, one goggle out)
- ❌ Kicking from knees instead of hips

**Variations:**
- **10-3-10:** Longer kick segment for more core/kick work
- **6-1-6:** Single stroke between kick segments (more difficult balance)
- **6-6-6:** Six strokes between kick segments (smoother transition to full freestyle)

**Sample sets:**
```
2×50 6-3-6 drill w/:15R
4×50 6-3-6 drill w/:10R
100: 50 6-3-6 drill/50 freestyle
8×25 alternate: 6-3-6 drill/freestyle w/:10R
```

### 3. Finger Tip Drag Drill

**What it is:** Freestyle with exaggerated high bent-elbow recovery and relaxed lower arm, causing fingertips to drag along the top of the water.

**Stroke:** Freestyle

**How to perform:**
1. Swim freestyle normally
2. During recovery, **keep elbow high** (leading the hand)
3. Let hand/fingers relax and **drag along water surface**
4. Fingertips should literally skim/drag on water
5. Entry and pull phase = normal

**What it teaches:**
- **High elbow recovery** — Elbow leads, hand follows
- **Relaxed recovery** — Lower arm/hand should be loose, not tense
- **Proper recovery path** — Hand travels close to body, not wide
- **Feel** — Tactile feedback (fingertips on water) reinforces correct path

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ Lifting entire arm out of water (defeats purpose)
- ❌ Tense/rigid hand (should be relaxed/floppy)
- ❌ Wide recovery (hand swinging out to side)
- ❌ Low elbow (elbow below hand)

**Why it works:**
- **Exaggeration principle** — By forcing fingertips to drag, you ensure elbow is high
- **Self-correcting** — If hand stops dragging, you know elbow dropped
- **Sensory feedback** — Feel of water on fingertips = instant confirmation

**Sample sets:**
```
100 finger tip drag drill
4×50: 25 finger tip drag drill/25 freestyle w/:15R
200 alternate: 25 finger tip drag drill/25 freestyle
2×100: 50 finger tip drag drill/50 freestyle w/:10R
```

**Progression to full stroke:**
1. Finger tip drag drill (full drag)
2. Fingertips *almost* touching water (same path, less exaggeration)
3. Full freestyle with high elbow (no drag, but same recovery path)

### 4. Fist Drill

**What it is:** Freestyle with each hand in a fist (fingers curled, thumb outside or inside fist).

**Stroke:** Freestyle

**How to perform:**
1. Make fists with both hands (thumb can be outside or tucked in)
2. Swim freestyle normally with closed fists
3. Focus on using **forearm** to feel/catch water
4. After set distance, open hands → instant increase in propulsion

**What it teaches:**
- **Forearm propulsion** — Catch with forearm, not just hand
- **Early vertical forearm (EVF)** — High elbow catch using whole forearm as paddle
- **Feel for the water** — Remove hand surface area → must use forearm
- **Appreciation for open hand** — When you open fist, hand feels HUGE

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ Dropping elbow to compensate (defeats purpose — keep elbow high!)
- ❌ Only using fingertips/knuckles (need to feel forearm pressing water)
- ❌ Swimming too fast (go slower, focus on catch)
- ❌ Not contrasting with open hand after drill

**Why it works:**
- **Sensory deprivation** — Remove the hand → forces awareness of forearm
- **Neural adaptation** — When you open hands, brain recruits forearm AND hand
- **Contrast training** — Fist drill → regular = everything feels easier/more propulsive

**Sample sets:**
```
100 fist drill
50 fist drill
2×100: 50 fist drill/50 freestyle w/:15R
4×50: 25 fist drill/25 stroke count w/:10R
8×25 alternate: fist drill/freestyle w/:10R
```

**Progression:**
1. Fist drill (full closed fist)
2. Half-fist (fingers curled but not tight)
3. Open hand with EVF focus (apply fist drill feeling to regular stroke)

### 5. Single Arm Drill (Left Arm / Right Arm)

**What it is:** Swimming freestyle with just one arm while the other arm is either extended overhead or at your side.

**Stroke:** Freestyle

**How to perform:**

**Left Arm Drill:**
1. Push off with left arm extended in front (streamline)
2. Right arm stays extended overhead OR rests at side
3. Pull with left arm only (full stroke cycle)
4. Breathe to right side (away from working arm) or bilaterally
5. Repeat for designated distance

**Right Arm Drill:**
- Same process, opposite arm

**Variations:**
- **Extended arm overhead** (easier, more streamlined)
- **Resting arm at side** (harder, mimics breathing position, tests balance)

**What it teaches:**
- **Individual arm mechanics** — Isolate and perfect each arm separately
- **Body rotation** — Must rotate to breathe without opposite arm for balance
- **Catch timing** — When does catch begin? Can you feel it?
- **Symmetry awareness** — Is one arm stronger/smoother than the other?
- **Balance** — Hard to stay streamlined with only one arm working

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ Not rotating body (swimming flat)
- ❌ Holding breath (must breathe regularly)
- ❌ Rushing the stroke (slow down, focus on mechanics)
- ❌ Dropping non-working arm (if extended, keep it streamlined)

**Sample sets:**
```
100; 25 left arm drill/25 right arm drill/50 freestyle
2×100: 25 right arm drill/25 left arm drill/50 freestyle w/:10R
3×100: 25 left arm drill/25 right arm drill/50 freestyle w/:15R
4×75: 25 right arm drill/25 left arm drill/25 freestyle w/:15R
```

**Coaching notes:**
- **Identify weak side** — Most swimmers have one arm significantly weaker
- **Extra reps on weak side** — If left is weaker, do 2 left : 1 right ratio
- **Progress to full stroke** — After single arm drill, full freestyle should feel coordinated

### 6. Head-Up Drill (Water Polo Freestyle)

**What it is:** Freestyle with the head slightly above the water line, eyes forward (like water polo players).

**Stroke:** Freestyle

**How to perform:**
1. Swim freestyle with head lifted so eyes look forward
2. Chin at or just above water surface
3. Keep stroke rate high (this is hard work!)
4. Maintain strong kick to keep hips up

**What it teaches:**
- **Core strength** — Lifting head drops hips → core must work to maintain position
- **Kick strength** — Need powerful kick to compensate for increased drag
- **Arm strength** — Higher body position = more resistance
- **Sighting skill** — Essential for open water swimming
- **Power development** — This is a resistance/strength drill

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ Head too high (should be *just* above water)
- ❌ Letting hips drop (defeats purpose)
- ❌ Slow stroke rate (need fast turnover to maintain speed)
- ❌ Holding breath (breathe by dropping chin, then lifting again)

**Applications:**
- **Open water training** — Practice sighting
- **Sprint sets** — Builds strength/power
- **Water polo swimmers** — Sport-specific
- **Contrast training** — Head up drill → regular freestyle = body feels lighter

**Sample sets:**
```
4×25 head up drill w/:10R
100; 25 head up drill/75 freestyle
3×50: 25 head up drill/25 freestyle w/:15R
```

**Note:** This is a **high-intensity drill** — can be done at L3-L4, not just L2.

### 7. Stroke Count Drill

**What it is:** Count how many strokes you take per 25 yards/meters. Objective is to decrease this number over time.

**Stroke:** Freestyle (primarily), but can be applied to any stroke

**How to perform:**
1. Swim 25 yards/meters
2. **Count every stroke** (one count per arm pull)
   - Some count each arm (1-2-3-4...)
   - Some count both arms as one cycle (1 = right + left)
   - **Pick one method and stay consistent**
3. Note your stroke count at wall
4. Goal: reduce count by 1-2 strokes over weeks/months

**What it teaches:**
- **Efficiency** — Fewer strokes = better distance per stroke (DPS)
- **Stroke length** — Longer stroke = fewer strokes
- **Glide/extension** — Don't rush, maximize each pull
- **Technique awareness** — Forces focus on efficiency, not just speed

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ Gliding too much (losing speed for lower count)
- ❌ Not counting off the walls (some people cheat by starting count mid-pool)
- ❌ Inconsistent counting (switching between per-arm vs. per-cycle)
- ❌ Only focusing on count, ignoring pace

**Ideal stroke count (freestyle, 25 yards):**
- **Elite swimmers:** 8-12 strokes
- **Competitive swimmers:** 12-16 strokes
- **Fitness swimmers:** 16-22 strokes
- **Beginners:** 20-30 strokes

**Key balance:** Stroke count AND pace
- Low stroke count with slow pace = inefficient glide
- High stroke count with fast pace = inefficient turnover
- **Goal:** Lowest stroke count at fastest sustainable pace

**Sample sets:**
```
4×50: 25 stroke count/25 freestyle w/:10R
4×25 stroke count w/:10R (try to decrease by 1 each rep)
6×75: 25 stroke count/50 freestyle w/:15R
100; 50 finger tip drag drill/50 stroke count
```

**Progression:**
1. **Week 1:** Establish baseline (e.g., 18 strokes per 25)
2. **Week 2-4:** Focus on extension/glide → reduce to 16-17
3. **Week 5-8:** Add drills (catch-up, fist) to improve catch → reduce to 15-16
4. **Week 9-12:** Maintain low count while increasing pace

### 8. Kick Variations

Schneider's workouts include several kick variations. Here's the breakdown:

#### Flutter Kick (Default)
**When to use:** Freestyle and backstroke  
**How:** Legs alternate, toes pointed, kick from hips (not knees), small/fast kicks

**Common in sets:**
```
100 kick (L2) — default to flutter kick unless specified
200 kick (L3)
6×50 kick (L3) w/:10R
```

#### Dolphin Kick
**When to use:** Butterfly, underwater (all strokes)  
**How:** Legs move together in wave-like motion, power from hips, two kicks per butterfly stroke cycle

**Common in sets:**
```
4×25 kick (butterfly style)
100 kick; 25 underwater dolphin/75 flutter
```

#### Breaststroke Kick (Frog Kick / Whip Kick)
**When to use:** Breaststroke  
**How:** Knees about body-width apart, heels to butt, whip feet out and back together, toes turned out

**Common in sets:**
```
4×50: 25 backstroke/25 breaststroke kick
150: 50 freestyle/50 breaststroke kick/50 freestyle
```

#### Back Kick
**When to use:** Backstroke  
**How:** Flutter kick on your back

**Common in sets:**
```
4×50 kick: 25 back/25 choice of stroke w/:20R
100 kick; 10 beats on each side (back kick, side position)
```

#### Kick on Side (10 Beats on Each Side)
**What it is:** Kick 10 beats on left side, rotate, kick 10 beats on right side, repeat

**How to perform:**
1. Push off on left side, left arm extended, right arm at side
2. Kick 10 beats (flutter kick) while maintaining side position
3. Rotate to right side
4. Kick 10 beats on right side
5. Repeat

**What it teaches:** Same benefits as 6-3-6 drill, but more kick-focused

**Common in sets:**
```
100 kick: 10 beats on each side (L2)
200 IM: 10 beat kicks on each side
```

#### Kick No Board
**What it is:** Kicking as designated, without a kickboard, arms extended overhead in streamline

**Why:** More realistic body position (mimics actual swimming), harder on core

**Common in sets:**
```
100 kick no board (L2)
4×50: 25 kick on back no board/25 stroke your choice w/:10R
```

### 9. Pull (with Pull Buoy)

**What it is:** Freestyle with a pull buoy between your thighs (no kick) and optional paddles.

**How to perform:**
1. Place pull buoy between upper thighs
2. Swim freestyle using only arms (legs float behind, don't kick)
3. Optional: add paddles for increased resistance

**What it teaches:**
- **Upper body focus** — Isolate arm stroke without leg propulsion
- **Body position** — Pull buoy lifts hips, simulates good position
- **Arm strength** — All propulsion from arms
- **Breathing pattern** — Common to practice bilateral or breathe-every-3

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ Squeezing buoy with knees (keep legs relaxed)
- ❌ Kicking (defeats purpose)
- ❌ Using only paddles without buoy (not pull, just paddle work)

**Variations:**
- **Pull + paddles** — Strength/power focus
- **Pull + breathe every 3rd** — Aerobic + breathing pattern
- **Pull locomotion** — See next section

**Common in sets:**
```
200 pull (L3)
300 pull (L3)
2×200 pull (L4) w/:30R
100 pull, 200 freestyle, 300 pull (L4) w/:30R
```

## Special Set Formats Using Drills

### Locomotion

**What it is:** Sprint/easy pattern that escalates in distance

**For 300 Locomotion:**
- 25 sprint / 25 easy
- 50 sprint / 50 easy
- 75 sprint / 75 easy
- **Total = 300**

**For 500 Locomotion:**
- 25 sprint / 25 easy
- 50 sprint / 50 easy
- 75 sprint / 75 easy
- 100 sprint / 100 easy
- **Total = 500**

**Common in sets:**
```
300 freestyle locomotion
300 pull locomotion
500 pull locomotion
```

**What it teaches:**
- **Speed changes** — Sprint → easy → sprint
- **Lactate tolerance** — Repeated surges
- **Mental toughness** — Distance increases each round

---

### Descend the Set

**What it is:** Progressively increase speed with each interval in a set.

**Example:**
```
4×100 freestyle descend the set w/:20R
  #1: Moderate (e.g., 1:30)
  #2: Faster (1:25)
  #3: Faster (1:20)
  #4: Fastest (1:15)
```

**What it teaches:**
- **Pacing** — Start controlled, build speed
- **Negative splitting** — Swim faster in later reps
- **Mental discipline** — Don't go out too hard

---

### Build / Build Each

**What it is:**
- **Build:** Progressively increase speed within one interval (start easy, finish fast)
- **Build each:** Progressively increase speed of each interval in a set

**Example (Build):**
```
4×50 freestyle build w/:15R
  Each 50: first 25 easy, second 25 fast
```

**Example (Build Each):**
```
2×100 freestyle build each w/:15R
  #1: 1:30
  #2: 1:25 (faster than #1)
```

---

### Fastest Average

**What it is:** Swim the fastest pace you can hold for all reps in the set (not just one fast swim).

**Example:**
```
6×100 freestyle fastest average w/:40R
```

**Goal:** All 6 should be within 2-3 seconds of each other, at your best sustainable pace.

**NOT:**
- Rep 1: 1:10 (too fast, unsustainable)
- Rep 6: 1:25 (blew up)

**YES:**
- All reps: 1:18-1:20 (consistent, best average)

## Drill Progression Framework

### Week 1-2: Isolation Drills
**Focus:** Learn each drill separately
- 100 catch-up drill
- 100 6-3-6 drill
- 100 fist drill
- 100 finger tip drag drill

**Goal:** Can perform drill correctly for 100+ continuous

---

### Week 3-4: Drill + Swim Contrast
**Focus:** Connect drill to full stroke

**Format:**
```
4×50: 25 drill/25 freestyle w/:15R
6×50: 25 catch-up drill/25 freestyle w/:10R
8×25 alternate: drill/freestyle w/:10R
```

**Goal:** Transfer drill feeling to regular swimming

---

### Week 5-8: Multiple Drills per Workout
**Focus:** Combine drills for comprehensive technique work

**Format:**
```
WARM-UP
200 choice (L2)
100 kick (L2)

DRILL SET
2×50 catch-up drill w/:10R
2×50 6-3-6 drill w/:10R
2×50 fist drill w/:10R
2×50 finger tip drag drill w/:10R
```

**Goal:** Build drill vocabulary, use different drills for different stroke issues

---

### Week 9-12: Targeted Drill Application
**Focus:** Use specific drills to fix specific problems

**Problem:** Overgliding, slow stroke rate  
**Solution:** Catch-up drill (teaches front quadrant without dead glide)

**Problem:** Low elbow recovery  
**Solution:** Finger tip drag drill (forces high elbow)

**Problem:** Hand-only catch (not using forearm)  
**Solution:** Fist drill (removes hand, forces forearm awareness)

**Problem:** One-sided stroke  
**Solution:** Single arm drill (isolate weak arm)

**Problem:** Poor body rotation  
**Solution:** 6-3-6 drill (practice rotating side to side)

## Actionable Coaching Applications

### For Beginners: Master 3 Core Drills First

**Drill 1: Catch-Up**  
**Why:** Teaches streamline + patience  
**Timeline:** 2-3 weeks to mastery

**Drill 2: 6-3-6**  
**Why:** Teaches rotation + balance  
**Timeline:** 3-4 weeks to mastery

**Drill 3: Finger Tip Drag**  
**Why:** Teaches high elbow recovery  
**Timeline:** 2-3 weeks to mastery

**Progression:**
```
Week 1: Introduce catch-up (100+ continuous)
Week 2: Contrast catch-up with freestyle (4×50: 25 drill/25 swim)
Week 3: Introduce 6-3-6 (50+ continuous)
Week 4: Catch-up + 6-3-6 in same workout
Week 5: Introduce finger tip drag
Week 6: All 3 drills in same workout
```

---

### For Intermediate: Use Drills as Diagnostic Tools

**Process:**
1. Identify stroke flaw (via video or coach feedback)
2. Select appropriate drill
3. Prescribe high-volume drill work (400-800 in a session)
4. Test improvement with timed swim

**Example:**

**Flaw detected:** Swimmer's catch is late (arm enters, glides, THEN catches)

**Drill prescription:**
```
DRILL SET (800 total)
4×50 fist drill w/:10R (feel forearm catch)
4×100: 50 catch-up drill/50 freestyle w/:15R (connect drill to full stroke)
200 freestyle stroke count (did efficiency improve?)
```

**Retest:** 4×50 freestyle on interval — is catch happening earlier?

---

### For Advanced: Layer Drills with Intensity

**Concept:** Drills aren't just for warm-up — integrate them into main sets at higher intensities.

**Example:**
```
MAIN SET
4×100 w/:20R
  Odd (1,3): 50 fist drill/50 freestyle (L4)
  Even (2,4): 100 freestyle (L5)
```

**Result:** Drill work under fatigue = tests ability to hold technique when tired (race-specific)

---

### For Teams: Build Drill Literacy

**Goal:** Every swimmer knows all 9 core drills by name and can execute on command.

**Method:**
- **Week 1:** Introduce 2 drills (catch-up, 6-3-6)
- **Week 2:** Add 2 more (fist, finger tip drag)
- **Week 3:** Add 2 more (single arm, stroke count)
- **Week 4:** Add final 3 (head-up, kick variations, pull)

**Test:** "Coaches call out random drill during warm-up — swimmers execute immediately."

**Result:** Coaches can write efficient workouts ("4×50 drill choice w/:10R") and swimmers self-select appropriate drill for their needs.

## Sample Drill-Heavy Workouts

### Workout 1: Technique Focus (1500m)
```
WARM-UP
200 choice (L2)
100 kick (L2)

DRILL SET
4×50: 25 catch-up drill/25 freestyle w/:15R
4×50: 25 finger tip drag drill/25 freestyle w/:15R
4×50: 25 6-3-6 drill/25 freestyle w/:15R

SWIM SET
300 freestyle stroke count (L2)

COOL-DOWN
100 choice (L1)

TOTAL: 1500
```

---

### Workout 2: Drill Progression (2000m)
```
WARM-UP
300 choice (L2)

DRILL PROGRESSION
100 fist drill (feel forearm)
100 catch-up drill (front quadrant)
100 finger tip drag drill (high elbow)
100 freestyle stroke count (check efficiency)

MAIN SET
4×100 freestyle (L4) w/:20R — apply drill learnings

COOL-DOWN
200 pull (L2)
100 choice (L1)

TOTAL: 2000
```

---

### Workout 3: Drill + Speed (2500m)
```
WARM-UP
200 choice (L2)
200 kick (L2)

DRILL SET
2×100: 50 catch-up/50 6-3-6 drill w/:15R
2×100: 50 fist drill/50 finger tip drag drill w/:15R

SPEED SET
6×50 freestyle (L5) w/:30R — MAX effort with good technique
300 pull (L3)

DRILL CONTRAST
4×25 single arm drill (left/right alternating) w/:10R
4×25 freestyle (L4) w/:10R — feel both arms working together

COOL-DOWN
200 choice (L2)

TOTAL: 2500
```
