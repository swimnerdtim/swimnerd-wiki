# Freestyle Breathing Mechanics

**Source:** Bob Bowman (Michael Phelps' coach)  
**Context:** Technical breakdown of elite breathing form  
**Athlete examples:** Michael Phelps, Katie Ledecky

## The Fundamental Rule

**Head moves WITH the body to take the breath. Never independently.**

### What NOT to Do
❌ **Lifting the chin** to breathe  
❌ **Looking forward** to breathe  
❌ **Looking to the side** of the pool  
❌ **"Checking the weather"** (looking up at ceiling)  
❌ **Over-rotating** (both goggles visible)

### What TO Do
✅ **Rotate head in line with body**  
✅ **Take breath in natural body rotation**  
✅ **One goggle in the water**  
✅ **Breathe inside the bow wave**

---

## The Bow Wave Phenomenon

### What Is It?
When you swim, your head creates a **bow wave** - a small trough of water that forms alongside your head as you move forward.

**Key insight:** You're not breathing air above the water - you're breathing **inside the bow wave**.

### Proper Breathing Position
- **One goggle out of water**
- **One goggle in water**
- **About half your mouth visible**
- **Full mouth is actually inside the bow wave**

**Visual check:** If you can see your whole face or both goggles when breathing → you're over-rotating

---

## Case Study: Michael Phelps

### Phelps Breathing Technique
**Watch for:**
- One goggle out of water
- Half his mouth visible
- Breathing inside bow wave
- Head rotation matches body rotation

**Result:** Minimal drag, maximum efficiency, no disruption to stroke rhythm

---

## Case Study: Katie Ledecky

### The Over-Rotation Issue
**Problem Bowman identified:**
- Katie sometimes over-rotates on breath
- Both goggles visible
- Head rotating more than body

### The Fix
**Coaching cue:** "Just have one goggle showing and minimize head rotation"

**Goal:** Head and body move together as one unit

---

## Breathing Drills

### Drill 1: One-Goggle Check
**Setup:** Have swimmer breathe, coach watches from front/side  
**Success:** Only one goggle visible during breath  
**Failure:** Both goggles visible = over-rotation

### Drill 2: Bow Wave Awareness
**Setup:** Slow swimming, focus on feeling the trough  
**Cue:** "Breathe in the pocket, not above it"  
**Success:** Swimmer reports breathing feels "easier"

### Drill 3: No Weather Checks
**Setup:** Video from side angle  
**Cue:** "If I can see your whole face, you're checking the weather"  
**Fix:** Rotate less, keep one goggle down

## Common Mistakes by Level

### Age Group (8-12)
- Looking forward to see wall
- Lifting chin to "get more air"
- Not rotating body at all (just turning head)

### High School (13-18)
- Over-rotating to "get a better breath"
- Breathing every stroke when tired
- Inconsistent breathing patterns

### Masters
- Old habits from childhood (checking the weather)
- Over-rotation to compensate for flexibility issues
- Breathing too high when fatigued

---

## Coaching Progressions

### Step 1: Body Rotation First
Before teaching breathing, teach body rotation:
- Side-kicking drills
- 6-kick switch drill
- Rotation awareness without breath

### Step 2: Add Breathing to Rotation
- Breathe only when body is already rotated
- One-goggle rule
- Bow wave awareness

### Step 3: Speed Integration
- Can they maintain one-goggle breathing at race pace?
- Does it hold up when tired?
- Competition breathing patterns

---

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Diagnosis | Fix |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| Both goggles visible | Over-rotating head | Minimize head turn, maximize body rotation |
| Looking at ceiling | Lifting chin | Keep chin tucked, rotate in line |
| Looking forward | Not rotating | Side-kicking drills, rotation first |
| Gasping/struggling | Breathing too late in rotation | Earlier breath timing, find bow wave |
| Sinking hips | Lifting head too high | One-goggle rule, breathe lower |

## The Bowman Test

**"Can you breathe with one goggle in the water?"**

If yes → mechanically sound  
If no → head and body aren't moving together

---

## Integration with Full Stroke

### Race Application
- Michael Phelps: One-goggle breathing even in 200 fly finals
- Consistent at all speeds
- Doesn't change under fatigue
- Automatic, not thought about

### Training Application
- Enforce in warm-up (build habits)
- Enforce in quality sets (no excuses when tired)
- Enforce in easy swimming (practice doesn't make perfect, practice makes permanent)
