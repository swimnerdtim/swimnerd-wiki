# Freestyle Drill Library: 25 Essential Drills

**Source:** *The 100 Best Swimming Drills* by Blythe Lucero  
**Context:** Step-by-step drills organized by skill progression  
**Audience:** Coaches building practice plans & swimmers working on technique

---

## How to Use This Library

**Organization:** Drills grouped by stroke aspect (7 categories)
1. Body Position
2. Kick
3. Arm Stroke
4. Recovery
5. Breathing
6. Leverage
7. Coordination

**Drill types included:**
- **Progression** — Build stroke piece by piece
- **Part Practice** — Isolate specific flaw
- **Contrast** — Compare wrong vs. right
- **X Drills** — eXaggerate to feel the point

**Before using:** Read [Drill Fundamentals](../../methodology/drill-fundamentals-lucero.md) to understand drill philosophy.

## Body Position Drills (3)

**Goal:** Experience advantageous float & effective core stability.

### 1. Downhill Float ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Learn to shift weight to achieve "downhill" floating position
- Understand importance of correct head position
- Feel effective core tension and stability

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Float face down, arms at sides, head leading. No forward motion. Notice body position. For most people, legs will sink = "uphill" floating position.

**Step 2:** Lower chin, look at pool bottom (not forward). This simple action raises sinking legs.

**Step 3:** Focus on spine. Make it straight by contracting abdominals, pulling bellybutton in. Straight spine through core tension is essential.

**Step 4:** While holding core stable, **lean forward on chest.** Hips & legs rise toward surface = desirable "downhill" floating position.

**Step 5:** Stand, breathe, repeat horizontally with **arms extended over head**, hands leading. Look at bottom, straight spine, stable core, shift weight forward, feel "downhill" float.

**Common problems:**
- **Legs still sink:** For muscular/low body fat people, legs may sink no matter what. MORE important to learn "downhill" core position.
- **Can't balance on chest:** Rotate shoulders back, round chest out. Pretend standing at attention horizontally. Focus on breastbone moving gently lower.
- **Can't breathe:** This drill requires holding breath. When you run out, stand up and start again.

---

### 2. Log Roll

**Purpose:**
- Maintain horizontal axis while rolling
- Access power from core
- Get comfortable floating "downhill" in non-flat position

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Float face down, arms at sides, head leading. No forward motion. Achieve "downhill" float, look at pool bottom.

**Step 2:** Maintaining core tension & head position, roll onto RIGHT SIDE by rotating right hip & shoulder ¼ turn clockwise. Hold 5 seconds.

**Step 3:** Maintaining core tension & head position, roll onto BACK by rotating right hip & shoulder ¼ turn clockwise. Hold 5 seconds.

**Step 4:** Maintaining core tension & head position, roll onto LEFT SIDE by rotating right hip & shoulder ¼ turn clockwise. Hold 5 seconds.

**Step 5:** Maintaining core tension & head position, roll back onto FRONT by rotating right hip & shoulder ¼ turn clockwise. Hold 5 seconds.

**Step 6:** Stand, breathe, repeat rolling COUNTER-CLOCKWISE.

**Common problems:**
- **Can't get ¼ turn:** Initiate turn from hip, let shoulder follow.
- **Sinking legs get in way:** Can use very light kick if necessary.
- **Can't breathe:** Catch a breath when you rotate to your back.

---

### 3. Twelve Kick Switch Freestyle ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Maintain "downhill" float while moving
- Begin to feel leverage from core
- Identify longest freestyle position

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Float on side, top arm at side, bottom arm extended over head. Engage core tension for "downhill" float.

**Step 2:** Begin gentle but continuous flutter kick, directed SIDE TO SIDE (not up and down).

**Step 3:** If properly aligned, face will be mostly submerged. To breathe: maintain stable head position, roll toward back, breathe, roll back to original side position. Create regular breathing interval.

**Step 4:** Kick 12 times (each leg = 1 kick).

**Step 5:** Just as you finish kick #12, bring side-laying arm over water to front AND forward-reaching arm through water to side. Simultaneously switch to opposite side of body to float.

**Step 6:** Do 12 more kicks in this position. Repeat switch with arms and floating side. Continue kicking 12 then switching to far end of pool.

**Common problems:**
- **Go crooked:** Align body and reaching arm.
- **Kick not side to side:** Hips not aligned to side with shoulders. Use more core tension.
- **Can't maintain "downhill" float:** Check head position. During 12 kicks, lower ear should rest on shoulder of reaching arm.

## Kick Drills (4)

**Goal:** Learn economical flutter kick that enhances freestyle stroke.

### 4. Toe Point Drill ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Understand importance of pointed toe kick
- Experiment with foot pitch
- Develop centralized kick

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Lay horizontal, face down, arms extended. To breathe: simple press outward with hands, raise chin gently to surface, then return face to water and bring hands back together.

**Step 2:** Begin flutter kick, alternating up/down motion with leg. Kick to depth of 12-15 inches, up to surface. Kick briskly with relaxed but fairly straight legs.

**Step 3:** Focus on foot position. To best engage water, use largest surface of feet. **Position feet so toes are pointed.** Kick briskly 30 seconds. Feel tops AND bottoms of feet push water as feet sweep up and down.

**Step 4:** Now **flex feet at ankles**, setting them at right angles to leg (standing foot position). Kick briskly 30 seconds. Feel smaller surface pushing water. Notice you don't move forward well. Some can even move backwards!

**Step 5:** Reposition feet so **toes are pointed AND rotate knees/feet inward** so toes are closer together than heels. Together, feet should form a point. Kick briskly 30 seconds. Feel larger foot surface pressing water. Notice kick is much more effective with much less effort. One centralized splash at feet.

**Step 6:** Continue kicking with toes pointed and feet rotated inward. Kick briskly with relaxed feet. Feel kick move you forward as you sweep water down with tops of feet, up with bottoms. Kick to far end.

**Common problems:**
- **Huge splash, no matter what:** Splash should result from foot moving water, not bringing air down into water. Bend knees less. Keep feet connected to water.
- **Big toes bump when turned inward:** Excellent foot pitch! Adjust knee rotation slightly to avoid bumping, but as little as possible.
- **Cramps in arches:** Try pointing foot but letting toes relax. Not natural for humans. Improves with practice.

---

### 5. Floppy Foot Drill

**Purpose:**
- Develop relaxed kick
- Experience fish-like propulsion
- Learn to conserve energy

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Observe a fish swimming. Any fish. Notice tail as fish swims. NOT rigid — moves back and forth like a flag in breeze. Fluid. Not frantic — appears effortless.

**Step 2:** Put on single long swim fin on one foot. Stand in water, back against wall, submerging entire fin underwater. Sweep leg up/down in water covering ~24 inches each way. Use enough speed to feel resistance of fin against water. **Notice how water bends the fin as it moves.** When leg sweeps one direction, fin bends opposite way. Fluid, like fish tail.

**Step 3:** Take off fin. Float face down, arms extended. Begin kicking briskly, feet connected to water, moving within ~15 inches depth. Point toes, but **relax feet so water bends them like it did the fin.** Kick briskly 30 seconds. Think effortless, fluid fish tail motion. Let water move your foot like a flag in wind. Rest, practice again.

**Step 4:** Once you develop fluid, floppy foot kick, notice splash from kick is much more compact, and kick is more productive. Practice several lengths.

**Common problems:**
- **Feet are stiff:** Try achieving feeling on land first. Relax wrist, flop hand back/forth. Stand on one leg, relax other ankle, shake foot until it flops like hand. Sweep leg forward as if kicking ball, foot relaxed. Watch foot — by farthest extent of movement, relaxed foot should flop forward. Now try in water.
- **Don't feel resistance on feet:** Kick more briskly. Try changing depth of kick (~15 inches). Relax ankles.
- **Get tired too quickly:** Using large leg muscles initially uses more oxygen than less effective kick. Muscles adapt relatively quickly with practice.

---

### 6. Vertical Kicking Sequence ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Understand importance of foot speed
- Engage correct muscles for flutter kick
- Feel the water with feet

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** In water at least as deep as you are tall, get vertical. Engage core. Begin gentle sculling (tracing side-to-side underwater figure-eights with hands) to keep head above water.

**Step 2:** Without producing forward/backward motion, begin flutter kick. Move extended legs in sweeping motion alternately (one foot forward, other back) within range of ~12-15 inches. Toes pointed toward pool bottom. Feet and knees rotated slightly inward.

**Step 3:** Focus on muscles you're engaging. Should be LARGE MUSCLES, HIGH IN LEGS. Moving leg forward: feel quadriceps. Moving leg back: feel hamstrings & glutes.

**Step 4:** Legs fairly straight, not locked at knee. As you increase kick speed forward/backward, avoid raising knees as if marching or bicycling. Should feel like legs sweeping back/forth like a broom through water.

**Step 5:** Kick even faster. Achieve fluid motion making legs feel boneless. Maintain head up while kicking 30 seconds.

**Step 6:** Once you achieve fluid, comfortable kick, check effectiveness by stopping sculling. **Cross hands over chest** (left hand on right shoulder, right hand on left shoulder). Kick, keeping head above water. Maintain 30 seconds.

**Step 7:** Advanced: Position arms side-to-side at surface. Raise hands out of water, bending at right angle at elbow. Kick! Can you keep head up 30 seconds?

**Common problems:**
- **Sink even when sculling:** Use straighter legs. Point toes. Kick faster.
- **Bounce up and down:** Use narrower, quicker kicking motion, not scissor kick.
- **Feel kick in calves:** Relax feet more. Use less knee bend. Initiate kick from higher on leg where muscles are larger and stronger.

---

### 7. Single Leg Kicking

**Purpose:**
- Develop effective, full leg kick
- Feel continuous kick
- Keep foot connected to water

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Float face down, arms extended. Establish regular breathing pattern (press hands outward, lift chin to surface).

**Step 2:** Begin kicking with RIGHT LEG ONLY. Kick from hip, fairly straight leg, pointed relaxed toe. Move through ~15 inches depth at moderate speed. Keep left leg still, trailing behind.

**Step 3:** Focus on maintaining continuous kick with right leg. Don't pause at top or bottom. Sweep through water without breaking surface or kicking too deep. Feel resistance on top AND bottom of foot throughout kick cycle.

**Step 4:** Kick 25 yards or until fatigued. Stand, rest. Repeat with LEFT LEG ONLY.

**Step 5:** Compare legs. Notice which leg is stronger, which has better foot pitch, which maintains better connection to water.

**Step 6:** Alternate single-leg kicking for several lengths, working to make weaker leg match stronger leg.

**Common problems:**
- **Kicking leg pulls me in circle:** Stabilize core. Align kicking leg with spine. Use gentle guiding kick with non-kicking leg if necessary.
- **Lose rhythm:** Single-leg kicking is unnatural. Keep kick continuous and compact. Think "sweep through water" not "up and down."
- **Non-kicking leg sinks too much:** Engage core more. Can do drill with pull buoy between legs if necessary, but try without first.

## Arm Stroke Drills (5)

**Goal:** Maximize stroke length, move forward in straightest path, position hands to feel water.

### 8. Pull/Push Freestyle ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Understand two-part arm stroke
- Feel acceleration through stroke
- Learn proper hand path

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Swim regular freestyle. As your hand enters water in front, **think "PULL"** as your hand moves from entry to your shoulder. Feel yourself pulling your body over your anchored hand.

**Step 2:** As your hand passes your shoulder moving toward your hip, **think "PUSH"** as you press water toward your feet. Feel yourself pushing water away.

**Step 3:** Focus on the transition point at your shoulder. This is where PULL becomes PUSH. Notice hand accelerates from entry → shoulder → hip. Not constant speed — builds speed through stroke.

**Step 4:** Practice several lengths, thinking "PULL" from entry to shoulder, "PUSH" from shoulder to hip. Feel acceleration in each stroke.

**Step 5:** Exaggerate the two parts. Really PULL yourself over your hand in front. Really PUSH water toward your feet in back. Feel the difference between pulling yourself forward and pushing water backward.

**Common problems:**
- **Can't feel transition:** Transition point is approximately where hand is under shoulder. If hand passes under centerline of body, transition happens earlier (under chest). If hand stays wide, transition happens later (outside shoulder).
- **Feel like I'm slowing down mid-stroke:** Likely dropping elbow. Keep elbow high through entire stroke. Hand should be deeper than elbow throughout.
- **Arm stroke feels choppy:** Acceleration should be smooth and continuous, not abrupt. Gradually increase hand speed from entry to exit.

---

### 9. Catch Up ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Maximize stroke length
- Develop patient front-end timing
- Feel fully extended position

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Push off wall in streamline. Begin freestyle, but each hand must WAIT at full extension in front until the other hand completes its stroke and returns to meet it.

**Step 2:** As recovering hand approaches extended hand in front, **touch hands together** (or nearly touch). Only then can the waiting hand begin its stroke.

**Step 3:** Focus on the glide phase when both hands are extended in front. Feel yourself balanced on your chest, rotated to side, one arm extended, waiting. Feel longest body position.

**Step 4:** When hands meet in front, smoothly transition — one hand stays extended while other begins stroke. No pause. Fluid exchange.

**Step 5:** Practice several lengths. Gradually reduce the "touch" to hands just passing close to each other in front. Maintain the fully extended glide feeling.

**Variations:**
- **Catch up with pull buoy:** Isolates arms, removes kick variable
- **Catch up with fins:** Makes drill easier, helps maintain momentum during glide
- **3/4 Catch up:** Front hand doesn't wait for full catch-up, but waits until stroking hand reaches shoulder. More like regular freestyle timing.

**Common problems:**
- **Lose momentum during glide:** Normal. Kick continuously to maintain some forward motion. Can use fins.
- **Hands don't meet in front:** Likely over-rotating or entering too wide. Align reaching hand with shoulder, rotate just enough to extend fully.
- **Feel unbalanced during glide:** Engage core. Rotate to side, rest lower ear on shoulder of extended arm.

### 10. All Thumbs Drill (Contrast Drill)

**Purpose:**
- Learn correct hand pitch
- Feel difference between effective and ineffective catch
- Understand importance of fingertip-first entry

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Swim freestyle, but enter the water THUMB-FIRST with each hand. Point thumb down into water on entry. Stroke with thumb leading throughout entire stroke.

**Step 2:** Notice how much harder it is to grab water. Notice hand slips through water with minimal resistance. This is INEFFECTIVE hand pitch. Swim 25 yards thumb-first.

**Step 3:** Now swim freestyle entering FINGERTIPS-FIRST, hand pitched slightly outward (thumb slightly higher than pinkie). Feel immediate difference. Hand grabs water, doesn't slip. Stroke has power.

**Step 4:** Alternate laps: 25 thumb-first (ineffective), 25 fingertip-first (effective). Contrast teaches you to choose better hand pitch.

**Common problems:**
- **Can't tell difference:** Exaggerate thumb-first even more. Point thumb aggressively down. Then exaggerate fingertip-first with hand pitched quite outward. Difference should be obvious.
- **Both feel the same:** Likely already entering correctly, making thumb-first feel impossible. Good problem to have!

### 11. Sculling ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Develop feel for water
- Learn to hold water with hand
- Understand propulsive hand movements

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Float face down, arms extended in front. Press palms outward (like spreading water to sides) until hands are ~18 inches apart. Then immediately press palms inward (like clapping) until hands are ~6 inches apart. Repeat continuously.

**Step 2:** Keep elbows still. Only forearms and hands move. Hands trace figure-eight pattern underwater. Pitch hands so palms always press against water (thumb up on outward sweep, thumb down on inward sweep).

**Step 3:** Feel resistance on palms throughout sculling motion. This is "holding water." Your hands are finding solid water to press against. This pressure should move you forward (though slowly).

**Step 4:** Practice three sculling positions:
- **Front scull:** Arms extended in front (as described above)
- **Middle scull:** Arms at sides with elbows bent 90°, forearms parallel to pool bottom, hands at chest level
- **Rear scull:** Arms at sides, hands sculling near hips

**Step 5:** Connect sculling motion to freestyle stroke. Notice that parts of your stroke path include similar pressing-outward and pressing-inward motions. You're sculling as part of your stroke!

**Common problems:**
- **Don't move forward:** Increase hand speed. Make sure palms are pitched to press against water, not slice through it. Elbows should stay relatively still.
- **Hands too deep:** Sculling should happen in top 12-18 inches of water. Hands too deep = ineffective.
- **Arms tired quickly:** Sculling uses muscles differently than regular swimming. Start with short intervals (15-30 seconds), rest, repeat.

### 12. Fist Freestyle

**Purpose:**
- Develop forearm propulsion
- Increase reliance on whole arm (not just hand)
- Improve elbow position

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Make fists with both hands. Swim regular freestyle with closed fists for entire length.

**Step 2:** Notice that without ability to use palms to press water, you must use FOREARMS to generate propulsion. Feel water pressing against forearms.

**Step 3:** To maximize forearm engagement, keep ELBOWS HIGH throughout stroke. High elbow = more forearm surface area pressing water.

**Step 4:** Swim 25 yards fist freestyle. Rest. Then swim 25 yards regular freestyle (hands open). Notice heightened awareness of forearm propulsion even with hands open.

**Step 5:** Alternate: 25 fist, 25 regular for several lengths.

**Advanced variation:** One fist, one regular hand (alternating which hand is fist each length).

**Common problems:**
- **Go nowhere with fists:** Keep elbows high! Dropped elbow = no forearm surface to press water. May need to slow stroke rate initially.
- **Forearms don't feel anything:** Focus on pressing water with entire arm surface from fingertips (fist) to elbow. Angle forearm to maximize water contact.
- **Shoulders hurt:** Don't force it. Fist swimming changes mechanics. Start with short distances, build gradually.

## Recovery Drills (2)

**Goal:** Relax during recovery, align recovery, balance the stroke.

### 13. Floppy Hand

**Purpose:**
- Learn to relax during recovery
- Conserve energy
- Prevent shoulder tension

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Swim freestyle. As your hand exits water at your hip and begins recovery, **completely relax your hand and wrist.** Let hand and fingers dangle loosely.

**Step 2:** As arm swings forward during recovery, let relaxed hand flop and swing naturally. Don't control it. Let gravity and momentum move it.

**Step 3:** Only as hand approaches entry point in front should you firm up hand and prepare for entry. Hand goes from floppy → ready just before entry.

**Step 4:** Feel the difference between tense recovery (hand rigid and controlled throughout) and relaxed recovery (hand floppy until necessary to prepare). Relaxed recovery conserves significant energy over course of workout or race.

**Step 5:** Practice several lengths, focusing on floppy relaxed hands during recovery.

**Common problems:**
- **Can't relax hand:** Practice on land first. Stand, swing one arm in circular motion, let hand flop around. Feel hand swing with momentum, not muscle control. Transfer to water.
- **Hand flops into water:** You're doing it right! Floppy hand = truly relaxed. Only issue is if you enter floppy — must firm up right before entry.
- **Feel uncoordinated:** Relaxation during recovery is skill that improves with practice. Be patient.

---

### 14. Shark Fin ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Develop high elbow recovery
- Maintain alignment during recovery
- Prevent shoulder injury

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Swim freestyle. During recovery, keep elbow HIGHER than hand throughout entire recovery path.

**Step 2:** Visualize your forearm and hand as a shark fin cutting through air above water. Elbow = top of fin (highest point). Hand dangles below elbow like bottom of fin.

**Step 3:** Lead recovery with elbow. Hand trails behind, relaxed, until just before entry.

**Step 4:** Check alignment: Imagine track or rail running from shoulder to entry point in front. Elbow should travel along this track. Hand dangles below track until entry.

**Step 5:** Practice several lengths. Feel high elbow recovery, aligned path, relaxed hand trailing below.

**Common problems:**
- **Hand higher than elbow during recovery:** This is "dropped elbow recovery" and can lead to shoulder injury. Really focus on leading with elbow, letting hand dangle below.
- **Elbow swings wide during recovery:** Caused by over-rotation or misalignment. Keep elbow traveling forward over water (not to side). Think "elbow drives forward."
- **Feels awkward:** High elbow recovery requires good shoulder flexibility. If painful, don't force it. Build flexibility gradually.

## Breathing Drills (4)

**Goal:** Breathe rhythmically within line of stroke without disrupting forward motion.

### 15. Inhale Arm / Exhale Arm

**Purpose:**
- Coordinate breathing with arm stroke
- Understand breathing timing
- Develop rhythmic breathing

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Swim freestyle breathing to your preferred side. Focus on WHICH ARM is stroking when you inhale.

**Step 2:** As the arm on your BREATHING SIDE enters the water in front, begin your inhale. This is your "Inhale Arm." Time the start of your breath to the entry of this arm.

**Step 3:** As your other arm (NON-BREATHING SIDE) enters the water in front, begin exhaling into the water. This is your "Exhale Arm."

**Step 4:** Establish rhythm: INHALE ARM enters → breathe in. EXHALE ARM enters → breathe out. Repeat continuously.

**Step 5:** Notice this timing means you're inhaling during recovery of breathing-side arm, and exhaling during power phase of both arms. This is optimal timing.

**Step 6:** Practice several lengths, establishing rhythmic breathing coordinated with arm stroke.

**Common problems:**
- **Get confused which arm:** Breathing side arm = Inhale Arm. If you breathe right, right arm entry = inhale. If you breathe left, left arm entry = inhale.
- **Run out of air:** Exhale actively and completely while face is in water. Many swimmers hold breath or exhale insufficiently.
- **Breathing feels rushed:** May need to adjust breathing frequency (breathe every 3rd or 4th stroke instead of every 2nd).

---

### 16. The Weightless Arm ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Breathe without lifting head
- Use body rotation for breath
- Maintain streamline during breathing

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Do Twelve Kick Switch drill (see Body Position #3). When you're floating on side with one arm extended forward, other arm at side, establish comfortable breathing pattern (roll slightly toward back to breathe, roll back to side position).

**Step 2:** Now add ONE arm stroke. As you roll toward back to breathe, the arm at your side (top arm) should feel WEIGHTLESS (out of water or nearly so). As you roll back to side position, recover this weightless arm over the water to extended position in front.

**Step 3:** Key point: You are NOT lifting your head to breathe. You are ROLLING to your back. Your head position relative to spine doesn't change. Lower ear stays on shoulder of bottom arm.

**Step 4:** Practice: Kick 12 times on one side, roll to breathe as you recover weightless arm, switch to other side. Repeat.

**Step 5:** Gradually add more strokes: kick 9 times, 1 stroke, switch. Then kick 6, 1 stroke, switch. Then kick 3, 1 stroke, switch. Finally, continuous freestyle with focus on weightless arm recovery and body roll breathing.

**Common problems:**
- **Lifting head to breathe:** Focus on rolling your body (especially hips), not lifting chin. Head should rotate with body as one unit.
- **Arm not weightless:** Not rotating enough to side. Roll further — shoulder should clear water during breath.
- **Going crooked:** Over-rotating. Need enough rotation to breathe comfortably, but not so much you're nearly on back.

---

### 17. Temple Press

**Purpose:**
- Maintain head position while breathing
- Anchor head to enable body rotation
- Prevent head lift during breath

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Stand in shallow water. Place palm of hand on your temple (side of forehead). Press your head into your hand while simultaneously pressing your hand into your head. Create firm pressure.

**Step 2:** While maintaining this pressure, rotate your body to the side. Your head rotates with your body — hand and head stay pressed together, moving as one unit.

**Step 3:** Notice that your head doesn't lift up or push forward. It rotates to the side, spine remaining aligned.

**Step 4:** Now swim freestyle. As you prepare to breathe, imagine pressing your temple into the water (just like pressing into your hand). Maintain this pressure as you rotate to breathe.

**Step 5:** Your head should rotate with your body, but not lift. Lower goggle stays in water (one-goggle rule). You're creating bow wave that gives you air pocket to breathe without lifting.

**Step 6:** Practice several lengths, maintaining temple press pressure during every breath.

**Common problems:**
- **Can't get enough air:** Likely over-rotating slightly forward instead of straight to side. Rotate directly to side — face should be perpendicular to pool bottom when breathing.
- **Water in mouth/nose:** Exhale actively right up until moment of breath. Explosive exhale right before inhale clears water.
- **Neck hurts:** Probably lifting or pushing head forward. Keep head anchored (temple press), rotate body only.

---

### 18. Dead Arm Freestyle

**Purpose:**
- Breathe within line of stroke
- Maintain stroke rhythm while breathing
- Prevent disruption to forward motion

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Swim freestyle. On non-breathing strokes, stroke normally with both arms.

**Step 2:** On BREATHING strokes, the arm on your BREATHING SIDE does NOT stroke. It remains extended in front (dead/still) while you breathe. Only non-breathing arm strokes.

**Step 3:** As non-breathing arm completes its stroke and recovers to front, breathing-side arm can stroke again. You've taken a breath with one arm remaining extended in front.

**Step 4:** Notice several things:
- You can breathe effectively with one arm extended
- Body rotation provides the breath (not head lift)
- Keeping one arm extended maintains streamline during breath

**Step 5:** Alternate: breathe every 3rd stroke (bilateral). Each time you breathe, opposite arm stays extended (dead).

**Step 6:** Progress to regular freestyle, but maintain the feeling of breathing with minimal disruption. One arm could stay extended if needed — you don't need both arms moving to breathe.

**Common problems:**
- **Sink when arm stays extended:** Kick actively during breath. Roll body enough to get air without lifting head.
- **Dead arm pulls during breath:** Requires discipline! Really keep it still and extended. This is the point of the drill.
- **Lose rhythm:** Dead arm drill creates asymmetrical timing. This is temporary learning tool. Regular freestyle is symmetrical.

## Leverage Drills (3)

**Goal:** Use core power, stabilize joints, access potential power.

### 19. Three Stroke Switch Freestyle ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Feel hip-driven freestyle
- Access core leverage
- Coordinate hip rotation with arm stroke

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Push off wall on your side (like Twelve Kick Switch position). Bottom arm extended, top arm at side. Begin kicking.

**Step 2:** Take THREE complete strokes, then return to side-lying position (opposite side from start). Hold and kick for 3-5 seconds.

**Step 3:** Take THREE more strokes, return to original side-lying position. Hold and kick 3-5 seconds.

**Step 4:** During the three strokes, focus on HIP ROTATION. Each stroke is powered by rotating from one hip to the other hip. Arms follow the hip rotation — they don't initiate it.

**Step 5:** Feel the leverage from your core as hips drive the stroke. Arms are connected to core rotation, not working independently.

**Step 6:** Practice several lengths: 3 strokes, pause on side, 3 strokes, pause on side. Feel core-driven freestyle.

**Progression:** Reduce pause gradually. Eventually pause becomes imperceptible but feeling of core-driven stroke remains.

**Common problems:**
- **Arms feel disconnected from hips:** Really exaggerate hip rotation during the 3 strokes. Rotate to nearly 90° on each side. Let arms follow hip movement.
- **Lose alignment during pause:** Engage core during pause position. Bottom ear should rest on bottom shoulder. Kick should be directed side to side.
- **Counting gets confusing:** Count strokes out loud (in your head). 1-2-3-pause. 1-2-3-pause. Establishes rhythm.

---

### 20. The Pendulum Effect

**Purpose:**
- Understand weight shift in freestyle
- Feel rhythmic hip rotation
- Access maximum leverage from core

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Stand on land. Place hands on hips. Shift weight to right hip (lean right). Notice left hip rises as right hip lowers.

**Step 2:** Shift weight to left hip. Notice right hip rises as left hip lowers.

**Step 3:** Shift back and forth rhythmically. Like a pendulum swinging side to side. Right hip down-left hip up, left hip down-right hip up.

**Step 4:** Now swim freestyle. With each stroke, shift weight from one hip to the other, just like on land. As right arm enters in front, shift weight to right hip (right hip lowers, left hip rises). As left arm enters, shift weight to left hip.

**Step 5:** Feel the rhythmic weight shift — like a pendulum swinging through your core. Each weight shift powers the stroke on that side.

**Step 6:** Practice several lengths, focusing on deliberate weight shift with each stroke. Feel increased leverage and power.

**Common problems:**
- **Can't feel weight shift in water:** Exaggerate initially. Really press hip down/rotate opposite hip up. Like side-to-side rocking motion.
- **Hips shift but no power increase:** Make sure arm entry coordinates with weight shift to same side. Timing is crucial for leverage.
- **Feel like I'm over-rotating:** Possibly. Goal is effective rotation (enough to access leverage), not maximum rotation. Find sweet spot.

---

### 21. Hip Skating ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Initiate stroke from core, not arms
- Feel "skating" on alternating hips
- Connect leverage to forward motion

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Do Three Stroke Switch drill. During the pause on each side, really feel yourself balanced on that hip — like balancing on one skating blade.

**Step 2:** As you initiate the next stroke, feel yourself PUSH OFF that hip (like pushing off a skating blade) to rotate to the other side.

**Step 3:** The push-off from hip INITIATES the stroke. Arm stroke follows hip rotation. You're "skating" from hip to hip through the water.

**Step 4:** Remove the pause, swim continuous freestyle. Maintain the feeling of skating on alternating hips — push off right hip to rotate left, push off left hip to rotate right.

**Step 5:** Each "push-off" generates momentum that powers the stroke. You're using core leverage, not just arm strength.

**Step 6:** Practice several lengths. Feel connected, powerful, core-driven freestyle — skating through the water.

**Common problems:**
- **Can't feel "push-off":** During pause position (Three Stroke Switch), really press the down hip into the water. Then explosively rotate to other side. That's the push-off feeling.
- **Still feels arm-driven:** Slow down. Focus on initiating rotation from hip FIRST, then letting arm follow. Hip leads, arm follows.
- **Hips moving but going nowhere:** Push-off must coordinate with arm catch. As you rotate to new side, opposite arm (extended in front) catches water and pulls. Timing is key.

## Coordination Drills (3)

**Goal:** Bring multiple freestyle skills together into coordinated effort.

### 22. Heads Up Freestyle

**Purpose:**
- Learn to grab water and stroke back
- Use continuous stroke
- Match kick to arm stroke

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Swim freestyle with HEAD UP (chin at surface, eyes focused on far end of pool). Keep head up entire time.

**Step 2:** To keep head up without pressing down on water, engage water IMMEDIATELY with fingertips, palm, and forearm when hand enters front. Stroke continuously. Keep elbows high. Accelerate to back with each short, quick stroke.

**Step 3:** Notice: stroke becomes shorter, recovery becomes wider. But by pressing BACK on water (not down), head stays above water while body moves forward.

**Step 4:** Coordinate kick with arm entry. Although kick rate is quicker than arm rate, one kick should match each arm entry. As one arm enters front, kick down with one foot.

**Step 5:** Notice this coordination creates additional forward motion, plus lift and balance. Even from "uphill" swimming position, you move forward well when all parts work together.

**Step 6:** Now swim regular freestyle (face in water). Use same quick stroke rate as heads-up. Press back, elbows high. Match kick downbeat to point when hand first takes hold of water in front. Feel lift and balance.

**Step 7:** Alternate several lengths: heads-up freestyle and regular freestyle.

**Common problems:**
- **Struggling to keep face up:** Stroke quickly, elbows high. Accelerate to back. Don't press down to stay up — press BACK to move forward. Kick with each arm entry.
- **Very tiring:** Yes! Swimming "uphill" is like running uphill. All body parts must work together. This is the point.
- **Hurts neck:** Balance on chest to reduce chin lift needed. If continues to hurt, skip this drill.

---

### 23. Horizontal Rope Climbing ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Utilize largest muscles possible
- Move past anchored hand
- Feel efficient forward motion

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Think about rope climbing. Hand over hand, climber uses one hand to grasp rope at point higher than body while other hand holds body steady. When reaching hand has good grip, climber moves body past that reaching hand. This is exactly how best swimming works!

**Step 2:** String a rope from one end of pool to other. Secure using lane line hooks, but allow slack so most of rope floats ~1 foot under surface. (Don't use lane lines — can snap!)

**Step 3:** Take big breath, float face down over rope. Reach as far forward as you can with one hand, take hold of rope, keeping elbow high. Pull body forward past reaching hand until arm is straight at hip. Other hand reaches to farther point. Continue arm over arm.

**Step 4:** When you need to breathe, lift face gently out of water, then return to face-down position.

**Step 5:** Continue to far end. Feel body advance through water past reaching hand. This is essence of efficient swimming.

**Step 6:** Try regular freestyle without rope. Take hold of WATER, then move yourself past that point with each stroke. Climb through the water.

**Common problems:**
- **This is hard!:** Keep elbows high — gives more leverage to lift body past reaching hand.
- **Legs wag back and forth:** Employ stable core. Can also kick gently.
- **Thought reach should align with shoulders:** True! This is shortcoming of this drill. Ignore this aspect, focus on how you advance past anchored hand.

---

### 24. Freestyle with Dolphin

**Purpose:**
- Coordinate kick and arm stroke
- Set stroke rhythm to kick
- Use momentum of kick to benefit arm stroke

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Push off wall in streamline. Do 6-12 good dolphin kicks. Focus on rhythmic kick with compact yet powerful downbeat, feet staying connected to water. Keep head stable (not bouncing).

**Step 2:** Once you've established strong rhythmic kick, begin freestyle arm stroke. Time kick so you produce ONE KICK for each single arm stroke. (Note: different from butterfly rhythm.)

**Step 3:** Time DOWNBEAT of each kick to happen precisely when hand strikes water in front.

**Step 4:** Continue to far end, focusing on coordination. Notice: when downbeat of dolphin kick is timed to hand entry, you reach forward with more momentum, encouraging farther extension. Notice also same kick assists fast finish of stroking arm.

**Step 5:** Quicken kick rhythm. Match arm stroke to quicker rhythm. As you plant hand in water in front, plant feet in water to rear.

**Step 6:** Now try same coordination using FLUTTER KICK. Make sure one of your kicks matches hand entry and simultaneously assists fast finish of other hand for EVERY stroke.

**Common problems:**
- **Dolphin kick seems out of alignment:** If done correctly, hips will roll (as they should in freestyle), making dolphin kick not exactly up-down. That's okay. Point is to match rhythm of kick to arms.
- **Using same side arm and leg for simultaneous motion (flutter kick):** This is fine. Depends on number of flutter kicks per stroke. Can be done with same side or opposite arm/leg.
- **Freestyle arms turn into butterfly:** Don't dive head down as arms enter. Use more compact kick. Reach forward upon entry, not down. Kick ONCE per arm, not twice like butterfly.

---

### 25. Bilateral Breathing ⭐

**Purpose:**
- Coordinate breathing with body roll
- Develop symmetrical roll
- Develop symmetrical kick

**How to do it:**

**Step 1:** Push off wall, establish good continuous kick. Swim to far end breathing toward RIGHT side of pool. Rest. Swim another length back, facing SAME side of pool when you breathe (now breathing to other side of body). Repeat until comfortable maintaining alignment and kick, rolling to both sides.

**Step 2:** Now swim breathing EVERY THIRD STROKE. Time inhale to start at same time as entry of hand you're breathing away from. Strike left, strike right, strike-and-breathe left. Strike right, strike left, strike-and-breathe right.

**Step 3:** Remember Weightless Arm drill and Temple Press drill while perfecting this timing.

**Step 4:** Practice breathing every third stroke for several lengths. One side may feel more natural (like writing hand). Keep practicing to make less natural side mirror image of natural side.

**Step 5:** Focus on hip roll. Notice with bilateral breathing, hip roll is more even on both sides than when breathing one side only. Notice kick has fewer pauses and is more compact, especially during breathing. Notice arm stroke is more symmetrical in depth, width, and length.

**Step 6:** Practice several lengths. Develop balanced stroke by breathing on both sides.

**Common problems:**
- **Neck hurts when breathing to left (or right):** Turn your BODY to breathe, not just neck. Focus on rolling opposite hip down when breathing.
- **Breathing every third stroke too often:** May not be right as regular swimming rhythm, but still very beneficial as drill to develop symmetrical stroke and kick.
- **Lose rhythm after a few strokes:** When rhythm is off, stop, rest, begin again. Be persistent. It will pay off.

## Drill Sequences & Training Integration

### Warm-Up Sequence (15-20 min)
1. Downhill Float (30 sec × 3)
2. Twelve Kick Switch (50 × 4)
3. Vertical Kicking (30 sec × 3)
4. Pull/Push Freestyle (50 × 4)
5. Catch Up (75 × 3)
6. Bilateral Breathing (100 × 2)

### Technique-Focused Practice (Main Set)
Example: 20 × 50 on interval that gives 10-15 sec rest
- Odds: Drill (rotate through 5 favorite drills)
- Evens: Swim, applying drill focus

### Pre-Competition Taper Drill Set
- 4 × 50 Shark Fin (high elbow recovery)
- 4 × 50 Temple Press (breathing mechanics)
- 4 × 50 Hip Skating (core leverage)
- 4 × 50 Bilateral Breathing (rhythm & symmetry)
All on easy interval, focus = QUALITY

### Stroke Problem Diagnosis Guide
- **Short stroke:** Catch Up, Horizontal Rope Climbing
- **Dropped elbow:** Fist Freestyle, Shark Fin
- **Poor body position:** Downhill Float, Log Roll, Twelve Kick Switch
- **Ineffective kick:** Toe Point, Floppy Foot, Vertical Kicking Sequence
- **Head lift when breathing:** Weightless Arm, Temple Press, Dead Arm
- **Arm-driven (not core-driven):** Three Stroke Switch, Pendulum Effect, Hip Skating
- **Lack of coordination:** Heads Up Freestyle, Freestyle with Dolphin, Bilateral Breathing

## Actionable Takeaways

✅ **Body position first** — Can't build good stroke on poor float  
✅ **Kick from the hip** — Engage large muscles, relaxed floppy feet  
✅ **Pull then push** — Acceleration from entry → shoulder → hip  
✅ **High elbow** — Recovery AND underwater (maximize forearm engagement)  
✅ **Breathe with body rotation** — Not head lift (temple press, one-goggle rule)  
✅ **Core drives stroke** — Hip skating, pendulum effect, not arm-driven  
✅ **Coordinate all elements** — Kick matches arm entry, breathing fits rhythm  
✅ **Start short, add distance** — When tired, revert to old habits  
✅ **Know the purpose** — Empty practice = no improvement  
✅ **Alternate drill & swim** — Transfer new technique to full stroke
