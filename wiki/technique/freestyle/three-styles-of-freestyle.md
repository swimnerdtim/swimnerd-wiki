# Three Styles of Freestyle

**Source:** *Fundamentals of Fast Swimming* by Gary Hall Sr. & Devin Murphy (2020)

## Overview

There are three distinct freestyle techniques used at the elite level:

1. **Hip-Driven** — Slowest stroke rate, maximum body rotation
2. **Shoulder-Driven** — Fastest stroke rate, minimal body rotation  
3. **Hybrid (Loping/Galloping)** — Mixed technique, used by sprinters

The choice of style depends on race distance, swimmer anatomy, and speed requirements.

> **Hall's Insight:** "After years of observing and analyzing freestyle techniques using all of our technologies at The Race Club, we agree with Coach Mike Bottom that there are basically three different freestyle techniques."

---

## 1. Hip-Driven Freestyle

### Characteristics
- **Stroke Rate:** Slowest (30-50 SPM typically)
- **Body Rotation:** Maximum rotation (often 70-90°)
- **Best For:** Distance swimming (400m, 800m, 1500m)
- **Power Source:** Large muscle groups (core, hips, lats)

### Technique Features
- Deep hip rotation drives the stroke
- Long, gliding stroke with extended front quadrant timing
- Powerful kick timed with rotation
- Lower stroke rate = longer distance per stroke (DPS)

### Example Swimmers
- **Katie Ledecky** — Distance freestyle dominance
- **Sun Yang** — 1500m world record holder
- Most distance specialists worldwide

### Coaching Points
✅ **DO:**
- Emphasize full hip rotation on each stroke
- Drill rotation timing with kick coordination
- Focus on long, patient strokes
- Use tempo trainers to control stroke rate

❌ **AVOID:**
- Rushing the stroke cycle
- Flat body position
- Over-kicking (wastes energy)

---

## 2. Shoulder-Driven Freestyle

### Characteristics
- **Stroke Rate:** Fastest (70-100+ SPM in sprints)
- **Body Rotation:** Minimal (30-50°)
- **Best For:** Sprint freestyle (50m, 100m)
- **Power Source:** Shoulders, chest, arms — rapid turnover

### Technique Features
- Minimal body rotation
- Very high stroke rate
- Shorter, quicker arm recovery
- Front quadrant catch position
- Rapid hand entry and exit

### Example Swimmers
- **Popov-era sprinting** — Classic high-tempo technique
- Some 50m specialists

### Coaching Points
✅ **DO:**
- Train high-tempo sets (cycle rate >85 SPM)
- Use short-axis rotation drills
- Emphasize quick hand entry/exit
- Build shoulder endurance for sustained turnover

❌ **AVOID:**
- Over-rotating (slows stroke rate)
- Long glide phases
- Low stroke rates in sprints

---

## 3. Hybrid Freestyle (Loping/Galloping)

### Characteristics
- **Stroke Rate:** Variable, asymmetric timing
- **Body Rotation:** Moderate, often asymmetric
- **Best For:** 100m, 200m freestyle
- **Power Source:** Combination of hip drive and shoulder speed

### Technique Features
- **Loping motion** — One arm recovers faster than the other
- Asymmetric timing between left and right arms
- Used to generate more power on breathing side
- Often appears as a "hitch" or rhythm break

### Example Swimmers
- **Jason Lezak** — 100m relay split 46.06 (2008 Beijing)
- **Michael Phelps** — Used hybrid in 200m free
- **Caeleb Dressel** — Modern power-sprinter

### When It Emerges
> "The hybrid technique is another name for loping or galloping freestyle technique. It was being used by Jason Lezak, Michael Phelps and others."

Often develops naturally when swimmers:
- Transition from distance to sprint training
- Need to breathe on one side preferentially
- Generate more power on one side
- Sprint at very high speeds (near-maximal effort)

### Coaching Points
✅ **DO:**
- Allow natural asymmetry if it enhances speed
- Focus on power generation over symmetry
- Use video analysis to identify timing differences
- Test both bilateral and unilateral breathing

❌ **AVOID:**
- Forcing perfect symmetry at max speed
- Overcorrecting natural technique adaptations
- Ignoring if it causes injury or imbalance

## Choosing the Right Style

### By Distance

| Event | Recommended Style | Why |
|-------|------------------|-----|
| 50m | Shoulder-driven or Hybrid | Max stroke rate, power |
| 100m | Hybrid or Shoulder-driven | Speed + efficiency balance |
| 200m | Hybrid or Hip-driven | Transitional race |
| 400m+ | Hip-driven | Energy efficiency, sustainability |

### By Swimmer Anatomy

**Favor Hip-Driven if:**
- Longer torso relative to limbs
- Flexible hips and core
- Stronger kick
- Better at sustained efforts

**Favor Shoulder-driven if:**
- Long arms relative to torso
- High shoulder mobility
- Fast-twitch muscle dominance
- Natural high turnover

**Hybrid may emerge if:**
- Sprinting at max effort
- Asymmetric breathing patterns
- Transitioning between styles
- Natural power imbalance

## The Stroke Rate Differentiator

> **Hall's Key Point:** "There are several parameters that differentiate hip-driven from shoulder-driven and hybrid freestyle techniques, but the most important one is stroke rate."

### Stroke Rate Ranges (Approximate)

| Style | 50m SPM | 200m SPM | 1500m SPM |
|-------|---------|----------|-----------|
| Hip-driven | 45-65 | 40-55 | 35-50 |
| Shoulder-driven | 75-100+ | 65-85 | N/A |
| Hybrid | 60-85 | 55-75 | 45-60 |

*SPM = Strokes Per Minute*

## Training Applications

### For Hip-Driven Swimmers
- **Drill:** Front quadrant catch-up drill
- **Sets:** Longer intervals (200-400m repeats) at race pace
- **Focus:** Distance per stroke (DPS), rotation timing
- **Strength:** Core rotation, hip flexibility, kick endurance

### For Shoulder-Driven Swimmers
- **Drill:** High-tempo 25s with tempo trainer
- **Sets:** Short, fast repeats (25-50m) with high turnover
- **Focus:** Rapid hand entry/exit, minimal glide
- **Strength:** Shoulder endurance, fast-twitch recruitment

### For Hybrid Development
- **Drill:** Single-arm freestyle at race pace
- **Sets:** Race-pace 100s and 200s
- **Focus:** Natural rhythm at max speed
- **Strength:** Asymmetric power development

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Know your race distance** → Choose the appropriate style
2. **Measure your stroke rate** → Use tempo trainer or video analysis
3. **Don't force symmetry** → Allow hybrid technique if it enhances speed
4. **Train your style** → Match drills and sets to your technique
5. **Test and adapt** → Video analysis + race results = optimization

**Bottom Line:** Elite swimmers naturally select the freestyle style that maximizes their speed for their event. Coaches should identify, refine, and train the style that best suits each swimmer's physiology and race distance — not force a one-size-fits-all technique.
