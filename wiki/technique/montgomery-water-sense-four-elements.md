# Water Sense: The Four Essential Elements
**Source:** *Mastering Swimming* by Jim Montgomery & Mo Chambers, Chapter 3 (2009)  
**Context:** Bill Boomer-influenced approach to developing superior water sense  
**Audience:** All swimmers from beginners to advanced seeking better feel, efficiency, streamlining

---

## Core Philosophy

> "The water is your friend. You don't have to fight with water; just share the same spirit as the water, and it will help you move." — Aleksandr Popov

**What is water sense?**
The ability to be completely at home in your body, relaxed in water, breathe naturally, and maintain balanced body position that allows cutting through water with minimal effort.

**Why world-class swimmers look effortless:**
1. Extraordinarily fit ✓
2. Good stroke technique ✓  
3. **Superior water sense** ← The missing piece

---

## The Four Building Blocks

All interrelated:
1. **Relaxation** → Natural breathing
2. **Breathing** → Balanced body position
3. **Balance** → Streamlined movement
4. **Streamlining** → Makes breathing easier (loop back)

**Key insight:** Better balance + streamlined position makes breathing easier, which allows more relaxation. It's a reinforcing cycle.

---

## 1. RELAXATION

### The Foundation Question

**Are you comfortable in any depth of water?**
- Pool, lake, or ocean
- Shallow or deep
- At surface or underneath
- Alone or in crowded practice

**When you feel in control:**
- You can relax
- Muscles contract and release with each stroke cycle
- No need to think about next breath - you just breathe
- You feel secure

### The Mistake Most Make

**Common belief:** "Learning freestyle will help me feel confident and safe."

**Truth:** **Opposite is true.**
- Swimmers breathe comfortably ONLY AFTER they feel safe in any depth
- Confidence in water → Relaxed stroke
- Relaxed stroke ≠ Confidence in water (cart before horse)

### Building Relaxation: The Aquatic Signature

**Coach Bill Boomer's contribution:**
Late 1980s/early 1990s revolutionized stroke teaching with theories on body position and balance. Recently focused on teaching relaxation.

**What is Aquatic Signature?**
Your relationship with yourself + your relationship with water.
- Learn to trust water enough to completely give yourself to it
- Let water manage you
- Built upon emotional trust + physical awareness
- Not dominated by survival reactions

> "Only when you establish an emotionally satisfying coexistence with the water's remarkably consistent intentions can you truly understand how to move from point A to point B efficiently." — Bill Boomer

### How to Do It

**Setup:**
1. Start in vertical position in deep water
2. Place face in water
3. Let body float freely
4. **Surrender to water**

**Process:**
- Keep lungs full of air
- Let body rotate from vertical to its natural resting place
- **No two swimmers have same position** (your "signature")
- Some swimmers actually vertical when relaxed!
- Most float between 90° (vertical) and 0° (horizontal)
- One-breath experience: 12-14 seconds
- Gradually learn to give yourself freely for longer periods

**Tip:** If uncomfortable, use nose clip to focus on relaxation without breathing worry.

---

### Bobs With Exhale Focus

**Purpose:** Learn to exhale fully, regulate air exchanges, reduce hyperventilation

**Traditional relaxation exercises (out of water):**
- Focus on deep breathing
- Prolonged exhalation expels CO₂
- Reduces anxiety

**In-water application:**

**How to Bob:**
1. Begin in 6-8 feet (2-3m) deep water
2. Vertical position, water over shoulders, arms at sides
3. **Exhale sufficiently** to allow body to fall feet-first to bottom
4. Pause at bottom
5. Push off with feet, travel up while completing exhale
6. Inhale at surface
7. Submerge again, exhaling as you go down
8. Repeat

**Result for relaxed swimmers:** Heart rate DECREASES after several bobs.

**If uncomfortable with bobs:** Try wall hangs
- Place two hands on wall
- Chin under water
- Expel air from lungs
- Allow body to drop while holding wall
- Repeat until comfortable with exhalation
- Progress to bobs without wall when ready

---

### The Relaxation Sequence (Recommended Start to Every Swim)

**Boomer's recommendation for ALL swimmers (rookie to veteran):**

1. Several aquatic signatures (2-3 reps)
2. Bob sequence (8-12 reps)
3. Swim four lengths, staying present in body
4. Breathe naturally with exhale focus

**Result:** 
- Likely take fewer strokes per length than before exercises
- Feel relaxed and ready for aquatic goals
- Body and mind prepared for swimming

## 2. BREATHING

### The Essential Truth

**When you can breathe as easily in water as on land:**
- Cover long distances faster with less effort
- Stay relaxed while controlling breathing rhythm
- Easily swim at higher speeds while staying relaxed

**Not just for beginners:**
Olympic swimmers spend HOURS on breathing strategies to maximize performance. Training makes breathing natural part of rhythm - they no longer think about when/how to breathe.

**Therefore:** Masters swimmers should spend training time working on breathing.

### Five Tips for Better Breathing

#### Tip 1: RELAX
**Most critical skill** for proper swimming breathing.

**Muscles to relax:**
- Face
- Jaw
- Mouth
- Neck

**The test:** Imagine how facial muscles feel when running or riding bicycle. Breathing should feel SAME during swimming as during other aerobic activities.

**Problem swimmers:** 
- Tense face in water
- Most likely holding breath underwater
- Forced to exhale AND inhale when above water
- Inefficient air exchange → anxiety → exhaustion

#### Tip 2: EXHALE
**As face enters water:**
- Mouth should be slightly open
- Trickle of air going out between lips

**Two methods (choose what works):**
1. Exhale through mouth AND nose
2. Exhale gently through mouth only
3. Nose plug option if more comfortable

**Key:** Blow air out SLOWLY
- Exhaling too quickly → gasping on inhalation → hyperventilation
- Slow exhalation → develop awareness of facial tension
- Notice tension around mouth, lips, teeth

**The crescendo:**
- As face begins to leave water: Increase rate of exhalation
- Expel remaining air with forceful puff
- Many swimmers use nose AND mouth for this final puff

#### Tip 3: INHALE
**Natural reflex** - quick but not forced.

**If you exhale adequately:** Air will flow in on its own.

**Most swimmers:** Breathe in through mouth.

#### Tip 4: MAKE EXHALATION LONG
**The 2:1 ratio**

Your exhalation should be **TWICE AS LONG** as inhalation.
- Longer exhalation = More relaxed air exchange

#### Tip 5: DON'T PANIC IF YOU BREATHE IN WATER
**When you gulp in water:**
- Shape tongue as if pronouncing letter "K"
- This tongue position keeps water from going down throat
- **Even greatest swimmers breathe in water from time to time**

### When Should You Breathe?

#### Freestyle, Breaststroke, Butterfly
**Complete exhalation:** At end of underwater pull phase, just before face leaves water

**Freestyle:**
- Increase exhalation rate toward end of underwater phase
- Body slides forward and rotates to breathing side
- Face leaves water

**Breaststroke:**
- Majority of exhalation as arms sweep in
- Hips snap forward
- Head and shoulders rise up out of water

**Butterfly:**
- Expel air as body passes over arms
- Chest rising
- Face clearing water

#### Backstroke
**Breathing takes place out of water BUT:**
- Important to establish smooth, rhythmic transition between inhalation/exhalation
- Similar to other strokes
- Find consistent place in stroke cycle when face clears water
- Time your exhalation to allow quality inhalation at this point

## 3. BALANCE

### What Is Good Balance?

**Definition:** Moving horizontally along surface of water.

**Experienced swimmers:**
- Cut through water with legs streamlined behind
- Long body lines positioned to swim with:
  - Less effort
  - Less resistance  
  - Greater ease of breath

### The Teeter-Totter Analogy

**Your body = Horizontal teeter-totter floating in water**

**Components:**
- **Fulcrum (center):** Your lungs (center of flotation)
- **Front end:** Head and neck
- **Back end:** Lower spine, hips, legs

**How to maintain balance:**

**1. Head-Neck-Spine Alignment**
- Keep head and neck in alignment with spine
- If you pick head up to breathe → lower body drops below surface

**2. Press Down With Upper Body**
- Eyes positioned on bottom of pool
- Gently press face, chin, chest toward pool bottom
- Lower body rotates toward surface
- Probably won't reach horizontal (that's OK)
- You'll come closer

**3. Extend Arms Overhead**
- Once as close to horizontal as possible
- Slowly extend arms over head
- Ears should be between biceps
- Lower body rotates up EVEN HIGHER

**Why this works:**
- Lungs now more centered in teeter-totter
- Extended arms help redistribute body weight

### The Long Boat Principle

**Maintain extension while swimming** - Particularly in long-axis strokes (freestyle, backstroke)

**Think:** 
- Extension of one or both arms → Better balance
- Extension → Longer body line
- Longer boat = Faster through water

**Formula:**
Shape body into long, streamlined boat with:
- Good head-neck-spine alignment
- Core muscles engaged

---

### Balance Killer: Bad Breathing

**Most common balance disrupter:** Breathing technique

**The pattern:**
1. Swimmer assumes balanced position in freestyle
2. Lifts head out of water each breath
3. Upsets body line
4. Must reposition after every breathing stroke
5. Progress becomes slow and tiring

**Solution:** Synchronize breathing with hip/shoulder rotation in freestyle
- Allows inhalation while maintaining head-neck-spine alignment
- Unnecessary to lift head
- Imagine keeping one goggle under water, one above

**The bow wave:**
- As you move through water, you create bow wave (like ship)
- Bow wave appears next to your face
- Breathe BEHIND this bow wave
- Easier to inhale quickly while keeping good body alignment

## 4. STREAMLINING

### Definition

**Streamlining = Decreasing Resistance**

**Process requires:**
- Recognition
- Concentration
- Commitment

**NOT necessarily great effort BUT:**
- Practice and perfect motor skills
- Become aware of water and resistance created by body
- Align body and limbs to reduce resistance of oncoming water
- **Timing of alignment sequences is crucial**

### The Skilled Swimmer Pattern

**Freestyle stroke cycle:**
- Spend MOST of time tipping body equally to one side or other
- NOT holding flat, central posture (produces increased resistance)
- Focus on eliminating inefficient vertical and lateral body movements

**Keys:**
1. Keep strokes long
2. Maintain proper head and body alignment throughout stroke cycle
3. **Longer body travels through water faster**

---

### The Three Types of Resistance

#### 1. Frictional Resistance
**What creates it:** Water contact with:
- Swimmer's skin
- Suit
- Cap
- Goggles
- Body hair

**Solutions:**
- Tight-fitting swimsuits (hold minimal water)
- Shave body hair (competitive swimmers for decades)
- Silicone or latex swim caps
- Streamlined goggles

**When speed doubles:** Frictional resistance also doubles (1:1 ratio)

#### 2. Form Resistance  
**What creates it:** Shape body makes in water

**Good shape:** Long, streamlined, arrow-like  
**Bad shape:** Barge-like

**Dimensional thinking:** What's happening:
- Above body
- Below body
- To both sides of body

**When speed doubles:** Form resistance increases FOUR TIMES (1:4 ratio)

**Good news:** Can be reduced with improved flexibility

#### 3. Wave Resistance
**What creates it:** Waves created by moving body

**When speed doubles:** Wave resistance increases EIGHT TIMES (1:8 ratio)

**Strategy:**
1. Focus FIRST on reducing wave resistance (biggest impact)
2. Then reduce form resistance
3. Frictional resistance last (smallest impact)

## Flow Shapers: Streamlining Drill Progression

**Purpose:** Teach how to recognize pressure against body during every phase of freestyle stroke cycle.

**Why they work:**
- Synchronize hand and arm for accurate stroke pattern
- Create and detect specific flows in water
- Produce positive results

### Drill 1: Streamline Out of Water

**Location:** Pool deck or shallow water

**How to:**
1. Extend arms over head
2. Place one hand on top of other
3. Two squeezes:
   - Wrap thumb of top hand around pinkie side of bottom hand
   - Squeeze ears between biceps
4. Align spine:
   - Move chin away from chest
   - Contract abdomen and buttocks
   - Place feet together
5. Lie down on deck with toes pointed
6. Raise shoulders toward ears (further streamline)

**Have partner:** Help make alignment adjustments

### Drill 2: Ship Drill

**In water:**
1. Push off wall in streamlined position
2. Body outstretched, arms extended
3. Shape resembles bow of ship
4. Hold streamlined position
5. Tighten muscles from core → trunk → fingertips

**Questions to explore:**
- How far can you glide after push?
- Do you glide farther underwater or on surface?

**Variations (feel the difference):**
- **Destroyer:** Tight streamline (fast, smooth)
- **Ocean liner:** Moderate streamline
- **Barge:** Loose body position (turbulent, cumbersome)

**Advanced:** Move head away from long axis to detect sensation of oncoming water pressure

### Drill 3: Kick With Head Leading

**Setup:**
- Maintain neutrally aligned spine position on water surface
- Hold arms to sides
- Add easy, relaxed flutter kick

**Focus:**
- Rotate whole body (maintaining alignment) to breathe

### Drill 4: Kick With One Arm Leading

**Setup:**
1. Extend right arm forward
2. Hold left arm to side
3. Hold body in streamlined balanced position
4. Begin to flutter kick lightly

**Focus:**
- No noise, no white water
- Proper body position
- Feeling of going downhill

**Breathing:**
- Take one arm stroke
- Roll left side down
- Allow right side to rotate toward surface to breathe
- Repeat with left arm extended (rotate right to breathe)

### Drill 5: Kick With Underwater Recovery

**Process:**
1. Extend right arm forward
2. Hold left arm at side
3. Kick lightly 6-10 times
4. Pull right arm through to side
5. Roll left side down
6. Recover left arm forward UNDERWATER
7. Breathe to right in natural flow of stroke

**Focus:**
- Relax muscles in neck, jaw, face during breathing
- Keep body line long
- Breathe with head in line with spine
- Maintain balance during transition between sides
- Think: Long boat

### Drill 6: Clock Drill

**Setup:**
1. Lie on side
2. Extend one arm in water (streamlined position)
3. Extend other arm out of water (fingers toward sky)
4. Kick from hips (not knees)

**Caution:**
- Careful not to overkick
- Don't gain dependency on fins for body positioning

## Practical Applications

### For Every Swim Session

**Recommended warm-up (revisit water sense basics):**
1. 2-3 Aquatic signatures
2. 8-12 Bobs with exhale focus
3. 4 × 25 easy swim, breathing focus
4. 2-3 Flow shapers (rotate which ones)
5. Ready for main set

### For Technique-Focused Workouts

**Choose 1-2 concepts per workout:**
- Can't think about all details at once
- Focus on priorities
- What improvements move you toward ultimate vision?

**Examples:**
- Week 1: Exhale focus + Head-neck-spine alignment
- Week 2: One goggle breathing + Body rotation
- Week 3: Streamlined push-offs + Long body line
- Week 4: Bow wave breathing + Reduced resistance

### For Competitive Swimmers

**Water sense under fatigue:**
- Easy to maintain when fresh
- Challenge: Keep it when tired
- Practice maintaining streamline in last 25 of hard sets
- Breathe comfortably during threshold work
- Stay balanced when lactic acid builds

**Championship secret:**
> "I could make good time because I was so long and skinny, shooting through the water like a stick." — Johnny Weissmuller

Modern version: Stay streamlined and balanced regardless of fatigue level.

## Flexibility Limitations

**Reality check:** Several factors affect swimmers' ability to maintain ideal form and body alignment.

**Common limitations:**
- Poor flexibility
- Injury history
- Age-related changes
- Body type

**Approach:**
- Stay focused on creating streamlined position that works best for YOU
- Work within your capabilities
- Improve flexibility through consistent practice
- Don't compare to others' positions

## Assessment Checklist

**Self-evaluate your water sense:**

**RELAXATION**
□ Comfortable in deep water (any depth)  
□ Can float freely without anxiety  
□ Face relaxed (jaw, mouth, neck loose)  
□ No holding breath underwater  
□ Heart rate calm while swimming  

**BREATHING**
□ Exhale twice as long as inhale  
□ No gasping for air  
□ Comfortable breathing in rhythm  
□ Can handle water in face without panic  
□ Breathing feels natural, not forced  

**BALANCE**
□ Hips/legs at or near surface  
□ Head-neck-spine aligned  
□ Can maintain position during breathing  
□ Extended arm position feels stable  
□ No "bicycle kick" to stay afloat  

**STREAMLINING**
□ Can glide 10+ yards (9m) off wall  
□ Minimal splash on entry  
□ Body feels "slippery" through water  
□ Reducing strokes per length over time  
□ Aware of resistance and how to reduce it  

**Goal:** Check all boxes. If not, focus training on unchecked areas.
