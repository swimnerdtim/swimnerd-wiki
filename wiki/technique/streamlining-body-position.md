# Streamlining & Body Position Fundamentals

**Source:** *Championship Swimming* by Tracey McFarlane Mirande (1988 Olympian, 100m/200m Breaststroke)  
**Context:** Core principles for reducing resistance and swimming efficiently  
**Audience:** All swimmers, especially those learning proper technique

---

## Core Principle: Streamlined = Efficient = Faster

**The Palm Test:**
- Push water with your palm → feel the resistance
- Push water fingers-first (thumb up) → glides through with minimal resistance

**This is the essence of streamlining:** Reduce resistance by putting your body in the right position.

---

## Stroke-Specific Body Position

### Freestyle & Backstroke: SIDE TO SIDE
**Never flat on your stomach in freestyle.**
- Olympic swimmers spend ~95% of freestyle on their sides
- Hips and shoulders rotate together
- Only flat when transitioning from one side to the other
- "Like a shish kebab pole through your body" — rotate around it

**Common mistake:** Swimming like a snowplow
- Shoulders push water out of the way
- Not streamlined, harder, inefficient
- Loses 6 inches of reach compared to proper rotation

**Goal:** Be as tall and skinny as possible
- Reach as far and long as you can
- Get 6 inches MORE reach by rotating

### Breaststroke & Butterfly: FLAT (Snowplow Position)
**Complete opposite of freestyle/backstroke.**
- Body flat on stomach
- Must work to overcome drag
- "Trick" the water with fast recovery & streamline

**Modern advantage:** Head can go underwater (not allowed pre-1988)
- Reduces drag significantly
- Old technique had much more resistance

## The Float Test

**Before you swim, can you float?**

### How to Test
1. Lie on stomach in water
2. Look down at pool bottom
3. Legs should be near surface
4. Back of heels just out of water

**If you can't float easily:**
- Put arms out to side (like flying)
- Very muscular people have harder time — but it's not impossible

**Why it matters:** If you can float naturally, you're in great position to learn proper freestyle.

## Kicking Mechanics (Freestyle/Backstroke)

### The Right Way
- **Mostly straight leg** but relaxed (not rigid)
- **Kick from the hip**, not bending at knee
- **Ankle bends** — foot moving back/forth, toes pointed but relaxed
- **Small kicks** — feet no more than 12 inches apart at widest point
- **Move your legs from the hip** to actually move water

### Three Common Mistakes

**1. Surface Kicking**
- Only kicking air
- Feet too high
- Gets you nowhere

**2. Kicking Too Deep**
- Legs too far apart
- Wastes energy

**3. Too Much Knee Bend**
- Using only half the leg
- Missing power from whole leg
- **Common mistake for runners/bikers** learning swimming

## Pool Features That Affect Speed

### Faster Pool = Better Times (Know the Difference!)

**Depth:**
- Ideal: 12 feet deep all the way across
- Most pools: 3-4 feet shallow end + diving well
- **Deeper water = faster speeds** (less turbulence)

**Flow-Through Gutters:**
- Water splashes into drain around perimeter
- Flows back into pool (doesn't crash onto surface)
- **Reduces waves = faster swimming**

**Impact on training:**
- Train in shallow pool with waves → feels different when racing in deep pool
- Expect bigger time improvements when racing in better pools
- **Be aware of this adjustment if competing**

### Safety Features

**Lane Lines:**
- Reduce waves
- Organize swim sessions
- Protect lap swimmers
- Should change color 5 yards from wall (visual cue)

**Backstroke Flags:**
- Essential for swimmer safety
- Not all pools have them (but should)

## Training Philosophy: Hold Your Stroke

### Practice Doesn't Make Perfect

> "Practice doesn't make perfect; **perfect practice** makes perfect. Practicing the wrong action over and over doesn't improve your stroke or speed. Practicing the right technique will."

### Start Short, Add Distance Gradually

**Why:**
- When tired, we revert to old habits
- We lose stroke, form, technique
- Best way to learn: start with shorter distances until new technique is ingrained

**Application:**
- Practice new technique for short distances at first
- Even experienced swimmers should start short when learning new technique
- Gradually add distance once technique is solid

**Goal:** Learn to hold your stroke for whatever distance you're swimming
- Sprinter: hold for 50-100
- Distance: hold for 1500+
- Triathlete (Ironman): hold for up to 2 miles

## Training Frequency

### Minimum: 3x per Week
- More than 1 day off = noticeable difference
- Day's break for recovery is beneficial

### Competitive Standards
- Age-group (12+): 5-6 days/week, occasional 2-a-days in summer
- College/Olympics: 6,000-9,000 yards per workout, 9x per week

### Beginners: 20-30 Minutes Per Session
- **NOT constant swimming** — includes breaks
- Might be only 400-500 yards at first
- Advanced: can go to longer workouts

**Reality check:** Swimmers seldom swim more than 400-500 yards at a time unless training for distance (800/1500).

**Typical training:** Combination of shorter distances — 50s, 100s, 200s

---

## Rest & Recovery

### When Learning New Technique
**Rest often.**
- Your body is performing in different ways
- Even experienced swimmers need rest when learning new technique

---

## Actionable Takeaways

✅ **Float test first** — if you can't float, fix body position before swimming  
✅ **Freestyle/back: ROTATE side-to-side** — never flat  
✅ **Breast/fly: FLAT on stomach** — opposite of free/back  
✅ **Kick from hip** with relaxed, mostly straight legs (12" max apart)  
✅ **Practice perfect technique short distances first**, then add volume  
✅ **Train 3x/week minimum** to see consistent improvement  
✅ **Know your pool** — depth/gutters affect speed & times
