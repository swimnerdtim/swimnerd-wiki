# Whitten's 5 Universal Swimming Principles: Foundations for All Strokes

**Source:** *The Complete Book of Swimming* by Dr. Phillip Whitten (1994), Chapter 6  
**Context:** Core biomechanical principles shared by all four competitive strokes  
**Audience:** Coaches teaching fundamentals, swimmers refining technique

---

## Overview: The Common Thread

All four competitive strokes (freestyle, backstroke, breaststroke, butterfly) share fundamental biomechanical principles. Master these and you'll improve **all** your strokes simultaneously.

**Dr. James "Doc" Counsilman's research** (legendary Indiana coach, 1960s-80s) identified five universal principles that underpin efficient swimming. These aren't stroke-specific—they're universal laws of moving through water.

---

## Principle 1: Streamline Your Body

**The problem:** Water is 800x denser than air. Moving through it creates massive drag.

**The solution:** Minimize resistance by maintaining a **flat, horizontal body position**.

### What Happens When You Don't:
- **Lifting head too high → hips/legs drop → swimming at an angle**
- Creates unnecessary resistance
- Wastes energy fighting against water instead of moving through it
- Excessive rolling side-to-side or wiggling hips compounds the problem

### The Fix:
- **Think:** Smooth, sleek dolphin gliding effortlessly
- **Feel:** Water flowing *around* you, not pushing against you
- Keep body horizontal from head to toes
- Minimize unnecessary movement

**Coaching cue:** "Be a torpedo, not a sailboat."

---

## Principle 2: Obey Newton's Law (The Elliptical Pull)

**Newton's Third Law:** For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

**Naive interpretation:** Push water straight back → move straight forward.

**Reality:** Water moves when you push it. If you continue pushing in a straight line, you're pushing water *that's already moving backward*—zero propulsion.

### Why the S-Curve / Elliptical Pattern Exists:

**Running analogy:** 
- Runner pushes ground backward → ground doesn't move → runner moves forward
- **But water DOES move** when you push it

**Swimming solution:**
- Move hands in an **elliptical (S-shaped) pattern**
- Constantly seek "still water" to push against
- Each stroke phase finds fresh water for maximum propulsion

### All Four Strokes Use This:
- **Freestyle:** S-pull underneath body
- **Backstroke:** S-pattern behind/beside body  
- **Breaststroke:** Elliptical sweep-in-recover pattern
- **Butterfly:** Keyhole pull pattern

**Coaching cue:** "Sculpt through the water, don't paddle."

---

## Principle 3: Use the Proper Pull (High Elbow Position)

**The three pull types:**

### 1. Dropped-Elbow Pull (WORST)
- Elbow drops below hand during pull
- Very little leverage
- Force applied **downward** at start, **upward** at finish
- Result: Lots of energy, minimal forward propulsion

### 2. Straight-Arm Pull (BETTER, BUT NOT OPTIMAL)
- Arm stays straight throughout stroke
- Same problem: Force goes up/down instead of backward
- Causes bobbing motion → creates drag

### 3. High-Elbow Pull (CORRECT)
**How it works:**
- Start with elbow *slightly* bent
- Elbow bends MORE as it moves to vertical position (directly below swimmer)
- Elbow **stays HIGH** throughout pull
- Upper arm rotates inward as elbow bends
- Elbow gradually straightens at end of stroke

**Why it works:**
- Minimizes downward/upward forces
- Maximizes backward push
- Propels swimmer smoothly through water
- No bobbing, no wasted energy

**Coaching cue:** "Reach over a barrel, pull under a barrel."

---

## Principle 4: Position Your Hands Efficiently

### A. Hand Entry: Knife, Don't Slap
- **Goal:** Slice cleanly through water surface
- **Why:** Slapping creates air bubbles around hands → reduces pull efficiency
- Fingers enter first, hand follows

### B. Hand Exit: Streamlined Release
- Hand should exit as streamlined as possible
- Don't push upward (forces body down)
- Clean, efficient exit

### C. Hand Position During Pull: Flat with Spread Fingers

**Old-school teaching:** Cup your hands.

**Counsilman's wind tunnel research (1960s):** Cupped hands are LESS effective.

**Why cupping is bad:**
- Reduces surface area
- Decreases pull efficiency

**Correct hand position:**
- **Flat palm**
- **Fingers firm but relaxed**
- **Slightly spread apart** (not pressed together, not wide open)

**The science:** Slight finger spacing creates small vortices between fingers that actually *increase* propulsion (similar to dimples on a golf ball reducing drag).

**Coaching cue:** "Paddle like you're moving water, not air."

---

## Principle 5: Apply Propulsion Evenly (Continuity of Movement)

**The physics:** Overcoming inertia requires massive energy. Once moving, maintaining motion requires far less.

### Examples:
- **Weightlifter:** Huge force to lift barbell off shoulders, little force to keep it moving
- **Pushing a car:** Enormous effort to start, much easier to keep rolling

### In Swimming:
- Constantly stopping/slowing = constantly overcoming inertia = wasted energy
- **Goal:** Maintain even, constant pace throughout each stroke

### Why Freestyle is Fastest:
**Continuous propulsive force.** There's no dead spot where both arms are recovering—one arm is always pulling.

**Other strokes have recovery phases:**
- Breaststroke: Glide phase (no propulsion)
- Butterfly: Both arms recover simultaneously (brief pause)
- Backstroke: One arm recovers while other pulls (similar to freestyle)

**Coaching cue:** "Smooth is fast. Jerky is slow."

---

## Application: How to Learn These Principles

**Don't expect perfection in a week, month, or even year.**

**Whitten's timeline:**
- After 35+ years of swimming, still learning
- 88-year-old teammate Abe Olanoff (70+ years swimming) still asks for stroke critique

**The Process:**
1. **Master one principle at a time**
2. Practice until it becomes automatic
3. Move to next principle
4. Repeat

**What improvement feels like:**
- More comfortable
- Less tired
- Sense of **flowing with water** instead of fighting it
- Noticeable speed gains

## Coaching Checklist: Teaching the 5 Principles

**Streamline (Principle 1):**
- [ ] Head position neutral (eyes down, not forward)
- [ ] Hips near surface
- [ ] Minimal side-to-side roll (except intentional body roll in free/back)
- [ ] No wiggling or kicking from hips

**Newton's Law (Principle 2):**
- [ ] Hand moves in S-curve or elliptical pattern
- [ ] Not pushing straight back
- [ ] Constantly finding "still water"

**High Elbow (Principle 3):**
- [ ] Elbow higher than hand during pull
- [ ] Elbow bends to ~90° at deepest point
- [ ] Upper arm rotates inward
- [ ] No dropped elbow

**Hand Position (Principle 4):**
- [ ] Clean entry (no slap)
- [ ] Flat palm, fingers slightly spread
- [ ] Not cupping hands
- [ ] Streamlined exit

**Continuity (Principle 5):**
- [ ] Even pace throughout stroke
- [ ] No jerky movements
- [ ] Smooth transitions
- [ ] Constant forward motion

## Key Takeaway

**These 5 principles are THE foundation.** Before worrying about perfect flip turns, race starts, or advanced interval sets, nail these fundamentals. They apply to every stroke, every level, every age.

**Old dogs CAN learn new tricks.** Continuous improvement is possible whether you're 30, 50, or 80.
