# Janet Evans' Log Book Method: Tracking Progress Like a Champion

**Source:** *Janet Evans' Total Swimming* (2007), Chapter 3  
**Context:** How an Olympic champion tracked every workout for 10+ years  
**Audience:** Coaches & serious swimmers committed to measurable improvement

---

## Core Philosophy: Memory is Limited, Files Last

> "I can't emphasize enough how important it is to keep a log book."

**The Evans mandate:** Write down EVERY aspect of EVERY workout.

**Why it matters:**
- Daily progress is invisible (too granular to see)
- Weekly/monthly progress is visible (flip back through pages)
- Discouragement antidote when times/distances plateau
- Provides objective proof you're improving

**The psychological shift:**
> "The process itself is therapeutic and motivating. Inspiration comes not only from flipping back through the pages to see how much faster you now swim or how substantial the gradual increase in total distance has become but also from simply wanting to record a positive workout."

**Translation:** Keeping a log makes you WANT to have good workouts so you can write good things.

---

## What to Log (Minimum)

**Evans' minimum standard:**

1. **Every swim workout**
   - Sets (kick, pull, drill, main, sprint, cool-down)
   - Distances (yards/meters)
   - Intervals (time per rep)

2. **Every dryland workout**
   - Type (bike, run, weights, etc.)
   - Distance
   - Time
   - Weight amounts

3. **Stretching**
   - Time spent

4. **Core work**
   - Number of exercises
   - Type of exercises

**Simple log entry example:**
```
Pre-swim: 30 min bike @ 12 mph, 15 min treadmill @ 3.0 mph / 5.0 grade
Stretching: 5 min
Sit-ups: 5 × 50 of normal sequence
Swimming: 200 free warm-up
          25 laps with 20-sec rest between sets of 5
          50s with 2-min rest between
          150 free cool-down
```

**Advantage:** Quick to write, captures essentials, better than nothing.

---

## What to Log (Detailed)

**For serious swimmers, competitors, and data lovers:**

**Everything from minimum PLUS:**
- How you felt during workout (tired, strong, sluggish, fast)
- Times for each interval
- Heart rate (if tracking ES/MHR zones)
- Effort scale ratings for each set
- Weather/pool conditions (outdoor pools)
- Notes on what worked / didn't work
- Patterns across weeks (good vs. bad weeks)

**Detailed log entry example:**
```
Pre-swim: 30 min bike @ 10 mph, 15 min treadmill @ 3.5 mph / 5.0 grade — ES 3
Stretching: 12 min — felt especially tight today, easier; go up to 25 tomorrow

Sit-ups: 5 × 20 normal sequence — these are getting easier; go up to 25 tomorrow

Swimming: 2,500 yards / 60 min — overall pretty good day; yardage getting easier

Warm-up: 300 free
Kick set: 8 × 25 on 1:00 (no fins) with 50 swim down — still tough to get through

Pull set: 4 × 75 on 1:45 (buoy/paddles) — feel good progress on pulling

Main set: Repeat 4 times 1 × 25 on :30, 1 × 50 on 1:00, 1 × 75 on 1:30, 1 × 100 on 2:00
— held 26-27 sec/25 on all but 3rd, and 4th 100 was 28-29 sec/25

Sprint set: 4 × 50 on 1:30 — didn't feel fast today; went :42, :44, :43, :46

Drill set: 1 × 50 H-S-F, 1 × 50 free, 1 × 50 FLA r/l, 1 × 50 free

Cool-down: 200 switching between free and back per 25
```

**Advantages:**
- Can analyze patterns (fatigue, improvement, plateaus)
- Helpful in preparing for next race/season
- Know which workouts felt good/bad and why
- Coach can review athlete performance over weeks/months

**Disadvantage:** Takes more time/effort. Must record while still at pool or details fade.

## The Legendary Status of Evans' Log Books

### Why Coaches Coveted Her Logs

**Context:**
> "In the swimming world, my log books reached urban legend status. The amount of training I put in and the pace at which I swam my workouts were crazy."

**What happened:**
- Evans' 1988 log book (Olympic year) was passed from coach to coach
- Coaches used it to write new workouts for their teams
- Swimmers DREADED their coach getting Janet's log book → knew an especially tough workout was coming

**What made her logs legendary:**
1. **Volume:** 15,000-18,000 meters/day spread across 2 workouts
2. **Pace:** Fast training times on massive yardage
3. **Consistency:** Every single workout logged in detail
4. **Proof:** Written record of what it takes to win 4 Olympic golds

**Example from her log (June 28, 1988 — 6 weeks before Seoul Olympics):**

**Morning workout (5:30-8:00 AM): 9,000 meters**
```
Warm-up: 500 meters
Main set: 20 × 400 IM
  #s 1-4 on 6:00
  #s 5-8 on 5:50
  #s 9-12 on 5:40
  #s 13-16 on 5:30 (actual times: 5:12, 5:07, 5:04, 5:09)
  #s 17-20 on 5:20 (actual time on last one: 5:04)

Cool-down: 500 meters
```

**Evening workout (4:30-6:30 PM): 7,500 meters**
```
Warm-up: 800 meters
Main set: 14 × 150 free (#1 easy on 2:05, #2 fast on 1:45, alternating)
Ladder set: 4 × 50, 1 × 200, 1 × 200, 3 × 100, 1 × 150, 1 × 150, 2 × 150, 1 × 100, 1 × 100, 1 × 200, 1 × 50, 1 × 50 (mix of back/breast/free, easy/fast)
  — Last 50 breast: :38.1
Pull set: 600 meters free
```

**Total for the day: 16,500 meters (over 10 miles)**

**Evans' reflection:**
> "This day of training was one of my most difficult of the season. My morning workout was obviously the hardest—20 × 400 IMs are very challenging. I was always proud of this particular workout, because even though the main set was 8,000 meters, my last few 400 IMs were swum at a very fast pace. At the 1988 Olympics, I won the 400 IM in a 4:37, and in the above practice, I swam a 5:04 on the last repeat of the set. Training this fast in the workout definitely helped me swim fast at the Olympics!"

## How to Start a Log Book (Practical Steps)

### Step 1: Buy a Notebook

**Evans' method:** Poolside on a towel.

**Options:**
- Traditional notebook (waterproof cover ideal)
- Waterproof notepad + transfer to main log later
- Digital app (SwimNerd, TrainingPeaks, Google Sheets)

**Key:** Whatever format you choose, USE IT CONSISTENTLY.

---

### Step 2: Establish a Routine

**When to write:**
- **Best:** At the pool, immediately after workout (details fresh)
- **OK:** Within 1 hour of finishing (before you forget)
- **Not ideal:** End of day (details fade)

**Where to write:**
- At pool deck (towel, bench, locker room)
- In car before driving home
- At desk if using digital log

**How long it takes:**
- Simple log: 2-3 minutes
- Detailed log: 5-10 minutes

---

### Step 3: Make It Part of Team Culture

**For coaches:**

**Require log books:**
- Check swimmer logs weekly
- Review progress/patterns
- Use data to plan next week's workouts

**Create accountability:**
- Log book check-ins at practice
- Swimmers share progress in team meetings
- Reward most consistent loggers

**Inspire through example:**
- Share Evans' log book story
- Show how other elite swimmers track progress
- Let swimmers see their OWN improvement over months

## What Log Books Reveal

### Pattern 1: Gradual Improvement (The Goal)

**Example progression over 8 weeks:**
- **Week 1:** 10 × 100 free on 2:00, held 1:45-1:48
- **Week 3:** 10 × 100 free on 1:55, held 1:42-1:45
- **Week 5:** 10 × 100 free on 1:50, held 1:38-1:42
- **Week 8:** 10 × 100 free on 1:45, held 1:35-1:38

**What it shows:** Base dropped 15 seconds, times dropped 10 seconds → measurable progress.

---

### Pattern 2: Plateau (Action Required)

**Example progression over 8 weeks:**
- **Week 1:** 10 × 100 free on 2:00, held 1:45-1:48
- **Week 3:** 10 × 100 free on 1:55, held 1:42-1:45
- **Week 5:** 10 × 100 free on 1:50, held 1:42-1:44
- **Week 8:** 10 × 100 free on 1:50, held 1:41-1:44

**What it shows:** Progress stalled after Week 3 → need to change training stimulus.

**Action:**
- Increase intensity (add speed work)
- Increase distance (longer main sets)
- Increase variety (different strokes, drills)
- Check for overtraining (rest week?)

---

### Pattern 3: Injury Risk (Warning Sign)

**Example notes over 2 weeks:**
- **Monday:** "Shoulders felt tight during pull set"
- **Wednesday:** "Right shoulder twinge on 3rd 200"
- **Friday:** "Had to stop main set early, shoulder pain"

**What it shows:** Injury developing → immediate action required.

**Action:**
- Rest 1-2 days
- Stretch shoulders 2-3x/day
- Ice after workouts
- Return with easy swims only
- See coach/doctor if pain persists

---

### Pattern 4: Good vs. Bad Weeks

**Example month:**
- **Week 1:** "Felt strong all week, hit all times"
- **Week 2:** "Tired, struggled through sets"
- **Week 3:** "Back to normal, good workouts"
- **Week 4:** "Sluggish again, couldn't hold pace"

**What it shows:** 2-week cycle of good/bad → overtraining or life stress?

**Action:**
- Review sleep, nutrition, stress
- Adjust training load (more recovery)
- Plan taper before important meet

## Common Log Book Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Logging Sporadically

**Problem:** Only write down "good" workouts or skip weeks.

**Why it fails:** No data = can't track patterns or progress.

**Fix:** Log EVERY workout, even bad ones. Bad workouts are data too.

---

### Mistake 2: Vague Entries

**Problem:** "Swam 2,000 yards. Felt OK."

**Why it fails:** No actionable data. Can't replicate or improve.

**Fix:** At minimum, write sets/distances/intervals. Add how you felt for each set.

---

### Mistake 3: Never Reviewing

**Problem:** Write it down, never look back.

**Why it fails:** Miss the motivational benefit of seeing progress.

**Fix:** Flip back through log every 2-4 weeks. Compare current to past.

---

### Mistake 4: Digital Log Without Backup

**Problem:** Use app/spreadsheet, phone dies, data lost.

**Why it fails:** Years of data = gone.

**Fix:** Auto-backup to cloud (Google Drive, Dropbox, iCloud). Export periodically.

## Application for Coaches

### Use Log Books to Plan Workouts

**Process:**
1. Review athlete log from past week
2. Identify what worked / didn't work
3. Adjust upcoming week accordingly

**Example:**
- **Athlete note:** "Main set felt too easy, finished with 15 sec rest per 100"
- **Coach action:** Lower base by 5 seconds next week

**Example 2:**
- **Athlete note:** "Struggled through pull set, shoulders burned"
- **Coach action:** Reduce pull yardage or add more warm-up

---

### Use Log Books to Track Team Trends

**What to look for:**
- Which workouts produced best times?
- Which workouts were too hard / too easy?
- Which swimmers improved most over season?
- What training volume led to best meet performances?

**Result:** Data-driven coaching instead of guesswork.

---

### Use Log Books for Parent Communication

**Show parents:**
- Objective proof of swimmer's progress
- Consistency (or lack thereof) in attendance/effort
- Areas where swimmer excels or needs work

**Result:** Parents see VALUE of program through data.

## The Motivational Power of the Log

**Evans' insight:**
> "Many swimmers find it motivating to keep a log book. There is no better way to witness the progress being made than by writing down every aspect of each of your workouts."

**Why it works psychologically:**

1. **Visible progress over weeks/months** (even when daily feels stagnant)
2. **Sense of accomplishment** (filling pages = work done)
3. **Accountability** (hard to skip when you have to write "0 yards today")
4. **Goal achievement** (written proof you hit targets)
5. **Inspiration during slumps** ("I was here 2 months ago, look how far I've come")

**The compounding effect:**
- Day 1: Log feels tedious
- Week 4: Log feels routine
- Month 3: Log feels essential
- Year 1: Log becomes your training bible

**Result:** Swimmers who log consistently improve faster than those who don't.

## Key Takeaway

**Janet Evans' log book method:**
1. Buy a notebook
2. Write down EVERY workout (minimum: sets, distances, intervals)
3. Add details if serious (times, feelings, notes)
4. Review every 2-4 weeks to see progress
5. Use data to adjust training

**Why it matters:**
- Progress is hard to see daily but obvious over weeks/months
- Log book = objective proof you're improving
- Motivates you to have good workouts so you can write good things
- Helps coaches plan better workouts based on data

**Evans' proof:** Her log books became legendary — coaches passed them around, teams studied them, swimmers feared their coach getting them.

**The secret:** Consistency. Log EVERY workout for months/years = measurable improvement.

**Start today:** Buy a notebook. Write down tonight's workout. Repeat daily.
