# Hannula's Goal-Setting & Motivation System

**Source:** *Coaching Swimming Successfully* by Dick Hannula (2003)

## The Power of Goals

> "Goals are the motivational foundation of a team. They dare you to reach new personal horizons."

### Why Goals Work

From Earl Nightingale's "The Strangest Secret":
- People with goals succeed because they **know where they're going**
- The key is **believing**
- Believe → Achieve

## Goal-Setting Principles

### Jim Wood (Berkeley Aquatic Club) Three Components

Goals must be:
1. **Specific** — measurable, concrete
2. **Exciting** — high enough to challenge
3. **Believable** — achievable with best effort

**Format:** Present tense, positively stated

### Hannula's 6 Core Principles

1. **State goal positively**
   - ✅ "I will win the 100 free"  
   - ❌ "I won't lose the 100 free"

2. **Be specific and lock in end result**
   - ✅ "46.80 in 100-yard freestyle"
   - ❌ "Swim faster in the 100"

3. **Avoid time limits on some goals**
   - Long-range: no deadline (keeps you motivated for years)
   - Short-range: specific deadline (this season, this meet)

4. **Keep personal goals private**
   - Share only with those who can help you attain them
   - Coach should ALWAYS know swimmers' goals
   - Parents/teammates optional based on support

5. **Reset goals as you approach/attain**
   - Dynamic, not static
   - Continuous improvement mindset

6. **Goals ≠ Wishes**
   - Must include ACTION PLAN
   - "How will you reach this?" required

## Earl Nightingale's 30-Day Method

1. **Picture goal in mind** — relaxed visualization
2. **Write on card** — carry for 30 days
3. **Look several times daily** — constant reinforcement
4. **Stop negative thoughts** — replace with positive
5. **Take control for 30 days** — confident you CAN achieve

**After 30 days:** Goal becomes reality through consistent focus + action.

## Hannula's Goal-Setting Process

### Step 1: DREAM (Preseason)
- Talk about things swimmers WANT to accomplish
- No limits, full imagination
- Write down everything

### Step 2: PRIORITIZE (Measurable & Specific)
- List in measurable terms
  - ✅ "Win state championship in 100 free"
  - ✅ "Swim 46.80 in 100-yard freestyle"
- Pick top 2-3 most important
- Write in logbooks, study lamps, bathroom mirrors, locker doors

### Step 3: REASONS (Critical Step — Hannula added this later)

**Why reasons matter:**
> "Reasons bring meaning to the pursuit of a worthy purpose. Reasons lead to commitment."

**Example reasons swimmers gave:**
- "I want my parents to be very proud of me"
- "I want to feel good about results of my own hard work"
- "I want to attain a college scholarship time"

**Visualization component:**
- SEE yourself winning (scoreboard, time)
- HEAR the crowd cheering
- FEEL congratulations from teammates
- Experience the EMOTION before it happens

### Step 4: HOW (Action Plan)
- What training is necessary?
- What technical skills must improve?
- What experience is needed?
- Lock workout plan to goal attainment

### Step 5: RECORD (Coach's Logbook)
Swimmer meets with coach when they truly believe they can attain goals.

Coach writes in personal training logbook:
- Swimmer's name
- Goals (top 2-3)
- Reasons
- Target times/placements
- Action plan

### Step 6: MONITOR (Throughout Season)
- **Early season** — establish goals
- **Midseason** — reexamine, adjust if needed (injury/illness)
- **Pre-championship** — final review, reinforce belief
- Track training necessary to help swimmers reach goals

## Team Goals

### Coach's Role
1. Realistically assess team opportunities
2. Determine highest possible finish (league, state, national)
3. Qualify X swimmers for championship
4. Place Y swimmers in top 6

### Team Agreement
- Goals must be reachable
- Worthy of best efforts  
- Swimmers must believe they're PART of determining goals
- NOT just coach-imposed

**Hannula's 24th State Championship Example:**

*Team agreement:*
- State championship = PRIMARY goal
- Train through all dual meets
- Unbeaten record = secondary

*Result:*
- Lost close dual meet (ended 24-year unbeaten streak)
- Team stayed focused on primary goal
- Won 24th consecutive state championship
- Every swimmer placed top 6 in individual events
- Multiple state and school records

**Lesson:** Don't let secondary setbacks distract from primary objective.

## Goal-Setting for Coaches

### Personal Coach Goals
- What do YOU want to accomplish?
- Team placement targets
- Individual swimmer development aims
- Program growth objectives

**Hannula's airplane example:**

Returning from 1968 Olympics (Kaye Hall won 2 gold, 1 bronze, world record), exhausted but had to start new high school season immediately.

*On the flight home:*
- Listed possible meet lineup for coming season
- Dreamed what talented young boys could achieve
- Set team goals  
- Planned teaching objectives
- Visualized winning state championship with young team

*Result:* Won 10th consecutive state championship that year, and 2 years later (boys' senior year) won every event except 50 free at state meet.

> "When that airplane landed in Seattle, I hit the ground running. Suddenly I was enthusiastic again."

## Monitoring Progress

### Race Pace Swims (Bill Sweetenham)
- Determine personal best time for event
- Divide by distance (200 ÷ 4 = 50-yard pace)
- Find optimal stroke count at that pace
- Lock in: time + stroke count = most efficient number
- To improve: maintain stroke count at faster pace

### Heart Rate (Fitness Measure)
- Track resting heart rate
- Monitor training heart rate zones
- Measure recovery rates

### Speed Measurements
- Personal best times
- Split times at race pace

## Attaining & Sustaining Motivation

### Primary Motivation Must Come From WITHIN
- Coach can't provide it
- Swimmer chooses effort level
- Swimmer chooses send-off times
- Internal drive > external pressure

### Coach's Role: Reinforce & Reward

**Rewards (non-material):**
1. **Meaningful attention** — look in eyes, express recognition of effort
2. **Thank you** — "Thanks, coach, for a great workout" reciprocated
3. **Recognition** — point tables, season-long tracking
4. **Praise** — when swimmers hit goal times in practice

### Fast Swimming Always Motivates
- Success breeds more success
- Swimming fast in practice = belief in race performance
- Teammates seeing fast swims = team motivation

### Plateaus Are Normal
- Not all swimmers adapt to training at same rate
- Some need full rest to swim fast
- Others swim fast during season
- **Coach response:** Talk to them, keep focused on end result

**Hannula's confidence builder:**
Even when swimmers swimming slowly in dual meet, ask: "Do you believe you can reach goals at championship?"

*Great competitors respond:* "Yes! Our training times are on schedule. We'll peak when it matters."

## Building Leaders

> "Leaders are made, not born. They are made by hard effort, which is the price all of us must pay to achieve any goal that is worthwhile." —Vince Lombardi

### Leadership Development
- **First person you lead:** Yourself
- **Coach's role:** Help people win
- If swimmers not performing well → look in mirror first
- Step up, accept responsibility
- Provide quality support

### Character > Talent
After undefeated Rose Bowl season, Miami coach asked key to success:

**Answer:** Character of the team.

### Leadership Qualities
- Optimism
- Excitement about commitment  
- Sharing themselves
- Giving hope

**Roadblocks to leadership:**
- Cynicism, pride, insecurity, moodiness, perfectionism
