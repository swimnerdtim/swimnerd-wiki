# Hydration and Hyponatremia in Swimmers
**Source:** *Nutrition Periodization for Endurance Athletes* by Bob Seebohar, MS, RD, CSCS

## Overview

**The swimmer's paradox:** You're surrounded by water, yet dehydration and electrolyte imbalance are major performance and safety risks.

**Two opposite dangers:**

1. **Dehydration** (too little fluid) → performance decline, heat illness
2. **Hyponatremia** (too much water, too little sodium) → medical emergency, potentially fatal

**This page covers both.**

---

## Dehydration in Swimmers

### The Reality: Swimmers DO Sweat

**Common myth:** "I'm in water, I don't sweat."

**Truth:** Swimmers sweat 0.5-1.5 liters/hour depending on:
- Pool temperature (warm pool = more sweat)
- Intensity (hard sets = more sweat)
- Individual sweat rate
- Ambient humidity

**Study data:**
- Swimmers training in 27-29°C (80-84°F) water: 0.4-1.0 L/hour sweat
- Swimmers training in 30°C+ (86°F+) water: 1.0-1.5 L/hour sweat
- High-intensity intervals: Up to 2 L/hour

**Why you don't notice:** Sweat immediately mixes with pool water. No visible wetness = illusion of no sweating.

---

### Signs of Dehydration

#### Mild Dehydration (1-2% body weight loss)

**Performance impact:**
- 5-10% reduction in aerobic capacity
- Decreased endurance
- Increased perceived effort (sets feel harder)
- Slower times

**Symptoms:**
- Thirst (by the time you're thirsty, you're already 1-2% dehydrated)
- Dark urine
- Dry mouth
- Mild headache

#### Moderate Dehydration (3-5% body weight loss)

**Performance impact:**
- 10-20% reduction in performance
- Impaired thermoregulation (overheat easier)
- Increased heart rate at same pace
- Mental fatigue, poor decision-making

**Symptoms:**
- Very dark urine (amber/brown)
- Dizziness
- Fatigue, weakness
- Headache
- Nausea

#### Severe Dehydration (>5% body weight loss)

**Medical emergency:**
- Heat exhaustion → heat stroke risk
- Muscle cramps
- Confusion, disorientation
- Rapid heartbeat
- Fainting

**Requires immediate medical attention.**

---

### Measuring Hydration Status

#### Method 1: Urine Color Chart

**Goal:** Light yellow ("straw-colored") all day.

**Urine Color Guide:**
- **Clear:** Overhydrated (or just drank a lot recently)
- **Pale yellow:** Well-hydrated ✅
- **Yellow:** Adequately hydrated ✅
- **Dark yellow:** Mild dehydration ⚠️
- **Amber/brown:** Moderate-severe dehydration 🚨

**Best practice:** Check first morning urine. Should be light yellow. If dark, you went to bed dehydrated.

#### Method 2: Body Weight Change

**Pre/post-practice weigh-in:**

1. Weigh yourself naked before practice
2. Weigh yourself naked after practice (towel dry first)
3. Calculate loss:
   - <1% body weight loss: Well-hydrated ✅
   - 1-2% loss: Mild dehydration (acceptable short-term)
   - 2-3% loss: Moderate dehydration ⚠️
   - >3% loss: Severe dehydration 🚨

**Example (70 kg swimmer):**
- Pre-practice: 70.0 kg
- Post-practice: 68.5 kg
- Loss: 1.5 kg = **2.1% dehydration** ⚠️

**Action:** Drink 2.25 L (150% of loss) over next 4 hours.

#### Method 3: Thirst

**Problem:** Thirst is a LATE indicator. By the time you feel thirsty, you're already 1-2% dehydrated.

**Rule:** Don't rely on thirst. Drink on schedule (every 15-20 min during practice).

---

### Daily Hydration Needs

**Baseline (non-training days):**
- 30-40 ml/kg body weight/day
- 70 kg swimmer: 2.1-2.8 L/day

**Training days:**
- Baseline PLUS sweat losses PLUS ~500ml buffer
- Example: 2.5 L baseline + 1.5 L sweat loss + 0.5 L buffer = **4.5 L total**

**Practical:**
- Carry water bottle everywhere
- Drink with every meal
- Sip throughout day (don't chug liters at once)

---

### Pre-Training Hydration

**Goal:** Start practice euhydrated (well-hydrated, not overhydrated).

**Protocol:**

**2-3 hours before practice:**
- 400-600 ml (14-20 oz) water or sports drink

**10-20 min before practice:**
- 200-300 ml (7-10 oz) water

**Don't chug liters right before practice:**
- Causes sloshing stomach
- Frequent bathroom trips during warm-up
- No performance benefit (kidneys just pee it out)

**Best practice:** Hydrate consistently all day. Don't try to "catch up" right before practice.

---

### During-Training Hydration

**General rule:**
- Drink 150-250 ml (5-8 oz) every 15-20 minutes
- **Target:** 600-1000 ml/hour depending on intensity and sweat rate

**Water vs Sports Drink:**

**Water adequate for:**
- Sessions <60 minutes
- Low-moderate intensity
- Cool pool temperatures

**Sports drink recommended for:**
- Sessions >60 minutes
- High-intensity training
- Warm pool temperatures (>28°C / 82°F)
- Two-a-day training

**Why sports drink?**
- Replaces sodium lost in sweat
- Provides carbs (30-60 g/hour for sessions >75 min)
- Tastes better → better compliance (swimmers drink more)

**Sports drink composition:**
- 6-8% carbohydrate (14-19 g per 8 oz)
- Sodium: 100-200 mg per 8 oz
- Examples: Gatorade, Powerade, Nuun Sport

---

### Post-Training Hydration

**Rule:** Drink 150% of sweat losses.

**Why 150%?**
- You continue losing fluid via urine after training
- Kidneys need to flush metabolic waste
- Overcompensate slightly to ensure full rehydration

**Timeline:** Spread over 2-4 hours (don't chug it all at once).

**Best fluids:**

**First 500 ml:**
- Sports drink (replaces sodium + carbs)
- OR chocolate milk (carbs + protein + sodium)

**Remaining:**
- Water
- Coconut water
- Diluted juice

**Check recovery:** Urine should be light yellow within 4-6 hours.

## Hyponatremia: The Hidden Danger

### What Is Hyponatremia?

**Definition:** Low blood sodium concentration (<135 mmol/L; normal: 135-145 mmol/L).

**Causes:**
1. **Excessive water intake** without electrolyte replacement
2. **Prolonged exercise** (>4 hours) with only water
3. **Sodium loss via sweat** not replaced

**Who's at risk?**
- Endurance athletes (marathon swimmers, triathletes)
- Swimmers who drink ONLY water during long sessions
- Overzealous hydrators ("drink as much as possible")
- Smaller athletes (lower total body water volume)

---

### Symptoms of Hyponatremia

#### Mild (130-135 mmol/L)

- Nausea
- Headache
- Bloating, swelling (hands, feet, face)
- Confusion, disorientation

#### Moderate (125-130 mmol/L)

- Severe nausea, vomiting
- Muscle cramps, weakness
- Altered mental status
- Difficulty breathing

#### Severe (<125 mmol/L)

**Medical emergency:**
- Seizures
- Loss of consciousness
- Pulmonary edema (fluid in lungs)
- Cerebral edema (brain swelling)
- **Can be fatal**

**Requires immediate IV saline treatment.**

---

### How Hyponatremia Happens in Swimmers

**Scenario 1: Open Water Swimmer**

- 10 km (6.2 mile) swim in warm water
- Duration: 2.5-4 hours
- Drinks ONLY water during swim (1.5-2 L)
- Loses 1-2 L sweat (with significant sodium)
- Net effect: Diluted blood sodium

**Result:** Hyponatremia.

**Fix:** Drink sports drink or add electrolyte tablets to water.

---

**Scenario 2: High-Volume Training Camp**

- 3-hour AM practice + 2-hour PM practice
- Warm pool (30°C / 86°F)
- Swimmer drinks 3-4 L water during sessions
- Eats low-sodium meals (no added salt)
- Repeats for 5-7 days

**Cumulative effect:** Sodium depletion over multiple days.

**Result:** Hyponatremia by end of week.

**Fix:** Sports drinks during training + adequate sodium in meals.

---

**Scenario 3: Overzealous Hydration**

- Athlete reads "drink as much as possible"
- Drinks 1+ liter/hour during practice (excessive)
- Only drinks water (no sodium)
- Pees frequently (clear urine = overhydrated)

**Result:** Water intoxication (dilutional hyponatremia).

**Fix:** Drink to thirst + urine color, not "as much as possible."

---

### Preventing Hyponatremia

#### 1. Don't Overdrink

**Myth:** "More water = better."

**Truth:** Drink to replace losses, not exceed them.

**Guideline:**
- Match fluid intake to sweat losses
- Urine should be light yellow, NOT clear
- If peeing every 30 minutes during practice → drinking too much

---

#### 2. Use Sports Drinks for Long Sessions

**Sessions >60 min OR high-intensity OR hot environment:**
- Sports drink (6-8% carbohydrate + 100-200 mg sodium per 8 oz)
- Or water + electrolyte tablets (Nuun, SaltStick, Drip Drop)

**Sodium needs during exercise:**
- 300-600 mg/hour for most athletes
- Higher sweat rate or "salty sweaters" (salt crust on skin after practice): 600-1000 mg/hour

---

#### 3. Salt Your Food

**Dietary sodium target:**
- Training days: 3,000-5,000 mg/day
- Normal days: 2,300-3,000 mg/day

**Sodium-rich foods:**
- Sports drinks
- Pretzels, crackers
- Pickles, olives
- Deli meat, cheese
- Salted nuts
- Broth-based soups
- Soy sauce, teriyaki

**Don't fear salt during heavy training:**
- Sweat losses = 500-1500 mg sodium/hour
- 2-hour practice = 1,000-3,000 mg sodium lost
- Needs replenishment

**Note:** Low-sodium diets are for sedentary people, NOT athletes in heavy training.

---

#### 4. Monitor Symptoms

**Warning signs of hyponatremia during/after long sessions:**
- Swollen hands, fingers (rings tight)
- Puffy face
- Nausea without other explanation
- Headache + confusion
- Clear urine despite drinking "normally"

**Action:** STOP drinking plain water. Switch to sports drink or salty snack + water.

---

### Treating Hyponatremia

**Mild (athlete still alert, able to eat/drink):**

1. **Stop drinking plain water**
2. **Eat salty foods:**
   - Pretzels
   - Salted crackers
   - Broth/soup
   - Deli meat
3. **Sip sports drink slowly**
4. **Monitor symptoms** (should improve in 1-2 hours)

**Moderate-Severe (confusion, vomiting, seizures):**

🚨 **Call 911 / Seek emergency medical care immediately.**

**DO NOT:**
- Give more water
- Force athlete to drink
- Wait to "see if it gets better"

**Treatment:** IV saline (3% hypertonic saline for severe cases).

## Heat Cramps: Related Issue

### What Are Heat Cramps?

**Definition:** Painful, involuntary muscle spasms during/after exercise.

**Common sites:**
- Calves
- Hamstrings
- Quadriceps
- Abdominals

**Cause:** Sodium depletion + dehydration.

**NOT the same as:** Regular muscle cramps from overuse/fatigue (those can happen when well-hydrated).

---

### Recognizing Heat Cramps

**Characteristics:**
- Severe, sustained muscle contraction (not just a twitch)
- Often multiple muscle groups
- Occurs during or shortly after intense training
- Associated with heavy sweating

**Risk factors:**
- "Salty sweaters" (salt residue on skin/suit after practice)
- Drinking only water during long sessions
- Hot, humid environment
- Inadequate sodium intake in diet

---

### Preventing Heat Cramps

1. **Adequate sodium intake:**
   - 300-600 mg/hour during training (via sports drink)
   - Salt meals adequately

2. **Sports drinks, not just water:**
   - Especially for sessions >60 min

3. **Acclimatization:**
   - Gradual exposure to heat/intensity
   - Don't jump into intense training in hot pool

4. **Pre-hydration:**
   - Start practice well-hydrated
   - Consistent daily hydration

---

### Treating Heat Cramps

**Immediate:**
1. Stop activity
2. Stretch affected muscle gently (below pain threshold)
3. Drink sports drink or water + salty snack
4. Ice massage on cramped muscle (optional)

**Recovery:**
- Resume activity only after cramp fully resolved
- Continue hydrating with electrolytes
- Monitor for recurrence

**Persistent/severe cramps:** See medical provider (may need IV saline).

## Practical Hydration Plan for Coaches

### Daily (Non-Training)

✅ **Morning:** Check urine color (goal: light yellow)  
✅ **Throughout day:** Sip water consistently (aim for 2-3 L total)  
✅ **With meals:** Drink 250-500 ml  

---

### Pre-Practice

✅ **2-3 hours before:** 400-600 ml water or sports drink  
✅ **10-20 min before:** 200-300 ml water  

---

### During Practice

✅ **Every 15-20 min:** 150-250 ml fluid  
✅ **Sessions <60 min:** Water OK  
✅ **Sessions >60 min OR high intensity:** Sports drink recommended  
✅ **Warm pool (>28°C / 82°F):** Sports drink mandatory  

**Coaching tip:** Build hydration breaks into practice structure. Don't rely on swimmers to self-hydrate.

**Example:**
- After warm-up (15 min): 1st water break
- After main set 1 (30 min): 2nd water break
- After main set 2 (60 min): 3rd water break
- After cool-down: Final hydration

---

### Post-Practice

✅ **Weigh yourself** (if tracking)  
✅ **Drink 150% of sweat loss** over 2-4 hours  
✅ **First 500 ml:** Sports drink or chocolate milk  
✅ **Remaining:** Water  

---

### Check Recovery (4-6 hours post)

✅ **Urine color:** Light yellow? ✅ Recovered  
✅ **Dark yellow?** Drink more

## Special Scenarios

### Open Water Swimming

**Challenges:**
- Can't carry water bottle
- Longer duration (often 2-4+ hours)
- Warmer water often
- Difficult to hydrate mid-swim

**Solutions:**

1. **Pre-hydrate aggressively:**
   - 600-800 ml 2-3 hours before
   - 300-400 ml 10-20 min before

2. **Feed stations (if available):**
   - Sports drink, not just water
   - Small salty snacks (pretzels, crackers)

3. **Support kayak:**
   - Carry sports drink in squeeze bottle
   - Stop every 20-30 min for quick sip

4. **Post-swim:**
   - Immediate rehydration (1-2 L sports drink)
   - Monitor for hyponatremia symptoms

---

### Hot Pool Training

**Challenge:** Pools >29°C (84°F) = excessive sweat, increased dehydration risk.

**Solutions:**

1. **Increase hydration frequency:**
   - Every 10-15 min instead of 20

2. **Sports drink mandatory:**
   - Sodium losses very high in hot environments

3. **Monitor athletes closely:**
   - Frequent urine color checks
   - Watch for heat illness symptoms (dizziness, nausea, excessive fatigue)

4. **Adjust training if needed:**
   - Reduce volume/intensity on extremely hot days
   - More rest intervals

---

### Altitude Training

**Challenge:** Increased respiratory water loss + dry air = higher dehydration risk.

**Solutions:**

1. **Increase baseline hydration:**
   - 40-50 ml/kg body weight/day (vs 30-40 ml/kg at sea level)

2. **Monitor urine more frequently:**
   - Easy to get dehydrated without noticing

3. **Humidify sleeping environment:**
   - Reduces overnight water loss

---

## Red Flags: When to Seek Medical Help

🚨 **Seek immediate medical attention if:**

- **Severe dehydration:**
  - Confusion, disorientation
  - Fainting, dizziness when standing
  - Rapid heartbeat at rest
  - No urination for 8+ hours

- **Hyponatremia:**
  - Nausea + vomiting + headache + swelling
  - Seizures
  - Altered mental status
  - Difficulty breathing

- **Heat illness:**
  - Core temperature >40°C (104°F)
  - Altered consciousness
  - Rapid, shallow breathing

**DO NOT wait.** These are medical emergencies.

---

## Key Takeaways for Coaches

✅ **Swimmers DO sweat** — even though they're in water. Treat hydration seriously.

✅ **Urine color is the easiest check** — teach athletes to monitor daily.

✅ **Sports drinks > water for sessions >60 min** — sodium and carbs matter.

✅ **Hyponatremia is REAL** — especially in long open-water swims. Don't over-hydrate with plain water.

✅ **Heat cramps = sodium depletion** — treat with sports drink + salty snacks.

✅ **Post-practice hydration = 150% of losses** — don't just drink to thirst.

✅ **Build hydration breaks into practice structure** — don't rely on swimmers to self-hydrate.
