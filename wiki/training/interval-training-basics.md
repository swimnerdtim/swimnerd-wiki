# Interval Training Basics: The Need for Speed

**Source:** *Championship Swimming* by Tracey McFarlane Mirande  
**Context:** How to structure interval training for speed development & conditioning  
**Audience:** Coaches designing sets, swimmers learning to train properly

---

## What is Interval Training?

**Definition:** Swimming several distances with controlled rest periods.

**Example terminology:**
- "Ten 50s on a minute 15" (1:15) = swim 10×50, starting a new 50 every 1:15
- "Ten 50s on the 45" (:45) = swim 10×50, starting a new 50 every :45

**Your interval = Swim time + Rest time**

---

## Why Interval Training Matters

### For Speed Development
"If you want to swim like a real pro, and particularly if you want to swim faster, you need to add [interval training] to just about every workout."

### For Race Preparation
- Teaches your body to hold pace under fatigue
- Builds mental toughness (4th rep is harder than 1st, guaranteed)
- Essential for triathletes and competitive swimmers

### The Animal Night Test (Age-Group Example)

**Set:** 30 × 100 on 2:00
- **Target time:** No more than +10 seconds from personal best 100
- **Miss standard:** Skip an interval, rest additional 2 minutes
- **Make all 30:** Get a pizza 🍕
- **Next week:** Drop to +9 seconds from best time

**Why it's hard:** Even swimmers in great shape struggle, even with 10-second cushion.

## Setting Your First Interval

### Step-by-Step Process

**1. Find Your Baseline Swim Time**
- Beginners: Swim 25 yards at moderate pace (75-80% effort)
- Experienced: Swim 50 yards at moderate pace
- **Don't go all-out** — sustainable speed, not maximum

**2. Add Rest Time**
- Add 20-30 seconds to your swim time
- Need more? Add longer rest
- Round up to nearest 5 seconds

**3. Calculate Your Interval**
- **Swim time + Rest time = Interval**

**Example 1 (Beginner):**
- 25 yards in 40 seconds
- Add 20 seconds rest
- Interval = 1:00 for 25s

**Example 2 (Experienced):**
- 50 yards in 60 seconds
- Add 20 seconds rest
- Interval = 1:20 for 50s

**Example 3 (Fast swimmer):**
- 50 yards in 30 seconds
- Add 20 seconds rest
- Interval = :50 for 50s

## Learning Speed: The 75% Rule

### The All-Out vs. Moderate Test

**First:** Swim 25 yards as fast as you can (all-out)
- See what that feels like
- This is 100% effort

**Second:** Swim 25 yards at ~75% of all-out pace
- Feels sustainable
- Not loafing, but not maximum

**Result:** You now have two different speeds — fast and moderate.

### Why NOT All-Out for Intervals?

**Try going all-out for multiple reps:** You'll be lucky to make it to 4 reps.

**Better approach:**
- Swim at moderate pace (75-80% effort)
- Push performance level slightly
- Over time, you'll get faster with less rest
- Eventually swim longer sets faster

**Goal:** Get better and faster progressively, not burn out on rep #3.

## How to Execute Intervals

### Using a Pace Clock (or Stopwatch)

**Method:**
1. When second hand hits :60 (or 12 on clock), push off wall with streamlined start
2. Swim your distance at moderate rate
3. When you finish, immediately look at second hand
4. **That's your swim time**
5. Rest until your interval time, then go again

**Example:**
- Interval = 1:30 for 25s
- Start at :60, finish at 1:25
- Swim time = 25 seconds
- Rest = 5 seconds (until 1:30)
- Push off again at 1:30

### What Makes It Tough

**The 4th lap is harder than the 1st** — even if you're not swimming your hardest.

**Why:** Accumulated fatigue + never allowing more than interval time for combined swim & rest.

---

## Rest Guidelines

### Beginners (Learning New Stroke)
- **At least 20 seconds rest**
- Allows swimming several 25s or 50s without difficulty

### Distance Swimmers (In Shape)
- Can swim 500 free, rest 15 seconds
- Repeat 2-3 more 500s with only 15 seconds rest

### When to Adjust

**Too easy?** (Not winded after 2-3 reps)
- Swim harder
- Make interval slightly shorter

**Too hard?** (Can't continue)
- Add more rest
- Slow down slightly

**Sweet spot:** Somewhat winded, but able to continue when it's time for next rep.

## Interval Training Structure

### Week 4 Goal (From Book)

**New swimmers:** 6-8 × 25 on short rest  
**Experienced:** 6 × 50 on short rest

**Pace:** Comfortable stroke, comfortable pace

### Sample Beginner Set
- 4 × 25 on :25 rest
- 2 × 50 on :25 rest
- Followed by: 4 × 50

### Advanced Sets (From Book)
- Include speed percentages: 70%, 75%, 80%, 85%, 90%, all-out
- For now: concentrate on good stroke for the distance

---

## The Goal: Hold Your Pace

**Challenge:** Swim the last rep as close to the same pace as the first rep.

**This is how you learn speed:**
- Not by swimming one fast 25
- By swimming many 25s/50s at controlled pace
- Pushing through accumulated fatigue

---

## Progression Over Time

### Personal Best vs. World Records

"Most people aren't training for the next Olympics, and so the most important thing is **to improve over time and improve your personal best times**."

**Even Olympic swimmers:**
- Work to beat personal best against the clock
- Sometimes, when trying to swim PB, we get lucky and set US/world record

### For Competitors

**Essential for:**
- Age-group swimmers
- Triathletes (especially important)
- Masters swimmers
- Senior Olympics

**Why:** This is how you learn speed.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Going all-out on rep #1** → Won't finish the set  
❌ **Not resting enough when learning new technique** → Groove bad habits when tired  
❌ **Swimming too slow** → Not challenging yourself  
❌ **No clear interval time** → Can't track improvement  
❌ **Comparing to others** → Focus on YOUR time, YOUR improvement  

---

## When to Revert to Old Habits

**Fatigue = Technique Breakdown**

When we get tired:
- We revert to old habits
- We lose our stroke
- We lose our form
- We lose proper technique

**Solution:** Start with shorter distances until new technique is ingrained, then gradually add distance.

**Goal:** Learn to hold your stroke for whatever distance you're swimming.

---

## Session Structure (20-30 Minutes)

### Not Constant Swimming

**20-30 minutes ≠ non-stop swimming** (difficult to impossible for beginners)

**Reality:**
- Swimmers take breaks
- Seldom swim more than 400-500 yards at a time (unless distance training)
- Combination of shorter distances: 50s, 100s, 200s

**Think of it as:** 20-30 minutes with many rest breaks.

### Typical Beginner Session
- **Total:** 400-500 yards in 20-30 minutes
- **Format:** Multiple short sets with rest

### Advanced Session
- **Total:** Longer workouts possible
- **Structure:** Still broken into sets, not continuous

## Actionable Coaching Applications

### Building Your First Interval Set

**For new swimmers:**
1. Test 25 yard time at 75% effort
2. Add 20-30 seconds
3. Assign 4-6 × 25 on that interval
4. Goal: hold pace across all reps

**For experienced swimmers:**
1. Test 50 yard time at 75-80% effort
2. Add 20 seconds
3. Assign 6-8 × 50 on that interval
4. Track times — are they holding pace?

### Progressive Overload

**Week 1:** 6 × 25 on X interval  
**Week 2:** 8 × 25 on X interval  
**Week 3:** 6 × 50 on Y interval  
**Week 4:** 8 × 50 on Y-:05 interval (reduce rest by 5 seconds)

### Speed Work Percentages

**When ready for speed variation:**
- 70% = Easy, technical focus
- 75% = Moderate, sustainable
- 80% = Solid effort
- 85% = Getting uncomfortable
- 90% = Hard effort
- 100% = All-out

**Application:** Mix percentages within sets
- Example: 8 × 50 — odds @ 75%, evens @ 85%

## Key Quotes
