## The Four Questions You Must Answer

Every interval set requires four decisions. Here's how to make them intelligently.

---

### Question 1: What Distance Per Repeat?

**The fundamental trade-off:**  
Longer repeats → more endurance, less speed  
Shorter repeats → more speed, less endurance

#### Decision Matrix by Race Distance

**Training for 50m/50y sprint:**  
- Primary: 25s, 50s  
- Secondary: 75s, 100s (build endurance base)  
- Avoid: 200s+ (too slow, wrong energy system)

**Training for 100m/100y:**  
- Primary: 50s, 75s, 100s  
- Secondary: 25s (speed), 150s (endurance)  
- Variety is key

**Training for 200m/200y:**  
- Primary: 50s, 100s, 150s, 200s  
- Secondary: 25s (speed), 300s (overdistance)  
- **Most variety needed** — 200 requires both speed and endurance

**Training for 400m/400y and distance:**  
- Primary: 100s, 200s, 300s, 400s  
- Secondary: 50s (maintain speed), 500s+ (overdistance)  
- Focus shifts to endurance, but don't neglect speed

#### Variety Within a Week

**Example for 200m swimmer:**

**Monday:** 20 × 50 (speed emphasis)  
**Wednesday:** 10 × 100 (balanced)  
**Friday:** 5 × 200 (race-pace simulation)

**Why variety matters:**  
1. Prevents boredom  
2. Develops multiple energy systems  
3. Keeps body adapting (avoid plateau)

---

### Question 2: How Many Repeats?

**General guideline:** 40-70% of total workout distance should be interval/repetition work.

**Example daily workout (7,000 yards total):**

| Component | Yards | % of Total |
|-----------|-------|------------|
| Warm-up | 1,000 | 14% |
| Interval set | 3,000 | **43%** |
| Kick set | 800 | 11% |
| Pull set | 1,000 | 14% |
| Cool-down | 800 | 11% |
| Sprint set | 400 | 6% |

**Within that 40-70%, how to choose rep count:**

#### Rep Count Guidelines by Distance

**25s/50s (short repeats):**  
- Typical: 20-40 reps  
- Elite: Up to 60 reps  
- **Why:** Lower fatigue per rep, can sustain volume

**100s (medium repeats):**  
- Typical: 10-30 reps  
- Elite: Up to 40 reps  
- **Why:** Balance of quality and quantity

**200s (long repeats):**  
- Typical: 5-15 reps  
- Elite: Up to 20 reps  
- **Why:** High fatigue per rep, quality over quantity

**400s+ (very long repeats):**  
- Typical: 3-8 reps  
- Elite: Up to 12 reps  
- **Why:** Approaches repetition training, limit volume

#### Progressive Overload Across Season

**Early season (building base):**  
High volume, moderate intensity  
→ 30 × 100 on 1:30 @ 1:05 average

**Mid-season (building speed):**  
Moderate volume, higher intensity  
→ 20 × 100 on 1:20 @ 1:00 average

**Late season (race preparation):**  
Lower volume, maximum intensity  
→ 10 × 100 on 2:00 @ 56 average

**Taper (peak performance):**  
Minimal volume, maintain intensity  
→ 4 × 100 on 3:00 @ 54 average

### Question 3: What Rest Interval?

**The critical principle:**  
**Shorter rest = more endurance emphasis**

**Why:** Heart rate stays elevated when rest is short.

#### Rest Interval Categories

**SHORT REST (5-15 seconds)**

**Physiological effect:**  
- Pulse drops from 180 → 130-160  
- Partial oxygen debt recovery  
- Heart rate stays in training zone  
- **Primary adaptation:** Cardiorespiratory endurance

**Example:** 30 × 100 on 1:00 (swim 55, rest 5)

**When to use:**  
- Early season base building  
- Distance swimmers  
- Recovery weeks  
- High-volume days

**MEDIUM REST (15-30 seconds)**

**Physiological effect:**  
- Pulse drops from 180 → 120-140  
- Moderate recovery  
- Balance of endurance and quality  
- **Primary adaptation:** Aerobic capacity + some speed

**Example:** 20 × 100 on 1:20 (swim 58, rest 22)

**When to use:**  
- Mid-season training  
- Most swimmers most of the time  
- Balanced development

**LONG REST (30-60 seconds)**

**Physiological effect:**  
- Pulse drops from 180 → 100-120  
- Significant recovery  
- Higher quality possible  
- **Borders on repetition training**

**Example:** 10 × 100 on 1:30 (swim 50, rest 40)

**When to use:**  
- Late season quality work  
- Speed development  
- After high-volume day (active recovery)

**FULL RECOVERY REST (3-5+ minutes)**

**This is repetition training, not interval training.**

**Physiological effect:**  
- Pulse drops from 180 → under 100  
- Full recovery  
- Maximum effort possible  
- **Primary adaptation:** Anaerobic capacity, speed

**Example:** 8 × 100 on 5:00 (swim 48, rest 4:12)

**When to use:**  
- 2-3x per week maximum  
- Race simulation  
- Peak performance periods

#### Departure Time (DP) vs. Rest Interval

**Most swimmers use departure times** because the math is simpler.

**Rest Interval Math (confusing):**  
- Swim 1: 58 seconds → depart at 1:28  
- Swim 2: 1:03 → depart at 1:33  
- Swim 3: 55 seconds → depart at 1:25  
- **Problem:** Constant mental math, easy to mess up

**Departure Time Math (simple):**  
- DP = 1:20  
- Swim 1: 58 → rest = 22 seconds  
- Swim 2: 1:03 → rest = 17 seconds  
- Swim 3: 55 → rest = 25 seconds  
- **Advantage:** Everyone leaves on same time, clock does the math

**How to set DP for a group:**  
1. Fastest swimmer's goal time + desired rest  
2. Example: Fastest swims 55, want 20s rest → DP = 1:15  
3. Slower swimmers leave on later DP (1:25, 1:35, etc.)  
4. Everyone gets similar rest, different DPs

### Question 4: How Fast Should Each Repeat Be?

**The brutal truth:** There is **no exact formula**.

**Counsilman's approach:** Learn from experience.

#### The Progressive Learning Method

**Step 1: Establish baseline**  
Swim the set once, record every time.

**Example:** 20 × 100 on 1:20  
→ Average: 1:02  
→ Range: 59.5 to 1:04  
→ **This is your baseline**

**Step 2: Repeat the set in a few days**  
Try to improve average time while maintaining range.

**Attempt 2:** 20 × 100 on 1:20  
→ Average: 1:00.5  
→ Range: 58 to 1:03  
→ **Improvement!**

**Step 3: Find your plateau**  
Keep repeating until you can't improve anymore.

**Attempt 5:** 20 × 100 on 1:20  
→ Average: 58.8  
→ Range: 56.5 to 1:01  
→ **Peak average achieved**

**Step 4: Track this as your PR for this set**  
Write in logbook: "20 × 100 on 1:20 — PR avg 58.8"

**Step 5: Improvement only comes from:**  
1. Better conditioning  
2. Improved stroke efficiency  
3. Mental toughness

**Not from:**  
- Going out too fast and dying  
- Inconsistent pacing  
- Skipping reps

#### The Logbook Culture

**What elite swimmers track:**

| Set | Best Average | Date | Notes |
|-----|--------------|------|-------|
| 20 × 100 on 1:20 | 58.8 | 2026-03-15 | Felt smooth, held pace |
| 10 × 200 on 2:40 | 2:08.5 | 2026-03-18 | Last 3 were tough |
| 30 × 50 on :45 | 28.2 | 2026-03-20 | New PR! |

**Post-practice conversations:**  
"Did you hear what Gary went for 10 × 150 fly?"  
"Yeah, 1:26.5 average. That's a PR by 2 seconds."  
"He should break the American record now."

**Why this works:**  
- Practice times predict race times  
- Objective progress measurement  
- Intrinsic motivation  
- Peer accountability

#### Speed Guidelines (General Ranges)

**Short rest interval sets:**  
- Typically **10-20% slower than race pace**  
- Example: 100 race pace = 55 → interval set = 1:01-1:05

**Medium rest interval sets:**  
- Typically **5-15% slower than race pace**  
- Example: 100 race pace = 55 → interval set = 58-1:03

**Long rest / repetition sets:**  
- Typically **0-10% slower than race pace**  
- Example: 100 race pace = 55 → repetition set = 55-1:00

**But remember:** These are approximations. Individual variation is huge.

## Real Example: John Kinsella (World Record Holder 1500m)

**Personal best 100 free:** 46.1

**Three different sets, three different purposes:**

### Set A: Endurance Emphasis
**30 × 100 on 1:00 (DP)**  
- Rest interval: 5 seconds  
- Average time: **55.2** (19% slower than PB)  
- Pulse after repeat: 174 bpm  
- Pulse before next repeat: 163 bpm  
- **Purpose:** Build cardiorespiratory endurance, maintain elevated HR

**Analysis:**  
Heart rate barely drops → sustained cardiovascular stress → aerobic adaptation.

---

### Set B: Balanced Development
**10 × 100 on 1:30 (DP)**  
- Rest interval: 20 seconds  
- Average time: **50.4** (9% slower than PB)  
- Pulse after repeat: 181 bpm  
- Pulse before next repeat: 124 bpm  
- **Purpose:** Blend speed and endurance, moderate quality

**Analysis:**  
Heart rate drops significantly but still in training zone → mixed aerobic/anaerobic.

---

### Set C: Speed Emphasis (Repetition Training)
**4 × 100 on 6:00 (DP)**  
- Rest interval: 5 minutes  
- Average time: **48.2** (4.5% slower than PB)  
- Pulse after repeat: 184 bpm  
- Pulse before next repeat: 88 bpm  
- **Purpose:** Race-pace simulation, anaerobic development

**Analysis:**  
Full recovery between reps → maximum effort → neuromuscular training + speed.

## Pulse Rate Monitoring (Counsilman's Innovation)

**What Counsilman did:**  
Stopped swimmers mid-workout, took pulse for 10 seconds, multiplied by 6.

**Modern method:**  
Heart rate monitors (chest strap or watch).

### Target Heart Rates by Set Type

**Overdistance / Long interval sets:**  
- Target: 130-150 bpm sustained  
- Minimum acceptable: 130 bpm  
- **If below 130:** "You're not working hard enough."

**Medium rest interval sets:**  
- Target: 140-170 bpm during repeats  
- Drop to: 120-140 bpm between repeats  
- **Pattern:** Saw-tooth (up and down but never below 120)

**Short rest interval sets:**  
- Target: 160-180 bpm during repeats  
- Drop to: 130-165 bpm between repeats  
- **Pattern:** Plateau (stays high throughout set)

**Repetition training:**  
- Target: 170-200 bpm during repeats  
- Drop to: under 100 bpm between repeats  
- **Pattern:** Spike and recover

### How to Use HR Data

**During practice:**  
"Sarah, your HR is only 125 on this set. You should be at 140+. Pick up the pace or shorten rest."

**Post-practice:**  
Review HR graph to confirm set achieved intended stimulus.

**Across season:**  
Track HR at same pace. As fitness improves, HR drops at same speed → increase intensity or decrease rest.

## Progression Models Across a Season

### Model 1: Volume Progression (Early → Mid Season)

**Week 1-4 (Base Building):**  
**Set:** 20 × 100 on 1:30  
**Goal:** Learn to hold pace, build aerobic base  
**Target avg:** 1:08

**Week 5-8 (Increase Volume):**  
**Set:** 30 × 100 on 1:30  
**Goal:** Sustain same pace for more reps  
**Target avg:** 1:08

**Week 9-12 (Maintain Volume, Increase Intensity):**  
**Set:** 30 × 100 on 1:25  
**Goal:** Faster pace, same volume  
**Target avg:** 1:03

---

### Model 2: Rest Interval Progression (Mid → Late Season)

**Week 1-4:**  
**Set:** 20 × 100 on 1:25 (25s rest)  
**Target avg:** 1:00

**Week 5-8:**  
**Set:** 20 × 100 on 1:20 (20s rest)  
**Target avg:** 1:00 (same speed, less rest)

**Week 9-12:**  
**Set:** 20 × 100 on 1:15 (15s rest)  
**Target avg:** 1:00 (same speed, even less rest)

**Principle:** Maintain speed while reducing rest → improves recovery capacity.

---

### Model 3: Intensity Progression (Late Season → Taper)

**Week 1:**  
**Set:** 20 × 100 on 1:20  
**Target avg:** 58

**Week 2:**  
**Set:** 15 × 100 on 1:30  
**Target avg:** 56

**Week 3:**  
**Set:** 10 × 100 on 2:00  
**Target avg:** 54

**Week 4 (Taper):**  
**Set:** 6 × 100 on 3:00  
**Target avg:** 52

**Principle:** Volume drops, intensity rises, rest increases → peak performance.

## Common Mistakes and Fixes

### Mistake #1: Going Out Too Fast

**What happens:**  
First 5 reps are fast, last 15 are slow, average is mediocre.

**Example:**  
20 × 100 goal average = 1:00  
Actual: 55, 56, 57, 58, 59, 1:01, 1:03, 1:05, 1:07... (death spiral)

**Fix: Negative Split or Hold Pace Strategy**

**Negative split:** First half slower than second half  
1:02, 1:02, 1:01, 1:01, 1:00, 1:00, 59, 59, 58, 58...

**Hold pace:** Even throughout  
1:00, 1:00, 1:00, 1:00, 1:00...

**Counsilman's recommendation:** Hold pace for most sets. Negative split for special occasions.

---

### Mistake #2: Rest Interval Too Short for Rep Count

**What happens:**  
30 × 100 on :50 rest → too ambitious → swimmer fails at rep 12.

**Fix: The 75% Rule**

**Rule:** You should be able to complete **at least 75% of planned reps** before significant slowdown.

**Example:**  
Plan: 20 × 100  
Minimum successful: 15 reps at target pace  
If you're dying at rep 10 → rest is too short OR target pace is too fast

**Adjustment:**  
Increase rest by 5-10 seconds OR slow target by 2-3 seconds.

---

### Mistake #3: Too Much Variety (No Consistency)

**What happens:**  
Every set is different → can't track progress → no motivation from PRs.

**Example:**  
Monday: 25 × 100  
Wednesday: 18 × 125  
Friday: 12 × 150  
**Problem:** No repeated sets → no PRs → no objective progress

**Fix: Anchor Sets**

**Define 3-5 "anchor sets" per season:**  
1. 20 × 100 on 1:20  
2. 10 × 200 on 2:40  
3. 30 × 50 on :45  

**Repeat these every 2-3 weeks.**  
Track PRs. Build intrinsic motivation from improvement.

---

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Stroke Count

**What happens:**  
Times improve but stroke count increases → inefficiency masked by fitness.

**Fix: Stroke & Time Sets** (Phelps method)

**Example:** 30 × 50 fly on 1:00  
**Goal:** 27 seconds @ 17 strokes  
**Rule:** If time hits 27 but strokes = 19 → **don't count it as success**

**Why this matters:**  
Racing is about efficiency, not just fitness. Low stroke count at speed = championship technique.

**See:** [Phelps 10-Day Cycle](../coaches/bob-bowman/phelps-10-day-cycle-2003.md) — Multiple "Stroke & Time" examples.

---

### Mistake #5: No Recovery Weeks

**What happens:**  
12 weeks of progressive overload → week 13 = exhaustion, illness, or injury.

**Fix: 3:1 or 4:1 Ratio**

**3:1 Model:**  
- Week 1: Hard  
- Week 2: Hard  
- Week 3: Hard  
- Week 4: **Easy (recovery week)**

**Recovery week characteristics:**  
- 60-70% of normal volume  
- Maintain intensity (speed) but reduce reps  
- Extra rest days  
- More technique focus, less grinding

**Example:**  
Normal week: 20 × 100 on 1:20 @ 1:00 avg  
Recovery week: 10 × 100 on 1:30 @ 1:00 avg (same speed, less volume, more rest)

## Sample Integrated Workout (Using These Principles)

**Total: 7,200 yards | Duration: ~90 minutes**

**Warm-up (1,000 yds)**  
400 swim easy  
4 × 100 IM order on 2:00 (easy)  
200 kick choice

**Main Set 1: Short Rest Interval (2,400 yds)**  
30 × 100 free on 1:25  
Goal: Average 1:05 or better  
**Purpose:** Aerobic base, sustained HR 140-160

**Kick Set (600 yds)**  
12 × 50 kick on 1:00  
Odds: fast, Evens: easy

**Main Set 2: Medium Rest Interval (1,600 yds)**  
8 × 200 free on 2:40  
Goal: Average 2:08 or better  
**Purpose:** Race pace endurance, HR 150-170

**Pull Set (800 yds)**  
8 × 100 pull on 1:30  
Build each 100 (easy → moderate → fast → faster by 25)

**Sprint Set (400 yds)**  
8 × 50 on 2:00 (full recovery)  
All-out effort, record times  
**Purpose:** Neuromuscular speed, max effort

**Cool-down (400 yds)**  
400 swim easy choice

| Pull | Interval | 11% | Upper body |

## Key Takeaways

✅ **Answer four questions** for every interval set: distance, reps, rest, speed  
✅ **Shorter rest** = more endurance; **longer rest** = more speed  
✅ **No exact formula** for speed — learn from experience and logbook tracking  
✅ **Variety matters** but keep anchor sets for progress tracking  
✅ **Monitor pulse rate** to confirm you're in the right training zone  
✅ **Progress across season:** volume → intensity, short rest → long rest  
✅ **Avoid death spirals:** Hold pace or negative split, don't go out too fast  
✅ **Track stroke count** along with time — efficiency = speed  
✅ **Build recovery weeks** into your plan (3:1 or 4:1 ratio)
