# Mental Preparation for Competition (Mark Schubert)

**Source:** *Competitive Swimming: Techniques for Champions* (1990, 1996)  
**Author:** Mark Schubert  
**Context:** Chapter 7 — Practical mental skills for peak performance  
**Significance:** **ACTIONABLE** mental training system: Relaxation + Concentration + Visualization = Peak Performance

---

## The Big Picture

### What Is a Peak Performance?

**Definition:** The very best the individual is capable of *at that time*

- Midseason best = peak performance (for that moment)
- Championship best = peak performance (for that moment)
- **Both should be judged as peaks**

**The magical feeling:**
> "Easy, relaxed, smooth, confident... I could go on forever... like I was the only one there... strong, fast... I could see everything so clearly... Time slowed down... I had all the time in the world to do things... I was always moving faster and sooner than everyone else... It was like a dream... I was watching myself perform... I had lots of air, I never got tired... at the end I wasn't tired at all... I was so happy!"

**Common elements:**
- Physical skill under guidance of **subconscious mind** ("on automatic")
- Muscles warm, relaxed, "loose" (fluid moving smoothly)
- Optimal muscular tension (ready but not tight)
- Focused state (time/space distortion, clear vision)
- Pre-set script activated from subconscious

---

## The Peak Performance Process

### Three Elements (Must Develop Together)

1. **Relaxation** — Muscular readiness + breathing control
2. **Concentration** — Focused attention, time distortion
3. **Visualization** — The pre-set script in your subconscious

**Applications:**
- **Long-term:** Daily practice over months before key events
- **Short-term trigger:** 10-second activation before race

**Key insight:**
> "You must exercise the appropriate muscles in the appropriate ways! Constant proper mental practice produces positive results."

**Advantage:** Can practice ANYWHERE, ANYTIME (not limited to pool)
- School
- Social situations  
- Every phase of life

## 1. RELAXATION

### What It Really Means

**NOT limp muscles** (that's useless for performance)

**Optimal state:**
- Small degree of muscular tension (readiness)
- Muscles warm, relaxed, loose
- Fluid between muscle fibers moving smoothly
- Breathing free and easy

**Two components:**
1. Prime mover muscles (arms, legs, core)
2. Breathing mechanism muscles

---

### Technique 1: Muscular Relaxation ("Let Go")

**Think of tension as a fluid (like water) that flows from place to place.**

**Setup:**
- Lie down (rug, grass, bed)
- Feel your weight on the surface
- Become aware of all contact points

**The Process:**

1. **Select trigger phrase:** "Let go"
2. **Right foot:** Tense it, count "1, 2, 3, 4, 5... Let go"
   - As you say "Let go," feel tension flow out like water
3. **Calf muscle:** Tense foot + calf, "1-5... Let go"
4. **Progress up the body:**
   - Thigh
   - Entire leg
   - Left leg (repeat process)
   - Buttocks (often overlooked, but critical!)
   - Core
   - Arms
   - Shoulders
   - Neck
   - **FACE** (most important!)

**Special attention: The face**

**Why it matters:**
> "Look at swimmers before an event — tension is visible in their faces. Those swimming fast have relaxed, serene, masklike faces. Those struggling have contorted, tense faces."

**Experiment:**
1. Tighten your face ("ugly mask")
2. Try to talk, notice your breathing
3. Tension creeps into neck → shoulders → arms (in under 2 minutes!)

**Solution:**
- Put face through "Let go" exercises
- Breathing becomes easier
- Tension stops flowing to upper body

**Duration:** 2-10 minutes per session

**When to use:**
- Every mental preparation practice
- Even if you feel relaxed (hidden tension is destructive)
- Before competitions (short-form trigger)

---

### Technique 2: Breathing Easy (Belly Breathing)

**How breathing works:**
1. **Diaphragm lowers** → chest cavity expands → air rushes in
2. **Chest lifts** → more room → more air (requires more effort)

**Three types of breathing:**

#### 1. "Hidden" Breathing (Most Relaxed)
- Belly breathing
- Stomach moves outward
- What you see in sleeping people
- Optimal for performance

#### 2. "Chest" Breathing (Moderate Effort)
- Lift chest to inhale deeply
- More muscular effort than belly breathing

#### 3. "Dragon's Breath" (Maximum Effort)
- Breathing as deeply and quickly as possible
- Great effort and energy
- Can cause hyperventilation (do for only a few seconds!)

---

**The Practice:**

1. Lie down, complete "Let go" muscular relaxation
2. **Try "hidden" breathing:**
   - Take in air without visible movement
   - Belly breathing only
3. **Do "chest breathing":**
   - Lift chest, inhale/exhale deeply
4. **Do a few "dragon breaths":**
   - Deep, fast, all-out (⚠️ only a few seconds!)
5. **Return to belly breathing:**
   - Relax into it
   - Say "Breathe easy" to yourself
   - Feel the difference

**Result:** Great sense of overall muscular relaxation + breath control

---

**Research finding:**
> "Belly breathing is the optimum breathing state to be in when you begin a competition."

**Your trigger phrases:**
- "Let go" = muscular relaxation
- "Breathe easy" = breathing control

**No magic in the words** — choose your own if you prefer  
**Magic is in the process** — real control over muscles and breath

## 2. CONCENTRATION

### What It Is

> "Concentration is the greatest art of all. Without concentration, there would be no art. Successful people in all walks of life share this skill."

**In sports:**
- Ability to focus totally on the task
- Exclude all distractions
- Enter "the zone"

**Peak performance state:**
- Time/space perception changes
- Vision becomes clearer
- Single-pointed focus
- "Automatic pilot" engaged

---

### The Challenge

**External distractions:**
- Crowd noise
- Competitors
- Pool conditions
- Weather

**Internal distractions:**
- Worry about performance
- Fear of failure
- Overthinking technique
- Self-doubt

**The solution:** Train concentration like a muscle

---

### Technique: Focused Attention Drills

**Practice 1: Object Focus**
1. Select a small object (coin, mark on wall, anything)
2. Focus on it exclusively for 1 minute
3. If your mind wanders, gently return focus
4. Gradually increase to 5 minutes

**Practice 2: Breath Counting**
1. Count your breaths (1 on inhale, 2 on exhale, up to 10)
2. Return to 1
3. If you lose count, start over
4. Practice 5-10 minutes daily

**Practice 3: Task Focus**
1. During warmup, focus ONLY on hand entry
2. During a set, focus ONLY on kick rhythm
3. During cool-down, focus ONLY on breathing pattern
4. Single-point attention for entire swim

**Application to racing:**
- Pre-race: Focus on your breathing ("Breathe easy")
- On blocks: Focus on your dive
- In water: Focus on your stroke (from visualization)
- Nothing else exists

---

### The "Zone" State

**Characteristics:**
- Time slows down (or speeds up)
- Effortless movement
- Clear perception
- Total absorption
- No self-consciousness

**How to access it:**
1. Relaxation (muscles + breath)
2. Concentration (single-point focus)
3. Visualization (activate the script)

All three together = consistent access to peak performance

## 3. VISUALIZATION

### What It Is

**The pre-set script in your subconscious**

- A mental movie of your perfect race
- Stored in subconscious mind
- Activated when you begin the race
- Allows "automatic" performance (no conscious thinking)

**Why it works:**
> "Your conscious mind cannot operate fast enough to perform a physical skill effectively. When you 'think your way through' a race, you perform poorly. Peak performance requires the skill to be under subconscious guidance."

---

### Building Your Visualization

**Step 1: Recall Your Best Swim**

Close your eyes. Remember:
- How did your body feel?
- What did you see?
- What did you hear?
- How did you breathe?
- What was your tempo?
- Where was your focus?

**Step 2: Create the Perfect Race**

Mentally rehearse:
1. **Pre-race:**
   - Walking to blocks
   - Shaking out arms
   - Taking deep breaths
   - Feeling calm, confident
   
2. **The start:**
   - Toes gripping the block
   - "Take your mark"
   - Explosive dive
   - Streamline entry
   
3. **The race (stroke by stroke):**
   - First 25: Smooth, controlled, establish rhythm
   - Turns: Fast feet to wall, explosive push-off
   - Mid-race: Hold tempo, stay relaxed, breathe easy
   - Last 25: Accelerate, high tempo, drive to wall
   - Finish: Explosive touch

4. **Post-race:**
   - Look at clock
   - Feel satisfied
   - Controlled breathing

**Step 3: Add Sensory Detail**

- **Visual:** See the pool, lane, competitors
- **Kinesthetic:** Feel your muscles, the water, your tempo
- **Auditory:** Hear the starter, the splash, your breathing
- **Emotional:** Feel confident, strong, relaxed

---

### Practice Schedule

**Daily (10-15 minutes):**
1. "Let go" muscular relaxation (5 min)
2. "Breathe easy" belly breathing (2 min)
3. Visualization of perfect race (5-10 min)

**Frequency:** Every day for 3-6 months before championship

**Result:**
- Script becomes embedded in subconscious
- Activates automatically on race day
- Conscious mind can "watch" as you perform

---

### Short-Term Trigger (Race Day)

**10 seconds before your race:**

1. "Let go" (quick body scan, release tension)
2. "Breathe easy" (2-3 belly breaths)
3. Activate visualization (see first stroke)

**Cue:** Shaking out arms, deep breath, eyes close briefly

**Result:** Script activates, automatic pilot engaged

## Putting It All Together

### Long-Term Preparation (Months Before)

**Daily practice (15-20 minutes):**
1. Muscular relaxation ("Let go")
2. Breathing control ("Breathe easy")
3. Visualization (perfect race)

**Consistency matters:** Like stroke technique, mental skills require repetition

**Practice anywhere:**
- Before bed (excellent time!)
- During class breaks
- On bus rides
- Waiting for practice

---

### Race Week

**3-7 days before:**
- Continue daily practice
- Add race-specific details (pool, competitors, time goals)
- Practice short-term trigger

**2 days before:**
- Reduce training volume
- Increase visualization frequency (2-3× per day)
- Stay relaxed, confident

**Race day morning:**
- Light practice + visualization
- Avoid overthinking
- Trust the process

---

### Race Day (The Moment)

**Pre-race (30 min before):**
- Find quiet space
- Full relaxation + visualization (10 min)
- Stay warm, stay loose

**Behind the blocks (5 min before):**
- "Let go" (shake out tension)
- "Breathe easy" (2-3 deep breaths)
- Activate script (see your start)

**On the blocks (10 seconds):**
1. Toes grip
2. "Let go" (quick scan)
3. "Breathe easy" (one deep breath)
4. See first stroke
5. Starter calls: **"Take your mark"**
6. **GO** (script runs automatically)

---

### During the Race

**What you should NOT do:**
- ❌ Think about technique
- ❌ Worry about competitors
- ❌ Count strokes obsessively
- ❌ Panic about pace

**What you SHOULD do:**
- ✅ Let the script run
- ✅ Stay focused on tempo/rhythm
- ✅ Breathe easy
- ✅ Trust your training

**The paradox:**
> "The less you consciously 'try,' the better you perform. Your subconscious knows what to do. Get out of its way."

## Psych-Ups & Psych-Outs

### Psych-Ups (Helpful)

**Internal motivation:**
- Recall your best swims
- Remember why you love swimming
- Focus on your strengths
- Trust your preparation

**External cues:**
- Coach's encouragement
- Teammate support
- Music (if allowed)
- Positive self-talk

**Effective self-talk:**
- "I'm ready"
- "I've trained for this"
- "I'm strong"
- "Breathe easy, let it flow"

---

### Psych-Outs (Harmful)

**What NOT to do:**

1. **Worry about opponents:**
   - You can't control them
   - Focus on YOUR race

2. **Overthink technique:**
   - Trust your training
   - Subconscious knows what to do

3. **Set outcome-dependent goals:**
   - "I MUST win" = pressure
   - Better: "I will execute my race plan"

4. **Get psyched up too early:**
   - Peak arousal on the blocks, not 2 hours before
   - Stay calm, conserve energy

5. **Negative self-talk:**
   - "I'm not ready"
   - "They're faster than me"
   - "What if I fail?"
   - **All destructive**

---

### Dealing with Pre-Race Nerves

**Nerves are NORMAL** (even helpful in moderation)

**Convert anxiety to excitement:**
- Reframe: "I'm nervous" → "I'm excited"
- Nerves = your body preparing to perform
- Channel energy into your race

**If overwhelmed:**
1. "Let go" (release physical tension)
2. "Breathe easy" (5-10 deep belly breaths)
3. Visualize (see yourself performing well)
4. Focus on process, not outcome

## The Coach's Role

### What Coaches Should Provide

**Before the race:**
- Clear race objectives (time, pace, strategy)
- Technical reminders (1-2 cues max)
- Confidence boost

**During the race:**
- Observation (take splits, watch technique)
- Mental notes for feedback

**After the race:**
- **Objective evaluation** (technical feedback)
- "How successfully did you achieve your aims?"
- "How might you improve next time?"

**What coaches SHOULD NOT do:**
- Monopolize swimmer's attention
- Provide only emotional reactions
- Expect swimmers to be dependent

---

### What Swimmers Should Do

**Be your own swim coach:**
- View meet as opportunity to analyze performance
- Take intellectual, objective point of view (not emotional)
- Review your race plan
- Evaluate execution
- Identify improvements

**With 20+ swimmers per coach:**
- You won't get constant attention
- That's GOOD (builds independence)
- Successful swimmers become self-sufficient

**Schubert's goal:**
> "The aim of most swim coaches is to work themselves out of a job, to make their swimmers so independent that the coach is needed less and less."
