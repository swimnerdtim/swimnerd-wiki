# Schneider's 5-Level Training System: Train by Feel & Heart Rate

**Source:** *The Swimmer's Workout Handbook* by Terri Schneider  
**Context:** How to use perceived exertion and heart rate zones to execute workouts correctly  
**Audience:** Coaches programming workouts, swimmers learning to self-regulate intensity

---

## Why Training Levels Matter

**The problem with "swim hard":**
- Too vague
- Everyone's "hard" is different
- Leads to overtraining or undertraining
- Impossible to track or replicate

**Schneider's solution:** 5 distinct training levels (L1-L5) based on:
1. **Purpose** — What physiological system you're targeting
2. **Perceived exertion** — How it feels (Borg PE Scale)
3. **Heart rate** — % of max HR (if you track it)

**Key insight:** You can train effectively using ANY of these three methods. Pick the one that works for you.

---

## How to Use the System

### Method 1: Train by Purpose (No Tools Needed)
**Best for:** Coaches writing workouts, beginners learning to train

**Example:**
- "This set is for **improving lactate threshold**, so swim at L4."
- Swimmer reads L4 chart → "difficult to speak, challenging effort"
- Swimmer swims at that intensity

---

### Method 2: Train by Perceived Exertion (Borg PE Scale)
**Best for:** Most swimmers, self-coached athletes

**Uses the Borg Scale (6-20):**
- 9-10 = Extremely easy
- 11-13 = Moderate
- 14-15 = Somewhat hard
- 16-18 = Hard
- 19-20 = Very, very hard

**Example:**
- Workout says "L3"
- Chart says PE = 14-15
- Swimmer asks: "Can I have short conversations?" → Yes → You're at L3

---

### Method 3: Train by Heart Rate (HR Monitor Required)
**Best for:** Data-driven athletes, those with HR monitors

**Requires knowing your max HR:**
- **NOT from a formula** (220 - age is inaccurate!)
- **From a max HR test** (supervised, all-out effort to failure)

**Example:**
- Max HR = 180 bpm
- Workout says "L4"
- Chart says "85-90% of max"
- Target = 153-162 bpm
- Swim at that HR zone

**Warning:** If you haven't taken a max HR test, stick to perceived exertion. Formulas can be off by 10-20 bpm.

## The 5 Training Levels: Full Chart

### Level 1: Recovery

**Used for:**
- Recovery between hard sessions
- Warm-up
- Cool-down
- Baseline endurance

**Perceived Exertion (PE):** 9-10  
- You can talk easily
- Effort is extremely easy
- Could swim for hours at this pace

**Heart Rate Range:** 65-75% of max  
**Example (max HR = 180):** 117-135 bpm

**Coaching cue:** "Conversational pace. If you can't talk in full sentences, slow down."

**Typical uses in workouts:**
- Warm-up swimming
- Cool-down swimming
- Active recovery days
- Between hard intervals (when resting)

---

### Level 2: Aerobic

**Used for:**
- Improving aerobic capacity (oxygen utilization)
- Warm-up / cool-down
- Longer training swims
- Distance races (1500+)

**Perceived Exertion (PE):** 11-13  
- Conversations are comfortable
- Effort is moderate to easy
- Breathing is controlled, rhythmic

**Heart Rate Range:** 75-80% of max  
**Example (max HR = 180):** 135-144 bpm

**Coaching cue:** "Sustainable pace. You could hold this for 20-30 minutes."

**Typical uses in workouts:**
- Main warm-up sets (300-500 choice)
- Kick sets
- Pull sets (when not specified otherwise)
- Longer aerobic intervals (400+)

---

### Level 3: High-End Aerobic to Low Anaerobic

**Used for:**
- Improving lactate system
- Intervals
- Tempo training
- Long- to moderate-distance training/races (200-800)

**Perceived Exertion (PE):** 14-15  
- Short conversations are possible
- Effort is moderate to challenging
- Breathing is elevated but controlled

**Heart Rate Range:** 80-85% of max  
**Example (max HR = 180):** 144-153 bpm

**Coaching cue:** "Race pace for middle distance. You can say a few words but don't want to."

**Typical uses in workouts:**
- Main set intervals (4×200, 8×100)
- Pull sets at moderate effort
- IM work
- Stroke-specific sets

**Key difference from L2:** You feel it. L2 feels easy-moderate. L3 feels moderate-challenging.

---

### Level 4: Lactate or Anaerobic Threshold

**Used for:**
- Improving ability to mobilize lactate for longer periods
- Intervals
- Moderate- to short-distance training/races (100-200)

**Perceived Exertion (PE):** 16-18  
- Difficult to speak
- Effort is challenging to difficult
- Breathing is heavy, controlled with effort

**Heart Rate Range:** 85-90% of max  
**Example (max HR = 180):** 153-162 bpm

**Coaching cue:** "This should hurt. You're working at threshold. One word answers only."

**Typical uses in workouts:**
- Main set race-pace work (8×100 on threshold)
- Hard pull sets
- Kick sets at high effort
- Broken swims (e.g., 400 broken at 100 w/:10R)

**Key marker:** You're building lactate faster than you can clear it. You'll feel the burn.

---

### Level 5: Sub-Maximum to Maximum Effort

**Used for:**
- Training fast-twitch muscles
- Developing power, strength, and speed
- Intervals
- Sprint training and events (25-50)

**Perceived Exertion (PE):** 19-20  
- Breathing is labored
- Effort is very difficult
- Cannot speak

**Heart Rate Range:** 90-100% of max  
**Example (max HR = 180):** 162-180 bpm

**Coaching cue:** "All out. Empty the tank. This is your fastest swimming."

**Typical uses in workouts:**
- Sprint sets (8×25, 4×50)
- Broken sprints
- Race simulation (swim your 50 best time)
- Max effort tests

**Key marker:** You can't sustain this for more than 30-60 seconds. If you can, it's not L5.

**Note on max HR:** Maximum HR is genetic, declines with age, and CANNOT be trained higher. You can train to be better at REACHING max HR, but the number itself is fixed.

## The Borg Perceived Exertion (PE) Scale

### Full 6-20 Scale

| PE Score | Feeling | Corresponding Level |
|----------|---------|---------------------|
| 6 | No exertion at all | — |
| 7-8 | Extremely light | — |
| **9-10** | **Very light** | **L1** |
| **11-13** | **Light to moderate** | **L2** |
| **14-15** | **Somewhat hard** | **L3** |
| **16-18** | **Hard to very hard** | **L4** |
| **19-20** | **Extremely hard** | **L5** |

**How to use it:**
1. Finish an interval
2. Ask yourself: "How hard was that?"
3. Match your feeling to the scale
4. Adjust next interval if needed

**Why it works:** Perceived exertion correlates strongly with HR and lactate levels. Your body knows how hard you're working.

## Practical Application: How to Dial In Your Levels

### Step 1: Learn What Each Level Feels Like

**Workout:**
```
200 freestyle (L1)
— Note: Can I talk easily? Yes? That's L1.

200 freestyle (L2)
— Note: Can I talk comfortably? Yes? That's L2.

200 freestyle (L3)
— Note: Can I say a few words? Yes. Do I want to? Not really. That's L3.

100 freestyle (L4)
— Note: Is talking difficult? Yes. Am I breathing hard? Yes. That's L4.

50 freestyle (L5)
— Note: Can I talk? NO. Is this all-out? YES. That's L5.
```

**Do this once a month.** Your levels will shift as fitness improves.

---

### Step 2: Use "Talk Test" During Sets

**During warm-up (L2):**
- Swim next to a lane-mate
- Try to have a conversation
- If you can't → slow down, you're at L3+

**During main set (L4):**
- Try to count out loud to 10 mid-interval
- If you can → you're at L3, push harder
- If you can't → you're at L4+, perfect

**During sprint set (L5):**
- If you can think clearly mid-sprint → not L5
- If your brain is saying "OW OW OW" → that's L5

---

### Step 3: Track Performance by Level

**Keep a log:**

| Date | Set | Target | Actual PE | Time | Notes |
|------|-----|--------|-----------|------|-------|
| 4/6 | 4×200 | L4 | 16-17 | 2:45 avg | Hard but manageable |
| 4/13 | 4×200 | L4 | 15-16 | 2:40 avg | Easier — fitter! |

**What you learn:**
- Same PE score, faster times = you're getting fitter
- Same times, lower PE = you're recovering better
- Same times, higher PE = you're fatigued or overtrained

## Common Training Level Mistakes

### ❌ Mistake 1: Swimming Everything at L4-L5
**Symptoms:** Always tired, hitting a plateau, getting slower  
**Why it happens:** "No pain, no gain" mentality  
**Fix:** 70-80% of training should be L2-L3. Save L4-L5 for 1-2 key sets per week.

**Better approach:**
- Monday: L2 aerobic (2500m)
- Wednesday: L3-L4 threshold (2000m)
- Friday: L2 with L5 sprints (2000m)

---

### ❌ Mistake 2: Never Going Hard Enough
**Symptoms:** Workouts feel comfortable, no improvement  
**Why it happens:** Fear of discomfort, misreading PE scale  
**Fix:** Force yourself to hit L4-L5 once a week. If you're not uncomfortable, you're not there.

**Test yourself:**
- Swim 8×50 at what you THINK is L5
- If your times are within 2 seconds of each other, you're probably at L4
- If times fall apart (rep 1: :30, rep 8: :38), you went too hard early
- If you finish feeling like you could do 4 more, you didn't go hard enough

---

### ❌ Mistake 3: Confusing "Fast" with "High Level"
**Problem:** Sprinter swims 25 in :12 at L2. Distance swimmer swims 25 in :18 at L5.

**Reality:** Training levels are RELATIVE TO YOU, not to absolute speed.

**Fix:** Stop comparing times across swimmers. Compare effort.

---

### ❌ Mistake 4: Ignoring Recovery Levels (L1-L2)
**Problem:** Skipping warm-up, cool-down, or swimming them too hard  
**Why it matters:** No adaptation without recovery. L1-L2 sets help you absorb hard training.

**Fix:** Treat L1-L2 as seriously as L5. They're not "junk" miles — they're recovery miles.

## Heart Rate Training: Deep Dive

### When to Use HR Monitoring

**Use HR if:**
- You have a waterproof HR monitor
- You've taken a max HR test
- You want objective data on effort
- You're recovering from injury (need to cap effort)

**Skip HR if:**
- You don't have a monitor (no problem, use PE)
- You haven't tested max HR (formulas are too inaccurate)
- You find it distracting
- You're a beginner (learn to feel first)

---

### How to Take a Max HR Test

**Warning:** This is HARD. Do it with supervision, ideally with a coach or training partner.

**Protocol:**
1. Warm up 10-15 minutes
2. Swim 100 all-out (close to 100% effort)
3. Rest 1:00
4. Swim 100 all-out (trying to beat previous time)
5. Check HR immediately after
6. Rest 2:00
7. Swim 50 all-out
8. Check HR immediately after
9. **Highest number = max HR**

**Frequency:** Test 1-2× per year. Max HR declines ~1 bpm per year after age 25.

---

### Training by HR Zones: Example Week

**Monday (Aerobic base, L2):**
```
Warm-up: 400 @ 130-140 bpm (75-80% max)
Main: 6×200 @ 140-150 bpm (75-80% max)
Cool-down: 200 @ 120-130 bpm (65-75% max)
```

**Wednesday (Threshold, L4):**
```
Warm-up: 400 @ 130-140 bpm
Main: 8×100 @ 155-165 bpm (85-90% max)
Cool-down: 200 @ 120-130 bpm
```

**Friday (Speed, L5):**
```
Warm-up: 400 @ 130-140 bpm
Main: 12×25 @ 165-180 bpm (90-100% max)
Cool-down: 300 @ 120-130 bpm
```

**Saturday (Recovery, L1):**
```
1000 continuous @ 115-135 bpm (65-75% max)
```

## Training Level Distribution: The 80/20 Rule

### Optimal Training Time by Level (Weekly)

**For most swimmers:**
- **L1-L2 (Easy/Aerobic):** 70-80% of total volume
- **L3 (Tempo):** 10-15% of total volume
- **L4-L5 (Threshold/Sprint):** 10-15% of total volume

**Example week (10,000m total):**
- L1-L2: 7,500m (warm-ups, cool-downs, easy sets)
- L3: 1,500m (tempo main sets)
- L4-L5: 1,000m (threshold/sprint work)

**Why this works:**
- Builds huge aerobic base (L1-L2)
- Allows recovery between hard sessions
- Maximizes adaptation from L4-L5 (because you're fresh)

---

### What Happens If You Flip It?

**Bad distribution (common mistake):**
- L1-L2: 20%
- L3: 30%
- L4-L5: 50%

**Result:**
- Always tired
- Immune system suppressed
- Performance plateau or decline
- Injury risk goes up

**Elite athletes train easy 70-80% of the time.** Amateurs often train hard 70-80% of the time. That's why amateurs plateau.

## Adjusting Levels by Season

### Off-Season (Base Phase)
**Focus:** Build aerobic engine

**Distribution:**
- L1-L2: 85%
- L3: 10%
- L4-L5: 5%

**Sample week:**
- 3× aerobic swims (L2)
- 1× tempo swim (L3)
- 1× sprint play (L5, low volume)

---

### Pre-Season (Build Phase)
**Focus:** Increase threshold capacity

**Distribution:**
- L1-L2: 70%
- L3: 20%
- L4-L5: 10%

**Sample week:**
- 2× aerobic swims (L2)
- 2× threshold swims (L4)
- 1× sprint swim (L5)

---

### In-Season (Race Phase)
**Focus:** Maintain fitness, sharpen speed

**Distribution:**
- L1-L2: 60%
- L3: 15%
- L4-L5: 25%

**Sample week:**
- 1× easy swim (L1-L2)
- 2× race-pace swims (L4)
- 2× speed swims (L5)

---

### Taper (Peak Phase)
**Focus:** Recover, maintain sharpness

**Distribution:**
- L1-L2: 70%
- L3: 10%
- L4-L5: 20% (but low volume)

**Sample week:**
- 2× easy swims (L1-L2, short)
- 1× race-pace swim (L4, very short)
- 1× speed swim (L5, very short)

## Actionable Coaching Applications

### For Beginners: Start with 3 Levels

**Simplify to:**
- **Easy (L1-L2):** Can talk
- **Moderate (L3-L4):** Breathing hard, can't talk much
- **Hard (L5):** All-out, can't talk

**Workout example:**
```
300 easy
4×50 moderate w/:20R
4×25 hard w/:30R
200 easy
```

**Progress to 5 levels once they can reliably differentiate these 3.**

---

### For Intermediate Swimmers: Learn to Self-Regulate

**Workout with choice:**
```
300 choice (L2)
200 kick (L2)
8×100 freestyle (L3-L4) w/:20R
— Swimmer chooses: L3 if tired, L4 if feeling good
6×50 freestyle (L5) w/:30R
200 choice (L1)
```

**Goal:** Teach swimmers to listen to their body and adjust.

---

### For Advanced Swimmers: Precision Training

**Workout with HR targets:**
```
400 @ 130-140 bpm (L2)
200 @ 140-150 bpm (L3)

6×200 @ 150-160 bpm (L4) w/:30R
— Must stay IN zone, not above/below

10×50 @ 165-180 bpm (L5) w/:40R

300 @ 120-130 bpm (L1)
```

**Goal:** Teach precise energy system targeting.

## Key Quotes

> "Each training level is described by: what it is used for, one's 'perceived exertion', and corresponding heart rate (HR) range."

> "You can choose which method of evaluating your intensity level suits you; however, in order to use the '% of max' numbers within each level, you must know your max HR."

> "Maximum HR is pre-set, or genetic, and can be determined optimally through a max heart rate test. Maximum HR will decline with age. We cannot train our max HR to become higher, but we can train to be better at taking a max HR test."
