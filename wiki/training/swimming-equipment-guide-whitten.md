# Swimming Training Equipment: The Complete Guide

**Source:** *The Complete Book of Swimming* by Dr. Phillip Whitten (1994), Chapter 14  
**Context:** Practical guide to using training tools effectively  
**Audience:** Coaches building equipment-based sets, swimmers learning proper use

---

## Overview: Tools for Isolation, Resistance, and Speed

Training equipment allows you to:
1. **Isolate** specific muscle groups (kick board = legs only, pull buoy = arms only)
2. **Add resistance** (hand paddles = strength building)
3. **Increase speed/power** (fins = leg strength + ankle flexibility)
4. **Improve technique** (forcing proper mechanics)

**Key principle:** Equipment is a TOOL, not a toy. Used correctly, it accelerates improvement. Used incorrectly, it causes injury or reinforces bad habits.

---

## The Pace Clock: Foundation of Interval Training

**What it is:** Large clock with black second/minute hands, mounted at pool end(s).

**Why it matters:** Interval training (the foundation of swim conditioning) requires precise timing.

### How to Read It:

**Example: 5 x 200 on 3:30** (five 200-yard swims, leaving every 3:30)

1. Start first 200 when second hand "on the 60" (pointing straight up)
2. Swim 200 in 2:50
3. You get **40 seconds rest** (3:30 - 2:50 = 0:40)
4. Start second 200 "on the 30" (when second hand points to 6)
5. Repeat

**Coaching note:** Teach swimmers to track their own intervals. Builds autonomy and race-day time awareness.

## Kick Board: Leg Isolation & Cardiovascular Overload

**Dimensions:** ~20" x 10", buoyant foam/plastic, rounded edges

**Purpose:**
- Isolate leg muscles
- Tone/strengthen legs
- Elevate heart rate (legs are far from heart → requires high cardiac output)
- Practice kick technique

### Proper Grip:
- Hold board **2/3 of the way up** (not at top, not at bottom)
- Hands on each side
- **Why:** Gripping too far back → legs sink; gripping top → chest rides board, butt/feet pop out

### Expected Experience for Beginners:
**Week 1:** 
- First lap = feels impossible, takes forever
- "Am I even moving?!"

**Week 2:**
- 2 laps with rest between
- Each lap faster than Day 1

**Week 3:**
- 2 laps without rest

**Week 4:**
- 4-6 laps continuous

**Week 8:**
- 16 laps (400 yards) continuous

**Whitten anecdote:** Friend wanted to firm thighs/butt. Day 1, nearly gave up after one lap. Week 2, kicked 400 yards continuously.

**Coaching tip:** Don't let beginners quit after first brutal kick set. Progress comes FAST.

### Common Mistakes:
- Kicking from knees (should kick from hips)
- Head too high (causes drag)
- Gripping board wrong (causes poor body position)

## Pull Buoy: Arm Isolation & Upper-Body Strength

**What it is:** Two small foam cylinders (~5-7" long, 13" circumference) connected by adjustable cord.

**Purpose:**
- Isolate arms (no kicking)
- Improve stroke technique
- Strengthen arms/shoulders/chest/back
- Often used with hand paddles for added resistance

### Proper Use:
1. Place buoy **between thighs**
2. Grip tightly with legs (don't let it slide down)
3. Swim with arms only—no kicking
4. Buoy keeps legs elevated = proper body position

### Why People Pull Faster Than They Swim:
- Buoy elevates heavy leg muscles → no energy spent maintaining body position
- All effort goes to propulsion
- Many swimmers pull 100s **faster** than they swim 100s

### Flip Turns with Pull Buoy:
- **Yes, you can do them**
- Takes practice but becomes automatic
- Squeeze buoy tighter as you approach wall

### Sample Pull Set:
**5 x 100 on 1:20**
- Focus: Stroke technique
- Breathing: Bilateral (every 3 strokes)
- Hand position: Flat palm, high elbow
- Goal: Feel the S-curve pull pattern

**Coaching application:** Use pulling during warm-up for technique focus OR as main set for upper-body strength.

## Hand Paddles: Resistance Training for the Pool

**What they are:** Hard plastic rectangles with rubber tubing straps on top.

**Purpose:**
- Strengthen arms, shoulders, chest, upper back
- Force correct high-elbow pull technique
- Increase cardiovascular load (heart works harder)

### How to Wear:
1. Slide fingers through first arc of tubing
2. Place middle finger through second arc
3. DO NOT grip edges with thumb/pinky

**Why not grip edges:**
- Defeats the purpose
- Paddles *should* slip if you drop elbow
- Slipping = instant feedback on bad technique

### The Teaching Function:
**Paddles force proper mechanics.**
- High elbow → paddle grips water, pulls efficiently
- Dropped elbow → paddle slips, zero propulsion

**Even when fatigued,** paddles enforce good habits.

### Progressive Use (Avoid Injury):
1. **Week 1:** 200 yards total with paddles
2. **Week 2:** 400 yards
3. **Week 3:** 600 yards
4. Build gradually

**WARNING:** Paddles stress shoulders significantly. At first sign of pain, STOP. Adult swimmers (especially Masters) are prone to shoulder injury from overuse.

### Common Combinations:
- **Paddles + pull buoy:** Maximum upper-body resistance
- **Paddles alone:** Full-body but arms emphasized

**Sample paddle set:**
**4 x 100 with paddles & pull buoy on 1:40**
- Goal: Power + technique
- Rest: 15-20 seconds per 100
- Focus: High elbow, complete extension

## Fins: Speed, Power, and Ankle Flexibility

**Purpose:**
- Develop leg strength
- Practice swimming at race speed
- Increase ankle flexibility
- **Rehab sprained/strained ankles** (water dampens sudden stress)

### How Fins Work:
- Increase foot surface area → more pressure on water → faster movement
- Allows body to experience what race speed *feels like*
- Builds muscle memory for proper body position at speed

### The Ankle Flexibility Benefit:
- Swimming requires flexible ankles (pointed toes)
- Fins stretch ankles through full range of motion
- Gradual, water-resisted stretching (safest form)

### Ankle Rehab (Dr. James Larson, *The Physician and Sportsmedicine*):
- Fins help recover strength + range of motion after ankle injury
- Water provides resistance without sudden stress
- Faster recovery than land-based rehab alone

### Sample Fin Set (Warm-Up):
**4 x 50 kick with fins, 15 seconds rest**
- Dolphin kick on back
- Focus: Core engagement, ankle flexibility
- Feel: Power from hips, not knees

### Sample Fin Set (Main):
**8 x 100 with fins on 1:20**
- Goal: Train at race pace
- Fins allow sustained speed without burnout
- Builds aerobic capacity at higher speeds

**Coaching tip:** Use fins to teach FEEL of fast swimming before athlete is strong enough to sustain it naturally.

## Equipment Guidelines: Whitten's Principles

### 1. Build Gradually
**Don't:**
- First day with paddles: 2000 yards
- First day kicking: 800 yards until failure

**Do:**
- Start small (200-400 yards)
- Add 10-15% per week
- Listen to your body

### 2. Use Tools for Specific Purposes
- **Kick board** = leg strength + cardio
- **Pull buoy** = arm technique + upper body strength
- **Paddles** = resistance + technique enforcement
- **Fins** = leg power + ankle flexibility + speed training

### 3. Stop at Pain
- Soreness = normal adaptation
- **Pain = warning signal**
- Shoulder pain with paddles? Stop immediately.
- Hip pain kicking? Reduce volume.

### 4. Combine Tools Strategically
**Effective combinations:**
- Pull buoy + paddles = maximum arm resistance
- Fins + kick board = leg power overload
- Pull buoy alone = technique focus

**Avoid:**
- Fins + paddles (too much too fast)
- Kick board + pull buoy (contradiction)

## Sample Equipment-Based Workout

**Total: 3000 yards**

**Warm-Up (600 yards):**
- 200 swim (easy)
- 200 pull (focus technique)
- 200 kick with fins

**Main Set 1 - Pull (800 yards):**
- 8 x 100 pull with paddles & buoy on 1:30
- Goal: Power + high elbow position

**Main Set 2 - Kick (600 yards):**
- 6 x 100 kick on 2:00
- Alternate: 1 with board, 1 with fins

**Sprint Set (400 yards):**
- 4 x 100 with fins on 1:20
- Goal: Race pace feel
- 95% effort

**Cool-Down (600 yards):**
- 300 swim (easy, choice of stroke)
- 200 pull (slow, focus on breathing)
- 100 easy kick

**Equipment used:** Pace clock, pull buoy, hand paddles, kick board, fins

## Key Takeaways

1. **Equipment accelerates improvement** when used correctly
2. **Start light, build gradually**—injury prevention is paramount
3. **Each tool has a purpose**—use strategically, not randomly
4. **Pain ≠ gain**—stop at first sign of injury
5. **Pace clock is non-negotiable** for structured training
6. **Beginners improve FAST** with equipment-based training (first 2-3 weeks show dramatic gains)
