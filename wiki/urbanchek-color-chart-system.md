## Overview

Jim Richardson presents the **Urbanchek Color Chart System** — a scientifically-based training methodology using color-coded zones to prescribe individualized training paces based on physiological testing. Developed at the University of Michigan with Jon Urbanchek and grounded in European exercise physiology research, this system uses heart rate and pace correlations to keep swimmers training in the correct energy zones.

**Why it matters:** Prevents overtraining, provides individualized pacing, and allows coaches to prescribe workouts that target specific physiological adaptations.

**The article that started everything:**
- Melanie Urbanchek brought Jon an article from the *European Journal of Sports Medicine*
- Authors: Jan Olbrich and Örjan Madsen (both of whom Jim has met)
- Research: Correlated lactate acid levels in blood with training paces
- Used a **T30 test** (30-minute steady-state swim) to establish threshold paces

**Jim's reaction:**

**What they did:**
- Took the European research percentages

## The T30 Test: Foundation of the System

### What Is a T30?

**T30 = 30-minute steady-state swim** (NOT 3,000 yards!)

**Purpose:**
- Determine anaerobic threshold pace per 100
- Establish baseline for all color zone paces
- Track progress throughout the season

### How to Conduct a T30 Test

**Setup:**
1. **Split the team in half** — half swim, half time/record
2. **Digital pace clocks** at each lane
3. **Pairing:** One swimmer on deck paired with one in the water

**Protocol:**
1. **Warm-up:** ~1,000 yards
2. **Test distances** (based on event specialty):
   - **Distance swimmers:** 3,000 yards (~1:00/100)
   - **Middle distance:** 2,800 yards (~1:04/100)
   - **Backstrokers:** 2,600 yards (~1:10/100)
   - **Breaststrokers:** 2,200 yards (~1:17-1:18/100)

3. **Recording:**
   - Deck swimmer writes down total split AND lap split (if possible)
   - **Why lap splits matter:** Shows how "homogenized" the swim was
   - Early learners go out too fast, then fade → affects test validity
   - Goal: Steady-state throughout (improves over time)

4. **Heart rate capture:**
   - **Immediately post-swim:** 10-second heart rate
   - **30 seconds later:** Another 10-second heart rate
   - **1 minute later:** Another 10-second heart rate
   - **Add all three together** → reveals energy system profile

**What heart rates tell you:**
- Degree of aerobic contribution
- How much anaerobic contribution (especially in men)
- Recovery capacity
- Individual energy system profile

### Stroke-Specific T30s

**Critical rule:** T30s should be done in the swimmer's primary stroke.

**Why:**
- Work is specific
- Breathing patterns affect heart rate (backstrokers breathe easier, recover faster)
- Breaststrokers hold breath longer → heart rates run higher

**Exception:** Do NOT swim butterfly for T30.

**Why butterfly is different:**
> "We did initially swim butterfly on our T30s because we believe work is specific. And man, our butterflyers got a whole lot better at the T30. Unfortunately, their 100 and 200 fly was slower at the end of the season than their lifetime bests."

**The problem:** Swimming a T30 really changes your fly stroke. It doesn't seem to affect breaststroke as much, but it ruins butterfly.

**For IMers:**
- Break the 30-minute swim into three 10-minute segments
- Backstroke, breaststroke, freestyle
- Get individual pace for each stroke

## The Color Zones: What They Mean

### The Five Primary Zones

| **Color** | **Zone** | **Lactate Level** | **Avg Heart Rate** | **Purpose** |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Clear** | Recovery | <2 mmol/L | ~131 bpm | Active recovery, technique work |
| **White** | Aerobic Threshold | 2 mmol/L | ~150 bpm | Aerobic base building |
| **Pink** | Aerobic Power | Between 2-4 mmol/L | ~160 bpm | Upper aerobic training |
| **Red** | Anaerobic Threshold | 4 mmol/L | ~175 bpm | Lactate threshold training |
| **Blue** | Anaerobic Threshold | 4 mmol/L | ~175 bpm | Lactate threshold training (different pace/distance than red) |

### Additional Zones

**Purple (VO2 Max):**
- ~80% anaerobic, 20% aerobic
- Can only do ~1,500 meters total per session
- **Warning:** Purple paces derived from aerobic testing (T30) can be misleading
- **Better method:** Use 88-90% of best 200 time pace for VO2 max work

### Pace Chart Structure

**Original system (broad chart):**
- Distances on top (50s, 100s, 200s, etc.)
- T30 paces on the left
- Color zones show what pace to hold for each distance

**Goal:** Prevent overtraining by keeping swimmers in the correct zone.

**The problem it solves:**
> "You've got some kids who are just huge overachievers and they, you know, well gosh, if I swim 1:10, I think I should probably try 1:07. Um, and that's not what you want because then they're going to be generating more lactic acid on that set than they should and if they do that regularly they get into overtraining."

## Individual Pace Charts: The Breakthrough

### How They Work

**Evolution from broad charts:**
- Joe Plane and Chris Garajio helped develop the formula
- Converts T30 pace into seconds → minutes:seconds
- Auto-generates individual 8.5x11" sheet for each swimmer

**What's on the chart:**
1. Swimmer's name
2. Their T30 pace
3. **Clickable distances** (50s, 100s, 200s, etc.)
4. **Clickable colors** (Clear, White, Pink, Red, Blue, Purple)
5. Automatic pace calculation for every distance/color combination

**Practical benefit:**
- Print out before practice
- Hand to the lane
- Swimmers see their name, know exactly what pace to hold
- No time wasted looking up paces
- Highly individualized training

### The T-Duration Adjustment

**Problem:** You don't want to do a full T30 in October. Too hard.

**Solution:** Regression equations for shorter tests
- **T5** (5-minute test)
- **T10** (10-minute test)
- **T15** (15-minute test)
- **T20** (20-minute test)

**How it works:**
- Enter the T-duration percentage in a blue box
- All paces automatically recalculate
- Shorter tests → slight degradation (more anaerobic contribution)
- Age groupers: Build up to T5, especially pre-pubertal kids

**Jim's recommendation:** Start season with T10 or T15, progress to T30 as fitness improves.

## Validating the System: The Step Test

### Why Do a Step Test?

**Goal:** Prove the color zones actually produce the predicted heart rates and lactate responses.

**Protocol:**
- **Freestylers:** 9x100 on 1:15
- **Backstrokers:** 8x100 on 1:25
- **Breaststrokers:** 7x100 on 1:35
- Repeat for 2-3 cycles
- Total: ~2,700 yards (aerobic-dominant, not anaerobic-driven)

**What happens:**
- Start at one pace level
- Go to next level
- Go to next level
- Measure heart rate response at each step

**Result:**
> "This kind of proves the validity of the research as we applied it to what we were doing."

**Key finding:** As you get to red pace, variability across the group is LESS. As you recover, variability increases again. (Jim doesn't fully know why — invites exercise scientists to explain.)

### Reading the Data: What Aberrations Tell You

**Example #1: RG (Breaststroker on World Championship team)**
- Heart rates were abnormally high across all zones
- Summer before that season, she got really sick at Marian Norm
- "She just did not look like herself"
- Sent her to doctors → checked cortisol, heart rates, everything
- Never figured it out
- **Jim's response:** Rested her. Gave her 4 mornings off per week.
- She swam "okay" at end of season, but not herself

**Takeaway:** The step test is a stress test. It reveals when something is wrong.

**Example #2: Distance Swimmer (NCAA Finalist)**
- Lowest resting heart rate
- Lowest clear, white, red pace heart rates
- Best recovery of anybody
- Finaled at NCAAs in 400 IM, then doubled on last day in mile + 200 fly (back-to-back)
- Finaled in both

**Takeaway:** No surprise. Terrific aerobic system. The data predicts performance.

**Example #3: Freshmen Freestylers with High Heart Rates**
- Never been exposed to sustained aerobic stimulus training
- Heart rates were "problematic"
- Some of it was talent, some was just lack of training history

**Jim's approach:** Evaluate individually, not just by averages. Look at stroke-specific patterns (backstrokers recover faster because they breathe easier).

## Reverse Engineering: Predicting Best Times from T30 Pace

### The Hypothesis

**Jon Urbanchek's question:**
> "If you want to swim a 4:05 500 free (college guy), what T30 pace do you need?"

**The method:**
- Take best times of swimmers
- Back-calculate what their T30 pace was
- Create prediction tables

### Men's Prediction Table (College-Aged)

**Example:**
- **Goal:** 4:05 500 free
- **Required T30 pace:** 55.0/100
- **Predicted times:**
  - 1650: (varies by aerobic capacity)
  - 500: 4:05
  - 200: (varies by anaerobic capacity)

**Important caveat:**
> "This is the mean of a normal curve. There are outliers on both sides. Some are actually going to be a little faster, some a little bit slower."

**Purpose:** Give swimmers a target. "If I want to go this fast at the end of the season, these are some of the things I have to do in my training to get there."

### Women's Prediction Table

**Example: Katie Ledecky**
- **T30 pace:** 57.0/100
- **Times:** 15:04 (mile), 4:24 (500), 1:40.x (200)

**Example: Emily Brunemann (Jim's swimmer)**
- **T30 pace:** 59.9/100
- **Times:** 4:38 (500), 15:51 (mile), couldn't break 1:48 (200)

**What it reveals:**
> "Katie Ledecky could go 1:40 back in the day for 200 yard freestyle. Emily Brunemann couldn't break 1:48. But they were much closer in their mile paces percentage-wise. So it just showed to me that Emily was — she just didn't have the anaerobic capabilities that Katie did."

**Emily's story:**
- Trained 10,000 yards/workout at Anderson Barracudas before college
- Very good aerobic base
- Jim tried to increase her anaerobic capabilities → improved somewhat
- Went on to become world champion in the 10K open water

**Takeaway:** The system reveals individual strengths/weaknesses. Don't train everyone the same way.

## Season Planning with Color Charts

### Weekly Training Structure (Michigan Example)

**Jon Urbanchek's schedule:**
- **Mornings:** Monday, Tuesday, Thursday, Friday, Saturday
- **Afternoons:** Monday-Friday
- **Total:** 11 workouts/week

**Jim's experiment:**
- Dropped Tuesday and Thursday mornings
- **Total:** 9 workouts/week instead of 11
- **Result:** "We swam better. We swam better."

**Takeaway:** More isn't always better. Recovery matters.

### Typical Weekly Plan (Mid-Season)

**Morning workouts:**
- Lots of pulling and kicking
- Lower intensity, technique focus

**Wednesday afternoons:**
- Low-intensity recovery
- Technique-focused
- Heart rate control

**Cooldowns:**
- Always technique-oriented
- Specific drills
- Active recovery

### Color Distribution Example (Mid-Season Week)

**Total yards:** ~60,000-70,000/week (college)

**Breakdown:**
- **Clear work:** Recovery days, technique
- **White + Pink:** Aerobic base building
- **Red + Blue:** Anaerobic threshold
- **Anaerobic power/capacity:** Shorter, harder sets
- **Alactate speed:** 15-second sprints with 1-1.5 min recovery

### The "Red Speed Bumps" Trick

**From Jan Olbrich:**
> "It's really good to sprinkle in some red shorter red stuff in between longer aerobic swims because it just stimulates the system to gear up a little more."

**Example:**
- Swimming long white pace repeats
- Throw in short bursts of red (50s or 25s)
- Back to white
- Keeps variety, prevents monotony
- Stimulates physiological response

**Benefit:** Kids like it. Adds variety to "rather mundane sets of repeats with colors."

## Butterfly Training: The Special Case

### The Problem with Long Aerobic Fly

**What happened at Michigan:**
> "We did initially swim butterfly on our T30s because we believe work is specific. And man, our butterflyers got a whole lot better at the T30. Unfortunately, their 100 and 200 fly was slower at the end of the season than their lifetime bests."

**Why:** Swimming a T30 changes your fly stroke. Doesn't seem to affect breaststroke, but ruins butterfly.

### The Solution: 200 Free + Fly Repeats

**From Bailey Weathers:**
- Swim a **200 free** first
- Gets through the oxygen deprivation at the beginning of a set
- Then kick it up a little
- Then **8x50 fly or 16x25 fly**
- Repeat the whole thing 4 times

**Result:**
- Lots of butterfly volume
- Relatively short rest
- Cruising, holding stroke
- Builds aerobic capacity without distorting technique

**Variations:** Can do 3x(200 free + fly repeats), adjust distances, adjust rest

## Age-Appropriate Application

### Pre-Pubertal Kids

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't give them paces
- Don't produce lactate intentionally
- Don't swim IM (doesn't develop stroke-specific endurance)

**What TO do:**
1. **Constantly develop aerobic capacity**
   - Swim longer distances with good stroke
   - Steady-state work
   - No paces — just "try to do this by 25, I don't care how long it takes"

2. **Alactate sprints**
   - Kids LOVE to go fast
   - Relays, 25s, fun races
   - 15-second efforts with full recovery
   - No lactate production

3. **Technique, technique, technique**
   - Bill Boomer balance work
   - Build platform → add kick → add arms
   - Distance per stroke focus
   - Film and compare to world-class swimmers (slow motion)

4. **Have fun**
   - Games in the water
   - Make it playful
   - "They're not playing a game unless you've figured out good games"

**Test sets for pre-pubertal:**
- **T5** (5-minute test)
- Build them up to be able to do a T5
- Track data, see what's going on
- Don't crush them with a T30

---

### During Puberty

**Key principle:** Gradual application of the color charts.

**What to do:**
1. **Use everything from clear to blue**
2. **Less red and blue early on** (they don't have anaerobic capabilities yet)
3. **Ensure red/blue actually produces lactate** (4mmol on average, some will hit 6-8mmol)
4. **Gradual introduction of anaerobic training**
5. **Intensities based on percentages of best time** (not just color zones)
6. **Spend a lot of time on technique**

**Why technique matters during growth:**
- Lever lengths are changing
- Torso length is changing
- Center of buoyancy vs. center of mass is changing
- **Skeletal growth precedes muscular growth by 3-6 months**
- Kids look uncoordinated during growth spurts → be patient

**Still have fun:** It's an individual sport. Make it team-oriented, but don't lose the joy.

---

### Post-Pubertal Athletes

**Full application:**
- All color zones
- Full volume
- Full anaerobic capacity work

**Still working on:**
- Technique (always)
- Having fun (always)

**Communication shift:**
> "If you're teaching your kids this system, they understand it, they know it, and they're engaged with the process, which means they have more ownership over their swimming. And then your ability to communicate with them is not authoritarian. It's transformational. It's somewhat transactional. It's informative."

**Key conversations:**
- "Why do you think this happened?"
- "Why do you think that?"
- Let them own their swimming
- "I've never been a fan of 'do because I said so.'"

## Real-World Example: The Breaststroker Who Knew

**The story:**
- Breaststroker came to Jim after junior year
- She consoled at NCAAs (her teammate won)
- She said: "Jim, I think I need to do more sprint training. I don't think [my aerobic training] is working for me."

**What Jim should have seen:**
- Her T30 pace was **4 seconds per 100 slower** than her teammate
- She didn't have the same aerobic capacity
- Needed different training

**What they did:**
- Sprinted 3 days/week (her idea)
- **Result:**
  - Finished 3rd at NCAAs in 100 breast (broke PB by 2 seconds)
  - Finished 5th overall in 200 breast (won conso finals)
  - Time would have been 5th fastest in the nation
  - **6 seconds faster** than her previous 200 breast PB

**Her response:**
> "See, I told you. I told you."

**Jim's response:**
> "Nah, you wouldn't have been that good if you hadn't done three years of aerobic breaststroke training."

**The real lesson:**
> "She was right. And if I had paid attention to heart rates and paces, I would have seen she's just not as aerobically capable as [her teammate] was. So I needed to readjust her training."

**Takeaway:** The system reveals individual differences. Listen to your swimmers. Adjust.

## Heart Rate Monitoring: The Future

### The Polar H10 (or H10 Plus)

**What it is:**
- Temporal heart rate monitor
- Attaches to goggle strap
- **Bluetooth broadcasts in real time** to iPad or phone

**Why it's game-changing:**
> "In real time you can see heart rates of people while they're swimming. While they're actually swimming."

**Jim's vision:**
> "I'm trying to get Tyr or somebody to superimpose that heart rate inside of the goggle so the athlete can actually see it in real time. Not only pace and laps, but what your heart rate is while you're actually doing it in real time. That's critical to me, I think."

**Benefit:** Athletes can self-regulate intensity. No guessing. Real-time biofeedback.

**Critical distinction:**
> "You've got some 12-year-old girls and some 12-year-old boys who they ain't your average 12-year-old."

**Example #1: The 12-year-old phenom (1978)**
- **Times:** 16:30 (mile), 5:01 (500), 1:52 (200), 4:30 (400m), 2:12 (200m IM)
- Fastest 12-year-old in the country
- **Outcome:** Didn't swim after freshman year in college

**Example #2: Stephanie Lahan (female, 12 years old)**
- **Time:** 1:48 (200 free)
- Jim thought 1:52 was fast. Then Stephanie went 1:48.
- > "Oh my goodness, I thought I knew what fast was."

**The danger of moving kids up too early:**

**Social risk example:**
- Jim had a swimmer who aged up to 13
- Parents transferred, she went to another state
- Moved into older training group
- Older swimmer "felt it was her duty to teach these girls how to drink"
- At 13 she started partying

**Jim's takeaway:**

- Okay to challenge them in a workout every now and then
- Don't do it 100% of the time
- Pay attention to social aspects of training with peer group

## Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

### 1. Using Purple (VO2 Max) Paces from T30 Testing

**The problem:**
- T30 is aerobic-dominant
- VO2 max is 80% anaerobic, 20% aerobic
- Using an aerobic test to prescribe anaerobic work → misleading paces

**The fix:**
- Use **88-90% of best 200 time** for VO2 max paces
- Based on anaerobic event with aerobic component

**Who struggles most:**
- True distance swimmers
- They don't have anaerobic capability
- Blue and purple become impossible to hold

### 2. Swimming Butterfly for T30

**Don't do it.**
- Butterflyers get great at T30
- Their 100/200 fly gets slower
- Changes stroke mechanics in a bad way

**Exception:** Breaststroke seems okay for T30.

### 3. Not Adjusting for Individual Differences

**Example: Brent Lang (Michigan)**
- **50 free:** 20.1
- **T30 pace:** 58.6/100
- Made Olympic team on 400 free relay

**Example: Amy McCulla (Jim's swimmer)**
- **1000 free:** Under 10 minutes in season
- **Relay split:** 22.0 flat (50 free)

**Takeaway:** These are outliers (>2 standard deviations from the mean). Testing reveals them. Train them differently.

### 4. Overtraining High-Achiever Kids

**The problem:**
> "You've got some kids who are just huge overachievers and they, you know, well gosh, if I swim 1:10, I think I should probably try 1:07."

**Result:** Too much lactate production, overtraining, burnout.

**The fix:** Individual pace charts. Keep them in the zone.

### 5. Not Teaching Steady-State Pacing

**Before the T30:**
- Teach cruising with repeat 100s
- **8x100 low effort** (perceived exertion scale)
- **5x100 medium effort**
- **5x100 see what you can hold**

**Goal:** They learn to pace before they test.

**Why lap splits matter:** Shows if they went out too fast. Early learners fade. Over time, they learn steady-state.

## Q&A Highlights

### Can this work for Masters swimmers?

**Jim's answer:**
- Many masters can't sustain T30 paces
- Heart rates will be problematic for 50+ swimmers
- **Solution:** Use T5 or T10 tests
- Get a number, submit those paces
- Build them up to a T5

**Key question:** Are you going to get an accurate number having masters swimmers go 30 minutes? Probably not (too diverse). T5 is more realistic.

---

### Does this apply to short course meters and long course meters?

**Jim's answer:**
> "We've done it both and we really haven't seen a significant difference. Even with the turns that you have short course versus long course, we really haven't seen a significant difference."

**Caveat:** The original European research was probably done long course (Germany).

**Interesting finding:**
> "When we transferred it to yards using the same percentages, we got almost identical results. We never correlated it with lactate, only heart rate."

---

### How fresh should athletes be for the T30 test?

**Jim's answer:**
- Always did it on a **Monday** (freshest day)
- Morning workout was not a killer
- **Why:** Wanted the test done under the same circumstances every time
- **Reliability = better validity**

**Can you just use best times from meets?**
- No. Test must be controlled.
- Same day of week, same conditions, same recovery state.

---

### For IMers: How much rest between 10-minute segments?

**Jim's answer:**
- **30 seconds** (enough time to take a heart rate)
- Now with Polar H10: Still give 30 seconds to correlate with real-time monitor
- You want 10-second heart rate to compare with what the monitor recorded

---

### Is pink hard to hold kids at that level?

**Jim's answer:**
- No, "we're pretty much equal opportunity with the colors"
- Used pink a fair amount
- Some days only did white and pink
- All distances have pink on the individual charts (Clear, White, Pink, Red, Blue, Purple)

## Key Takeaways

1. **The T30 test is the foundation** — 30-minute steady-state swim (NOT 3,000 yards). Shorter tests (T5, T10) for younger/newer swimmers.

2. **Color zones are physiological, not arbitrary:**
   - Clear = Recovery (<2mmol lactate, ~131 bpm)
   - White = Aerobic threshold (2mmol, ~150 bpm)
   - Pink = Aerobic power (2-4mmol, ~160 bpm)
   - Red/Blue = Anaerobic threshold (4mmol, ~175 bpm)
   - Purple = VO2 max (use 88-90% of best 200 time, NOT T30)

3. **Individual pace charts prevent overtraining** — High achievers will push too hard without structure. Keep them in the zone.

4. **Heart rate + pace = practical physiology** — You don't need lactate testing. Heart rate correlates well enough for practical application.

5. **Step tests validate the system** — Proves the zones work. Also reveals aberrations (illness, overtraining, individual differences).

6. **Reverse engineering predicts performance** — If you want to swim X time, you need Y T30 pace. Gives swimmers targets.

7. **Don't swim butterfly for T30** — It ruins their race stroke. Use 200 free + fly repeats instead for aerobic fly work.

8. **IMers need stroke-specific T30s** — Break into 10-minute segments (back, breast, free). Get individual paces for each stroke.

9. **Age-appropriate application is critical:**
   - Pre-pubertal: T5, no lactate, technique, fun
   - During puberty: Gradual color application, less red/blue
   - Post-pubertal: Full system

10. **Biological age > chronological age** — 12-year-olds vary wildly. Don't move kids up just because they're fast. Social development matters.

11. **Season planning:** Linear plans allow for curvilinear adaptation. Weekly structure matters. Recovery days (low intensity, technique) are critical.

12. **Red speed bumps** — Sprinkle short red efforts into long aerobic sets. Stimulates the system, adds variety.

13. **Real-time heart rate monitoring is the future** — Polar H10 broadcasts to iPad in real time. Athletes can self-regulate intensity.

14. **Teach swimmers the "why"** — When they understand the system, they own their training. Communication shifts from authoritarian to transformational.

15. **Outliers exist** — Brent Lang (20.1/58.6), Amy McCulla (sub-10 mile/22.0 split). Testing reveals them. Train them differently.

16. **Listen to your swimmers** — The breaststroker who asked to sprint 3 days/week was right. Dropped 6 seconds in the 200. Trust the data AND the athlete.

## Bottom Line

The Urbanchek Color Chart System is a **practical, scientifically-grounded methodology** for individualizing training based on physiological testing. It prevents overtraining, reveals energy system strengths/weaknesses, and gives both coaches and swimmers a shared language for prescribing and executing workouts.

**Jim's philosophy:**
> "If you're teaching your kids this system, they understand it, they know it, and they're engaged with the process, which means they have more ownership over their swimming. And then your ability to communicate with them is not authoritarian. It's transformational."

**The real power:** It shifts swimming from "do what I say" to "here's the data, here's the plan, let's execute together."

**For coaches:** Download the Excel charts (available from ASCA). Start with T10 or T15 if you're new to the system. Build gradually.

**For age group coaches:** Pre-pubertal kids do T5. Focus on technique, fun, and alactate sprints. Save the full system for post-pubertal athletes.

**For masters coaches:** T5 or T10. Adjust for age and fitness. Same principles apply, just shorter tests.

**Most important:** Test consistently, track progress, adjust training based on individual responses. The system works when you use it systematically.
